>*“The world didn’t break in half. It unraveled—thread by thread, name by name, crown by crown.”* 
___
## **The First Era — Age of Crowns**

_The Reign of Bloodlines and the Birth of Law_

The Age of Crowns saw the rise of the Twelve Kingdoms and the forging of dominion through divine right and arcane inheritance. Human and dwarven houses claimed rule by celestial favor, codifying caste and custom. Elves retreated to their silent groves, while other peoples—goblinoids, orcs, tieflings, and changelings—were pressed under heel. Magic was a noble’s tool, and law served the blood.

- The **Twelve Kingdoms Pact** carves the lands into hereditary thrones.
    
- The **High Council of Flame** is formed to license and regulate arcane study.
    
- Sub-Civilization Laws ban non-humans from guilds, temples, or property.
    
- Elven border-wards are cast, severing ties with the low kingdoms.
    
- “Blood purity” decrees restrict marriage and magical breeding rights.
    
- The Arcane Codex establishes noble bloodlines as vessels of divine favor.

## **The Second Era — Age of Chains**

_Law Refined. Voices Bound._

This era enshrined cruelty within parchment. New orders arose—clerical, scholastic, and economic—to harden the lines of power. Non-noble races were made into symbols of obedience or rebellion. And somewhere in the lowlands and forests, a new people emerged: **the Beastfolk**. None knew whence they came—only that they were not human, and thus, unworthy.

- The **College of Law and Light** issues the Codex of Harmony, defining “proper civility.”
    
- Beastfolk appear in scattered wilds and borderlands. Superstition brands them cursed.
    
- Marking Laws are passed—branding Beastfolk for trade and travel identification.
    
- Mixed-blood children denied inheritance and temple rites.
    
- Goblinoid labor uprisings crushed beneath magical collapse.
    
- “Integration Monasteries” built to erase Beastfolk culture.
    
- Clerics proclaim the Beastfolk were “birthed from failed prayer or wild spirits.”
    
- Changelings and tieflings forbidden from speaking in public forums.

## **The Third Era — The Ember Age**

_Sparks in the Dust. Secrets in Song._

Resistance took root in whispers and fires. Pamphlets, chants, and hidden rites filled the backstreets of the great cities. A strange phenomenon was seen—silent, angular ruins blooming where none stood before. Those who entered did not always return. Those who did spoke in riddles... or never spoke again. The crowns denied it. The people listened.

- Blackmarket presses circulate “the Unwritten Sermons.”
    
- Uprisings erupt across merchant quarters and working districts.
    
- Elven outcasts begin smuggling changeling youth to hidden sanctuaries.
    
- A strange ruin appears in the north, its halls shifting with no wind.
    
- Survivors from within claim visions, and some bear objects not made by hand.
    
- The first informal **Delver bands** are formed—unlicensed, outcast, and hunted.
    
- Beastfolk rebels begin fleeing to deeper woodlands and storm-ravaged highlands.
    
- Cultural fires are kept alive in song, dance, and story—hidden in plain sight.

## **The Fourth Era — Age of Collapse**

_Thrones Shatter. The World Fractures._

The old world buckled. Crowned houses fell, not to blades, but to doubt, fire, and silence. The strange places grew in number—vaults beneath lakes, doors in skyless caverns, endless stairs in forgotten ruins. All spoke of trials, monsters, and relics. No kingdom could control them. No priest could explain them. Power changed hands.

- Several noble dynasties fall to revolt, madness, or vanishing heirs.
    
- Beastfolk leaders form loose alliances, demanding sovereignty.
    
- Cities collapse beneath internal guild revolts and delver-led uprisings.
    
- Strange structures begin appearing in populated regions—uncharted and hungry.
    
- Armies desert en masse as soldiers report shared visions of impossible places.
    
- Religious schisms fracture temples—some declare the divine has moved on.
    
- Delver guilds arise—self-governing, funded by relic trade and martial contracts.
    
- The High Council of Flame is overrun and sealed behind iron and spell.

## **The Fifth Era — Age of Delving**

_Merit Ascends. Glory Guides._

In the void left by the crowns, new power grew. Those who entered the ruins and returned became legends. Their spoils reshaped economies. Their stories reshaped culture. A meritocracy formed, ruled not by lineage, but by what one could retrieve—and survive. Beastfolk, once chained, rose swiftly in the ranks, remembered now not as laborers, but champions.

- The **Free Guild Accords** enshrine delver guilds as neutral sovereign bodies.
    
- Racial restrictions are outlawed within guild territories—though prejudice lingers.
    
- Beastfolk-led companies become regional powers, trading relics for land and law.
    
- The ruins grow stranger—some now shift, react, or vanish at will.
    
- Public scrying begins—allowing cities to _watch_ delves unfold in real time.
    
- Divine miracles become increasingly rare outside of Labyrinth-touched zones.
    
- Nobility rebrands as sponsors, patrons, and financiers of delvers.

## **The Sixth Era — Age of Spectacle**

_The World Watches. The Labyrinths Respond._

Today, delving is not just a calling—it is the heartbeat of Aerathon. Guilds replace kings, relics replace laws, and fame flows through arcane threads to every home. Yet the ruins—these Labyrinths—have begun to shift again. They no longer wait in the wilds. They now bloom in the streets. And some whisper… they are listening.

- Delvers ranked globally through merit, survival, and crowd judgment.
    
- Guild tournaments and relic bounties overshadow national diplomacy.
    
- A great ruin appears in a capital city—overnight, and without warning.
    
- Memory distortion, voice echoes, and reality folds reported near major delve sites.
    
- A new order rises: the **Bellkeepers**, warning that delving has become desecration.
    
- Civil unrest brews in outer provinces; some claim the guilds have become the new tyrants.
    
- The Beastfolk banner known as the **Horned Covenant** calls for a reckoning—not of bloodlines, but of memory.
