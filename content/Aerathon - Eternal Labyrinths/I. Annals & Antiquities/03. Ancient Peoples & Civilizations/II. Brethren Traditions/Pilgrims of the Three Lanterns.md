## **Overview**

The **Pilgrims of the Three Lanterns** were neither kingdom nor faith, but a fellowship of travelers who made the road itself sacred.  
In an era when borders were blades and strangers were suspect, they built and maintained **lantern-hostels** — waystations where food, warmth, and refuge were offered to all who swore to spill no blood within the light.

Their emblem: three interlocked lanterns, each representing **Passage, Provision, and Peace**.  
These became the foundation of modern _guest-right_ and _waystone etiquette_, both later adopted into guild law.

---

## **Origin & Philosophy**

The fellowship began as a loose alliance of traders and mendicants fleeing the temple purges of the Age of Chains. Their first leader, **Yarrow the Kind**, was said to have lit three lanterns along the trade road between Louvain and Vinyot — one for each soul who’d died that day without shelter.

When he returned a year later, travelers had added new lamps beside his.  
From this act of empathy grew a continent-wide tradition.

They believed that **light itself was law**: a universal code that required no crown, god, or nation to enforce.  
A lit lantern on the roadside meant safety; an extinguished one, mourning.  
The saying _“Walk by three lights and fear no man”_ entered common speech soon after.

---

## **Cultural Practice**

Each Lantern Hostel (or **“Hearthlight”**) was part inn, part sanctuary, part message exchange.  
Pilgrims of any race or faith could enter so long as they:

1. Declared themselves without arms.
    
2. Offered a tale, skill, or small service.
    
3. Left a token behind — usually a carved charm or coin marked with initials.
    

These “guest tokens” became a primitive identification network; centuries later, the Ministry would model its **Delver License markers** on this very system.

The Pilgrims also standardized **waystones**—engraved obelisks marking distance between lanterns and towns.  
At night, they reflected light magically amplified by alchemical salts, ensuring travelers could find their way even through mist or storm.

Many waystones remain active, faintly glowing across Aerathon’s old roads.

---

## **Social Impact**

The Lantern fellowship softened centuries of xenophobia between Brethren and Beastfolk.  
Mixed caravans of artisans, monks, and hunters traveled under their protection, laying cultural groundwork for the eventual **Great Drift**.  
Every Lantern Hostel doubled as a gathering place where stories, laws, and recipes crossed species lines.

In time, the Pilgrims were recognized not as outlaws but _necessary infrastructure_.  
Cities began funding hostel maintenance and collecting “light tax” contributions, formalizing the Lantern Network into a public service.

Even now, delvers swear variations of the Lantern Oath before entering labyrinths:

> _“By flame unquenched, we walk and return together.”_

---

## **Decline**

As peace and commerce grew under early guild law, the Pilgrims’ network was absorbed by bureaucratic couriers and noble-funded caravans.  
Many hostels became tradeposts or shrines.  
Yet a few remain operational, maintained by **Lantern Wards** — quiet volunteers who keep the lights burning for no payment but remembrance.

Each year on **Lanternwake**, small lanterns are floated down the Seric River to honor those who never made it home.
