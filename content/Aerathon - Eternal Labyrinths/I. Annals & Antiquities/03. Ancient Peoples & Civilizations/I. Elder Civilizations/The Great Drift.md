## **Overview**

The **Great Drift** describes the long, uneven migration that followed the collapse of the Twelve Kingdoms and the disintegration of centralized faith.  
It was not a single march, but a thousand slow scatterings—families, guilds, and entire cultures uprooted by famine, relic corruption, or labyrinth expansion.

Where the Age of Collapse ended in silence, the Drift began with footsteps. Rivers changed course, coasts swallowed ports, and wandering peoples left behind the bones of an age too heavy to carry.

---

## **Causes**

Three converging catastrophes set the Drift in motion:

1. **The Shattering of Wards** — the magical infrastructure of the old crowns failed, dissolving trade lines and teleportation circles.
    
2. **The Delver Reclamations** — early guilds seized ruins as sovereign ground, displacing nearby populations.
    
3. **The Breath Famines** — regions once lush with divine energy grew inert; crops and prayers alike failed.
    

With no stable power to replace the fallen crowns, the world became a map of shifting camps and provisional oaths.

---

## **Cultural Impact**

Out of necessity, new **Drift Communities** formed—temporary, portable cultures built on the promise of shared survival.  
Among them emerged the first **Hearth-Stone Compacts**, small circles of Brethren and Beastfolk who pledged mutual shelter and recognition regardless of origin. These Compacts evolved into the foundation of modern delver guild charters centuries later.

Language fractured and blended. The road-cant now known as **Common** was born during this age, merging fragments of Harmonium script, Beastfolk sign, and mercantile shorthand into a lingua of trade and trust.

Seasonal festivals became migratory markers: **The Feast of First Steps**, **Lanternwake**, and **The Day of the Empty Road**—celebrations still honored in Louvain and Allemance today.

---

## **Legacy**

By the fifth century of the Drift, nomadic routes hardened into trade roads, and temporary encampments became cities.  
The Ministry of Delving Operations traces its earliest precursors to **The Compact of Saint Vellin**, a settlement of archivists who preserved route-maps and relic catalogues to prevent knowledge from being lost again.

When the **Age of Delving** finally dawned, the world’s borders had been redrawn not by conquest, but by endurance.
