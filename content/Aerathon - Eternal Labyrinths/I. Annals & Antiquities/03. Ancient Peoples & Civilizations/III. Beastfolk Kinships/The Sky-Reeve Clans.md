## **Overview**

The **Sky-Reeve Clans** once governed the very weather of Aerathon’s upper reaches.  
Each clan held dominion over a cardinal current—**North Gale, South Zephyr, East Tempest, West Cyclone**—and was responsible for maintaining its flow through ritual offerings called **Wind-Tithes**.  
To falter in their duty was to invite famine, drought, or the wrath of the gods themselves.

Yet as centuries passed and faith curdled into curiosity, reverence gave way to obsession.  
What began as solemn tribute evolved into competition, and from that competition rose the **Stormriders**—younger skyfolk who sought not to calm the wind, but to master it.

---

## **The Wind-Tithe Tradition**

Each season, the Clans gathered upon the **Eyrie-Spire of Taellen**, their floating citadel, to renew the Pact of the Winds.  
Offerings of song, crafted feathers, and captured lightning were cast into the sky’s heart to sustain divine balance.

Tithes were graded by risk and altitude:

- **Blue Tithes**—harvest songs carried on trade winds.
    
- **Silver Tithes**—conductive relics launched into stormclouds.
    
- **Crimson Tithes**—lightning rods of living metal flown into tempests by oath-pilots.
    

Each act of devotion was recorded in the **Ledger of Gales**, a tome that hummed faintly in changing barometric pressure.

The rite was less about appeasement than balance: to give to the storm was to remain remembered by it.

---

## **Rise of the Stormriders**

In the later centuries of the **Age of Collapse**, a generation of disillusioned reeves began turning the Wind-Tithes into sport.  
They challenged the tempest itself, strapping harnesses of charged metal to their bodies and diving through lightning sheaths for glory.  
These “Stormriders” became folk heroes and outlaws both, claiming they were fulfilling the Pact in their own way — through courage rather than sacrifice.

Their greatest gatherings, the **Skyraces of Daal-Ven**, attracted spectators from as far as Louvain and Arneria.  
Prizes were relic shards, wind-kissed coins, or naming rights over new routes through the upper currents.  
Where the Wind-Tithe had once been prayer, it became performance.

Still, many of the elder reeves approved in secret; they saw in the riders a renewal of the wild respect the wind demanded.

---

## **Faith & Hierarchy**

Clans were matrilineal, ruled by the **Wind-Mothers** — priest-engineers who balanced theology with aerodynamics.  
Under them served **Reeves**, sworn enforcers of sky-law who measured flight paths and collected tithe quotas from lower settlements.

Stormriders, meanwhile, existed half within and half outside the law.  
Each bore a **Tithe Brand** — a feather-mark burnt into their wing membranes — denoting which current they belonged to.  
Only four living riders ever bore the **All-Wind Brand**, signifying that they had survived flying through all four cardinal gales.

Their oaths were short and beautiful:

> _“If I fall, let the sky remember my name in thunder.”_

---

## **Technology & Craft**

The Sky-Reeves pioneered the earliest **aerolith engines**, rune-etched stones that counteracted gravity by resonating with pressure differentials.  
Stormrider harnesses integrated these with **lightning conductors** forged from salvaged Ember Collegia alloys.  
Each harness contained a sealed prayer—an inscription meant to anchor the rider’s soul should they die mid-flight.

Modern skyships, courier gliders, and Louvain’s weather towers all trace their design to the Reeves’ blueprints.  
Even the MDO’s **Arcane Breach Division** uses modified Sky-Reeve navigation runes to stabilize teleportation drafts.

---

## **Decline & Dispersal**

During the **Age of Delving**, when labyrinth anomalies began to distort local weather, the Eyrie-Spire broke apart in a storm of its own making.  
Half the clans vanished into the upper jetstreams; the survivors scattered to ground settlements, founding the first aerial guilds.

Stormriders became mercenaries and explorers, charting unstable currents above labyrinth entrances.  
Their surviving descendants, like **Chandelle of Vinyot**, carry both traditions — duty and daring — as two halves of the same inheritance.

Today, the Wind-Mothers’ songs have faded from the peaks, but every thunderhead still carries a whisper of their pledge.
