## **Overview**

The **Mourn-Howl Circles** arose during the **Age of Collapse**, when war and famine left countless Beastfolk orphaned or feral.  
Where other clans sought vengeance, the Mourn-Howlers sought catharsis.  
They believed the **moon itself absorbed the grief of the world**, waxing and waning with mortal sorrow, and that their duty was to help it bear the burden through ritual lamentation.

Their gatherings were held beneath full moons, where voices united in carefully tuned howls meant to vent communal pain and prevent the moon’s “heart” from cracking — a myth scholars now interpret as an early form of psychological and magical release.

---

## **The Circle Doctrine**

The Circles were neither tribe nor cult, but rotating communities led by **Lunarchs**, individuals who had survived personal tragedy and returned from isolation changed — their fur marked with pale streaks called _Moonlines_.

Membership required only loss.  
Upon joining, initiates carved their grief into a **Sorrow-Drum**, a hollow log wrapped in lunar-charged leather, then buried it after their first howl. The drum symbolized what pain must be left beneath the soil.

Their tenets, known as the **Four Moons of Mourning**, defined their cycle of life:

1. **New Moon — Silence:** Accept pain without voice.
    
2. **Crescent — Naming:** Speak the grief aloud.
    
3. **Full Moon — Howling:** Release what must not be carried.
    
4. **Waning — Remembering:** Turn loss into story.
    

Breaking this rhythm was said to risk “lunar poisoning,” a curse of eternal sleeplessness and echoing cries.

---

## **Culture and Practice**

Mourn-Howlers lived nomadically, moving by moonlight, trading song for shelter.  
Their camps were recognizable by **spiral fire-pits** lined with polished bones; as the fire crackled, shadows formed moving rings — the “echoes of the departed.”

Howling was not mere mourning: it was a structured art.  
Each Circle tuned its pitch to its region’s geography — canyon, coast, or forest — using natural acoustics to magnify the effect.  
The Ministry’s modern sonic mapping confirms that many old Howl sites have **unnatural reverb patterns**, possibly remnants of harmonic magic similar to Stone-Song resonance.

Even Brethren priests tolerated them; wherever the Circles settled, violent crime and nightmares reportedly dropped.  
Grief had, at last, found its proper channel.

---

## **Conflict and Persecution**

The rise of militant faiths in the later Collapse Era saw the Circles accused of necromancy and lunar heresy.  
Many were hunted, their rites outlawed as “wolf-witchcraft.”  
Some survived by **secularizing** their ceremonies — offering grief-songs as funerary services to the Free Guilds.

Ironically, these same songs later evolved into **labyrinth memorial hymns**, used by delvers to honor fallen comrades.  
The Ministry still broadcasts a variation of the **Howl of the Waning Moon** annually on Remembrance Day.

---

## **Beliefs About Death**

Mourn-Howlers taught that death is a reflection:

> _“The moon dies each dawn so the world can wake.”_

Their funerals involved burning the body while the Circle howled its life story in reverse order — a symbolic undoing of pain.  
Ashes were mixed with moon-water and buried under standing stones that reflected light even during new moons.

The Circles saw undeath as a failure of grieving, a soul trapped mid-howl, still trying to be heard.  
Their chants, when translated, contain phrases later adopted by divine exorcists as banishment formulas.

---

## **Legacy**

Though the organized Circles vanished, their influence lingers in subtle ways:

- **Delver guilds** adopted their _“First Howl”_ practice — a shared yell before entering labyrinths to release fear.
    
- **Aarakocra storm-divers** incorporated mourn-howl rhythm into flight chants for emotional synchrony.
    
- **Beastfolk temples** still train **Lunarch mediators**, spiritual healers who interpret dreams of loss.
    

The moon continues to bear scars of ancient grief: pale streaks visible from Louvain observatories align perfectly with known Circle sites.  
Perhaps the old belief was true after all — the moon remembers.
