## **Overview**

The **Pride of Seven Roads** was not one nation but seven **itinerant caravans**, each claiming mastery of a trade route that once linked the fractured river kingdoms of the south to the deserts of Arneria and the coasts of Vinyot.  
Bound by **coin and duel**, their culture turned commerce into ceremony and travel into creed.

Each Pride acted as merchant guild, militia, and judiciary in one — enforcing the **Coin-Law**, a code that settled disputes not with parchment or politics, but with ritual combat.  
Wherever the sand turned gold beneath clawed feet, the Pride’s law held sway.

---

## **Structure & Hierarchy**

At the head of each caravan stood a **Strider-King** or **Strider-Queen**, elected through trial by duel and testimony of trade. Victory granted not permanent rule but _temporary sovereignty_ until another could best them in open challenge.

Below them were the **Tally-Masters** (record-keepers and negotiators), the **Mane-Bound** (oath-fighters), and the **Velari** (youths learning both sword and scale).  
The seven roads they claimed were named not for geography, but for **virtue and vice**:

1. **Road of Dust** – endurance.
    
2. **Road of Spice** – wealth.
    
3. **Road of Iron** – conquest.
    
4. **Road of Silk** – deceit.
    
5. **Road of Ember** – invention.
    
6. **Road of Bone** – sacrifice.
    
7. **Road of Gold** – truth.
    

The seventh was mythic — said to circle the world and return to its beginning.

---

## **The Coin-Law**

The heart of Pride culture lay in the **Coin-Law**, an evolving legal tradition upheld across species.  
Every agreement, insult, or breach of contract could be resolved by duel — but not necessarily to the death.  
Each side wagered a **Sandglass Coin**, a two-faced token that recorded the duel’s terms. When turned mid-fight, the sand measured **honor**, not time: if it ran out before resolution, both parties were bound to share spoils rather than claim victory.

This custom prevented prolonged vendettas and promoted cunning over brute strength — a philosophy that shaped mercantile law across Aerathon.

Modern **guild arbitration duels**, still sanctioned under Ministry supervision, trace their origin to this code.

---

## **Culture & Symbolism**

Pride life was a performance of wealth and prowess.  
Caravans were lavishly decorated with banners of shifting dyes, their beasts painted with trade symbols and road runes.  
Clothing signified both rank and profit margin: a merchant’s sleeve-length was proportional to their last year’s success.

**Dueling etiquette** was equally ritualized:

- Blades drawn with the right hand denoted business; the left, vengeance.
    
- Bows and spears were considered “coward’s tools,” forbidden in formal contests.
    
- Combatants often exchanged jewelry or braid-clasps mid-fight — the loser’s token later displayed on the victor’s tail or mane as proof of earned respect.
    

Stories of famed Strider-Kings blend myth and memory. The most celebrated, **Taaru of the Iron Road**, was said to have dueled a sandstorm itself to protect a trade route, emerging scarred and laughing — earning his epithet “The Storm that Bows.”

---

## **Economic Influence**

By the dawn of the **Age of Delving**, the Pride roads formed the backbone of inter-guild commerce.  
Their caravans transported relic fragments, labyrinth spoils, and enchanted goods between nations.  
To this day, Louvain’s **Merchant Quarter** bears signage in the Pride’s tongue — a curving script of claw and flourish designed to be read even while riding.

The **Ledger of Dust**, preserved by the MDO, lists 3,412 recorded duels and 512 trade routes — the oldest surviving record of autonomous Beastfolk governance.

---

## **Decline**

As the Free Guild Accords formalized international trade law, the Prides lost autonomy.  
Some integrated into merchant consortia; others turned outlaw, preferring the law of the blade to bureaucratic seals.  
The **Road of Bone**, once symbol of sacrifice, became literal when a delver war caravan vanished beneath the dunes of Arneria — their duel marks later recovered from petrified remains.

Today, descendants of the Pride run courier companies and defense guilds, their symbols repainted on caravan sides — stylized claws grasping a coin.

When two traders shake hands and flick their wrists to reveal the underside of their palm, they unconsciously repeat an ancient gesture:  
the **“open paw”**, the sign that ends all duels.
