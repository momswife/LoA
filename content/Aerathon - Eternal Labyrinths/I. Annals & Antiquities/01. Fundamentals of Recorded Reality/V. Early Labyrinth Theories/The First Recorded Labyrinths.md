> _“The first Labyrinth was not discovered when someone found the first door. It was discovered when survivors realized the door had remembered them.”_  
> — **Archivist Yelna Stonevoice**, _Lecture upon the Earliest Descents_

# **The First Recorded Labyrinths**

### _Ancient Accounts, Early Descents, and the Historical Recognition of Impossible Places_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Historical Reconstruction Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice  
**Contributing Scholars:** Professor Arav Dhal-Senn, Researcher Falyn Onarra, Inspector Jao “Greyglass” Vell, Lt. Salvia Korrin  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Historical Reconstruction / Grade-II Chronological Reliability with Grade-I Phenomenological Correlation_  
**Primary Evidence:** oral histories, foundation tablets, funerary records, surviving expedition journals, relic provenance, Echo examinations, architectonic surveys

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

No scholar can presently identify the first Labyrinth.

The structures now called Labyrinths may predate:

- the Ministry;
    
- formal delving;
    
- the current calendar;
    
- the oldest surviving nations;
    
- and perhaps the division between the Two Heavens itself.
    

What can be studied is the history of mortal recognition.

This record examines:

- the oldest accounts that may describe Labyrinths;
    
- the earliest surviving descents;
    
- the terms ancient peoples used for them;
    
- the first evidence that these sites were not ordinary ruins;
    
- and the moment at which separate cultures recognized a shared phenomenon.
    

The phrase **First Recorded Labyrinths** must therefore be understood carefully.

It does not mean the first Labyrinths to exist.

It means the earliest surviving cases that modern scholars can identify with reasonable confidence.

---

## **II. The Problem of the First Record**

A structure does not enter history merely because someone encounters it.

The encounter must survive.

Early Labyrinth evidence is difficult to assess because:

- many witnesses never returned;
    
- records were destroyed during later wars and collapses;
    
- ancient cultures described Labyrinths through religious language;
    
- oral traditions changed during transmission;
    
- entrances closed before later verification;
    
- and Labyrinths themselves sometimes altered or reproduced the records made about them.
    

An old account of a temple larger within than without may describe:

- a Labyrinth;
    
- a Divine Realm;
    
- a celestial-road crossing;
    
- an illusion;
    
- or a later storyteller’s exaggeration.
    

Similarly, a tale of the dead repeating their final movements might describe:

- an Echo;
    
- a Ghost;
    
- a curse;
    
- or ritual theater misunderstood by a later culture.
    

For this reason, the Ministry does not accept age alone as proof.

A credible early Labyrinth record must demonstrate several recognized characteristics.

---

## **III. Criteria for Historical Identification**

An ancient site or account is classified as a probable Labyrinth when it contains multiple signs such as:

1. **Interior space exceeding exterior dimensions**
    
2. **Routes changing without physical reconstruction**
    
3. **Repeated or impossible architecture**
    
4. **Rooms responding to identity, intent, or conduct**
    
5. **Creatures appearing without a sustainable local ecology**
    
6. **Relics incompatible with the surrounding culture**
    
7. **Events or people reproduced from memory**
    
8. **Time passing differently inside and outside**
    
9. **Entrances opening or closing without ordinary cause**
    
10. **Survivors disagreeing about the same physical route while each retains credible evidence**
    

One sign may have another explanation.

Several appearing together strongly suggest Labyrinth activity.

---

## **IV. Before the Word “Labyrinth”**

The modern term is relatively recent.

Earlier cultures used names based upon local experience.

Brethren records refer to:

- **Deep Temples**
    
- **Unmeasured Houses**
    
- **God-Cellars**
    
- **Buried Courts**
    
- **Roads beneath Roads**
    
- and **Halls without Foundation**
    

Old Court and Beastfolk traditions preserve names translated as:

- **Places the Earth Dreams**
    
- **The Returning Paths**
    
- **Hollows That Remember**
    
- **Mouths beneath the World**
    
- **The Rooms That Follow**
    
- and **Where the Dead Remain Standing**
    

None of these terms described every feature now associated with Labyrinths.

They show that ancient peoples recognized impossible places long before they possessed a shared classification.

---

## **V. The Song of the Buried Horizon**

The oldest widely studied account is an Old Court oral cycle commonly translated as **The Song of the Buried Horizon**.

Its original date is unknown.

The surviving versions describe a migrating Beastfolk community sheltering within a cave during a season of violent storms.

The cave initially contained one chamber.

By the third night, witnesses described:

- a second fire burning beyond a wall where no passage had existed;
    
- tracks belonging to members of the community who had not yet entered;
    
- a river flowing beneath the cavern despite the region’s drought;
    
- and a distant horizon visible through a door far below the earth.
    

A group crossed the door and returned carrying fruit from an unknown season.

They claimed to have been absent for less than a day.

The community outside had waited for eleven nights.

The song’s final verses describe a child seeing her deceased mother sitting beside the second fire, repeating the same gesture of mending a torn blanket.

Modern scholars interpret this as a possible early Echo.

The song does not state that the mother spoke, changed position freely, or recognized her child.

She simply repeated the act she had been performing before her death.

### **Reliability**

The account is preserved in several geographically separated traditions.

The versions differ in names, weather, and the kind of fruit recovered.

They agree on:

- impossible interior expansion;
    
- temporal difference;
    
- a newly formed doorway;
    
- and the repeated image of the dead mother.
    

This consistency makes the Buried Horizon one of the strongest early oral candidates.

It remains impossible to locate the original entrance.

---

## **VI. The Ledger of the Ninth Foundation**

The oldest substantial Brethren record is known as **The Ledger of the Ninth Foundation**.

It was recovered from the ruins of a temple archive and appears to have been written by a master mason overseeing the expansion of a royal sanctuary.

The mason recorded that workers excavating a crypt uncovered a staircase beneath the lowest planned foundation.

The staircase was not present on the previous day.

Surveyors descended and counted nine landings.

When they returned, the staircase contained twelve.

Later measurements recorded:

- thirty-seven chambers beneath a structure capable of containing six;
    
- stone from regions hundreds of miles apart;
    
- doors opening according to spoken titles rather than keys;
    
- and a burial room depicting monarchs who had not yet been born.
    

The supervising priest declared the discovery a divine annex.

The mason disagreed.

His final entry reads:

> _“The stone is obedient to something, but not to us, and I no longer believe it is obedient to the god whose name we placed above it.”_

The lower foundation was sealed.

Centuries later, excavators reopened the temple ruins and found no staircase.

A fragment of the original lower stone was preserved, however. Modern analysis identifies it as architectonically consistent with Labyrinth-generated material.

### **Reliability**

The ledger is incomplete but possesses strong physical provenance.

Its greatest weakness is the absence of a surviving entrance.

Its greatest strength is that the author recorded practical measurements rather than religious visions.

---

## **VII. The House of Returning Doors**

A third early account survives through civic complaints rather than scripture.

The structure later called the **House of Returning Doors** was reportedly an ordinary residence at the edge of a growing settlement.

Over several years, occupants complained that:

- rooms appeared in the wrong order;
    
- one door repeatedly opened into an unfamiliar stone corridor;
    
- missing household objects were found in rooms no family member remembered entering;
    
- and visitors occasionally encountered earlier versions of conversations they had already completed.
    

Authorities accused the family of fraud, sorcery, and unlawful construction.

The house was eventually condemned.

During demolition, workers removed the outer walls and discovered that the stone corridor remained standing without physical support.

Three workers entered.

One returned immediately.

The other two emerged several days later from a burial tunnel outside the settlement.

Each carried a household plate belonging to the condemned family.

The surviving structure disappeared before systematic study could begin.

### **Historical Importance**

The House of Returning Doors demonstrates that early Labyrinths were not always discovered as ancient underground complexes.

Some appeared within:

- homes;
    
- wells;
    
- mines;
    
- cellars;
    
- shrines;
    
- or other familiar structures.
    

This challenged the early belief that Labyrinths were exclusively ancient ruins.

---

## **VIII. The Ash-Stair Descent**

The first surviving record resembling a modern expedition is the **Ash-Stair Descent**, conducted late in the Ember Age.

The entrance opened after a hillside collapsed during an industrial excavation.

Witnesses described a broad staircase descending through warm black stone while ash rose upward against the direction of the wind.

Unlike earlier encounters, the explorers entered with:

- measured rope;
    
- marked lanterns;
    
- surveying instruments;
    
- a written route plan;
    
- and a designated record keeper.
    

Eleven people descended.

Four returned.

Their surviving report describes:

- a first floor resembling the excavation camp;
    
- a second floor resembling an abandoned city none of them recognized;
    
- a chamber that changed according to which explorer entered first;
    
- a creature assembled from mining tools and burned animal bone;
    
- and a series of doors that returned the expedition to earlier rooms in altered condition.
    

The record keeper reported seeing the missing members standing motionless inside a distant chamber.

When approached, the figures repeated the same few movements and did not respond naturally.

At the time, these manifestations were classified as trapped spirits.

Modern review suggests they were Echoes created by the deaths of the missing explorers.

---

## **IX. The Ash-Stair Maps**

Three maps survive from the Ash-Stair expedition.

None agree.

The first shows twenty-one rooms.

The second shows sixteen.

The third contains only nine but includes a courtyard larger than the hill containing the entrance.

All three maps bear the same expedition seal.

Material analysis indicates that each was drawn during the descent.

Early critics cited the contradiction as evidence that the survivors fabricated their story.

Modern cartographers recognize the maps as a defining example of Labyrinth instability.

The disagreement does not necessarily mean any map was false.

Each may have accurately represented the structure at the time it was drawn.

The Ash-Stair Descent established one of the earliest principles of delving:

> **A map records where a Labyrinth was willing to be, not necessarily where it will remain.**

---

## **X. The First Recovery of a Verified Relic**

The Ash-Stair survivors returned with an object now called the **Foundation Key**.

The item resembles a simple iron key with no matching lock.

When placed against a wall, it briefly reveals:

- the nearest hidden chamber;
    
- the former location of a removed doorway;
    
- or the route most strongly remembered by the surrounding structure.
    

The key demonstrated that the expedition had entered something beyond an ordinary ruin.

It also produced the first major dispute over Labyrinth ownership.

The excavation guild claimed the key as recovered property.

The local temple claimed it as a divine object.

The ruling council claimed it as a danger to public order.

The surviving explorers argued that none of those institutions would have possessed it without their descent.

This dispute became an early ancestor of modern relic law.

---

## **XI. The First Recognized Echo Record**

The earliest surviving account now confidently identified as an Echo comes from the Ash-Stair report.

The record keeper described one missing explorer:

- kneeling beside a broken lantern;
    
- raising one hand;
    
- beginning to speak;
    
- and snapping back to the beginning of the motion.
    

The figure repeated the sequence at irregular intervals.

Its face occasionally failed to appear.

The surviving party believed the explorer’s soul had been imprisoned.

A funerary priest attempted to release it.

The manifestation continued.

Decades later, the missing explorer’s authenticated spirit was reportedly contacted during an unrelated ancestral rite and showed no knowledge of remaining within the Ash-Stair.

The apparent contradiction was never resolved during that period.

Modern soul doctrine provides the answer:

- the soul had departed;
    
- the Labyrinth retained the death;
    
- and the survivors encountered the memory rather than the person.
    

---

## **XII. The Proliferation during the Age of Collapse**

Labyrinth reports increased dramatically during the Age of Collapse.

Possible reasons include:

- new Labyrinths forming;
    
- dormant entrances opening;
    
- weakened governments losing the ability to conceal them;
    
- magical instability making them more accessible;
    
- or desperate populations entering sites previously avoided.
    

During this era, Labyrinths were commonly treated as:

- refuges;
    
- tombs;
    
- cursed ruins;
    
- divine punishments;
    
- sources of food and water;
    
- or passages away from collapsing settlements.
    

Some communities survived because a nearby Labyrinth produced:

- clean water;
    
- edible growth;
    
- useful metal;
    
- or defensible shelter.
    

Others disappeared after relying upon a structure that later changed its rules.

The Age of Collapse transformed Labyrinths from rare wonders into unavoidable elements of mortal survival.

---

## **XIII. Beastfolk and Brethren Interpretations**

The two peoples preserved different aspects of early encounters.

### **Brethren Records**

Brethren accounts emphasized:

- measurements;
    
- ownership;
    
- architecture;
    
- divine authority;
    
- and the legal status of recovered objects.
    

They preserved detailed written descriptions but often forced unfamiliar phenomena into established religious or political categories.

### **Beastfolk Records**

Beastfolk traditions emphasized:

- repeated behavior;
    
- relationships;
    
- warnings;
    
- ritual responses;
    
- and how places changed across generations.
    

They often preserved less exact physical measurement but recognized patterns overlooked by written scholars.

One Beastfolk warning appears in several old traditions:

> _“Do not teach the hollow your grief unless you wish to meet it again.”_

Modern Echo research has given this warning renewed significance.

Neither tradition was complete.

Together, they provide a clearer account than either alone.

---

## **XIV. When Separate Sites Became One Phenomenon**

For centuries, impossible places were studied as unrelated local events.

A buried temple belonged to its priesthood.

A cursed mine belonged to its town.

A returning road belonged to the clan that avoided it.

The conceptual breakthrough came when scholars began comparing accounts across regions.

They identified recurring features:

- impossible interior volume;
    
- shifting routes;
    
- symbolic doors;
    
- repeated environments;
    
- generated creatures;
    
- relic production;
    
- altered time;
    
- and preserved memories.
    

The sites differed in appearance.

Their underlying behavior was recognizably related.

The term **Labyrinth** entered scholarly use not because every structure resembled a maze, but because no path through one could be trusted to remain only physical.

The word described a problem of causality as much as navigation.

---

## **XV. The First Delvers**

The earliest people to enter Labyrinths did not call themselves delvers.

They were:

- miners;
    
- pilgrims;
    
- soldiers;
    
- refugees;
    
- thieves;
    
- masons;
    
- priests;
    
- hunters;
    
- and grieving relatives seeking the dead.
    

Professional delving emerged only after repeated expeditions proved that survival required specialized knowledge.

Early delvers developed practices that remain recognizable:

- marking doors on both sides;
    
- recording the order of entry;
    
- testing whether names altered a room;
    
- carrying physical anchors from outside;
    
- documenting deaths separately from apparent post-mortal manifestations;
    
- and comparing every map before trusting any of them.
    

These methods arose through loss.

Most early safety rules exist because someone died before the rule had a name.

---

## **XVI. Early Misunderstandings**

The first records contain several recurring errors.

Ancient explorers often assumed:

- every Labyrinth was a temple;
    
- every repeated figure was a Ghost;
    
- every reward indicated divine favor;
    
- every monster was deliberately placed as a guardian;
    
- every familiar room came from the explorer’s own memory;
    
- and every exit led back to the same world entered.
    

Some errors were harmless.

Others caused generations of death.

The most dangerous misunderstanding was the belief that recognition implied benevolence.

A Labyrinth might know:

- a name;
    
- a prayer;
    
- a childhood room;
    
- or the face of a dead loved one
    

without understanding or caring what any of them meant to the observer.

---

## **XVII. Records Created by Labyrinths**

Certain supposed ancient records may have been produced by Labyrinths rather than by the cultures attributed to them.

Researchers have recovered:

- journals written in the hand of explorers who denied writing them;
    
- maps depicting expeditions that never occurred;
    
- monuments naming rulers absent from all surface history;
    
- and tablets accurately describing events centuries after their apparent creation.
    

Such objects complicate every attempt to identify the first Labyrinth.

A sufficiently old record found inside a Labyrinth does not prove the Labyrinth existed when the record claims.

It may have:

- copied the past;
    
- predicted or reconstructed the future;
    
- combined several histories;
    
- or produced a convincing artifact from incomplete memory.
    

Provenance must include the origin of the record, not merely the age of its material.

---

## **XVIII. What the Earliest Records Prove**

The earliest credible accounts demonstrate that Labyrinths have long possessed the ability to:

- exceed ordinary space;
    
- alter routes;
    
- reproduce places and people;
    
- distort time;
    
- generate physical material;
    
- respond to Meaning;
    
- preserve death as Echoes;
    
- and adapt to mortal presence.
    

They also show that these behaviors were recognized before formal Ministry theory.

Ancient witnesses lacked modern terminology.

They did not lack observation.

Their gods, laws, and cultural expectations shaped the explanations they gave.

The underlying phenomena remain familiar to modern delvers.

---

## **XIX. What the Earliest Records Do Not Prove**

These records do not establish:

- when the first Labyrinth formed;
    
- whether all Labyrinths share one origin;
    
- whether early Labyrinths behaved exactly like modern ones;
    
- whether the gods created them;
    
- whether they became more common after the Quiet Edict;
    
- or whether Labyrinths existed before mortal civilization.
    

The absence of older records may indicate that:

- no older Labyrinths existed;
    
- no witnesses survived;
    
- no records endured;
    
- or ancient peoples understood them so differently that modern researchers have failed to recognize the evidence.
    

The historical record begins with survival.

Reality may have begun remembering long before anyone returned to write it down.

---

## **XX. Ministry Chronological Classification**

The Ministry presently divides early Labyrinth history into four broad periods.

### **Period One: Unrecognized Encounters**

Ancient accounts describe impossible places without identifying a shared phenomenon.

### **Period Two: Sacred and Local Interpretation**

Communities classify individual Labyrinths as temples, curses, tombs, ancestral places, or divine wounds.

### **Period Three: Comparative Recognition**

Scholars compare distant accounts and identify consistent Labyrinth behavior.

### **Period Four: Organized Delving**

Expeditions become repeatable, documented, licensed, and eventually institutionalized.

These periods overlap.

Remote communities may still encounter a Labyrinth through the assumptions of Period One while modern Ministry expeditions operate nearby.

History does not advance evenly.

---

## **XXI. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established or strongly supported:

1. No first Labyrinth has been conclusively identified.
    
2. The earliest surviving records describe discovery, not necessarily formation.
    
3. Beastfolk oral traditions preserve some of the oldest credible accounts.
    
4. Early Brethren engineering and foundation records provide valuable physical observations.
    
5. The Song of the Buried Horizon is a strong oral candidate for an early Labyrinth encounter.
    
6. The Ledger of the Ninth Foundation is among the oldest credible written cases.
    
7. The House of Returning Doors demonstrates that Labyrinths may emerge within ordinary structures.
    
8. The Ash-Stair Descent is the earliest surviving account resembling a systematic expedition.
    
9. The Ash-Stair record likely contains one of the first documented Echo encounters.
    
10. Early explorers commonly mistook Echoes for trapped souls.
    
11. Labyrinth activity became substantially more visible during the Age of Collapse.
    
12. Brethren and Beastfolk traditions preserved different but complementary evidence.
    
13. The term Labyrinth arose when scholars recognized shared behavior across otherwise dissimilar sites.
    
14. Early delving practices developed through repeated fatal experience.
    
15. Ancient Labyrinth records may themselves be Labyrinth-generated reconstructions.
    
16. No early account conclusively establishes divine authorship or universal purpose.
    
17. The known historical record cannot determine how long Labyrinths existed before mortals recognized them.
    

The first people to enter Labyrinths did not know they stood at the beginning of an age.

They thought they had found:

- a cave;
    
- a crypt;
    
- a temple;
    
- a road;
    
- or a place where someone they loved had not entirely disappeared.
    

Only later did the world understand that these were not separate discoveries.

They were the same impossible question opening its doors in many places.

> _“We do not know who made the first descent. We know only that something below remembered the footfall.”_
