> _“The Old Gods were never one court in the manner of the Dawn. They were a family of necessary powers—sun and river, womb and tomb, oath and storm—bound together because the world required all of them to continue.”_  
> — **Researcher Falyn Onarra**, _Those Who Remember the Shape of the World_

# **The Old Beastfolk Pantheon**

### _The Principal Gods of the Far Heaven and the Ancestral Powers of the Beastfolk_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Celestial Geography and Divine Relations Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Researcher Falyn Onarra  
**Contributing Scholars:** Warden Hessa Reedstep, Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Archivist Yelna Stonevoice, Curator Rook-of-Rings  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Principal Divine Catalogue / Grade-I Verified Identities with Grade-II Historical Reconstruction_  
**Primary Evidence:** Dust-Crowned Stelae, Sun-Barrow inscriptions, Jackal mortuary tablets, ancestral dream records, Far-Heaven relics, surviving Beastfolk rites

---

## **I. Purpose and Scope**

The **Old Beastfolk Pantheon**, commonly shortened to the **Old Court** or the **Old Gods**, is the divine tradition primarily responsible for the creation and early guidance of the Beastfolk.

Surviving tablets call them:

- **The First Sovereigns**;
    
- **The Keepers of the Living Cycle**;
    
- and **Those Who Remember the Shape of the World**.
    

Unlike the Dawn Pantheon, the Old Court was never organized around one permanent celestial government.

Its gods were joined through:

- kinship;
    
- complementary responsibility;
    
- sacred opposition;
    
- natural cycle;
    
- and the shared obligation to preserve the relationships upon which Aerathon depended.
    

The pantheon includes gods of:

- kingship and rebellion;
    
- birth and death;
    
- order and disruption;
    
- river and desert;
    
- protection and predation;
    
- creation and destruction;
    
- memory and transformation.
    

Some are gentle.

Some are dangerous.

Many are both.

Their retreat after the War of the Two Heavens caused their public temples and priesthoods to collapse. Their influence survived through ancestral roads, bloodlines, funerary rites, sacred animals, dreams, the Feywilds, and customs whose divine origins have since been forgotten.

This catalogue records the thirty-seven principal Old Court deities most widely supported by surviving evidence.

Local gods, sacred ancestors, deified rulers, divine beasts, and disputed Far-Heaven powers are recorded separately.

---

## **II. Recorded Alignment**

Each deity is assigned a **Recorded Alignment**.

This classification reflects the god’s dominant conduct, divine responsibilities, and surviving traditions within Aerathon.

It does not mean that:

- every worshipper shares that alignment;
    
- every myth describes the deity accurately;
    
- the god has never acted outside that tendency;
    
- or modern moral judgment perfectly reflects the values of the First Making.
    

The Old Court frequently understood morality through balance, obligation, and relationship rather than simple law or personal virtue.

A dangerous god might still perform a necessary function.

A protective god might respond violently when a sacred obligation was broken.

Recorded Alignment is therefore a useful summary—not a complete judgment.

---

## **III. Joined Gods, Aspects, and Names**

Old Court theology does not always treat divine identity as fixed.

Two gods may combine their authority without ceasing to exist separately. A god may possess several forms associated with different times, regions, or responsibilities.

The clearest example is **Re-Horakhty**, the joined solar authority of Ra and Horus.

Some traditions treat Re-Horakhty as:

- a distinct god;
    
- a shared office;
    
- a deliberate joining of two divine minds;
    
- or the sovereign form assumed when Ra’s celestial authority and Horus’s kingship act together.
    

The Ministry records joined names separately when the combined identity displays distinct magical authority.

This does not imply that every joined form is an independent being.

# **V. Gods of the Sun, Sky, and Sacred Kingship**

## **Re-Horakhty — The Solar Sovereign**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** A falcon beneath a solar disk encircled by a serpent  
**Divine Concerns:** Solar sovereignty, divine judgment, celestial order, rightful kingship

Re-Horakhty is the joined authority of Ra and Horus, traditionally invoked when the power of the sun and the responsibility of rulership must act as one. He was regarded as the closest thing the Old Court possessed to a high sovereign, though his authority depended upon the cooperation of the other gods rather than absolute command.

During the War, he attempted to unite the scattered Beastfolk confederations under one celestial mandate. That effort preserved many communities but also introduced the centralized authority the Old Court had once criticized in the Dawn Pantheon.

---

## **Ra — The Sun upon the River of Heaven**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A solar disk above an open horizon  
**Divine Concerns:** Sunlight, warmth, daily renewal, protection from darkness

Ra carries the sun through its daily passage, bringing light, heat, and the promise that darkness does not rule forever. His journey was understood as a repeated act of protection rather than a passive celestial cycle.

The divine war weakened many of the roads by which his light reached Aerathon. His influence now survives through solar rites, desert sanctuaries, dawn dreams, and relics that awaken only beneath natural sunlight.

---

## **Amun — The Hidden One**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A tall double-plumed crown  
**Divine Concerns:** Hidden power, breath, unseen authority, concealed purpose

Amun governs forces that exist without displaying themselves openly. He is associated with breath, invisible presence, concealed divinity, and authority that does not depend upon public recognition.

His worship survived the collapse of Old Court institutions more successfully than most because secrecy was already central to his nature. Some scholars believe Amun continues influencing Aerathon through unnamed patrons and powers that deliberately refuse divine identification.

---

## **Atum — The Complete One**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** The double crown above the first mound  
**Divine Concerns:** Completion, creation, boundaries, final form

Atum represents the moment when possibility becomes a finished thing. Old Court tradition credits him with helping stabilize the first land and establishing that creation must eventually accept boundaries.

His role placed him between Dawn permanence and Old Court continuity. During the War, both sides sought his authority to declare their preferred order the final form of Aerathon; surviving accounts suggest he refused them all.

---

## **Khepri — He Who Becomes**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A scarab carrying the rising sun  
**Divine Concerns:** Dawn, transformation, renewal, new identity

Khepri governs becoming rather than simple rebirth. He blesses the moment when a person, community, or living system emerges from one state into another without losing all connection to what came before.

His traditions survived strongly within displaced Beastfolk communities. Migrants, transformed bloodlines, and those rebuilding their identities after catastrophe continue invoking Khepri even when they no longer recognize him by name.

---

## **Horus — The Far-Seeing King**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** A falcon eye beneath a crown  
**Divine Concerns:** Kingship, sky, protection, rightful succession

Horus represents rulership earned through the defense of a people rather than merely inherited through title. His eye symbolizes vigilance, perspective, and the duty to see threats before they reach those under one’s protection.

He fought fiercely during the War of the Two Heavens and became one of the Old Court’s principal military leaders. His modern rites survive among guardians, clan leaders, scouts, and rulers expected to place responsibility above privilege.

---

# **VI. Gods of Death, Mourning, and the Ancestral Roads**

## **Anubis — Keeper of the Final Road**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A black jackal above golden scales  
**Divine Concerns:** Burial, judgment, funerary rites, protection of the dead

Anubis taught the earliest jackal kinships to preserve names, prepare bodies, settle final obligations, and protect souls during passage. His authority concerned the integrity of the journey more than the destination reached afterward.

His attempt to close the Gate of Ordered Passage at Avarra became the final provocation before the War. He now acts through psychopomps, funerary relics, ancestral dreams, and rites that remain effective despite the loss of his public temples.

---

## **Osiris — The Verdant Sovereign**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** A crook and flail above a djed pillar  
**Divine Concerns:** Death, renewal, rightful succession, restored life

Osiris governs the order by which death becomes renewal rather than meaningless destruction. He is associated with the honored dead, fertile soil, lawful inheritance, and life returning after devastation.

His murder and restoration form one of the central sacred histories of the Old Court, though surviving versions differ over whether the event preceded or occurred during the divine war. His realm remains one of the most stable known destinations within the Far Heaven.

---

## **Nephthys — Lady of the Mourning Veil**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** Veiled wings surrounding a funerary vessel  
**Divine Concerns:** Mourning, lost dead, night journeys, those who die far from home

Nephthys guards those who cannot reach familiar rites, family tombs, or ancestral roads. Her servants seek the forgotten, unnamed, and abandoned dead.

She became especially important during the War, when entire populations died far from their homelands. Modern funerary traditions often preserve her gestures without remembering the goddess whose compassion gave them authority.

---

## **Wepwawet — Opener of the Ways**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A black jackal upon a processional standard  
**Divine Concerns:** Opening roads, funerary passage, military paths, safe crossings

Wepwawet clears routes through dangerous or obstructed territory. His authority applies equally to armies crossing hostile land and souls crossing the boundaries of death.

During the War, his roads were used for both rescue and invasion. After the Quiet Edict, several surviving celestial routes associated with him became essential to moving between distant regions of the Far Heaven.

---

# **VII. Gods of Knowledge, Creation, and Sacred Order**

## **Thoth — Scribe of the First Word**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** An ibis quill crossing a lunar crescent  
**Divine Concerns:** Knowledge, writing, language, mathematics, time

Thoth preserves the measurement and description of things. He taught calculation, sacred language, celestial observation, and the methods by which spoken memory could be preserved without becoming lifeless.

His role during the Night of Empty Names remains disputed. Some traditions accuse him of helping erase Brethren records; others credit him with restoring the names before their loss became permanent.

---

## **Ma’at — The Feather of Truth**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** A single white ostrich feather  
**Divine Concerns:** Truth, balance, justice, proportion, cosmic order

Ma’at is both goddess and principle. She represents the balance required for creation to continue without collapsing into tyranny or chaos.

Her law differs from rigid uniformity. A judgment is just only when it preserves the relationships and responsibilities surrounding it. Many scholars believe the Quiet Edict borrowed heavily from Ma’at’s authority, though no complete Old Court account confirms her role in its creation.

---

## **Ptah — The Whisper-Smith**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A joined ankh, djed pillar, and was-staff  
**Divine Concerns:** Craft, divine names, intentional creation, hidden design

Ptah creates by conceiving a form, naming it, and giving that name material expression. He is associated with smiths, sculptors, builders, artificers, and those who understand that construction begins before the first tool is raised.

His teachings influenced early Archaic Weaving. Unlike Gond, Ptah emphasized that every created object inherited an obligation from the purpose for which it was made.

---

## **Seshat — Mistress of the Measured Line**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A seven-pointed star beneath a notched arc  
**Divine Concerns:** Records, architecture, measurement, surveying

Seshat governs accurate record, sacred proportion, and the measurement of land and structure. She worked closely with Thoth but focused upon the practical act of preserving dimensions, boundaries, and construction.

She became an important mediator during early disputes between Beastfolk stewardship and Brethren surveying. Her surviving traditions insist that measurement creates responsibility, not unquestioned ownership.

---

## **Imhotep — The First Learned Mortal**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A seated scribe before a step pyramid  
**Divine Concerns:** Healing, architecture, medicine, mortal scholarship

Imhotep is remembered as a mortal who rose into divine authority through service, learning, and works that preserved life. Whether he was born before or after the First Concord remains disputed.

He represents the Old Court’s belief that divinity could recognize mortal excellence without erasing mortal origin. Physicians, architects, and teachers preserve his name more openly than most Old Gods.

---

## **Khnum — The Potter at the River’s Source**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A ram beside a potter’s wheel  
**Divine Concerns:** Formation, river sources, bodies, crafted life

Khnum shapes living vessels as a potter shapes clay. He is associated with bodily form, birth, river beginnings, and the patient work required to make life capable of holding identity.

Several Beastfolk traditions credit him with refining the first physical kinships after the Old Gods determined their sacred roles. He is invoked in difficult births, bodily transformation, and healing that requires rebuilding rather than simple restoration.

---

# **VIII. Gods of Family, Healing, Joy, and Household Protection**

## **Isis — Weaver of Hidden Threads**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A winged throne surrounding an ankh  
**Divine Concerns:** Magic, family, protection, restoration, sacred names

Isis governs magic formed through relationship, knowledge, patience, and the protection of those bound together by love or obligation. She is among the most skilled divine users of names and concealed authority.

Her intervention at the Fields of Namar became one of the earliest major conflicts between the Old and Dawn Courts. She later became central to the restoration of Osiris and the preservation of displaced Beastfolk families during the War.

---

## **Bastet — The Cat of the Hearth**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Good  
**Symbol:** A black cat bearing a golden sistrum  
**Divine Concerns:** Home, joy, cats, music, sudden protection

Bastet protects households through warmth, vigilance, and swift violence against threats. Her gentleness and ferocity are not treated as contradictions.

Her worship survived in household customs, doorway symbols, children’s songs, and feline guardians. Many families honor her without knowing that their “luck cat” traditions descend from an Old Court goddess.

---

## **Bes — The Laughing Guardian**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Good  
**Symbol:** A grinning guardian mask  
**Divine Concerns:** Households, infants, laughter, luck, protection from hidden danger

Bes guards ordinary happiness: children sleeping safely, families laughing together, travelers surviving one more night. His appearance is deliberately unrefined, mocking the belief that divine authority must always appear beautiful or dignified.

He was widely loved among both Beastfolk and Brethren commoners before the War. His surviving relics are famously unpredictable, but their apparent accidents often protect the most vulnerable person nearby.

---

## **Taweret — Great Mother of the Threshold**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A hippopotamus guardian holding a protection knot  
**Divine Concerns:** Childbirth, fertility, family protection, dangerous transitions

Taweret guards the threshold between danger and new life. She is invoked during childbirth, adoption, family formation, and moments when a household is especially vulnerable.

Her fierce form reflects the violence with which she defends those under her protection. Midwives across Aerathon preserve practices connected to her even where Old Court worship is publicly unknown.

---

## **Hathor — Lady of Song and Welcome**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Good  
**Symbol:** Cow horns surrounding a solar disk  
**Divine Concerns:** Love, music, joy, motherhood, hospitality

Hathor sustains emotional and communal life. Her rites taught that music, affection, hospitality, and celebration were necessary forms of survival rather than luxuries enjoyed after danger had passed.

She worked closely with Lliira and Sune during parts of the First Concord. Their shared sanctuaries became important refuges for mixed families during the divine war.

---

## **Mut — The Great Mother**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** A vulture crown above spread wings  
**Divine Concerns:** Motherhood, royal protection, adoption, communal authority

Mut represents parenthood as chosen responsibility rather than blood alone. She protects adopted children, blended families, rulers who treat their people as dependents, and communities that accept outsiders as their own.

Her traditions became important among Beastfolk displaced from their ancestral lands. She opposed rulers who claimed parental authority over subjects while refusing the obligations of provision and protection.

---

## **Serqet — She Who Lets Breath Return**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A scorpion beneath two protective hands  
**Divine Concerns:** Healing, venom, breath, protection from poison

Serqet governs poison as both danger and medicine. She teaches that what kills in one measure may preserve life in another.

Her priesthoods became expert healers of venom, disease, and spiritual contamination. Several modern apothecary traditions preserve Serqetan methods while incorrectly attributing them to later Dawn institutions.

---

# **IX. Gods of War, Defense, and Sacred Authority**

## **Sekhmet — The Burning Lioness**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Neutral  
**Symbol:** A lioness beneath a burning sun  
**Divine Concerns:** War, plague, vengeance, fierce protection

Sekhmet is the destructive heat of protection carried beyond restraint. She defends the innocent with the same force by which she destroys those judged dangerous.

During the War of the Two Heavens, Sekhmet became one of the Old Court’s most feared battlefield powers. Several traditions claim even the Old Gods struggled to halt her once she had accepted a target as an enemy.

---

## **Montu — The Falcon of Battle**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** A falcon crowned with double plumes and a solar disk  
**Divine Concerns:** Battle, courage, martial skill, victory

Montu governs war as direct contest, courage, and the willingness to meet danger openly. Unlike Set, he does not celebrate disorder; unlike Horus, he does not require a claim of rightful rulership.

His followers served as champions, defenders, and professional warriors. Modern Beastfolk martial traditions often preserve Montu’s ritual challenges without retaining his name.

---

## **Neith — The First Strategist**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** Crossed arrows behind a shield  
**Divine Concerns:** War, hunting, weaving, strategy, preparation

Neith governs the careful arrangement of actions before conflict begins. Her weaving symbolizes plans, alliances, traps, and consequences crossing one another like threads.

She frequently opposed impulsive divine intervention during the collapse of the First Concord. When war became unavoidable, she designed many of the defensive strategies that prevented the Old Court from being destroyed outright.

---

## **Wadjet — The Rising Cobra**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** An upright cobra crowned with a solar disk  
**Divine Concerns:** Sovereignty, warning, guardianship, sacred boundaries

Wadjet protects rulers, temples, bloodlines, and borders through vigilance and sudden retaliation. Her cobra form represents the warning offered before the strike.

Her authority was often invoked to protect Beastfolk leaders from assassination during the War. Modern relics associated with Wadjet commonly remain dormant until a protected person or place faces immediate betrayal.

---

## **Nekhbet — The White Wing**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** A white vulture with outstretched wings  
**Divine Concerns:** Shelter, rulership, communal protection, high places

Nekhbet protects from above, watching broad territories and shielding those gathered beneath her authority. She is associated with rulers who shelter rather than possess their people.

She and Wadjet were frequently worshipped together as complementary guardians. Their joint symbol remains present on several Beastfolk royal artifacts whose original kingdoms no longer exist.

---

# **X. Gods of Earth, River, Sky, and Living Cycle**

## **Geb — Father of the Living Earth**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** A goose standing upon green earth  
**Divine Concerns:** Earth, stone, fertility, inheritance, burial ground

Geb governs the living ground from which bodies rise and to which they return. He represents both fertility and the physical memory preserved within land.

His disputes with Dawn gods often concerned ownership. Geb accepted settlement and cultivation but rejected the idea that a deed could sever land from every earlier obligation embedded within it.

---

## **Nut — Mother of the Starry Vault**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A star-filled arching figure  
**Divine Concerns:** Sky, stars, celestial shelter, the passage of night

Nut stretches across the world as the sheltering sky. She protects the daily passage of the sun and the nightly journeys of souls and travelers.

During the War, she concealed refugees and divine roads within the stars. Several Far-Heaven paths are still entered by following constellations no longer visible from every region of Aerathon.

---

## **Shu — Bearer of the Open Air**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** A feather supporting the sky  
**Divine Concerns:** Air, distance, breath, separation, open space

Shu maintains the space between things. Without him, sky and earth, body and breath, self and world would collapse into one another.

His authority made him important in establishing boundaries that preserved relationship without total separation. Some scholars believe the division between the heavens relies partly upon laws originally associated with Shu.

---

## **Tefnut — Lady of Returning Rain**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** A lioness beneath a falling raindrop  
**Divine Concerns:** Rain, moisture, necessary wrath, restored balance

Tefnut governs water returning to dry places and the anger produced when balance is denied too long. Her rains nourish, but her storms punish systems that consume without replenishing.

She opposed many permanent Dawn waterworks during the First Concord. Not because she rejected construction, but because she believed no structure should prevent water from completing its wider obligations.

---

## **Hapi — Bringer of the Flood**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** Bound lotus and papyrus above three river lines  
**Divine Concerns:** Floods, abundance, river fertility, communal provision

Hapi represents the flood that appears destructive before leaving fertile earth behind. His rites coordinated planting, storage, evacuation, and the shared use of river abundance.

He was among the strongest supporters of cooperation with Brethren engineers. Many early mixed settlements succeeded because Hapi’s seasonal knowledge was joined with permanent channels and reservoirs.

---

## **Sobek — Lord of the River’s Teeth**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A crowned crocodile above three river lines  
**Divine Concerns:** Rivers, strength, predation, protection, territorial authority

Sobek embodies the river as provider and predator. He protects those who respect its strength and destroys those who mistake access for mastery.

His priests defended marshlands and river settlements during the War. Sobek’s aggression made him feared, but his authority was rarely random; he responded most violently to violations of territory, water, and sworn protection.

---

## **Khonsu — The Moon-Wanderer**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Neutral  
**Symbol:** A lunar disk above a falcon’s head  
**Divine Concerns:** Moonlight, travel, time, wandering, night healing

Khonsu governs journeys made beneath the moon and the changes experienced by those who remain away from home. His movement marks time through repeated departure and return.

He maintains several roads between the Far Heaven, Feywilds, and mortal dream. Travelers who follow him may reach places no fixed celestial route can access, but they are not always returned to the time or identity from which they began.

---

# **XI. Gods of Disorder and Cosmic Opposition**

## **Set — Lord of the Red Waste**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Neutral  
**Symbol:** A Set-beast beneath a broken horizon  
**Divine Concerns:** Desert, storms, disorder, opposition, dangerous change

Set represents the force that prevents order from becoming permanent stagnation. He governs deserts, violent weather, outsiders, conflict, and necessary disruption.

He became one of the strongest advocates for open war against the Dawn Court, believing compromise had become a slow method of Beastfolk erasure. His actions caused immense suffering, but Old Court traditions remain divided over whether he betrayed divine balance or defended it through terrible means.

---

## **Apep — The Serpent Beyond Dawn**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol:** A coiled serpent swallowing a blackened sun  
**Divine Concerns:** Unmaking, endless hunger, cosmic ruin, the destruction of order

Apep is not universally accepted as a true member of the Old Court.

It is the devouring force against which the solar gods renew their journey. Where Set disrupts order so that change remains possible, Apep seeks an ending from which nothing returns.

Old Court tradition does not worship Apep. It records, names, and opposes it so that its presence cannot hide behind ignorance.

Some scholars classify Apep as:

- a primordial enemy;
    
- a failed divine remnant;
    
- a being older than both pantheons;
    
- or the personification of creation’s ability to destroy itself.
    

No interpretation makes it safe.

---

## **XII. The Old Court After the Quiet Edict**

The Old Beastfolk Pantheon lost most of its public authority after the War of the Two Heavens.

Its gods surrendered:

- direct territorial seats in Aerathon;
    
- centralized mortal priesthoods;
    
- much of their formal temple network;
    
- public political influence;
    
- and the ability to openly govern Beastfolk societies.
    

They retained influence through:

- ancestral roads;
    
- funerary rites;
    
- dreams;
    
- sacred animals;
    
- natural cycles;
    
- Feywild crossings;
    
- inherited customs;
    
- bloodlines;
    
- and Divine Remnants embedded within the world.
    

This has made Old Court worship difficult to separate from Beastfolk culture.

A household may practice a rite associated with Bastet without believing in her. A healer may use Serqetan methods under another god’s name. A family may follow an Anubian burial custom while publicly worshipping Kelemvor.

The Old Gods survive not because their institutions remained intact.

They survive because many of their divine responsibilities were woven into ordinary life.

## **XIII. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established or strongly supported:

1. The Old Beastfolk Pantheon is a broad divine court with responsibilities extending far beyond ancestry and death.
    
2. Its principal gods contributed to the creation and guidance of the Beastfolk.
    
3. The pantheon contains protective, neutral, violent, and openly destructive powers.
    
4. Old Court divine identities may combine, divide, or operate through joined forms.
    
5. Re-Horakhty represents a recognized joined authority of Ra and Horus.
    
6. Recorded Alignment is a modern interpretive tool rather than an ancient Old Court doctrine.
    
7. The Old Gods lost centralized mortal authority after the Quiet Edict but did not cease to exist.
    
8. Their influence survives through relationships that do not require temples or formal churches.
    
9. Many modern Beastfolk traditions retain Old Court origins without recognizing them.
    
10. Beastfolk identity does not require worship of the Old Gods.
    
11. Non-Beastfolk mortals may form legitimate relationships with Old Court deities.
    
12. Fey, ancestral, and divine influences must be distinguished carefully when examining claimed contact.
    
13. Set remains a member of the Old Court despite his role in escalating the divine war.
    
14. Apep is best classified as an adversarial power rather than a trusted pantheonic authority.
    
15. The collapse of Old Court religion allowed later institutions to misclassify its gods as extinct, primitive, or merely symbolic.
    
16. The surviving evidence confirms that the Old Court remains active within the Far Heaven.
    

The Dawn Gods survived through temples, scriptures, and offices.

The Old Gods survived through births, burials, storms, songs, rivers, and the names families still whisper when no priest is listening.
