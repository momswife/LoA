# **Divine Realms and Celestial Roads**

### _The Domains of the Gods, the Passages Between Them, and the Broken Routes Left by the War of the Two Heavens_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Celestial Geography and Divine Relations Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Surveyor Pell Aster  
**Contributing Scholars:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Researcher Falyn Onarra, Magister Selka Vorr, Archivist Yelna Stonevoice  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Foundational Celestial Geography / Grade-I Verified Phenomena with Grade-II Structural Reconstruction_  
**Primary Evidence:** authenticated celestial crossings, temple-gate surveys, psychopomp testimony, divine-realm fragments, relic transit records, Near- and Far-Heaven expedition accounts

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The Near Heaven and Far Heaven are not single places.

Each contains numerous **Divine Realms** shaped by the gods, aspects, ancient offices, and celestial powers associated with them. These realms are connected by routes commonly called **Celestial Roads**.

Before the War of the Two Heavens, the roads allowed gods, servants, souls, and chosen mortals to travel between divine territories and Aerathon.

The war damaged that system.

Some roads were destroyed. Others were sealed under the Quiet Edict. Several now lead through the Feywilds, the Veiled Meridian, abandoned divine territories, or spaces no longer governed by a recognized god.

This record establishes:

- what Divine Realms are;
    
- how they differ from ordinary planes and mortal territories;
    
- how Celestial Roads function;
    
- what became of them during the War;
    
- and why their surviving fragments remain important to religion, death, relic study, and Labyrinth theory.
    

---

## **II. What a Divine Realm Is**

A Divine Realm is a place where a god’s authority shapes the surrounding reality more strongly than ordinary mortal law.

Within Aerathon, a god of justice may influence a court, relic, or covenant.

Within that god’s Divine Realm, justice may become part of the environment itself.

A lie might acquire visible weight. An unresolved accusation might prevent a traveler from crossing a bridge. A broken oath might appear as a physical wound.

Divine Realms may resemble:

- cities;
    
- forests;
    
- palaces;
    
- deserts;
    
- libraries;
    
- rivers;
    
- workshops;
    
- battlefields;
    
- tomb complexes;
    
- gardens;
    
- seas;
    
- or skies without visible ground.
    

Their appearance is not arbitrary.

The realm expresses the god’s:

- concerns;
    
- history;
    
- emotional nature;
    
- remembered victories and failures;
    
- worship;
    
- and current condition.
    

A god’s realm is therefore both residence and extension.

To enter one is to enter a place built from the god’s understanding of reality.

---

## **III. Divine Realms Are Not Perfect Reflections**

A Divine Realm does not always represent the complete personality of its god.

Some realms were established before the War and preserve an older form of their ruler.

Others were damaged, divided, or altered by later events.

A god may possess several realms reflecting different responsibilities.

For example:

- a war god may hold one realm for honorable contest and another for the memory of the fallen;
    
- a death god may govern separate halls for judgment, passage, and preserved names;
    
- a nature god may possess different territories for cultivation, wilderness, winter, or renewal.
    

A realm may also preserve values the god no longer emphasizes.

This is similar to a Divine Fragment retaining the nature of a god at the moment of separation.

A merciful deity may possess an ancient fortress built during a period of fear and retaliation. The realm may continue treating every stranger as a possible enemy even if the god now regrets that command.

Divine geography should therefore not be treated as flawless evidence of present divine intent.

It is also historical evidence.

---

## **IV. The Great Realms of the Near Heaven**

The Near Heaven contains realms associated with the Dawn Pantheon and the institutions that survived their withdrawal.

Among the most frequently recorded are:

### **The Courts of the Even Hand**

A vast judicial dominion associated with Tyr, where testimony, obligation, and consequence are said to take visible form.

No single court hears every dispute. The realm contains countless chambers built for different kinds of judgment, from personal oaths to conflicts between kingdoms.

### **The House of the Final Measure**

A mortuary realm associated with Kelemvor.

Its halls do not serve as the final destination of every dead soul. They are places of classification, lawful passage, and protection against undeath or wrongful seizure.

### **The Sevenfold Expanse**

A shifting magical territory connected to Mystra and the Natural Weave.

Visitors describe rivers of spelllight, structures composed of unfinished formulae, and regions where Matter, Meaning, or Motion temporarily dominates the other Weaves.

### **The First Archive**

A vast but incomplete record associated with Deneir and Oghma.

It is believed to contain works lost in Aerathon, though access depends upon whether the visitor seeks knowledge, ownership, suppression, or understanding.

### **The Vigilant Gate**

A fortified crossing associated with Helm.

It once guarded several roads between the heavens and Aerathon. Many of its gates remain sealed, and some continue denying passage according to orders issued during the War.

### **The Fields of the Flaming Sword**

A realm associated with Tempus where battles may be remembered, studied, or repeated without producing ordinary mortal death.

Reports disagree over whether the warriors encountered there are spirits, memories, celestial servants, or idealized reconstructions of those who fought.

These represent only a fraction of the Near Heaven.

Many Dawn gods maintain lesser domains, private sanctuaries, sealed strongholds, or shared territories.

---

## **V. The Great Realms of the Far Heaven**

The Far Heaven is organized less by fixed border and more by relationship, passage, and sacred role.

Among its most strongly attested regions are:

### **The Reed-Crowned Kingdom**

A realm associated with Osiris, renewal, and the honored dead.

Its fields are described as fertile without exhaustion, though entry may require the visitor to surrender claims that no longer belong to the living.

### **The Hall of the Feather**

A judicial and funerary realm associated with Ma’at, Anubis, and related powers.

Judgment there concerns not only individual deeds, but whether a life maintained or violated the relationships that sustained it.

### **The River beneath the Stars**

A celestial waterway linked to Ra, Nut, Khonsu, and the nightly passage through darkness.

It functions as road, boundary, and repeated divine journey.

### **The House behind the Name**

A hidden realm associated with Isis, sacred identity, and protective magic.

It is reportedly accessible only through the correct relationship between name, speaker, and purpose. Knowing a divine word is insufficient without understanding why it was entrusted.

### **The Potter’s Source**

A realm associated with Khnum, bodily form, and the creation of living vessels.

Some accounts describe unfinished bodies, extinct kinships, and possible forms never introduced into Aerathon.

### **The Red Horizon**

A vast and unstable region associated with Set.

It includes desert, storm, exile, conflict, and paths that appear only after every safer route has failed.

The Far Heaven also contains ancestral settlements, sacred-beast territories, and divine regions so closely connected to the Feywilds that their boundaries cannot be reliably mapped.

---

## **VI. Shared and Contested Realms**

Not every Divine Realm belongs exclusively to one god or pantheon.

Before the War, several realms were jointly maintained.

These included places associated with:

- judgment;
    
- mortal death;
    
- healing;
    
- navigation;
    
- shared law;
    
- harvest;
    
- and the protection of mixed settlements.
    

Most were damaged, divided, or abandoned.

A shared realm may now exist in several states:

### **Partitioned**

The realm was divided into separate Dawn and Old Court territories.

### **Overlapping**

Both versions occupy the same celestial location but follow different laws depending upon who enters.

### **Abandoned**

Neither pantheon accepts responsibility for it.

### **Contested**

Several gods, fragments, or successor powers claim it.

### **Sealed**

Access was closed under the Quiet Edict or by mutual agreement.

The ruined shared court above Talarin Ford is believed to possess a celestial counterpart. Some travelers report two intact halls occupying the same place, each preserving a different judgment regarding the final dispute.

Such realms are especially dangerous because they may enforce contradictory divine laws simultaneously.

---

## **VII. What Celestial Roads Are**

A Celestial Road is a stable relationship between places that allows passage across divine, mortal, ancestral, or planar boundaries.

It need not resemble a physical road.

A Celestial Road may appear as:

- a temple doorway;
    
- a procession;
    
- a river;
    
- a staircase;
    
- a constellation;
    
- a repeated dream;
    
- a funerary song;
    
- a sacred animal’s migration;
    
- a chain of divine names;
    
- or a sequence of offices passing authority from one bearer to the next.
    

The route exists because its origin and destination recognize the relationship between them.

A temple gate may connect to a god’s realm because:

- the architecture reflects a celestial design;
    
- a covenant establishes lawful passage;
    
- a relic anchors both ends;
    
- and generations of ritual have reinforced the route.
    

An ancestral road may exist because the living and dead continue recognizing one another through names, burial, memory, and obligation.

The road is therefore not simply space.

It is **recognized connection made traversable**.

---

## **VIII. The Cost of Passage**

Celestial travel is rarely free.

A road may require:

- a name;
    
- an oath;
    
- a guide;
    
- a sacrifice;
    
- a memory;
    
- lawful authority;
    
- a bloodline;
    
- a completed ritual;
    
- or the consent of the destination.
    

These requirements are not always tolls imposed by a conscious gatekeeper.

They may be part of what allows the road to recognize the traveler.

For example:

- a funerary road may require the traveler to be properly named;
    
- a judicial road may require an unresolved question;
    
- a road to a divine forge may open only to someone carrying an unfinished work;
    
- a path through the Feywilds may require the traveler to surrender certainty about distance or time.
    

Attempting to force passage can damage both traveler and road.

Known consequences include:

- loss of identity;
    
- arrival in the wrong realm;
    
- separation of body and soul;
    
- temporal displacement;
    
- possession by a waiting entity;
    
- or the road attaching itself permanently to the traveler.
    

Some people return from celestial passage and continue opening doors unintentionally.

---

## **IX. The Breaking of the Roads**

The War of the Two Heavens transformed the celestial network into a battlefield.

Roads were used to:

- move divine armies;
    
- attack rival realms;
    
- redirect souls;
    
- deploy avatars;
    
- hide refugees;
    
- and transport relics or celestial weapons.
    

Both pantheons destroyed routes to prevent invasion.

Others were changed so that only one species, faith, bloodline, or divine servant could use them.

Some were trapped.

A road once used for pilgrimage might begin leading travelers into a battlefield. A funerary passage might deliver souls into hostile custody. A temple gate might allow a god’s servants to enter Aerathon but refuse anyone attempting to leave.

The Quiet Edict sealed many surviving roads.

The gods were not required to destroy every route.

They were required to ensure that the routes could no longer support unrestricted divine descent or renewed celestial war.

This left Aerathon with a network of:

- narrowed roads;
    
- broken roads;
    
- sleeping roads;
    
- one-way roads;
    
- and roads whose destinations no longer possess recognized rulers.
    

---

## **X. Roads after the Quiet Edict**

Celestial Roads still serve several permitted functions.

They allow:

- limited divine messages;
    
- clerical power to pass through covenants;
    
- celestial servants to travel under restriction;
    
- souls to complete lawful passage;
    
- relics to maintain connection with their source;
    
- and rare mortals to enter divine realms under controlled conditions.
    

What they generally cannot support is the full passage of a god.

A Divine Realm may remain connected to a temple, yet the god ruling that realm cannot simply walk through the gate.

The road may permit:

- power but not presence;
    
- a servant but not the sovereign;
    
- a dream but not a body;
    
- an aspect but not the complete deity.
    

These distinctions are central to the enforcement of the Quiet Edict.

Many manifestation attempts begin by widening a road beyond the capacity it is now permitted to carry.

---

## **XI. Empty Realms and Vacant Thrones**

A Divine Realm may survive the death, fragmentation, or disappearance of its god.

Such places are commonly called **Empty Realms**, though they are rarely truly empty.

A vacant divine territory may contain:

- celestial servants continuing their last orders;
    
- fragments claiming to be the original god;
    
- accumulated worship;
    
- functioning covenants;
    
- dead souls waiting for judgment;
    
- relics tied to the former ruler;
    
- or the realm’s own self-sustaining laws.
    

Other beings may attempt to claim the vacant authority.

Possible successors include:

- divine heirs;
    
- powerful celestials;
    
- ascended mortals;
    
- rival gods;
    
- joined fragments;
    
- or entities that merely imitate the lost deity convincingly enough for the realm to accept them.
    

The Near and Far Heavens contain several suspected Empty Realms.

Their roads are heavily restricted because succession within a Divine Realm can affect:

- mortal covenants;
    
- clerical magic;
    
- soul passage;
    
- relic behavior;
    
- and the divine identity recognized by worshippers in Aerathon.
    

A throne accepted in heaven may begin rewriting sacred authority below.

---

## **XII. Celestial Roads and Mortal Religion**

Every functioning divine tradition depends upon some form of celestial connection.

Large churches often maintain roads through:

- temples;
    
- relics;
    
- formal offices;
    
- scripture;
    
- and repeated rites.
    

Old Court traditions more often preserve roads through:

- burial;
    
- ancestry;
    
- sacred landscapes;
    
- seasonal movement;
    
- and inherited custom.
    

Neither model is automatically stronger.

A great cathedral may maintain a broad but weakened route.

A nearly forgotten family rite may preserve a narrow road with extraordinary clarity.

Religious conflict often occurs when institutions mistake control of a road for ownership of the god at its destination.

A temple may regulate access to a covenant.

It does not necessarily possess exclusive authority over the divine realm connected to it.

Similarly, an ancestral road may be genuine without granting one clan the right to speak for every worshipper of the god it reaches.

Celestial access is evidence of relationship.

It is not proof of total representation.

---

## **XIII. Celestial Roads and Labyrinths**

Some Labyrinths contain structures resembling celestial roads.

These may include:

- temple gates opening into impossible skies;
    
- stairways that lead into divine architecture;
    
- rivers carrying souls or memories;
    
- chambers that reproduce the laws of known Divine Realms;
    
- and doors marked with symbols of gods who no longer possess mortal temples.
    

Several explanations remain possible.

A Labyrinth may:

- grow around a damaged celestial road;
    
- imitate a route observed in mortal memory;
    
- trap part of a Divine Realm;
    
- use a divine passage as structural support;
    
- or generate a false destination resembling heaven.
    

This creates a serious danger.

A delver may believe they have entered a true Divine Realm when they have entered only a Labyrinth’s reconstruction of one.

The imitation may contain:

- accurate divine symbols;
    
- functioning Archaic Magic;
    
- copied celestial servants;
    
- and a being that remembers itself as a god.
    

Authenticity cannot be established by appearance or power alone.

Investigators must determine whether the route continues beyond the Labyrinth or terminates within its own governing structure.

---

## **XIV. Ministry Standards for Celestial Mapping**

The Ministry does not map celestial territory solely through distance or direction.

A valid celestial map records:

1. **Origin** — where and how the route begins;
    
2. **Recognition** — what allows the traveler to enter;
    
3. **Authority** — which power governs passage;
    
4. **Medium** — dream, body, soul, ritual, relic, or another form;
    
5. **Destination** — the realm or relationship reached;
    
6. **Return Condition** — what permits departure;
    
7. **Temporal Difference** — whether time passes equally;
    
8. **Known Interference** — Fey, Labyrinthal, Archaic, divine, or ancestral influence;
    
9. **Edict Capacity** — how much power or presence the road can safely carry.
    

A road that has opened once is not considered stable.

A road that returns every traveler is not necessarily safe.

A road that leads to the correct realm may still deliver the traveler to the wrong authority within it.

Celestial mapping is therefore the study of conditions, not merely destinations.

---

## **XV. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established or strongly supported:

1. The Near and Far Heavens contain numerous Divine Realms rather than existing as single uniform locations.
    
2. A Divine Realm expresses the authority, history, and nature of its ruling power.
    
3. Divine geography may preserve older or incomplete forms of a god’s will.
    
4. Some realms are shared, partitioned, contested, abandoned, or sealed.
    
5. Celestial Roads are stable relationships made traversable across mortal, divine, ancestral, and planar boundaries.
    
6. Such roads may take ritual, symbolic, geographical, institutional, or dreamlike forms.
    
7. Passage commonly requires conditions that allow the road to recognize the traveler.
    
8. The War of the Two Heavens damaged, trapped, redirected, and weaponized much of the celestial network.
    
9. The Quiet Edict allows limited passage while preventing most roads from carrying a complete god into Aerathon.
    
10. Divine Realms may survive the death or disappearance of their rulers.
    
11. Succession within an Empty Realm can affect mortal religion, clerical power, relics, and soul passage.
    
12. Control of a Celestial Road does not establish ownership of the divine power at its destination.
    
13. Some Labyrinths contain, imitate, or grow around damaged celestial routes.
    
14. A realm encountered inside a Labyrinth must not be assumed authentic solely because it displays divine power.
    
15. Celestial geography is governed by recognition, authority, and relationship more than ordinary distance.
    

The gods withdrew from Aerathon.

The roads between mortal and divine existence did not entirely close.

They narrowed, broke, changed direction, and waited.

> _“Every temple door is only wood until something on the other side remembers where it was meant to lead.”_
