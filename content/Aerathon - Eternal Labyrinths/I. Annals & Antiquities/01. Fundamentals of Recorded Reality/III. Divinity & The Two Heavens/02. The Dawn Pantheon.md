# **The Dawn Pantheon**

### _The Principal Gods of the Near Heaven and Their Surviving Authority in Aerathon_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Celestial Geography and Divine Relations Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan

**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle

---

## **I. Purpose and Scope**

The **Dawn Pantheon** is the principal divine court associated with the Brethren and the Near Heaven.

Its gods participated in the First Making, helped shape the Brethren peoples, governed the earliest Dawn institutions, fought in the War of the Two Heavens, and accepted the restrictions of the Quiet Edict.

They are neither a morally unified body nor a single religion.

The pantheon includes gods of:

- justice and tyranny;
    
- healing and suffering;
    
- cultivation and destruction;
    
- knowledge and deception;
    
- protection and murder;
    
- fortune and calamity;
    
- peace and war.
    

Their shared membership in the Dawn Court reflects common origin and celestial association—not common virtue.

Modern churches often organize the gods into opposing moral camps. During the First Making, their relationships were more complicated. Gods now regarded as enemies still cooperated in constructing Aerathon, while gods remembered as righteous participated in acts of divine war that caused immense mortal suffering.

This catalogue records the thirty-seven principal Dawn deities most widely recognized across Aerathon.

Regional gods, ascended servants, disputed successors, forgotten divine offices, and minor powers are recorded separately.

---

## **II. Recorded Alignment**

Each deity is assigned a **Recorded Alignment**.

Alignment is a scholarly and theological summary of the god’s most consistent behavior, values, and surviving divine laws.

It does not mean:

- every follower shares that alignment;
    
- every church represents the god accurately;
    
- every historical act of the god was consistent with it;
    
- or the deity is incapable of change.
    

A Lawful Good god may have supported a destructive war out of duty. A Neutral Evil god may preserve a covenant that protects thousands because doing so serves an older purpose.

Alignment describes an enduring tendency.

It is not an acquittal, condemnation, or complete personality.

# **IV. Gods of Nature, Season, and Mortal Sustenance**

## **Auril — The Frostmaiden**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Evil  
**Symbol:** A six-pointed snowflake  
**Divine Concerns:** Winter, cold, endurance, isolation

Auril governs winter as deprivation, preservation, and trial. During the First Making, she helped establish dormancy and seasonal cold, but resisted every attempt to make winter gentle or predictable.

She supported increasingly severe measures during the War, arguing that hardship revealed which peoples were strong enough to endure. Under the Quiet Edict, her influence remains strongest through ancient glaciers, winter covenants, isolated priesthoods, and regions where her Archaic commands continue preventing the arrival of spring.

---

## **Chauntea — The Earthmother**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A sheaf of grain beneath a blooming rose  
**Divine Concerns:** Agriculture, cultivation, fertility, mortal nourishment

Chauntea taught the earliest Brethren how to cultivate land without depending upon constant divine provision. She favored permanent settlement, seed preservation, and the belief that caring for the soil created an obligation between one generation and the next.

Her blessings became controversial when Dawn agriculture expanded into Beastfolk wetlands and migration grounds. Chauntea opposed needless destruction but frequently believed responsible cultivation could improve land that the Old Court considered already complete.

---

## **Eldath — The Quiet Pool**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A waterfall descending into a still pool  
**Divine Concerns:** Peace, sanctuary, still waters, reconciliation

Eldath represented restraint within a pantheon increasingly drawn toward open conflict. Her sacred places were among the last shared sanctuaries to shelter Brethren and Beastfolk during the War of the Two Heavens.

She accepted the Quiet Edict willingly and is believed to maintain one of the clearest remaining connections between the two divine courts. Her influence survives through neutral sanctuaries, healing springs, nonviolent orders, and places where weapons lose their willingness to harm.

---

## **Malar — The Beastlord**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol:** A clawed beast’s paw  
**Divine Concerns:** Predation, bloodlust, pursuit, the savage hunt

Malar embodied the Dawn Court’s understanding of predation: the contest between hunter and prey stripped of ancestral obligation or ecological restraint. His teachings praised strength, fear, pursuit, and the right of the hunter to prove dominance.

His resemblance to certain Old Court principles caused bitter disagreement during the First Concord. Beastfolk wardens accused him of turning the sacred necessity of the hunt into sport, while Malar condemned their customs as attempts to weaken the natural triumph of the predator.

---

## **Mielikki — Lady of the Forest Paths**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A unicorn’s head  
**Divine Concerns:** Forests, rangers, woodland travel, harmony with the wild

Mielikki taught that civilization and wilderness did not need to be enemies. She protected roads that passed through forests without destroying them and favored mortals who learned to travel through the wild rather than conquer it.

She remained sympathetic to many Beastfolk communities during the breakdown of the First Concord. Several modern theologians believe her surviving celestial roads still cross into regions of the Far Heaven and Feywilds that most Dawn Gods can no longer reach.

---

## **Silvanus — The Oak-Father**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** An oak leaf  
**Divine Concerns:** Wilderness, natural balance, growth, decay

Silvanus governs nature beyond mortal preference. He accepts growth and destruction, abundance and starvation, predator and prey as parts of a balance larger than any settlement.

Though a member of the Dawn Court, he frequently opposed Brethren expansion and rejected the idea that cultivated land was inherently superior to wilderness. His refusal to fully support either pantheon during the War preserved some ancient forests but left many communities uncertain whether he had protected them or merely protected the land beneath them.

---

# **V. Gods of Magic, Knowledge, Light, and Creation**

## **Azuth — The High One**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A flaming hand pointing upward  
**Divine Concerns:** Wizardry, magical discipline, formal spellcraft

Azuth taught mortals to organize magical principles into repeatable practices. He favored control, clarity, notation, and the belief that dangerous power became safer when its workings could be examined.

He played a major role in the early study of Divine Remnants and the first attempts at Archaic Weaving. Modern arcane colleges still invoke his name when establishing rules for experimentation, though he has little ability to correct those who use disciplined methods toward destructive ends.

---

## **Deneir — The First Scribe**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A candle above an open eye  
**Divine Concerns:** Writing, literacy, records, language

Deneir gave sacred authority to the preservation of mortal knowledge. He helped establish writing not merely as communication, but as a means by which truth could outlive rulers, witnesses, and divine silence.

During the War, his servants attempted to preserve records from both sides. Portions of his celestial archive were destroyed during the Night of Empty Names, and some scholars believe many lost histories remain trapped within damaged roads leading toward his Near-Heaven realm.

---

## **Gond — The Wonderbringer**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** A four-spoked cog  
**Divine Concerns:** Craft, invention, engineering, constructed life

Gond represents creation through experimentation. He encouraged mortals to improve tools, test new designs, and build works that surpassed the limitations of tradition.

His followers helped create divine hosts, celestial weapons, and artifact bindings during the War. Gond’s modern churches celebrate innovation, while critics argue that his covenants rarely distinguish between inventions that advance civilization and those that merely prove something can be built.

---

## **Lathander — The Morninglord**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A road leading into a sunrise  
**Divine Concerns:** Dawn, renewal, birth, beginnings

Lathander governs the promise that failure need not be final. He supported reconstruction after the First Making’s early disasters and blessed settlements willing to abandon harmful systems rather than defend them through pride.

He opposed the prolonged War but repeatedly underestimated how destructive forced renewal could become. Several failed Dawn reforms were conducted in his name by rulers who believed destroying an old society was justified if something brighter might rise afterward.

---

## **Mystra — Lady of the Weave**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** Seven stars encircling flowing red mist  
**Divine Concerns:** Magic, spellcraft, magical law, the Natural Weave

Mystra governs the ordered magical structure through which mortals safely influence Matter, Meaning, and Motion. She did not create all magic, but helped stabilize the Natural Weave after divine conflict left Aerathon saturated with incompatible Archaic commands.

Her relationship with the Quiet Edict is unusually close. Some traditions claim she helped embed its restrictions into the magical structure of Aerathon, while others accuse her of supporting a system that preserved Dawn institutions at the expense of the Old Court.

---

## **Oghma — The Binder of What Is Known**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** A blank scroll  
**Divine Concerns:** Knowledge, ideas, inspiration, discovery

Oghma governs knowledge before judgment is placed upon it. He values the preservation and circulation of ideas, including those considered dangerous, heretical, or politically inconvenient.

He resisted attempts by both pantheons to destroy opposing histories during the War. His churches now maintain libraries, schools, and archives, though they remain divided over whether knowledge must ever be withheld to prevent catastrophe.

---

## **Savras — The All-Seeing**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A crystal sphere filled with many eyes  
**Divine Concerns:** Divination, fate, prophecy, hidden truth

Savras perceives patterns that connect choices to their likely consequences. His prophecies are often accurate but incomplete, revealing structure without explaining how mortals should respond.

During the First Concord, both courts accused him of concealing knowledge that might have prevented the War. His defenders argue that seeing an outcome does not grant the authority—or ability—to force every participant away from it.

---

## **Selûne — The Moonmaiden**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Good  
**Symbol:** A pair of eyes surrounded by seven stars  
**Divine Concerns:** Moonlight, navigation, tides, change, wanderers

Selûne governs guidance through uncertainty rather than fixed law. She aided travelers, refugees, sailors, and communities forced to live between established nations and traditions.

Her opposition to Shar predates the War of the Two Heavens and continued throughout it. Selûne now maintains influence through moonlit roads, navigational rites, dreams, and communities whose lives do not fit comfortably within formal Dawn institutions.

---

# **VI. Gods of Protection, Endurance, and Sacred Duty**

## **Helm — The Vigilant One**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A staring eye upon an upright gauntlet  
**Divine Concerns:** Guardianship, vigilance, duty, watchfulness

Helm teaches that a guardian must remain at their post regardless of fear, affection, or personal cost. He protected divine roads and neutral gates during the First Concord, but his obedience to assigned duty sometimes placed him against those seeking mercy or escape.

During the War, Helm defended several Dawn strongholds even after their rulers had committed atrocities. His followers continue debating whether perfect guardianship requires obedience to authority or loyalty to those the authority was created to protect.

---

## **Ilmater — The Crying God**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** Two hands bound at the wrists with red cord  
**Divine Concerns:** Endurance, compassion, suffering, protection of the oppressed

Ilmater bears suffering so others need not face it alone. His earliest covenants empowered healers, caretakers, prisoners, and those who remained beside the abandoned.

He opposed the cruelty of both pantheons during the War and is credited with protecting refugees regardless of species or faith. The Quiet Edict sharply limited his ability to intervene, leaving his mortal orders to carry burdens he can now acknowledge more readily than remove.

---

# **VII. Gods of Death, Shadow, Pain, and Ending**

## **Bhaal — The Lord of Murder**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Evil  
**Symbol:** A skull encircled by blood droplets  
**Divine Concerns:** Murder, assassination, violent death, betrayal

Bhaal governs death inflicted deliberately by one mortal upon another. He distinguishes murder from warfare, accident, sacrifice, and natural death, claiming authority over the intention to kill unlawfully or in secret.

He prospered during the War of the Two Heavens as assassination replaced failed diplomacy. His surviving fragments are particularly dangerous because many remember only specific victims, bloodlines, or unfinished killings.

---

## **Kelemvor — Judge of the Dead**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Neutral  
**Symbol:** A skeletal arm holding balanced scales  
**Divine Concerns:** Death, judgment, funerary order, proper passage

Kelemvor oversees the orderly passage of the dead within the Dawn tradition. He opposes undeath when it prevents a soul from completing its lawful journey and rejects the use of fear as the primary instrument of death’s authority.

His jurisdiction brought him into conflict with Anubis and Osiris during the disputes preceding Avarra. Modern scholars believe Kelemvor now cooperates indirectly with Old Court psychopomps, though neither pantheon publicly acknowledges a restored agreement.

---

## **Loviatar — The Maiden of Pain**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Evil  
**Symbol:** A nine-tailed barbed scourge  
**Divine Concerns:** Pain, discipline, torment, endurance through suffering

Loviatar treats pain as revelation, punishment, pleasure, and control. Her covenants distinguish carefully between pain freely accepted, pain imposed as discipline, and suffering used to break the will of another.

During the War, her temples became places of interrogation and ritual punishment. Some modern orders emphasize controlled endurance, while others preserve practices indistinguishable from sanctified cruelty.

---

## **Myrkul — Lord of Bones**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Evil  
**Symbol:** A white human skull  
**Divine Concerns:** Decay, mortality, fear of death, inevitable ending

Myrkul rules not the fair passage of death, but the dread mortals feel before it. He reminds kingdoms, bloodlines, and institutions that every structure eventually fails.

He gained immense influence during the War as mass death became ordinary. Though diminished under the Quiet Edict, his authority remains present in abandoned tombs, decaying covenants, and relics that force mortals to confront the certainty of their own endings.

---

## **Shar — Mistress of the Night**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Evil  
**Symbol:** A black disk bordered in violet  
**Divine Concerns:** Darkness, loss, secrets, oblivion

Shar offers relief through erasure. She governs the desire to forget grief, surrender identity, extinguish painful memory, and return created things to silence.

Her war against Selûne often crossed the boundaries of the wider pantheonic conflict. Shar’s surviving institutions promise peace from suffering, but that peace commonly requires surrendering the memories, relationships, and purposes that made the suffering meaningful.

---

## **Talona — Mistress of Disease**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol:** Three teardrops arranged in a triangle  
**Divine Concerns:** Disease, poison, infection, corruption

Talona governs the forces that enter living systems and turn them against themselves. She is feared as both sender and restrainer of disease, leading many communities to offer worship as protection rather than devotion.

During the War, both pantheons accused her of creating plagues that ignored species and allegiance. Her modern cults often possess genuine knowledge of medicine and poison, making them useful in crises and dangerous once the crisis grants them authority.

---

# **VIII. Gods of War, Law, Storm, and Dominion**

## **Bane — The Black Hand**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Evil  
**Symbol:** An upright black hand  
**Divine Concerns:** Tyranny, domination, conquest, enforced order

Bane believes peace is most reliably achieved when one will possesses enough power to silence all rivals. He supported the faction of the Dawn Court that sought to replace the First Concord with one consistent divine order.

His armies and churches survived the War better than many because they were designed for hierarchy and discipline. Under the Quiet Edict, Bane cannot openly seize Aerathon, but his covenants continue empowering rulers who define stability as unquestioned obedience.

---

## **Talos — The Stormlord**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol:** Three lightning bolts radiating from one point  
**Divine Concerns:** Storms, destruction, catastrophe, fear of nature

Talos governs destruction too large to negotiate with: storms, earthquakes, fires, and disasters that reduce mortal certainty to wreckage.

He treated the War of the Two Heavens as proof that creation was never truly under control. Many Talosian remnants remain embedded in permanent storm systems and regions where weather continues following commands issued during ancient battles.

---

## **Tempus — Lord of Battle**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** An upright flaming sword  
**Divine Concerns:** War, battle, martial courage, honorable conflict

Tempus governs conflict once words and laws have failed. He does not promise that the righteous will win, only that courage, skill, and conduct within battle possess meaning.

He supported neither peace nor conquest consistently during the divine war, instead condemning cowardice and dishonorable methods on both sides. His refusal to oppose war itself remains one of the greatest criticisms directed at his modern church.

---

## **Torm — The Loyal Fury**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** A white right gauntlet  
**Divine Concerns:** Duty, loyalty, courage, sacrifice

Torm teaches that power is justified only when placed in service to something beyond the self. He inspired guardians, paladins, and soldiers willing to accept danger on behalf of others.

During the War, his loyalty to the Dawn Court repeatedly conflicted with his duty to protect innocents. Many of his modern paladin orders treat this history as proof that loyalty without moral judgment becomes obedience to wrongdoing.

---

## **Tyr — The Even-Handed**

**Recorded Alignment:** Lawful Good  
**Symbol:** Balanced scales resting upon a warhammer  
**Divine Concerns:** Justice, law, righteous judgment, honorable authority

Tyr helped establish the earliest laws intended to mediate disputes between unrelated peoples. He supported the First Concord longer than many Dawn Gods, but eventually accepted the argument that one consistent law was necessary to prevent Aerathon from collapsing into contradictory divine systems.

His churches remain among the most influential legal institutions in Aerathon. They disagree fiercely over whether justice resides in the written law, the intention behind it, or the protection of those the law was created to serve.

---

## **Umberlee — The Bitch Queen**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol:** Two opposing curling waves  
**Divine Concerns:** Seas, drowning, tides, oceanic wrath

Umberlee embodies the sea as a power that refuses ownership. Sailors worship her because ships, laws, and titles mean little when the water chooses to take them.

She clashed with both Dawn and Old Court river powers during the First Concord. Under the Quiet Edict, she remains capable of influencing storms and sacred waters through old maritime covenants, but cannot freely rise from the sea as she did during the War.

---

# **IX. Gods of Fortune, Deception, Art, and Mortal Ambition**

## **Beshaba — Lady Doom**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol:** Black antlers  
**Divine Concerns:** Misfortune, accidents, calamity, cruel chance

Beshaba governs the moment when possibility turns against the hopeful. She is most often worshipped through appeasement, with offerings made so that disaster might pass elsewhere.

Her influence became especially feared during the War, when carefully prepared strategies failed through impossible coincidence. Modern scholars disagree over whether she causes misfortune or merely claims authority wherever it naturally occurs.

---

## **Cyric — The Prince of Lies**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Evil  
**Symbol:** A jawless skull upon a dark sunburst  
**Divine Concerns:** Lies, strife, betrayal, destructive ambition

Cyric teaches that truth is merely the story that survives opposition. He favors mortals who destroy trust, turn allies against one another, and replace shared reality with narratives that benefit themselves.

He played little constructive role in the First Making but thrived as the Concord broke apart. Several contradictory histories of the divine war show signs of deliberate Cyricist alteration, making his influence a continuing threat to any reconstruction of the past.

---

## **Leira — Lady of the Mists**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Neutral  
**Symbol:** A point-down triangle of mist  
**Divine Concerns:** Illusion, uncertainty, hidden truth, unreality

Leira governs the boundary between falsehood and concealed truth. Her illusions do not always exist to deceive; some protect knowledge until the observer is prepared to understand it.

She resisted attempts by both pantheons to define one authorized version of reality. Since the Quiet Edict, her fragments and covenants are frequently encountered around hidden settlements, false ruins, secret roads, and records that reveal different contents to different readers.

---

## **Lliira — Lady of Joy**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Good  
**Symbol:** Three six-pointed stars arranged in a triangle  
**Divine Concerns:** Joy, celebration, emotional freedom, community

Lliira teaches that survival without joy is merely prolonged suffering. Her rites preserved music, dance, festivals, and shared celebration even during the darkest periods of the divine war.

She opposed efforts to turn public ritual entirely toward obedience and mourning. Her modern churches often serve as civic centers, though more austere religions accuse them of treating happiness as sacred even when solemn duty remains unfinished.

---

## **Mask — Lord of Shadows**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Neutral  
**Symbol:** A black mask  
**Divine Concerns:** Thieves, stealth, secrecy, opportunism

Mask governs the unnoticed path, the hidden hand, and the advantage gained by seeing what others overlook. His followers include thieves, spies, fugitives, smugglers, and those who survive by existing outside formal authority.

He supported no single side consistently during the War. Mask aided refugees and assassins with equal secrecy, maintaining that the morality of a hidden road belonged to the one who chose where it led.

---

## **Milil — Lord of Song**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral Good  
**Symbol:** A five-stringed harp formed from leaves  
**Divine Concerns:** Poetry, song, performance, shared memory

Milil governs the preservation of experience through performance. He taught mortals that a song could carry memory where records, temples, and kingdoms failed.

His servants preserved testimonies from the divine war in forms that survived the destruction of written archives. Many apparent folk songs still contain names, routes, warnings, and ritual instructions whose original importance is no longer widely understood.

---

## **Sune — The Firehair**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Good  
**Symbol:** The face of a beautiful red-haired woman  
**Divine Concerns:** Love, beauty, desire, passion

Sune teaches that love and beauty possess value beyond utility, law, lineage, or divine purpose. She opposed attempts to regulate affection solely through political and religious institutions.

Her influence during the War protected many mixed families and relationships condemned by both pantheons. Modern Sunite traditions remain divided between those who celebrate beauty as liberation and those who have allowed beauty to become another instrument of status and exclusion.

---

## **Tymora — Lady Luck**

**Recorded Alignment:** Chaotic Good  
**Symbol:** A face-up silver coin  
**Divine Concerns:** Fortune, courage, risk, favorable chance

Tymora favors those who act despite uncertainty. Her blessings rarely guarantee success; they create openings for mortals willing to recognize and seize them.

During the War, she became associated with improbable escapes, failed executions, and refugees surviving events that should have killed them. Her followers teach that fortune is not safety, but the brief opportunity to change an expected ending.

---

## **Waukeen — Merchant’s Friend**

**Recorded Alignment:** Neutral  
**Symbol:** An upright golden coin bearing her profile  
**Divine Concerns:** Trade, wealth, negotiation, exchange

Waukeen governs value created through exchange. She supported roads, markets, contracts, credit, and the belief that strangers could cooperate without blood relation or shared worship.

Her institutions helped rebuild trade after the divine war, but also contributed to the treatment of land, relics, and sacred resources as transferable property. Modern Waukeenar theology struggles with the boundary between fair exchange and the belief that everything must possess a price.

---

## **X. The Dawn Pantheon After the Quiet Edict**

The Dawn Gods remain the most publicly recognized divine powers in modern Aerathon.

Their survival rests upon structures established before their withdrawal:

- churches;
    
- temples;
    
- written scriptures;
    
- clerical offices;
    
- paladin orders;
    
- divine covenants;
    
- holy relics;
    
- religious calendars;
    
- and political institutions built around their authority.
    

This gives the appearance that the Dawn Court won the War of the Two Heavens.

The truth is more complicated.

The Dawn Gods retained public recognition but surrendered direct access to mortal hosts. Their churches may continue for centuries without clear correction. Their covenants can remain active even when mortal institutions distort their original purposes.

The gods cannot freely:

- enter Aerathon;
    
- speak through their highest priests;
    
- replace corrupt church leaders;
    
- personally settle doctrinal disputes;
    
- or prevent rulers from using divine names for political legitimacy.
    

The pantheon’s greatest strength is the endurance of its institutions.

Its greatest weakness is that those institutions now belong largely to mortals.

## **XI. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established or strongly supported:

1. The Dawn Pantheon consists of multiple divine powers rather than one unified moral or political body.
    
2. Its principal gods participated in the First Making, though their individual contributions varied.
    
3. The pantheon contains benevolent, neutral, destructive, and openly malevolent deities.
    
4. Membership in the Dawn Court reflects celestial association rather than shared alignment.
    
5. The gods’ modern symbols and alignments are useful classifications but do not completely define them.
    
6. Dawn institutions survived the War more successfully than those of the Old Beastfolk Pantheon.
    
7. Institutional survival allowed Dawn religion to become dominant across much of Aerathon.
    
8. The Quiet Edict prevents the gods from exercising direct control over those institutions.
    
9. Authentic divine magic does not prove that a church accurately represents its god.
    
10. Relics and fragments may preserve earlier or incomplete versions of a god’s will.
    
11. Rival churches dedicated to the same deity may possess equally valid access to different covenants.
    
12. No Dawn deity is exempt from the restrictions governing divine manifestation.
    
13. The Dawn Pantheon should not be treated as the sole creator, rightful owner, or exclusive divine authority of Aerathon.
    
14. Every principal Dawn god remains relevant through some combination of covenants, relics, celestial servants, cultural influence, or Archaic remnants.
    

The Dawn Gods remain known because mortals preserved their names.

Whether the gods still recognize everything done beneath those names is another matter.

> _“The Dawn Court built institutions intended to outlive their founders. It succeeded more completely than its gods ever expected.”_
