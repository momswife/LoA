# **Mortal Magic and the Three Weaves**

### _The Natural Structure of Magic and the Methods by Which Mortals Alter the World_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Magical Foundations and Applied Weave Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Magister Selka Vorr  
**Contributing Scholars:** Professor Arav Dhal-Senn, Archivist Yelna Stonevoice, Inspector Jao “Greyglass” Vell, Warden Hessa Reedstep  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Foundational Magical Doctrine / Grade-I Verified Practice_  
**Primary Evidence:** Collegium spell records, early artifact studies, healer registries, failed-casting reports, Beastfolk ritual archives, Ministry field examinations

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

Modern magic is not a lesser form of divine power.

It is a different method of changing the world.

The gods of the First Making could place commands directly upon Aerathon. Their surviving power became Archaic Magic: strong, lasting, and often resistant to modern control.

Mortals could not safely reproduce that authority.

Instead, they studied how the world responded to divine action. They observed which parts of an object changed, which remained stable, and why similar magical effects produced different results.

From those studies came the doctrine of the **Three Weaves**:

- **Matter**
    
- **Meaning**
    
- **Motion**
    

Together, these form the Natural Weave through which mortal magic operates.

A mortal spell does not command reality without limit.

It creates a temporary arrangement that reality can understand.

---

## **II. The Natural Weave**

The Three Weaves are not physical threads.

They are the three conditions scholars use to describe how a thing exists and changes.

Every person, object, creature, and place possesses all three.

### **Matter**

Matter is physical substance and form.

It includes:

- flesh;
    
- stone;
    
- metal;
    
- water;
    
- temperature;
    
- weight;
    
- shape;
    
- and location.
    

Matter determines what can be touched, measured, injured, broken, or physically transformed.

### **Meaning**

Meaning is identity, purpose, recognition, and relationship.

It includes:

- a person’s name;
    
- the distinction between a tool and a weapon;
    
- the authority of a crown;
    
- the boundary of a home;
    
- the purpose of a ritual;
    
- and the difference between a corpse and the person who once inhabited it.
    

Meaning is why two objects made of the same material may behave differently under magic.

### **Motion**

Motion is action, change, sequence, and direction.

It includes:

- movement;
    
- growth;
    
- ignition;
    
- decay;
    
- transformation;
    
- the passage of time;
    
- and the order in which events occur.
    

Motion determines what something is doing and how one state becomes another.

A stable thing exists because its Matter, Meaning, and Motion broadly agree.

Magic changes one or more of them for a limited purpose.

---

## **III. How a Spell Works**

Every successful spell requires four basic steps.

### **Recognition**

The caster identifies what is being changed.

This may involve sight, touch, a true name, a marked target, a sympathetic object, or a known location.

### **Formation**

The caster establishes the desired change.

Words, gestures, runes, instruments, prayer, song, or concentration give the magic a usable shape.

### **Passage**

Power moves through the relevant Weaves.

The caster may supply that power personally, draw it from the environment, use a prepared focus, or access it through another magical source.

### **Release**

The spell completes and the Weaves settle into their altered state.

Most spells are temporary because the world gradually returns toward its prior condition. Permanent magic requires either repeated reinforcement or a change that the world can accept as its new natural state.

A spell fails when one of these steps is missing, unclear, or incompatible with the others.

---

## **IV. Matter Magic**

Magic focused upon Matter changes physical conditions.

Common examples include:

- creating or shaping flame;
    
- freezing water;
    
- moving stone;
    
- repairing broken objects;
    
- altering the body;
    
- producing light;
    
- and creating physical barriers.
    

Matter magic is often considered the easiest to observe and the most difficult to conceal.

A wall moved by magic still occupies a new location. A healed wound leaves changed tissue. A created flame produces heat, smoke, and damage.

Matter cannot usually be created without cost.

A spell that produces stone may gather existing earth, convert another substance, or create a temporary form that eventually collapses.

The more permanent the desired result, the more the spell must account for:

- available material;
    
- structural stability;
    
- heat and pressure;
    
- the target’s existing form;
    
- and the consequences of the change.
    

A mage may raise a stone bridge quickly.

Making one that survives a century requires engineering as well as spellcraft.

---

## **V. Meaning Magic**

Magic focused upon Meaning changes how something is identified, understood, or allowed to function.

Common examples include:

- illusions;
    
- disguises;
    
- wards;
    
- curses;
    
- blessings;
    
- truth magic;
    
- enchantment;
    
- and effects tied to names, ownership, or authority.
    

Meaning magic does not always change physical Matter.

An illusion may leave an object untouched while changing how observers understand it. A ward may permit one person through a door and reject another even though the door itself remains ordinary.

Meaning magic is powerful because civilization already depends upon shared recognition.

A crown is physically metal and stone.

Its Meaning may include sovereignty, succession, lawful command, and the memory of those who wore it.

Magic can use those existing associations.

It can also misuse them.

A forged title supported by strong enough magic may convince a gate, relic, or legal ward that the bearer possesses authority they never earned.

Meaning magic is therefore closely regulated in:

- courts;
    
- inheritance disputes;
    
- identity records;
    
- religious appointments;
    
- and agreements involving relic ownership.
    

---

## **VI. Motion Magic**

Magic focused upon Motion governs action and change.

Common examples include:

- telekinesis;
    
- acceleration;
    
- slowing;
    
- teleportation;
    
- growth;
    
- decay;
    
- transformation;
    
- and the redirection of energy.
    

Motion magic is not limited to physical movement.

A healing spell uses Motion to guide the sequence by which tissue repairs itself. A transmutation spell directs one form toward another. A preservation spell slows decay.

Teleportation is one of the most demanding uses of Motion because the caster must move Matter while preserving Meaning.

The person arriving must remain the same person who departed.

A failed teleportation may move the body without maintaining its orientation, possessions, memory, or exact identity.

For this reason, long-distance teleportation usually depends upon:

- prepared circles;
    
- recognized destinations;
    
- linked objects;
    
- mapped routes;
    
- or anchors that help the spell determine where and how the traveler should arrive.
    

Motion without direction is rarely useful.

Motion without preserved Meaning is often fatal.

---

## **VII. Spells Usually Use More Than One Weave**

Most practical magic uses all three Weaves in different proportions.

|Magical Effect|Matter|Meaning|Motion|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Fire spell**|Heat, flame, fuel|Recognized as fire|Ignition and spread|
|**Healing spell**|Flesh and blood|The body’s recognized healthy state|Repair and recovery|
|**Illusion**|Little or no physical change|Altered appearance or interpretation|Maintained projection|
|**Teleportation**|Body and possessions|Preserved identity|Change of location|
|**Curse**|Possible bodily effect|Harmful condition or judgment|Trigger and continuation|
|**Ward**|Door, wall, or boundary|Permission and exclusion|Activation when crossed|
|**Necromancy**|Corpse or remains|Incomplete or altered identity|Artificial continuation|
|**Transformation**|New physical form|Negotiated identity|Ordered change between forms|

A spell becomes harder when the Weaves resist one another.

Turning a person into stone requires Matter to change while Meaning preserves enough of the person for restoration to remain possible.

Creating an undead servant moves a dead body but cannot fully restore the Meaning of the person who inhabited it.

A perfect resurrection would need to restore Matter, recover the soul’s identity, and reverse the Motion of death without producing a copy or an empty body.

This is why such magic remains extraordinarily difficult.

## **VIII. Where Mortal Power Comes From**

The Three Weaves describe how magic operates, not where every caster obtains power.

Mortal spellcraft draws upon several sources.

### **Personal Aether**

Some casters produce or retain magical energy naturally.

This is common among sorcerous bloodlines, magically altered peoples, and those exposed to divine remnants.

### **Ambient Magic**

Power may be drawn from the surrounding world.

Forests, storms, cities, ancient ruins, and sacred sites possess different magical conditions.

### **Prepared Foci**

Wands, staves, crystals, runes, instruments, and spellbooks help gather and direct power.

### **Ritual Participation**

Several people may contribute time, energy, movement, voice, or symbolic roles to create an effect none could sustain alone.

### **Inherited Practice**

Some Beastfolk and regional traditions access magic through repeated customs, ancestral forms, songs, dances, or seasonal rites.

### **External Channels**

Clerics, paladins, Warlocks, and certain relic-bearers access power through covenants, oaths, patrons, or bound sources.

These casters still produce effects through the Three Weaves.

Their difference lies in who or what supplies the power and establishes the conditions of use.

---

## **IX. Schools, Traditions, and Different Methods**

No single culture discovered all mortal magic.

Brethren academies favored written formulae, measured gestures, controlled materials, and repeatable instruction.

Beastfolk traditions often preserved magic through:

- oral teaching;
    
- inherited ritual;
    
- environmental timing;
    
- movement;
    
- scent;
    
- rhythm;
    
- and relationships with land or ancestors.
    

Neither method is inherently more natural or more advanced.

A written spell can be copied incorrectly.

An oral rite can change as it passes between generations.

Academic practice excels at standardization. Traditional practice often retains context that formal spell structures overlook.

Modern magical institutions increasingly combine both.

A Ministry field-healer may use a standardized restorative sequence while consulting a Beastfolk practitioner about how the patient’s lineage responds to transformation.

A weather mage may understand atmospheric Motion but still require a local rite to avoid disrupting seasonal obligations.

Spellcraft works best when the caster understands both the effect and the world in which it will occur.

---

## **X. Why Mortal Magic Is Safer Than Archaic Magic**

Natural magic is not harmless.

It is safer because it possesses clearer limits.

A mortal spell usually depends upon:

- the caster’s available power;
    
- range;
    
- duration;
    
- concentration;
    
- physical materials;
    
- and a defined magical structure.
    

When the caster can no longer sustain the effect, the spell weakens or ends.

Archaic Magic may continue because its original authority does not depend upon a living mortal caster.

A modern flame spell burns what it reaches until its fuel or magic is exhausted.

An Archaic flame might continue until every object it recognizes as an enemy has been destroyed.

Modern magic is also easier to revise.

A failed spell formula can be corrected. An enchantment can be dispelled or overwritten. A ritual can be stopped before completion.

Archaic Magic may treat attempts to alter it as resistance to its original command.

Mortal spellcraft sacrificed absolute power in exchange for control.

That sacrifice is one of the principal reasons civilization survived the magical ages that followed the Quiet Edict.

## **XI. Common Forms of Spell Failure**

Spell failure is not random.

It usually reveals which part of the casting was misunderstood.

### **Material Failure**

The spell lacks the substance or physical conditions required.

Examples include:

- a fire spell failing in an airless chamber;
    
- healing magic closing a wound around a foreign object;
    
- a summoned barrier collapsing under its own weight.
    

### **Meaning Failure**

The spell identifies the target incorrectly or uses an unclear purpose.

Examples include:

- a ward rejecting its owner;
    
- an illusion altering the wrong person;
    
- a truth spell treating metaphor as deception;
    
- a curse attaching to a title rather than an individual.
    

### **Motion Failure**

The change begins but follows an incorrect sequence or direction.

Examples include:

- teleportation arriving at the wrong anchor;
    
- transformation stopping halfway;
    
- accelerated healing producing uncontrolled growth;
    
- a slowing spell affecting thought rather than movement.
    

### **Overreach**

The caster attempts a change larger than their power or preparation can sustain.

### **Contamination**

A relic, Divine Remnant, Labyrinth, planar breach, or nearby spell alters the casting.

### **Contradiction**

The spell requires incompatible outcomes.

A command to preserve a structure while simultaneously destroying every part of it cannot resolve cleanly.

Contradictory magic is especially dangerous because it may continue seeking an impossible solution.

---

## **XII. Permanent Magic**

Most mortal spells fade.

Permanent magic requires one of four conditions.

### **Physical Completion**

The magic creates a mundane result capable of continuing without it.

A magically lit forge may produce ordinary heated metal. A repaired bridge remains standing because its structure is physically sound.

### **Repeated Renewal**

The effect is maintained through regular rituals, power, or attention.

City wards and temple protections commonly operate this way.

### **Stable Enchantment**

The spell is anchored into an object or place through a carefully constructed magical system.

### **Accepted Change**

The altered condition becomes integrated into the target strongly enough that the Weaves begin treating it as normal.

This may occur with long-term transformation, inherited magic, or repeated exposure.

Accepted change is difficult to predict.

A magically altered bloodline may eventually pass its traits naturally. A city protected by the same ward for centuries may begin responding to its rules even when the original enchantment weakens.

This is one way mortal magic can gradually begin resembling Archaic Magic.

The difference is that the authority was built through long repetition rather than issued by a god.

---

## **XIII. Magic and the Changed World**

Modern spellcraft developed after Aerathon had already been altered by divine war.

The Natural Weave is therefore not untouched or perfectly uniform.

Magic behaves differently near:

- Primordial Scars;
    
- Divine Remnants;
    
- ancient battlefields;
    
- strong celestial or fey crossings;
    
- damaged soul routes;
    
- and Labyrinths.
    

A spell considered reliable in Louvain may behave differently in a valley still carrying an Old God’s command.

Healing may be easier near a sacred spring. Fire may resist summoning in a place once protected from a Dawn assault. Illusions may become physical inside a Labyrinth that does not clearly distinguish Meaning from Matter.

Competent spellcasters therefore study location as carefully as formula.

The question is not only:

> “What spell am I casting?”

It is also:

> “What rules is this place already obeying?”

---

## **XIV. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established:

1. Mortal magic operates through Matter, Meaning, and Motion.
    
2. These Three Weaves collectively form the Natural Weave.
    
3. Modern spellcraft developed through observation, experimentation, traditional practice, and the study of contained Archaic Magic.
    
4. Mortal magic changes reality through existing rules rather than issuing absolute divine commands.
    
5. Different traditions may use different methods while working through the same underlying structure.
    
6. Clerical, paladin, Warlock, ancestral, and arcane effects all interact with the Three Weaves, even when their power comes from different sources.
    
7. Most spell failure can be traced to mistaken recognition, formation, passage, or release.
    
8. Permanent magical effects require completion, renewal, stable enchantment, or long-term acceptance by the Weaves.
    
9. Local conditions can alter otherwise reliable magic.
    
10. Sites containing Archaic Magic or Labyrinth influence require separate examination before ordinary magical assumptions are applied.
    
11. The Three Weaves are a practical model supported by repeated observation, not proof that every magical phenomenon is fully understood.
    
12. Any effect that appears to bypass all three Weaves should be investigated for Archaic, divine, planar, or Labyrinthal influence.
    

The gods shaped Aerathon through authority.

Mortals learned to shape it through understanding.

That difference made mortal magic weaker than the miracles of the First Age.

It also made magic something that could be taught, questioned, corrected, and shared.

> _“A god could raise a mountain in a word. A mortal mason learned how to make sure it did not fall.”_
