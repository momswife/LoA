# **Patrons, Pacts and Borrowed Power**

### _Private Magical Agreements, Warlock Practice, and the Beings That Operate Beyond Established Divine Covenants_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Pact Examination and External Influence Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Inspector Jao “Greyglass” Vell  
**Contributing Scholars:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Magister Selka Vorr, Researcher Falyn Onarra, Curator Rook-of-Rings  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Foundational Pact Doctrine / Grade-I Verified Phenomenon with Grade-III Source Variability_  
**Primary Evidence:** registered Warlock testimonies, recovered pact instruments, patron manifestations, severed-channel examinations, Feywild covenant records, Divine Remnant case studies

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

Warlocks receive magic through private agreements with powerful beings.

This places them outside the familiar structures of clerical office, paladin oath, and academic spellcraft.

A cleric draws upon a covenant established for a religious community or sacred office. A paladin binds themselves to a principle recognized by the Natural Weave.

A Warlock enters into an arrangement with a specific patron.

The patron may be divine, celestial, fey, infernal, ancestral, Labyrinthal, or otherwise difficult to classify. It may grant power willingly, offer knowledge in exchange for service, place a fragment of itself within the Warlock, or create a channel through which the Warlock can reach magic unavailable by ordinary means.

This arrangement is called a **pact**.

Pacts are neither inherently corrupt nor inherently safe.

Their danger lies in their privacy.

A public covenant may be studied across centuries. A pact may be known only to the patron and the person who accepted it.

---

## **II. What a Pact Is**

A pact is a magically enforceable relationship between a mortal and an external power.

It normally establishes four things:

1. **What the patron provides**
    
2. **What the mortal provides**
    
3. **What conditions maintain the agreement**
    
4. **What happens when the agreement ends or is violated**
    

The exchange may involve:

- magical knowledge;
    
- access to a hidden source;
    
- transformation of the body or soul;
    
- a bound relic;
    
- protection;
    
- a fragment of the patron’s authority;
    
- service;
    
- worship;
    
- secrecy;
    
- recovery of lost objects;
    
- restoration of forgotten names;
    
- or preparation for the patron’s eventual return.
    

Not every pact is written.

Some are spoken before witnesses. Others are sealed through blood, dreams, names, gifts, inherited marks, or repeated acts of service.

A person may also enter a pact without fully understanding that an agreement has been made.

Accepting a weapon, answering a voice in a dream, speaking a hidden name, or carrying a relic beyond its prison may satisfy conditions the patron considers binding.

For this reason, Ministry law does not recognize ignorance alone as proof that no pact exists.

It does, however, distinguish an informed agreement from magical entrapment.

---

## **III. How Pact Magic Differs from Divine Covenants**

A divine covenant is usually broad, inherited, and public.

A pact is narrow, personal, and negotiated.

|Question|Divine Covenant|Pact|
|---|---|---|
|**Primary participant**|Religious office or community|Individual mortal|
|**Source**|God, domain, or established sacred system|Specific patron|
|**Terms**|Inherited and often standardized|Private and individually defined|
|**Oversight**|Church, order, temple, or tradition|Often none|
|**Power channel**|Ancient sacred structure|Direct patron connection, gift, or alteration|
|**Common risk**|Institutional drift|Hidden terms or patron manipulation|
|**Loss of power**|Covenant damage or incompatibility|Depends upon how power was granted|

The essential distinction is:

> **A covenant allows a mortal to use a surviving divine system. A pact allows a patron to create a private exception.**

This is why Warlocks may gain power from beings that possess no organized church, recognized domain, or public authority.

The patron does not need to establish a religion.

It needs only enough influence to reach one mortal.

---

## **IV. Why Patrons Can Act Despite the Quiet Edict**

The Quiet Edict prevents gods from returning to Aerathon in their former strength.

It does not prevent every exchange of power.

A patron can usually act through a pact because the mortal intermediary bears the burden of manifestation.

The patron provides a fragment, channel, lesson, or permission. The Warlock then produces the magical effect through their own body and the Natural Weave.

This remains below the threshold of full divine descent.

The distinction is similar to carrying water from a sealed reservoir. The reservoir has not entered the city. A small portion has been brought through a permitted vessel.

The arrangement becomes more dangerous when the patron attempts to use the Warlock for more than power transfer.

Warning signs include:

- speaking continuously through the Warlock;
    
- suppressing the Warlock’s personality;
    
- altering nearby reality without the Warlock casting;
    
- using the Warlock’s senses without permission;
    
- assembling multiple Warlocks into a shared body;
    
- or gradually transforming the Warlock into a suitable avatar.
    

Such conduct may indicate that the pact is being used to bypass the Edict.

At that stage, the Warlock is no longer merely receiving power.

They are becoming an entrance.

---

## **V. Recognized Patron Classes**

Patrons resist simple classification, but the Ministry recognizes several recurring categories.

### **Celestial Patrons**

These include divine servants, exiled heavenly beings, surviving aspects, and lesser powers connected to the Near or Far Heaven.

Some act on behalf of gods.

Others operate independently because the gods are too distant, weakened, or restricted to interfere.

### **Divine Fragments**

A fragment may be a portion of a god separated during the War of the Two Heavens.

It may remember only one period, duty, emotion, or command belonging to the greater deity.

Such fragments may disagree with the god from which they came.

A fragment of a merciful god created during a massacre may remember only wrath. A fragment of a war god left while protecting refugees may despise the church later built in that god’s name.

### **Old Gods and Far-Heaven Powers**

Some Old Gods maintain private relationships through dreams, ancestry, sacred animals, or the Feywilds.

These pacts are often mistaken for ordinary ancestral callings.

The difference is usually the presence of a specific exchange and an identifiable patron will.

### **Fey Patrons**

Powerful fey may offer magic in exchange for names, memories, promises, stories, emotions, seasonal service, or influence within Aerathon.

Their terms are often precise but unfamiliar.

A fey patron may follow every word of an agreement while ignoring what the mortal believed the words meant.

### **Archaic Patrons**

Some Divine Remnants retain enough awareness to negotiate.

A sword, temple, severed divine hand, or unfinished miracle may offer power to someone willing to continue its original purpose.

Such patrons may not understand the modern world.

### **Labyrinthal Patrons**

Certain Warlocks claim to receive power from Labyrinths, floor-intelligences, sealed inhabitants, or entities encountered beyond known depths.

Whether a Labyrinth can truly act as a patron remains disputed.

The resulting magic is sufficiently consistent that the claim cannot be dismissed.

### **Interstitial and Unknown Patrons**

Some beings originate from regions between recognized planes or refuse all known classifications.

These patrons require the highest level of investigation because their goals and limitations are rarely understood.

---

## **VI. What Patrons Actually Grant**

Pact magic does not always involve a continuing stream of power.

A patron may grant magic in several different ways.

### **Continuing Channel**

The Warlock draws power through an active connection to the patron.

If the connection is severed, access usually weakens or disappears.

### **Granted Knowledge**

The patron teaches magical methods the Warlock can thereafter use independently.

The patron may regret the lesson, but cannot easily remove knowledge already learned.

### **Permanent Transformation**

The patron changes the Warlock’s body, soul, blood, or relationship to the Weaves.

The resulting power may remain even after the pact ends.

### **Embedded Fragment**

A piece of the patron is placed within the Warlock.

This may function as a power source, advisor, parasite, or future seed of manifestation.

### **Relic Access**

The patron grants authority to use a specific relic or artifact.

The power belongs primarily to the object, while the pact determines who may command it.

### **Borrowed Authority**

The Warlock is permitted to invoke the patron’s name, domain, servants, or hidden paths.

This power depends heavily upon whether the patron’s authority is recognized in the location where it is used.

These models may overlap.

A Warlock might receive knowledge, carry a patron fragment, and retain access to a bound relic through the same pact.

---

## **VII. The Price of Power**

Not every patron demands cruelty, obedience, or a soul.

Many pacts involve practical exchange.

Examples include:

- recovering a stolen relic;
    
- preserving a sacred place;
    
- carrying the patron’s name into regions where it has been forgotten;
    
- recording events the patron cannot witness;
    
- protecting a bloodline;
    
- destroying a rival fragment;
    
- delivering messages between realms;
    
- or completing unfinished work from the divine war.
    

The danger lies in how patrons value things.

A forgotten god may consider the restoration of one shrine worth more than a mortal lifetime.

A fey sovereign may view a childhood memory as fair payment because it cannot understand why the memory matters.

A Divine Remnant may ask a Warlock to continue a war that ended millennia ago.

A Labyrinthal intelligence may grant power in exchange for being entered, studied, remembered, or fed new experience.

The apparent size of the request is not always equal to its consequence.

A patron asking for “one open door” may be requesting access to Aerathon.

---

## **VIII. Pact Instruments and Marks**

Many pacts leave physical or magical evidence.

Known pact instruments include:

- signed contracts;
    
- branded symbols;
    
- enchanted rings;
    
- bound weapons;
    
- shared names;
    
- ritual scars;
    
- altered shadows;
    
- patron familiars;
    
- dream-seals;
    
- embedded crystals;
    
- and objects that cannot be discarded.
    

These instruments may serve as:

- proof of the pact;
    
- a focus for power;
    
- a restraint upon the patron;
    
- a restraint upon the Warlock;
    
- a method of communication;
    
- or the actual channel through which magic passes.
    

Destroying a pact instrument does not automatically end the pact.

In some cases, it removes the only restraint preventing the patron from acting more freely.

Likewise, removing a visible mark may alter only the evidence, not the underlying agreement.

Ministry procedure requires investigators to determine what function the instrument serves before attempting destruction.

---

## **IX. When Pact Magic Disappears**

Warlock power may vanish for several reasons.

### **Patron Withdrawal**

A continuing channel may be closed by the patron.

This is the clearest form of lost pact magic, but not the most common.

### **Broken Conditions**

The Warlock violates a term necessary for access.

Some pacts allow correction. Others end immediately.

### **Patron Death or Fragmentation**

A patron may be destroyed, divided, imprisoned, or altered beyond the state in which the pact was made.

The power may disappear, become unstable, or begin answering to a successor fragment.

### **Planar Separation**

The Warlock enters a region where the patron cannot be reached.

This is common in isolated Labyrinth floors, divine dead zones, and places under incompatible Archaic laws.

### **Exhaustion of the Grant**

Some pacts provide a limited amount of power rather than permanent access.

The Warlock may unknowingly spend the final portion.

### **Completed Bargain**

The pact ends because both sides have fulfilled their obligations.

Depending upon its structure, the Warlock may lose all power, retain learned abilities, or keep permanent transformations.

### **Severance**

A ritual, relic, institution, or stronger patron may cut the connection.

Severance can free the Warlock.

It can also damage whatever part of the Warlock was reshaped to hold the pact.

---

## **X. Power That Cannot Be Reclaimed**

The common belief that patrons can remove every Warlock ability at will is incorrect.

A patron cannot easily reclaim:

- knowledge already learned;
    
- scars that have become part of the body;
    
- changes accepted by the Natural Weave;
    
- power transferred permanently into the Warlock;
    
- abilities inherited by descendants;
    
- or fragments the patron no longer controls.
    

A Warlock who learned forbidden spellcraft may retain it after betraying the patron.

A person transformed into a living gateway may remain altered even after the gateway is sealed.

A bloodline descended from an ancient pact may display power long after the original patron has been forgotten.

This is one reason patrons choose their gifts carefully.

Power given permanently creates a mortal who may later use it against its source.

The oldest patrons often prefer continuing channels because dependence is easier to enforce.

The most desperate patrons may give away permanent pieces of themselves because they lack another means of acting.

---

## **XI. Inherited and Accidental Pacts**

Some pacts pass across generations.

A founder may have promised that their descendants would guard a place, carry a patron’s mark, or provide a vessel when certain conditions were met.

Whether descendants are morally bound by such promises is heavily disputed.

The magic may treat inheritance as valid even when mortal law does not.

Inherited pacts commonly produce:

- unusual bloodline abilities;
    
- recurring dreams;
    
- patron familiars appearing at a certain age;
    
- family taboos;
    
- compulsory journeys;
    
- or powers awakening when an ancestral duty is neglected.
    

Accidental pacts occur when a person fulfills terms without knowing their significance.

Examples include:

- freeing a sealed entity;
    
- accepting food in a Feywild court;
    
- speaking a forgotten god’s full title;
    
- carrying a relic across a divine boundary;
    
- answering a voice from within a Labyrinth;
    
- or promising aid to a disguised celestial.
    

The Ministry does not automatically classify such individuals as willing Warlocks.

It does classify them as pact-bound until the relationship is examined.

---

## **XII. False Patrons and Stolen Identities**

A patron may lie about what it is.

This is especially common when the claimed identity belongs to a silent or forgotten god.

An entity may present itself as:

- a divine messenger;
    
- a dead ancestor;
    
- an Old God;
    
- a Dawn deity;
    
- a guardian spirit;
    
- or the surviving consciousness of a relic.
    

The existence of real gods makes such deception easier, not harder.

A mortal knows divine communication is possible but has limited means to verify the speaker.

Known false-patron methods include:

- using authentic fragments of old prayers;
    
- granting powers associated with the claimed god;
    
- occupying abandoned covenant channels;
    
- sending visions drawn from the recipient’s memories;
    
- and controlling a genuine relic linked to the stolen identity.
    

Even authentic power does not prove authentic identity.

A being that possesses a fragment of a god may imitate the god convincingly without being the greater deity.

Verification requires examining:

- the source of the power;
    
- the exact terms of the pact;
    
- the patron’s historical knowledge;
    
- its reaction to authenticated relics;
    
- and whether its behavior matches the limitations of the Quiet Edict.
    

---

## **XIII. Pacts and the Threat of Manifestation**

The greatest danger of pact practice is not personal corruption.

It is **distributed manifestation**.

A weakened patron may be unable to enter Aerathon through one vessel. It may therefore establish pacts with many mortals, placing a small fragment or instruction within each.

Individually, none violates the Edict.

Together, they may form:

- a shared mind;
    
- a distributed divine body;
    
- a ritual network;
    
- a living gate;
    
- or the separate pieces of a future avatar.
    

Warning signs include Warlocks who:

- dream the same dream;
    
- speak unfamiliar words simultaneously;
    
- carry complementary marks;
    
- lose memories that appear in another pact-bearer;
    
- feel compelled to gather in one location;
    
- or manifest stronger power when standing together.
    

The patron may describe this as fellowship, communion, ascension, or completion.

The Ministry classifies it as a potential Edict breach.

---

## **XIV. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established or strongly supported:

1. Warlock magic arises through a private pact with a specific patron.
    
2. Pacts differ from divine covenants because they are personal, negotiated, and commonly lack public oversight.
    
3. Patrons may include celestials, divine fragments, Old Gods, fey powers, Archaic intelligences, Labyrinthal entities, and unknown beings.
    
4. Pact magic still produces effects through Matter, Meaning, and Motion.
    
5. Patrons may grant continuing access, knowledge, permanent transformation, embedded fragments, relic authority, or borrowed status.
    
6. A patron cannot necessarily reclaim all power it has granted.
    
7. The disappearance of pact magic may result from withdrawal, broken terms, planar separation, severance, exhaustion, completed obligations, or patron fragmentation.
    
8. Inherited and accidental pacts are genuine magical phenomena.
    
9. A patron’s claimed identity must not be accepted solely because its magic resembles that of a known god.
    
10. Divine fragments may act independently from—and even oppose—the greater gods from which they came.
    
11. Some patrons use Warlocks as limited instruments because the Quiet Edict prevents direct manifestation.
    
12. Networks of pact-bearers may be used to assemble a distributed avatar or divine entrance.
    
13. Not every pact is exploitative, but no pact should be considered safe merely because its terms appear beneficial.
    
14. The complete terms of a pact are often more important than the visible nature of its granted powers.
    

A Warlock is not necessarily a servant.

A patron is not necessarily a master.

Both have entered a relationship shaped by need, ambition, desperation, or opportunity.

The question is not merely who receives power.

It is what the agreement permits the power to become.

> _“A patron rarely gives a mortal a key without caring which door it will eventually open.”_
