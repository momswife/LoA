# **Divine Covenants and Sacred Oaths**

### _The Surviving Channels of Clerical and Paladin Magic After the Quiet Edict_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Divine Practice and Covenant Review Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan  
**Contributing Scholars:** Canon-Jurist Maela Thorn, Magister Selka Vorr, Archivist Yelna Stonevoice, Oath-Marshal Verran Hale  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Foundational Divine Practice / Grade-I Verified Phenomenon with Grade-II Historical Reconstruction_  
**Primary Evidence:** functioning temple covenants, paladin oath records, post-Edict liturgies, failed ordination reports, divine-channel examinations

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The Quiet Edict ended unrestricted divine rule.

It did not end divine magic.

Clerics still heal the wounded, sanctify the dead, repel hostile spirits, and wield powers associated with gods who have not spoken clearly in centuries. Paladins continue to draw strength from oaths of protection, justice, vengeance, mercy, and conquest.

These practices are sometimes treated as proof that the gods remain constantly attentive to their followers.

The evidence does not support that conclusion.

Most clerical and paladin magic operates through **surviving divine covenants**: agreements, offices, and sacred principles established before or during the Quiet Edict and left functioning after the gods withdrew.

The power is real.

The god’s immediate approval is not always required.

This distinction is necessary for understanding both the strength of modern religion and the dangers created by divine silence.

---

## **II. What a Divine Covenant Is**

A divine covenant is a lasting agreement between a divine power and a mortal office, people, institution, bloodline, or sacred duty.

Before the Quiet Edict, gods commonly granted power through direct contact. A deity might inhabit a chosen vessel, speak through a priest, appear before a ruler, or personally invest a champion with authority.

Such relationships became dangerous during the War of the Two Heavens.

After the Edict, unrestricted divine occupation was forbidden. The gods could no longer maintain intimate control over every mortal servant.

The surviving covenants became the permitted alternative.

A covenant may establish:

- who may receive power;
    
- what duties accompany it;
    
- which rites open the channel;
    
- what symbols or offices represent it;
    
- what forms of magic may pass through it;
    
- and what actions can damage or sever it.
    

Once established, a covenant can continue functioning without continual divine instruction.

It behaves less like a conversation and more like a sacred road.

The god built the road.

Later generations may still walk it, even when the builder no longer appears upon it.

---

## **III. Divine Magic and the Three Weaves**

Divine magic does not bypass the Natural Weave.

When a cleric heals a wound or a paladin surrounds an ally with protective force, the resulting effect still changes Matter, Meaning, and Motion.

What distinguishes divine practice is the source of authority and power.

A trained mage constructs a spell through personal knowledge and control.

A divine practitioner invokes a covenant that already possesses an established purpose.

For example, a clerical healing rite may use:

- **Matter** to restore damaged flesh;
    
- **Meaning** to identify the body’s rightful condition;
    
- **Motion** to accelerate recovery;
    
- and a divine covenant to provide power and establish that restoration as a sacred act.
    

The covenant does not replace the Three Weaves.

It gives the caster recognized authority to use them in a particular way.

This is why divine spells often carry consistent themes even across distant cultures. The mortal methods may differ, but the underlying covenant still reflects the god, domain, or sacred purpose that created it.

---

## **IV. Clerics and Sacred Office**

A cleric receives power through participation in a recognized divine office.

That office may be part of a large church, a local shrine, a hereditary priesthood, an isolated funerary order, or a tradition preserved by only a few practitioners.

The cleric does not merely believe in a god.

They occupy a role through which divine power has historically been permitted to pass.

That role may be established through:

- ordination;
    
- initiation;
    
- possession of a holy symbol;
    
- service at a consecrated site;
    
- inheritance of a priestly duty;
    
- acceptance by an existing religious order;
    
- or completion of a rite recognized by the covenant itself.
    

The church and the covenant are related, but they are not identical.

A church is a mortal institution.

A covenant is the sacred structure from which that institution claims authority.

A church may misinterpret the covenant, conceal parts of it, or continue using it long after its original purpose has been forgotten.

---

## **V. Why Clerics Receive Power Without Answers**

Most clerics do not hear their gods speak directly.

They may receive dreams, omens, impressions, or rare visions. Such experiences are difficult to verify and often shaped by the cleric’s culture and expectations.

The regular granting of magic is more mechanical.

A cleric performs the rites, accepts the obligations, and opens a channel established by the covenant.

If the cleric remains compatible with its terms, power may answer.

This means that divine magic does not prove that:

- the god personally heard the prayer;
    
- the god agrees with the cleric’s interpretation;
    
- the institution is morally correct;
    
- every doctrine taught by the church is divine;
    
- or the god remains fully aware and coherent.
    

A priest may perform an authentic miracle while teaching a false history.

A corrupt official may retain divine power because the covenant was attached to an office rather than personal virtue.

A heretic may lose recognition from a church but continue receiving power because the covenant accepts them.

These cases are uncommon, but well documented.

---

## **VI. The Difference Between Faith and Compatibility**

Faith is important to many divine traditions.

It is not always the sole condition for power.

A cleric may sincerely believe while becoming incompatible with the covenant’s purpose. Another may struggle with doubt while continuing to fulfill the duties the covenant requires.

For example:

- A priest of healing who doubts the god but continues treating the sick may retain power.
    
- A priest who possesses complete faith but deliberately denies care to those protected by the covenant may lose access.
    
- A funerary cleric may no longer believe in the traditional account of the afterlife while faithfully preserving the names and passage of the dead.
    
- A temple official may publicly honor the god while using sacred authority for personal wealth, yet retain limited magic if the covenant’s original restrictions were poorly defined.
    

Covenants judge through their surviving terms.

Those terms are not always identical to modern religious morality.

This is one reason ancient divine systems can become dangerous.

A covenant may continue rewarding behavior that was acceptable during the First Making but is considered cruel, unlawful, or intolerable in the present age.

---

## **VII. Paladins and Sacred Oaths**

A paladin’s power does not usually come through priestly office.

It comes through a **Sacred Oath**.

An oath is a deliberate promise aligned with a principle powerful enough to possess lasting magical authority.

Common paladin principles include:

- protection;
    
- justice;
    
- mercy;
    
- liberation;
    
- vengeance;
    
- conquest;
    
- duty;
    
- truth;
    
- guardianship;
    
- and resistance against corruption.
    

Some oaths are associated with specific gods.

Others are connected to broader divine laws established during the First Making or strengthened through generations of mortal devotion.

A paladin may serve a god without belonging to that god’s priesthood.

They may also uphold a sacred principle without claiming allegiance to any deity.

The essential requirement is not worship alone.

It is that the paladin binds their identity and actions to the oath strongly enough for the Natural Weave to recognize the promise as part of who they are.

---

## **VIII. How an Oath Grants Power**

A Sacred Oath changes the Meaning of the person who swears it.

The paladin is no longer merely someone who wishes to protect others. They become a recognized bearer of protection as a sacred duty.

This altered Meaning allows power to gather around the oath.

The stronger and more consistently embodied the promise becomes, the more firmly it can influence the other Weaves.

An oath of protection may:

- strengthen the paladin’s body;
    
- allow them to stand against fear;
    
- create wards around allies;
    
- identify threats;
    
- or punish those who deliberately harm the protected.
    

An oath of vengeance may grant pursuit, endurance, judgment, and destructive power against a declared offender.

An oath of mercy may provide healing, restraint, purification, and the ability to prevent violence.

The oath does not merely provide abilities.

It defines when those abilities are appropriate.

A paladin who treats the oath as a list of powers rather than a binding identity risks weakening the very structure supporting them.

---

## **IX. Cleric and Paladin: The Essential Difference**

Clerics and paladins may serve the same god and perform similar miracles, but their relationships to divine power are different.

|Question|Cleric|Paladin|
|---|---|---|
|**Primary channel**|Sacred office or covenant|Personal Sacred Oath|
|**Source of authority**|An inherited religious role|A deliberately accepted promise|
|**Relationship to institution**|Often central|Optional|
|**Relationship to deity**|Usually tied to a god, domain, or established tradition|May serve a god or a broader sacred principle|
|**Primary danger**|Institution drifting from the covenant|Individual breaking or corrupting the oath|
|**Common loss of power**|Channel, office, rite, or covenant becomes inaccessible|Paladin knowingly ceases to embody the oath|
|**Central identity**|Servant, intermediary, or keeper of sacred practice|Living bearer and enforcer of a promise|

The simplest distinction is:

> **A cleric inherits a sacred responsibility. A paladin makes themselves into one.**

Neither path is inherently stronger.

They fail in different ways.

---

## **X. When Clerical Magic Disappears**

Clerical magic can weaken or vanish without the god personally revoking it.

Known causes include:

### **Covenant Violation**

The cleric acts against a condition built into the sacred office.

### **Loss of Office**

The rite, title, symbol, or institutional recognition required to access the covenant is removed or destroyed.

This does not always mean excommunication is sufficient. A church may remove a title while the covenant continues recognizing the cleric.

### **Broken Sacred Site**

Some covenants depend upon a temple, altar, relic, or consecrated region.

If that anchor is damaged, every cleric connected to it may suffer.

### **Divine Fragmentation**

The god may be dead, divided, imprisoned, or changed so severely that the covenant no longer receives enough power to function.

### **Planar Separation**

Certain regions, including some Labyrinth floors and Primordial Scars, obstruct access to the Near or Far Heaven.

### **Conflicting Covenants**

A cleric may unknowingly accept obligations from two incompatible sacred systems.

### **Institutional Corruption**

A religious order may alter its rites so extensively that it no longer opens the original channel, even though it retains the names and appearance of the faith.

In many cases, loss of magic is gradual.

Healing weakens. Omens cease. Consecrations fail. Spells require greater effort.

The complete disappearance of power is often the final stage of a longer separation.

---

## **XI. When Paladin Magic Disappears**

Paladin power is usually more directly connected to personal conduct.

It may weaken when:

- the paladin knowingly breaks the oath;
    
- the paladin rejects the oath’s authority;
    
- repeated compromises make the promise meaningless;
    
- the paladin obeys the wording while betraying its purpose;
    
- two obligations within the oath become impossible to reconcile;
    
- or another magical influence alters the paladin’s identity.
    

Not every failure causes immediate loss.

Sacred Oaths are not generally destroyed by honest mistakes, ignorance, or inability.

Intent matters.

A paladin who fails to save someone may remain faithful to an oath of protection.

A paladin who abandons someone because saving them is inconvenient may not.

The most dangerous cases involve **contradictory oaths**.

A paladin sworn to obey a crown and protect the innocent may face a ruler who commands the killing of civilians. Both promises cannot be honored.

The resulting magical crisis can produce:

- temporary loss of power;
    
- uncontrolled divine effects;
    
- a fractured oath;
    
- adoption of a new vow;
    
- or transformation into an oathbreaker whose power comes from the remains of the broken promise.
    

---

## **XII. Oathbreakers and Severed Clerics**

The loss of divine magic does not always leave a person powerless.

A broken covenant or oath may leave behind magical damage.

An oathbreaker may retain:

- fragments of former abilities;
    
- inverted sacred protections;
    
- power drawn from anger, guilt, or betrayal;
    
- or access to a new patron willing to occupy the broken channel.
    

A severed cleric may continue performing rituals whose power now comes from:

- a relic;
    
- a divine fragment;
    
- accumulated temple energy;
    
- an ancestral spirit;
    
- a celestial servant;
    
- or a being pretending to be the former god.
    

This creates significant risk.

A practitioner may believe their power has returned when an entirely different source has entered the abandoned covenant.

Ministry investigators therefore distinguish between:

- **restored power**, where the original channel has been repaired;
    
- **residual power**, where remnants of the former covenant remain;
    
- and **replacement power**, where another being now supplies the magic.
    

The visible effects may appear identical.

The obligations rarely are.

---

## **XIII. Corrupt Churches and Valid Miracles**

A functioning miracle is not proof that a church deserves obedience.

This principle is disputed by many religious institutions but supported by repeated examination.

A corrupt church may retain power because:

- its covenant is tied to a public duty it still performs;
    
- its rituals remain structurally valid;
    
- its god cannot easily revoke the channel;
    
- the Edict prevents direct intervention;
    
- or the covenant lacks conditions addressing the present form of abuse.
    

For example, a temple may exploit the poor while continuing to maintain a sacred hospital required by its covenant.

The healing magic may remain real.

That does not make the exploitation sacred.

Similarly, two opposing religious orders may both wield divine power through different portions of the same god’s historical covenants.

This may occur when a deity established separate duties that later entered political conflict.

The god’s silence leaves mortals to determine which institution, if either, still represents the original purpose.

---

## **XIV. The Limits of Divine Discipline**

Before the Quiet Edict, gods could directly confront disobedient servants.

They could speak, withdraw, punish, replace, or destroy a chosen host.

Modern gods possess far less freedom.

They may sometimes:

- send warnings;
    
- alter dreams;
    
- withhold a rare blessing;
    
- cause a relic to reject its bearer;
    
- send a celestial servant;
    
- or weaken a covenant at great cost.
    

They generally cannot reorganize an entire church merely because it has become politically inconvenient or morally compromised.

This explains why ancient religions can remain powerful while drifting far from their founders.

The gods still exist.

Their inability to act should not be mistaken for approval.

---

## **XV. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established or strongly supported:

1. Clerical and paladin magic continues through surviving divine covenants and Sacred Oaths.
    
2. Divine effects still operate through Matter, Meaning, and Motion.
    
3. A cleric receives power primarily through a sacred office, rite, institution, or inherited covenant.
    
4. A paladin receives power primarily by binding their identity to a sacred promise.
    
5. Divine magic does not prove that a god personally approved a particular act or doctrine.
    
6. Clerics may retain power despite doubt, while losing it through incompatibility with the covenant.
    
7. Paladins may retain faith while losing power through the breaking of their oath.
    
8. Corrupt institutions may continue performing authentic miracles.
    
9. Divine silence limits the gods’ ability to correct mortal churches.
    
10. Lost divine power may result from damaged channels, planar interference, broken obligations, divine fragmentation, or institutional change.
    
11. Power that returns after severance must be examined to determine whether its source is original, residual, or replacement.
    
12. Oathbreakers and severed clerics may remain magically dangerous.
    
13. No religious institution may treat possession of divine magic as conclusive proof of legal, historical, or moral authority.
    
14. The continued function of a covenant proves only that some portion of its ancient conditions remains satisfied.
    

A divine covenant is not a god standing beside every cleric.

A Sacred Oath is not a god personally judging every choice of a paladin.

They are structures left behind by powers no longer free to govern the world directly.

Mortals inherit their strength.

They also inherit the responsibility of deciding what that strength should serve.

> _“The miracle proves that the road remains. It does not prove the traveler knows where the god intended it to lead.”_
