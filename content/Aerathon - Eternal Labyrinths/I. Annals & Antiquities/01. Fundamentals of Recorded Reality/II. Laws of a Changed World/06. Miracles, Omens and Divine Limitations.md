> _“The gods are not myths. Their absence is not proof of death, and their existence is not proof of freedom.”_  
> — **Canon-Jurist Maela Thorn**, testimony before the Louvain Synod

# **Miracles, Omens and Divine Limitations**

### _The Verified Existence of the Gods and the Boundaries of Their Influence After the Quiet Edict_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Divine Practice and Covenant Review Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan  
**Contributing Scholars:** Canon-Jurist Maela Thorn, Magister Selka Vorr, Researcher Falyn Onarra, Inspector Jao “Greyglass” Vell  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Foundational Divine Doctrine / Grade-I Verified Existence with Grade-II Interpretive Limits_  
**Primary Evidence:** authenticated relic responses, celestial encounters, divine-realm expeditions, covenant examinations, verified omens, post-Edict manifestation records

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The existence of the gods is not a matter of serious scholarly dispute.

Aerathon possesses physical, magical, and historical evidence of divine beings:

- relics carrying identifiable divine authority;
    
- covenants that continue producing sacred magic;
    
- celestial servants with direct memory of divine courts;
    
- surviving aspects and fragments;
    
- bloodlines altered by divine presence;
    
- documented crossings into the Near and Far Heavens;
    
- and sites where the gods permanently changed the laws of the world.
    

The question is not whether the gods exist.

The question is what they can still do.

Following the War of the Two Heavens and the establishment of the Quiet Edict, the gods lost much of their former ability to enter Aerathon, inhabit mortal hosts, govern nations, and correct every use of their names.

They remain powerful.

They are no longer free.

This record establishes the difference between divine existence, divine communication, divine intervention, and mortal interpretation.

---

## **II. What Constitutes Proof of a God**

Not every miracle, vision, or supernatural being proves the presence of a god.

The Ministry recognizes several forms of evidence.

### **Authenticated Divine Authority**

Certain relics, covenants, and sacred sites carry forms of power traceable to known gods or divine offices.

Their behavior may be reproduced and examined.

### **Celestial Testimony**

Some celestial beings possess memories extending to the divine war or maintain direct ties to a divine court.

Their testimony is valuable, though not automatically unbiased.

### **Divine-Realm Contact**

Expeditions, visions, and planar crossings have confirmed the existence of realms associated with the Dawn Court and Old Court.

Reaching such a realm does not guarantee an audience with its rulers.

### **Consistent Covenant Response**

Clerical traditions separated by distance and culture may access the same divine patterns, suggesting a common original source.

### **Surviving Aspects and Fragments**

Pieces of divine beings remain active in Aerathon.

Their existence proves the greater power from which they were separated, though not necessarily that the greater god remains unchanged.

### **Historical Alteration**

Some regions bear effects no known mortal tradition could have created, including divine commands that predate modern spellcraft.

Taken together, this evidence establishes that the gods are real beings rather than symbols invented solely by mortal institutions.

It does not establish that every teaching about them is true.

---

## **III. What a Miracle Is**

A **miracle** is a magical event produced through divine authority rather than mortal spell structure alone.

A miracle still affects Matter, Meaning, and Motion.

Its distinction lies in the source that authorizes or empowers the change.

Examples include:

- a dying person restored after a sacred covenant answers unexpectedly;
    
- a river changing course in response to an Old Court rite;
    
- a relic awakening to protect a threatened community;
    
- a disease ceasing after a divine vow is fulfilled;
    
- or a celestial servant acting under a god’s ancient command.
    

Not every divine spell is considered a miracle.

Routine clerical healing may use a stable covenant in a predictable manner. It is divine magic, but not necessarily direct divine intervention.

A miracle usually includes something beyond the practitioner’s expected power:

- unusual scale;
    
- impossible timing;
    
- clear symbolic relevance;
    
- an effect outside the normal covenant;
    
- or evidence of an active divine will.
    

The difference is important.

A functioning sacred system proves that divine power remains.

A miracle may suggest that a divine intelligence has noticed and acted.

---

## **IV. Omens and Their Interpretation**

An omen is an event believed to carry divine meaning.

Common omens include:

- unusual animal behavior;
    
- repeated dreams;
    
- sacred flames changing shape;
    
- relics activating without touch;
    
- names appearing upon stone or water;
    
- celestial alignments;
    
- spontaneous healing or decay;
    
- and natural events occurring in symbolic patterns.
    

Omens are difficult to verify because Meaning is influenced by expectation.

A priest anticipating war may interpret a red sunrise as warning. A farmer may see the same sunrise as a sign of heat. Both interpretations may later appear correct.

The Ministry classifies omens in three levels.

### **Personal Omen**

Meaningful primarily to one recipient and difficult to verify independently.

### **Covenant Omen**

Recognized through the established symbols and behavior of a particular sacred tradition.

### **Authenticated Omen**

Observed by multiple independent witnesses, accompanied by measurable magical activity, and linked to a verified divine source.

Even an authenticated omen does not explain itself.

A god may warn that a city will burn without identifying:

- who will ignite it;
    
- whether the event can be prevented;
    
- whether the fire is literal;
    
- or whether destroying the city is itself the warning.
    

Divine communication is often real and still incomplete.

---

## **V. Why the Gods Cannot Simply Speak**

Before the Quiet Edict, gods could enter hosts, appear through avatars, and communicate through extended divine presence.

Those methods are now heavily restricted.

Modern divine communication must usually pass through:

- dreams;
    
- omens;
    
- relics;
    
- established covenants;
    
- celestial intermediaries;
    
- sacred animals;
    
- visions;
    
- or brief and limited aspects.
    

Each method introduces distortion.

A dream passes through the dreamer’s memory and expectations.

A priest interprets an omen according to doctrine.

A celestial messenger may possess its own loyalties.

A relic may preserve the will of the god at the time it was created rather than the god’s present intentions.

A fragment may speak with a divine voice while knowing only one part of the greater being.

The god may send a true message.

The mortal recipient may still understand it incorrectly.

This is one reason the gods rarely settle theological disputes through clear public declarations.

The means capable of carrying such declarations were among those surrendered under the Edict.

---

## **VI. What the Gods Can Still Do**

Within the limits of the Quiet Edict, gods may still exert influence.

They may:

- sustain ancient covenants;
    
- empower sacred offices and oaths;
    
- send dreams, visions, and omens;
    
- awaken or reject relics;
    
- dispatch celestial servants;
    
- strengthen limited aspects;
    
- grant brief blessings;
    
- influence their own divine realms;
    
- act through established sacred sites;
    
- and offer power through restricted private channels.
    

Some gods can influence Aerathon more easily than others.

Their reach may depend upon:

- surviving temples;
    
- active worship;
    
- intact relics;
    
- the strength of old covenants;
    
- proximity to the Near or Far Heaven;
    
- bloodlines carrying their power;
    
- and whether their domains remain embedded in the world.
    

An Old God may act strongly along an ancestral route while remaining almost unreachable within a Dawn-dominated capital.

A Dawn deity may answer through a great temple yet possess little influence in a Feywild crossing.

Divine power is no longer evenly available.

---

## **VII. What the Gods Cannot Freely Do**

The gods generally cannot:

- descend into Aerathon in their complete forms;
    
- maintain full avatars for long periods;
    
- occupy mortal hosts without severe restriction;
    
- wage direct war against another god within the mortal world;
    
- openly govern kingdoms;
    
- reclaim all divine power left in Aerathon;
    
- rewrite the Quiet Edict by individual choice;
    
- personally answer every prayer;
    
- restore every dead person;
    
- prevent every misuse of their religion;
    
- or correct every false statement made in their names.
    

These limits do not make the gods harmless.

A limited divine intervention can still alter the fate of a city.

A relic can still destroy an army.

A dream can still begin a crusade.

The limitation is one of reach, duration, and method—not complete powerlessness.

A god may possess immense authority within a divine realm while being unable to carry more than a fraction of it into Aerathon.

---

## **VIII. Why Prayers Go Unanswered**

The silence following prayer has many possible causes.

### **The Prayer Was Not Heard**

The god may lack a functioning path to the worshipper.

### **The God Cannot Act**

The requested intervention may require a form of manifestation forbidden by the Edict.

### **The Covenant Does Not Cover the Request**

A healing covenant may sustain medicine and restoration without allowing the god to destroy the army causing the wounds.

### **The God Chose Not to Act**

Divine limitation does not remove divine judgment, indifference, calculation, or cruelty.

### **The God Is Damaged**

The deity may be fragmented, dormant, imprisoned, or diminished.

### **Another Power Interfered**

A dead zone, Labyrinth, relic, rival divine law, or planar boundary may block the connection.

### **An Answer Was Misunderstood**

The response may have arrived through an omen, person, opportunity, or warning that the worshipper did not recognize.

### **The Institution Receiving the Prayer Is Broken**

A temple may continue performing rites after losing the covenant that once carried them.

No reliable method can distinguish every form of silence.

Claims that all unanswered prayers reflect unworthiness are unsupported and have caused considerable harm.

---

## **IX. Divine Knowledge Is Not Unlimited**

The gods are powerful, but evidence does not support the claim that every god knows everything occurring in Aerathon.

Their awareness appears strongest where they possess:

- worshippers;
    
- covenants;
    
- relics;
    
- sacred sites;
    
- active domains;
    
- or direct attention.
    

A god of the sea may sense events across oceans more easily than events inside a sealed mountain.

A god associated with knowledge may possess vast records without understanding every private thought.

An ancestral power may know the history of a bloodline while remaining ignorant of a distant kingdom.

The Quiet Edict may also restrict divine observation.

A god unable to enter Aerathon freely may depend upon:

- prayers;
    
- celestial agents;
    
- relics;
    
- dreams;
    
- and mortal reports.
    

This creates the possibility that a church knows more about current mortal affairs than the god it serves.

It also means divine decisions may be based upon incomplete information.

Power should not be confused with perfect understanding.

---

## **X. Resurrection and the Limits of Divine Mercy**

The inability of the gods to restore every dead worshipper is often treated as evidence of indifference.

The matter is more complicated.

Death involves:

- the condition of the body;
    
- the identity of the deceased;
    
- the location and willingness of the soul;
    
- the state of the passage beyond death;
    
- and the authority under which the return is attempted.
    

A god may possess power over healing without authority over the soul’s destination.

A soul may refuse to return.

The body may no longer be capable of holding its former identity.

Another divine or ancestral law may oppose the restoration.

The Quiet Edict may prevent the direct seizure of a soul from its accepted passage.

Reliable resurrection therefore requires a rare alignment of Matter, Meaning, Motion, consent, and lawful access to the dead.

Divine power improves the possibility.

It does not remove every condition.

---

## **XI. Why the Gods Tolerate Corrupt Institutions**

The existence of corrupt churches is sometimes taken as proof that the gods approve of them.

This conclusion is unreliable.

A god may be unable to dismantle a church because:

- its covenant functions independently;
    
- the institution controls the surviving relics;
    
- direct intervention would violate the Edict;
    
- competing factions all retain portions of valid authority;
    
- or the god lacks sufficient access to distinguish reformers from opportunists.
    

A temple may continue producing miracles while acting against the wider purpose of its god.

The miracle proves that a sacred channel remains active.

It does not prove that every person controlling that channel is righteous.

Some gods may attempt correction through:

- prophetic dreams;
    
- rejected relic bearers;
    
- rival clerical orders;
    
- celestial messengers;
    
- or the awakening of forgotten covenants.
    

Such efforts often resemble political or doctrinal conflict rather than unmistakable divine judgment.

Mortals must therefore evaluate institutions through conduct, history, and consequence—not miraculous power alone.

---

## **XII. Divine Fragments and Conflicting Messages**

A divine fragment may possess genuine authority and still misrepresent the current god.

Fragments often preserve the circumstances in which they were created.

A fragment formed during a battle may remember the god only as a warrior.

A fragment left within a hospital may preserve endless mercy.

A fragment placed in a crown may recognize obedience to monarchy as sacred even if the greater god later rejected royal rule.

Two fragments of the same god may issue contradictory instructions.

Both may be authentic.

Neither necessarily represents the complete deity.

This is especially dangerous when churches discover ancient relics and treat their messages as final doctrine.

The relic may reveal something true about the god’s history.

It may also be trapped within a purpose that no longer serves the living world.

---

## **XIII. Ministry Standards for Divine Claims**

Claims of direct divine intervention require examination.

Investigators seek evidence of:

1. measurable magical activity;
    
2. connection to a known covenant, relic, domain, or divine signature;
    
3. consistency across independent witnesses;
    
4. absence of an identifiable mortal spell;
    
5. absence of patron substitution or Archaic imitation;
    
6. behavior consistent with the Quiet Edict;
    
7. and a clear distinction between the observed event and its interpretation.
    

The Ministry may verify that a relic awakened.

It may verify that the relic carries the authority of a known god.

It may not be able to verify why the god—or the fragment—chose to awaken it.

Historical and theological meaning must be recorded separately from magical fact.

This separation prevents institutions from presenting interpretation as evidence.

---

## **XIV. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as established or strongly supported:

1. The gods of the Dawn and Old Courts continue to exist.
    
2. Their existence is supported by physical, magical, celestial, and planar evidence.
    
3. The Quiet Edict sharply limits their ability to enter and govern Aerathon.
    
4. Divine magic may continue through old covenants without direct divine attention.
    
5. Miracles indicate divine authority but do not always prove the immediate presence of a complete god.
    
6. Omens may be authentic while remaining difficult to interpret.
    
7. Gods possess limited reach and may lack complete knowledge of mortal events.
    
8. Unanswered prayer does not prove unworthiness, divine death, or rejection.
    
9. The gods cannot freely resurrect the dead, prevent all suffering, or correct every misuse of their names.
    
10. Corrupt institutions may retain valid sacred power.
    
11. Divine fragments may issue genuine but incomplete or outdated commands.
    
12. A deity may be immensely powerful in its own realm while capable of only limited influence within Aerathon.
    
13. No miracle grants a mortal institution immunity from historical, legal, or moral examination.
    
14. The strongest proof that the gods remain is not that they rule the world, but that the world still bears the limits of their former rule.
    

The gods are present through roads they built, powers they abandoned, and promises that continue to answer.

They are absent from the throne they once occupied.

Between those truths lies modern faith.

> _“We know the gods are real because their hands remain upon the world. We know they are bound because those hands can no longer close around it.”_
