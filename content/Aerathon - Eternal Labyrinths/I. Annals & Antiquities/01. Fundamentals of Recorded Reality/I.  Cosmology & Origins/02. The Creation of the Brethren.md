# **The Creation of the Brethren**

### _The Mortal Stewards of Law, Craft, and Lasting Works_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Cosmological Reconstruction Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice  
**Contributing Scholars:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Professor Arav Dhal-Senn, Historian Merrow Vey  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Dominant Origin Reconstruction / Grade-II Authority_  
**Primary Evidence:** First Mason Hymns, Dawn-Creed fragments, Orichalcum foundation records, early mixed-settlement ruins, pre-Crown funerary tablets

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The Brethren are commonly described in modern religious teaching as the favored children of the Dawn Pantheon.

The oldest surviving evidence does not support that claim.

The Dawn Gods did not create the Brethren to stand above the other peoples of Aerathon. They created them to perform a particular kind of work: to build structures, preserve knowledge, maintain agreements, and carry ordered systems beyond the lifespan of any one mortal.

Where the Beastfolk were shaped to preserve living relationships, the Brethren were shaped to preserve deliberate works.

Their cities, laws, archives, roads, schools, and magical traditions all descend from that original purpose.

So do many of their oldest failures.

This record concerns the Brethren as they were first intended—not as later kingdoms claimed they were intended to be.

---

## **II. Why the Dawn Court Needed Mortals**

During the First Making, the Dawn Gods could raise mountains, mark borders, and establish laws of form. What they could not do was remain personally responsible for every road, settlement, judgment, and work of craft.

Divine construction was powerful, but dependent upon divine attention.

When a god withdrew from a work, that work could weaken, become rigid, or lose the purpose for which it had been made. A perfect wall raised by divine will might stand for centuries, yet no mortal would know how to repair it. A law spoken by a god could settle one dispute while creating ten more once circumstances changed.

The Dawn Court therefore needed mortals capable of understanding design rather than merely obeying it.

The Brethren were created to:

- learn rather than inherit every answer;
    
- repair what had been damaged;
    
- alter old systems when conditions changed;
    
- preserve knowledge outside divine memory;
    
- and continue building after their creators stepped away.
    

The Brethren were not intended to be passive servants.

They were intended to become capable successors.

---

## **III. The First Design**

The oldest Dawn texts describe the creation of the Brethren as a series of trials rather than a single act.

Early forms were too rigid, retaining one purpose but failing to adapt. Others were highly adaptable but lacked continuity between generations. Some could build, but not teach. Others could remember, but not question.

The final design emphasized five qualities.

### **Reason**

The Brethren were made capable of comparing possibilities rather than relying only upon instinct or inherited custom.

### **Individual Identity**

Each person was understood as distinct from family, office, and community. This allowed individuals to accept duties, form contracts, and be judged for their own choices.

### **Teachability**

Skills could pass between unrelated people. A craft did not need to remain within one bloodline.

### **Adaptability**

The Brethren could survive in many climates and alter their habits quickly when circumstances changed.

### **Institutional Memory**

They could create offices, archives, schools, and traditions that survived the individuals who founded them.

These gifts allowed Brethren communities to grow beyond kinship.

A group of strangers could agree upon a law, build a settlement, and leave functioning institutions to descendants who had never known the founders.

---

## **IV. The Brethren Peoples**

The term **Brethren** does not refer to one species alone.

It is the traditional collective name for the mortal peoples shaped primarily under the Dawn Court’s design. Their exact earliest divisions remain disputed, though later records most often include humans, elves, dwarves, and related lineages within the broader Brethren tradition.

The differences among them appear deliberate.

|Brethren Tradition|Original Emphasis|
|---|---|
|**Humans**|Adaptability, expansion, rapid institutional change|
|**Elves**|Long memory, refinement, stewardship across generations|
|**Dwarves**|Craft, endurance, material knowledge, structural permanence|
|**Other Brethren Lineages**|Specialized expressions of art, magic, travel, or social order|

These were not separate experiments competing for divine favor.

They were intended to preserve different parts of the same mortal task.

Humans could establish new systems quickly.

Elves could observe their consequences across centuries.

Dwarves could ensure that structures and tools endured.

In practice, these complementary roles often became grounds for rivalry. Each people developed traditions claiming that its own gift represented the Dawn Court’s highest intention.

No surviving creation record grants that distinction to any one lineage.

---

## **V. The Gifts of the Dawn Court**

The Dawn Gods did not merely create the Brethren and leave them without guidance.

They gave them tools intended to support their role in Aerathon.

### **Names**

Brethren names were treated as statements of individual identity. A person could carry family, civic, professional, and sacred names, each marking a different relationship.

This made names useful in law, magic, inheritance, and oath-making.

### **Written Record**

Writing allowed knowledge to survive beyond memory and travel beyond the community where it began.

Early script was used for construction measures, agreements, maps, and funerary names before it became a tool of literature or royal authority.

### **Craft**

The Brethren were taught to alter Matter deliberately. Stone could be cut, metal joined, timber shaped, and magical forces arranged into repeatable forms.

### **Oaths**

An oath allowed unrelated people to create a binding relationship through chosen words.

This was one of the Dawn Court’s most important contributions to mortal society. It made alliances, guilds, military orders, marriages, and governments possible beyond immediate kinship.

### **Office**

Authority could be attached to a role rather than solely to a person or bloodline.

A judge could die while the court continued. A master builder could be replaced. A crown could pass to a successor.

These gifts formed the basis of Brethren civilization.

They also gave later rulers powerful tools for control.

---

## **VI. The First Law**

The earliest confirmed Brethren legal text is commonly called the **Law of the Shared Wall**.

It was discovered on three Orichalcum plates beneath a settlement whose surviving foundations show both Brethren and Beastfolk construction.

The law concerns a defensive wall built jointly by several communities.

Its central rule can be translated as:

> _“What shields more than one household cannot belong to one household alone.”_

The law required every protected community to contribute labor, material, or food toward the wall’s maintenance. No ruler, family, or divine patron could claim exclusive ownership over it.

This principle appears to have shaped early Brethren law:

- responsibility followed benefit;
    
- shared works created shared obligations;
    
- authority required maintenance;
    
- and ownership did not erase communal duty.
    

Later kingdoms preserved the language of the law while weakening its limits.

Kings claimed that because they maintained walls, roads, and armies, they possessed the right to command everyone protected by them.

A rule meant to prevent private control of public works became an argument for centralized power.

This pattern would repeat throughout Brethren history.

---

## **VII. Stewardship Becomes Dominion**

The Dawn Court created the Brethren to improve and maintain the world.

It did not clearly define where improvement should end.

A marsh could be drained to prevent disease, but doing so might destroy a Beastfolk breeding ground. A road could connect cities, but cut across a migration route. A permanent border could prevent conflict between kingdoms while dividing communities that had crossed the same land for generations.

The Brethren possessed a strong tendency to treat change as proof of progress.

When a system produced visible order—more grain, safer roads, larger settlements—it was assumed to be better than what came before.

This caused growing tension with Beastfolk communities, whose obligations often included preserving routes, ecosystems, burial grounds, and seasonal uses that did not resemble permanent development.

The disagreement was not initially based upon hatred.

It arose from incompatible measures of success.

A Brethren governor might point to a new dam and say:

> “The river no longer destroys the villages.”

A Beastfolk warden might answer:

> “The river no longer reaches the wetlands that keep the valley alive.”

Both observations could be true.

The divine courts increasingly chose which truth to defend.

---

## **VIII. Crown, Temple, and the Problem of Authority**

The first Brethren leaders were likely temporary stewards appointed for a specific need: war, construction, harvest storage, dispute, or travel.

Permanent crowns emerged later.

As settlements grew, Brethren institutions became more difficult to coordinate. Offices gained prestige. Priests claimed unique access to divine instruction. Families who controlled archives, fortifications, or magical knowledge became increasingly powerful.

The Dawn Gods often favored this development because organized authority made large works possible.

A single ruler could coordinate roads, irrigation, defense, and law across several communities.

But permanent authority created a dangerous question:

> Did the ruler serve the system, or did the system belong to the ruler?

Early Dawn teaching favored the first answer.

Later royal doctrine increasingly favored the second.

Kings began claiming that:

- divine approval passed through bloodlines;
    
- law originated with the crown;
    
- settled land belonged to whoever possessed recognized title;
    
- and resistance to royal order was resistance to the gods who created order itself.
    

These claims laid the foundation for the Age of Crowns.

They also convinced many Old Gods that the Dawn Court had allowed its creations to mistake stewardship for divine entitlement.

---

## **IX. The First Brethren Disagreement with Their Gods**

Brethren tradition often presents rebellion against the gods as a much later development.

Evidence from the First Making suggests otherwise.

One fragment from the **Tablets of the Unfinished Tower** records a dispute between a Dawn deity and a group of mortal builders. The god commanded them to complete a tower according to a perfect divine design. The builders refused after discovering that its foundations would crush an occupied Beastfolk burial chamber.

The surviving text preserves the builders’ response:

> _“You taught us measure so that we would know when a work was wrong.”_

The tower was never completed.

Later Dawn churches interpreted the story as a lesson in humility and divine forgiveness. Beastfolk traditions remembered it as proof that mortals recognized the failure of the gods before the gods recognized it themselves.

The Ministry considers the event significant because it reveals something central to Brethren design.

The Dawn Court created mortals capable of questioning a plan.

It was inevitable that they would eventually question their creators.

---

## **X. The Brethren Legacy**

Modern Brethren civilization still reflects the gifts and contradictions of its creation.

Its greatest strengths remain:

- preserving knowledge;
    
- organizing large and diverse communities;
    
- building durable infrastructure;
    
- teaching skills beyond family lines;
    
- developing repeatable magic;
    
- and adapting institutions to new conditions.
    

Its recurring dangers remain equally clear:

- treating written law as more legitimate than lived custom;
    
- confusing ownership with stewardship;
    
- allowing offices to protect themselves instead of the people they serve;
    
- forcing uniform systems upon communities with different needs;
    
- and assuming that the ability to transform the world grants the right to do so.
    

The Brethren were created to build what the gods could not maintain alone.

They succeeded.

They built cities, kingdoms, libraries, roads, temples, guilds, and eventually the Ministry itself.

What their creators failed to anticipate was that mortals capable of continuing divine work would eventually decide that they no longer required divine permission.

---

## **XI. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as the strongest current reconstruction:

1. The Brethren were created primarily by the Dawn Court.
    
2. They were intended as stewards and builders, not rulers over other mortal peoples.
    
3. Their diversity reflects several complementary approaches to preserving civilization.
    
4. Written law, individual oaths, formal craft, and enduring institutions were central divine gifts.
    
5. Brethren expansion grew from genuine strengths rather than inherent malice.
    
6. Later claims of divine racial supremacy are unsupported by the oldest evidence.
    
7. The capacity to question authority was part of Brethren design, not a corruption of it.
    
8. Brethren institutions became one of the main causes of conflict between the two divine systems when they extended their authority over Beastfolk land, custom, and sacred obligation.
    

The Brethren were not created to conquer Aerathon.

They were created to ensure it could be repaired, taught, governed, and rebuilt.

The difference between stewardship and dominion was left for mortals—and eventually gods—to decide.

> _“The first Brethren were given tools. Their descendants made crowns from them.”_
