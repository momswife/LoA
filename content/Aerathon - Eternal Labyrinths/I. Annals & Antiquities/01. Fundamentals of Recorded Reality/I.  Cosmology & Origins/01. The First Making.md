> _“Before there were two heavens, there was one sky—and beneath it, two courts attempting the same work.”_

# **The First Making**

### _The Joint Creation of Aerathon and the First Agreement Between the Divine Courts_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Cosmological Reconstruction Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice  
**Contributing Scholars:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Professor Arav Dhal-Senn, Researcher Falyn Onarra  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Dominant Creation Reconstruction / Grade-II Authority_  
**Primary Evidence:** Celestial Braid Tablets, First Choir Hymns, Sun-Barrow inscriptions, early Orichalcum sites, prehistorical Labyrinth records

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

No complete account of Aerathon’s creation survives.

The oldest Brethren scriptures credit the Dawn Gods with raising the world from darkness. Ancient Beastfolk inscriptions instead describe the Old Gods giving shape, blood, season, and memory to an already forming land.

Both traditions are incomplete.

Neither explains the shared symbols found beneath the oldest temples. Neither explains why Dawn and Beastfolk divine marks appear together at sites that predate both civilizations. Neither explains why certain creation hymns begin in one liturgical language and conclude in the other.

The Ministry’s current position is that Aerathon was not created by one pantheon alone.

It was a joint work.

The two divine courts came from different celestial traditions, possessed different ideas about order, and expected different things from mortal life. Yet for a time, they agreed to create one world together.

This record establishes the strongest reconstruction of that agreement and the world it produced.

---

## **II. Before Aerathon**

The surviving sources do not describe an empty void.

They describe an unfinished world.

Matter existed without stable shape. Seas moved without permanent shores. Mountains rose and collapsed over spans that may have lasted days or centuries. Seasons followed no fixed order, and distance itself appears to have changed according to divine attention.

The gods did not create existence from nothing.

They found—or awakened—a place that could be shaped.

The oldest tablets call this place **the Unmeasured World**. Beastfolk tradition names it **the Land Before Footprints**. Later Brethren texts refer to it simply as **the First Foundation**.

Whatever its original nature, it lacked permanence.

A forest could flourish while watched by a god and vanish when that attention moved elsewhere. Rivers followed divine command rather than gravity. Creatures appeared briefly, fulfilled some immediate purpose, and disappeared without descendants.

Aerathon began when the gods decided the world should continue without requiring their constant presence.

---

## **III. The Two Divine Courts**

The beings involved in the First Making belonged to two distinct divine traditions.

The Ministry refers to them as the **Dawn Court** and the **Old Court**.

### **The Dawn Court**

The Dawn Gods favored structure, names, craft, law, and deliberate construction. They believed the world could be made stable by giving each thing a recognized purpose and place.

Their works included:

- fixed boundaries;
    
- durable materials;
    
- written and spoken names;
    
- predictable celestial movement;
    
- ordered spellcraft;
    
- and institutions capable of surviving their founders.
    

### **The Old Court**

The Old Gods favored continuity, kinship, season, ancestry, and living balance. They believed the world could remain stable only if its parts were allowed to change without losing their relationship to one another.

Their works included:

- birth and inheritance;
    
- migration;
    
- predator and prey;
    
- growth and decay;
    
- ancestral memory;
    
- and the passage from life into death.
    

These were not opposing goals.

One court sought to give the world shape.

The other sought to keep that shape alive.

---

## **IV. The Accord of Making**

The oldest shared accounts describe an agreement between the two courts.

Its original name is lost. Ministry scholars call it the **Accord of Making**.

Under the Accord, neither pantheon would claim Aerathon alone. Each would contribute to its creation, and both would accept limits upon how completely the world reflected their own laws.

The Dawn Court would establish permanence.

The Old Court would establish continuation.

A city could possess walls and roads, but the land beneath it would still age, shift, and demand care. A bloodline could preserve a title, but inheritance would remain tied to birth, family, and death. A forest could grow wild, but it would still possess boundaries that allowed neighboring settlements to endure.

Many features of modern Aerathon appear to result from compromise between the two courts.

|Part of Creation|Dawn Contribution|Old Court Contribution|
|---|---|---|
|**Land**|Boundary, structure, mineral form|Fertility, erosion, seasonal change|
|**Water**|Course, depth, navigable passage|Flood, renewal, hunger, migration|
|**Living Creatures**|Distinct form and recognized identity|Reproduction, adaptation, instinct|
|**Magic**|Pattern, language, repeatable practice|intuition, inheritance, natural response|
|**Death**|separation, judgment, appointed passage|ancestry, mourning, renewal, return|
|**Civilization**|law, construction, office|kinship, custom, communal obligation|

The Accord did not make the courts identical.

It required them to tolerate a world neither could fully control.

---

## **V. Orichalcum and the First Stable Works**

Orichalcum is the oldest confirmed material associated with the First Making.

It appears in structures beneath later civilizations, often in places where no known people possessed the means to mine or shape it. Surviving fragments react to sound, magic, proximity to divine relics, and certain ancestral rites.

The material was likely used because it could hold the work of both pantheons.

Dawn inscriptions describe Orichalcum as a metal capable of retaining design. Old Court hymns describe it as a substance that remembered every hand, voice, and living presence that touched it.

The earliest stable places in Aerathon were built around Orichalcum foundations.

Among the strongest candidates are:

- the buried terraces beneath Sunwound Crater;
    
- the sealed foundations under the oldest Jackal tomb-complexes;
    
- several submerged pillars beneath the eastern sea;
    
- and unidentified structures reproduced on the deepest known Labyrinth floors.
    

These sites differ in design, but each contains joined divine marks.

The evidence suggests that the First Making was not one act.

Aerathon was stabilized region by region.

The gods tested mountains, rivers, climates, and living forms before allowing them to continue without direct supervision.

Some regions succeeded.

Others became the first scars upon the world.

---

## **VI. Why the Gods Created Mortal Peoples**

The gods could shape the world, but they could not remain equally present in every part of it.

As Aerathon grew more stable and complex, it required caretakers capable of making decisions without waiting for divine command.

This appears to be the original purpose of mortal life.

Mortals were not created only to worship.

They were created to continue the work.

The Dawn Court argued that caretakers needed reason, skill, ambition, and the ability to build systems larger than one generation.

The Old Court argued that caretakers needed instinct, memory, adaptation, and an unbroken relationship with land, kin, and the dead.

Neither court believed one design could fulfill every need.

They therefore created two broad mortal traditions.

The **Brethren** would preserve the world through construction, record, office, and deliberate change.

The **Beastfolk** would preserve it through kinship, adaptation, inherited duty, and living relationship.

The two peoples were intended to share Aerathon.

Their differences were deliberate.

Those differences were also the first weakness in the Accord.

---

## **VII. The First Brethren**

The earliest Brethren were shaped to be adaptable.

They were not bound to one climate, animal form, or ancestral landscape. They could cross regions, learn new crafts, build settlements, and organize strangers under shared laws.

Their divine gifts included:

- long memory preserved through writing;
    
- the ability to formalize magical practice;
    
- strong individual identity;
    
- skill in construction and transformation;
    
- and the creation of offices that could survive the deaths of those who held them.
    

The Brethren were expected to raise cities, maintain roads, preserve agreements, and carry knowledge beyond the limits of one family or homeland.

They were builders of permanence.

Yet the same gifts that made them effective stewards also made expansion possible.

A road could connect distant peoples.

It could also cut through sacred ground.

A law could protect strangers.

It could also replace local customs with the judgment of a distant ruler.

The Dawn Gods considered these risks manageable.

The Old Gods were less certain.

---

## **VIII. The First Beastfolk**

The earliest Beastfolk were shaped through distinct animal kinships.

These forms were not ornamental. Each reflected a relationship the Old Gods considered essential to the world.

Predatory kinships embodied vigilance, hunger, territory, and necessary violence. Herd and prey kinships preserved cooperation, movement, awareness, and survival through community. Burrowing, climbing, flying, river-dwelling, and nocturnal peoples carried other relationships between life and environment.

The Beastfolk were given:

- strong ancestral memory;
    
- sensitivity to natural and spiritual change;
    
- identity tied to kinship and inherited obligation;
    
- diverse bodies suited to different regions;
    
- and traditions capable of surviving without centralized institutions.
    

They were expected to maintain migration routes, sacred landscapes, burial customs, animal populations, and agreements between settled peoples and the living world.

They were keepers of continuity.

But continuity could become resistance.

A route followed for generations might remain sacred after a city rose across it. An ancestral obligation might outweigh a new law. A clan might reject an agreement because those who made it lacked the authority of the dead.

The Old Gods considered these tensions necessary protections.

The Dawn Gods increasingly regarded them as obstacles.

---

## **IX. The First Shared Settlements**

The earliest settlements were not divided cleanly between Brethren and Beastfolk.

Archaeological evidence indicates that both peoples lived beside one another during the final stages of the First Making.

Brethren stonework appears beside Beastfolk burial grounds. Jackal judgment inscriptions have been found beneath early Dawn courts. Old irrigation systems combine fixed Brethren channels with seasonal flood routes maintained through Beastfolk rites.

The arrangement appears to have worked.

Brethren communities provided storage, roads, written agreements, and permanent defenses. Beastfolk communities managed seasonal movement, ecological changes, funerary obligations, and relationships with surrounding creatures and spirits.

Later traditions call this period the **First Harmony**.

The name is probably misleading.

Even the oldest shared sites show signs of disagreement:

- walls repeatedly rebuilt along different boundaries;
    
- law tablets amended by separate divine authorities;
    
- burial grounds moved and then restored;
    
- shrines containing symbols from one court deliberately carved over the other;
    
- and settlements abandoned after disputes over land or water.
    

Coexistence existed.

Ease did not.

---

## **X. The First Fault in the Accord**

No surviving source identifies the exact beginning of divine rivalry.

The earliest evidence suggests small acts rather than open hostility.

A Dawn God granted permanent ownership of land the Old Court considered seasonally shared. An Old God redirected animals away from a Brethren settlement after new roads disrupted their migration. A king received divine approval to expand a city, while a Beastfolk oracle received an equally divine command to prevent it.

At first, these contradictions were treated as mistakes.

Then as insults.

Then as proof that the other court could not be trusted.

The gods began altering their creations to protect them from one another’s laws. Blessings became counters. Sacred customs became defenses. Mortal disputes became tests of divine authority.

The First Making succeeded.

The world continued.

But the Accord that created it had left one question unanswered:

> When the needs of the two peoples came into conflict, which pantheon had the right to decide?

Everything that followed began there.

---

## **XI. Ministry Position**

The Ministry accepts the following conclusions as the strongest current reconstruction:

1. Aerathon was created through the joint work of two divine courts.
    
2. The Dawn and Old Courts possessed different but initially compatible goals.
    
3. Brethren and Beastfolk were deliberately created as complementary peoples.
    
4. Neither species was intended to rule the other.
    
5. Early coexistence was real, though never free from tension.
    
6. The divine war began from failures within the original agreement rather than an ancient command that the two peoples should be enemies.
    
7. Several churches and political movements benefit from concealing this shared origin.
    

The final conclusion remains the most disputed.

Modern institutions often present one pantheon as the rightful creator and the other as an intruder, corrupter, or lesser court. The oldest evidence does not support those claims.

Aerathon belonged to both.

The tragedy of its creation was not that the gods began as enemies.

It was that they began as partners and could not remain so.
