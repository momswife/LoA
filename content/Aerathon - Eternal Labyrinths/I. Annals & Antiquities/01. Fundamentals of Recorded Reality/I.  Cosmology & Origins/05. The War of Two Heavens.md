> _“The gods had argued through rivers, kings, harvests, and prophets. At Avarra, they began arguing with weapons.”_  
> — **The Ash Chronicle**, surviving account of the first day of the War

# **The War of the Two Heavens**

### _The Divine Conflict That Broke the First World and Ended the Age of Open Gods_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Cosmological Reconstruction Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice  
**Contributing Scholars:** Researcher Falyn Onarra, Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Professor Arav Dhal-Senn, Marshal-Historian Corren Vaust  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Dominant Historical Reconstruction / Grade-II Authority_  
**Primary Evidence:** Ash Chronicle fragments, Avarran funerary tablets, divine-weapon remains, Sun-Barrow records, Dawn military hymns, deep Labyrinth reconstructions

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The War of the Two Heavens was the final collapse of the partnership that created Aerathon.

It was not one battle. It was a long divine conflict fought through mortal armies, celestial servants, altered landscapes, stolen souls, sacred beasts, living weapons, and the direct presence of the gods themselves.

Both pantheons entered the war claiming they were defending their creations.

The Dawn Court argued that the Old Gods had made law and permanent civilization impossible. The Old Court argued that the Dawn Gods were placing every living thing beneath systems that recognized no limit to their own expansion.

Neither side began with the intention of destroying Aerathon.

Both eventually accepted destruction as the price of preventing the other from controlling it.

The war ended only when the surviving gods understood that victory would leave them with no world worth ruling.

---

## **II. The Final Provocation at Avarra**

The event that began the open war is known as the **Severing at Avarra**.

Avarra was a large mixed city built around one of the earliest shared burial grounds. Brethren and Beastfolk dead had been laid there for generations under separate but cooperating rites.

As divine relations worsened, disputes arose over what happened to souls from mixed families or those who had lived outside their ancestral traditions.

The Dawn priesthood proposed a solution: the **Gate of Ordered Passage**.

The Gate was intended to guide every soul leaving Avarra into a safe, judged passage. Its builders claimed it would prevent spirits from becoming lost, trapped, or claimed by hostile powers.

The Old Court objected.

Its priests argued that the Gate did not guide souls. It placed them under Dawn law before ancestral rites could reach them.

Construction continued.

When the Gate was activated, hundreds of recently dead Beastfolk were drawn toward it. Several jackal priests reported that ancestral paths vanished from their rites entirely.

Anubis sent an aspect to close the Gate.

Dawn guardians ordered it to withdraw.

Neither side yielded.

The Gate broke during the confrontation.

For nine nights, the dead of Avarra appeared throughout the city, unable to complete passage. Some called for relatives. Others repeated only their names. Many were never seen again.

The Dawn Court accused Anubis of destroying a structure built to protect mortal souls.

The Old Court accused the Dawn Gods of attempting to seize the dead of another people.

Within a month, the first divine strongholds were attacked.

---

## **III. The Courts Divide**

Neither pantheon entered the war with complete unity.

Several gods had supported the First Concord and opposed open conflict. Others had spent centuries preparing for it.

Within the Dawn Court, the strongest war faction argued that Aerathon required one consistent divine order. They did not always demand the destruction of Beastfolk, but they believed Old Court law had to be removed from mortal governance.

Other Dawn gods feared what a victory would require. Gods of peace, healing, knowledge, and shared labor attempted to preserve neutral temples and protect mixed communities.

The Old Court was equally divided.

Some demanded the restoration of the First Concord. Others believed the Brethren kingdoms had already proved that coexistence would end in the slow erasure of Beastfolk life.

Set and several allied powers argued that restraint had only encouraged Dawn expansion. Isis, Ptah, Thoth, and other traditions are recorded as seeking negotiation at different stages, though none remained untouched by the conflict.

The war was therefore never simply Dawn against Old.

It also divided each court against itself.

Gods who refused to fight were accused of weakness. Gods who protected the other people were called traitors. Lesser powers were forced to choose patrons or risk being consumed by both sides.

---

## **IV. The War Through Mortal Hands**

The first years of the war were fought mainly through mortals.

Dawn-aligned kingdoms formed armies under temple command. Their soldiers carried blessed weapons, marched beneath divine standards, and relied upon clerics who could receive direct instruction from their gods.

Old Court forces were less centralized. Beastfolk clans, river kingdoms, mortuary orders, sacred-beast hosts, and ancestral companies fought according to local obligations rather than one command.

The Dawn armies possessed stronger fortifications, supply lines, and organized spellcraft.

The Old Court forces possessed greater knowledge of land, spirit routes, seasonal movement, and regions where permanent roads could not be trusted.

Early battles established the pattern of the war:

- Dawn armies captured cities and crossings.
    
- Old Court forces made the surrounding land impossible to hold.
    
- Brethren temples declared new laws.
    
- Beastfolk rites caused older boundaries and obligations to reassert themselves.
    
- Each victory created another reason for divine intervention.
    

Not every mortal supported the gods.

Mixed settlements formed neutral leagues. Brethren soldiers deserted rather than attack Beastfolk neighbors. Beastfolk clans hid Dawn clergy who had rejected temple orders.

These communities were often punished by both sides.

The gods had created mortals to continue their work.

During the war, many mortals discovered that survival required refusing it.

---

## **V. The Descent of the Gods**

The conflict changed when the divine courts began entering Aerathon directly.

Before the war, full divine appearances were rare. Gods worked through chosen hosts, sacred animals, temporary aspects, or consecrated places.

During the war, those limits were abandoned.

Dawn gods entered rulers, paladins, priests, and constructed bodies prepared to hold part of their power. Old Gods took form through sacred beasts, ancestral vessels, rivers, storms, and living landscapes.

These manifestations transformed warfare.

A city protected by a Dawn avatar could resist an army for years. A river occupied by Sobek’s power could tear down every bridge along its course. A desert carrying Set’s anger could move across settled land. A battlefield claimed by a god of war might force every armed person within it to keep fighting until one side was destroyed.

The presence of a god also changed the surrounding world.

Stone remembered divine footsteps. Bloodlines inherited unfamiliar gifts. Animals were altered by proximity. Weather continued obeying commands long after the god departed.

Every manifestation left part of the god behind.

At first, this was considered an acceptable cost.

By the war’s end, it had permanently changed Aerathon.

---

## **VI. The Three Great Fronts**

Ministry historians divide the war into three connected fronts.

### **The Mortal Front**

This included cities, rivers, roads, temples, farmland, and the territories occupied by Brethren and Beastfolk.

Most mortal deaths occurred here.

The war redrew coastlines, emptied kingdoms, destroyed shared settlements, and created regions that remain dangerous in the modern era.

### **The Celestial Front**

The two divine courts attacked each other’s roads, halls, gates, and seats of power.

Surviving accounts describe the sky changing color for weeks, stars disappearing, and divine symbols falling like burning metal.

The conflict damaged the shared celestial order established during the First Making. The division between the Near and Far Heavens became more pronounced during this period.

### **The Front of the Dead**

After Avarra, both courts feared that the other would use mortal souls as a source of power or leverage.

Psychopomps were attacked. Funerary paths were blocked. The dead were redirected, hidden, or held until their loyalty could be determined.

This front produced some of the war’s most lasting horrors.

Entire battlefields failed to release their dead. Tombs became crowded with spirits that had completed every mortal rite but could find no open road beyond them.

The damage done to the passage of souls would later become one of the strongest arguments for ending the war.

---

## **VII. The Burning of the Nine Banners**

The largest confirmed Dawn victory was the **Burning of the Nine Banners**.

Nine Beastfolk confederations had gathered near the River Isheth to prevent a Dawn-aligned empire from crossing into the southern sacred lands.

The Dawn armies could not break the confederation by ordinary means.

A group of Dawn gods therefore joined their power through nine chosen hosts. Each host carried a banner representing a divine office: law, war, fire, craft, judgment, guardianship, conquest, light, and victory.

The combined manifestation burned across the battlefield in a single advancing line.

The Beastfolk defenses collapsed. Three confederations were destroyed almost entirely. The river itself boiled for several miles, and the surrounding soil remained sterile for centuries.

The victory allowed the Dawn armies to advance south.

It also terrified several Dawn gods who had supported the attack.

The hosts carrying the banners did not survive. Their bodies continued burning after death, and portions of the participating gods became trapped within the ruined standards.

Fragments of the Nine Banners are believed to remain in Ministry vaults and sealed royal collections.

Their activation is forbidden.

---

## **VIII. The Night of Empty Names**

The Old Court’s most devastating retaliation came during the **Night of Empty Names**.

Rather than attack a Dawn army, Old Court powers struck the records that allowed several Brethren kingdoms to function.

For one night, names vanished from civic ledgers, family archives, temple rolls, grave markers, and royal genealogies across a wide region.

The people themselves retained their memories, but the institutions surrounding them no longer recognized who they were.

Soldiers could not prove their rank. Heirs could not prove their lineage. Judges could not identify prisoners or property holders. Sacred wards failed because they no longer recognized the names under which they had been established.

The disruption lasted only until dawn.

The consequences lasted generations.

Inheritance wars began immediately. Several rulers were overthrown. Thousands of prisoners disappeared into systems that had lost the records of why they were confined. Families were divided by rival restored genealogies.

Dawn sources describe the event as an attack upon identity itself.

Old Court sources claim it was intended to show the Brethren that a civilization built entirely upon documents could be made helpless by removing them.

Thoth’s involvement is disputed. Some texts credit him with restoring the names before greater damage occurred. Others accuse him of designing the attack.

No conclusive evidence survives.

---

## **IX. Divine Death and Fragmentation**

Gods died during the war.

Most of their names are no longer known.

Some were lesser powers whose worship did not survive them. Others may have been absorbed into stronger gods, leaving their former titles and symbols behind.

Divine death rarely resembled mortal death.

A slain god might break apart into:

- relics;
    
- independent aspects;
    
- sacred bloodlines;
    
- hostile spirits;
    
- altered regions;
    
- or several successor gods each claiming the same identity.
    

One Dawn power associated with safe roads was reportedly destroyed while protecting refugees from both peoples. Afterward, travelers across Aerathon began reporting different unnamed guardians appearing along dangerous paths.

The Ministry cannot determine whether these guardians are fragments of the same dead god or new spirits born from the event.

Several Old Court powers were driven from their earthly seats and never returned. Their temples continued functioning for centuries, granting limited blessings despite receiving no clear divine response.

The loss of a god did not remove its influence.

It scattered it.

Much of Aerathon’s oldest divine magic dates from this stage of the war, when broken gods became easier for mortals to find and far more dangerous to use.

---

## **X. The Wounding of Aerathon**

The war altered the world physically and magically.

Regions repeatedly occupied by divine power stopped obeying ordinary expectations.

Known consequences include:

- forests that follow extinct seasonal cycles;
    
- mountains containing the remains of celestial weapons;
    
- rivers that refuse certain names or bloodlines;
    
- ruins existing in several historical states;
    
- animals born with divine marks;
    
- families inheriting powers no ancestor remembers receiving;
    
- and battlefields where the dead continue waiting for a judgment that never came.
    

The Ministry classifies the oldest and most severe of these places as **Primordial Scars**.

Not every Primordial Scar was created during the war, but many of the strongest are directly associated with it.

The gods also left behind immense quantities of power.

Some of this power remained in relics and ruined divine bodies. Some entered the land. Some became attached to mortal lineages or magical traditions.

The gods could not simply reclaim it.

By the later years of the war, too much of their strength had become part of Aerathon itself. Removing it would have destroyed the people, places, and natural systems that had grown around it.

This would become central to the terms of the eventual truce.

---

## **XI. Why Neither Side Could Win**

The Dawn Court achieved greater control over cities, institutions, and permanent settlements.

The Old Court retained stronger influence over wilderness, ancestry, death rites, and the living systems upon which those cities depended.

Neither advantage could eliminate the other.

When Dawn armies conquered a region, Old Court power remained in its rivers, animals, dead, and ancestral obligations.

When the Old Court destroyed a kingdom, Brethren records, roads, fortifications, and surviving institutions allowed it to be rebuilt.

The two peoples had been designed to preserve different parts of the same world.

Destroying one completely would have crippled the other.

The gods began to understand this only after several attempted victories produced disasters for their own followers.

Dawn-aligned cities starved after Beastfolk ecological systems collapsed. Old Court territories lost healing, construction, and long-distance trade after Brethren institutions were destroyed.

The First Making had made the peoples complementary.

The war proved that complementarity through suffering.

---

## **XII. The Road to Truce**

The war’s final years were marked less by decisive battles than exhaustion.

Divine hosts failed more often. Sacred animals turned upon the gods occupying them. Clerics lost the ability to distinguish divine command from the thoughts of the beings inhabiting them.

The dead continued gathering along broken passages.

Several gods withdrew without permission from their own courts.

Mortal rebellions spread through both Brethren and Beastfolk territories. Temples were closed by their own worshippers. Divine weapons were buried, stolen, or turned against the powers that had created them.

The final negotiations began after an event known as the **Silence at Merath**.

Two divine armies approached the same city from opposite directions. Merath contained refugees from both peoples and one of the largest collections of stranded dead remaining from the war.

When the gods attempted to manifest, nothing answered them.

Their hosts remained mortal. Their weapons would not awaken. Their servants heard no commands.

For seven days, the city stood between two armies while every divine power nearby remained silent.

No source agrees on what caused the Silence.

Some claim Aerathon itself refused the gods.

Others believe a third power intervened.

Whatever the cause, the warning was understood.

The gods had spent so much of themselves upon the war that the world was beginning to reject their presence.

Negotiations for the Quiet Edict began soon afterward.

---

## **XIII. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as the strongest current reconstruction:

1. The Severing at Avarra began the open War of the Two Heavens.
    
2. Both pantheons entered the conflict divided.
    
3. Mortal rulers and institutions expanded the war for their own purposes.
    
4. The gods abandoned earlier limits upon manifestation.
    
5. Divine warfare damaged mortal lands, celestial realms, and the passage of souls.
    
6. Both courts committed acts that would constitute atrocities under modern law.
    
7. Several gods died, fragmented, disappeared, or were permanently diminished.
    
8. Large portions of divine power became trapped in Aerathon.
    
9. Neither pantheon could destroy the other without also destroying the systems needed by its own creations.
    
10. The Silence at Merath forced both courts to consider a binding truce.
    
11. Modern religious claims that one pantheon fought a purely defensive war are unsupported.
    
12. Many present-day relics, bloodlines, sacred regions, and magical anomalies descend directly from the conflict.
    

The War of the Two Heavens did not determine which pantheon deserved Aerathon.

It proved that neither could possess the world without the other’s creations living within it.

> _“The gods ended the war when they finally understood what mortals had known for generations: there would be no victory, only survivors.”_
