> _“The Concord did not fail because the two peoples were too different. It failed because their gods decided every difference required a winner.”_

# **The First Concord and Its Breaking**

### _The Failed Agreement Between the Dawn Court, the Old Court, and Their Mortal Peoples_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Cosmological Reconstruction Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice  
**Contributing Scholars:** Researcher Falyn Onarra, Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Magistrate Talar Shellmark  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Dominant Historical Reconstruction / Grade-II Authority_  
**Primary Evidence:** Concord Stones, shared-settlement law tablets, Sun-Barrow testimony, Dawn judicial fragments, early divine relics, mixed liturgical records

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The Brethren and Beastfolk were not created as enemies.

The earliest settlements show both peoples sharing roads, water, defenses, markets, and burial grounds. Their gods also worked together during the First Making, dividing responsibilities rather than territory.

The **First Concord** was the agreement intended to preserve that cooperation.

It established how the two divine courts would guide their peoples without allowing either system to dominate Aerathon. It also created rules for resolving disputes between Brethren law and Beastfolk obligation.

For a time, the Concord worked.

Its failure did not begin with open hatred or a declaration of war. It began with practical disagreements over land, authority, worship, inheritance, and death.

The gods were expected to settle those disagreements.

Instead, they gradually made them worse.

---

## **II. The Terms of the Concord**

No complete copy of the First Concord survives.

Its major terms have been reconstructed from repeated clauses found across Brethren and Beastfolk sites. The wording differs, but the obligations remain broadly consistent.

### **Shared Creation**

Neither pantheon could claim sole ownership of Aerathon or exclusive authority over mortal life.

### **Separate Guidance**

Each court could instruct and protect its own creations, provided that its actions did not directly harm the other people.

### **Mutual Passage**

Brethren and Beastfolk were to retain access to roads, rivers, migration routes, markets, and sacred paths required for their survival.

### **Protected Custom**

Neither people could be forced to abandon its laws, rites, burial practices, or divine obligations merely to satisfy the customs of the other.

### **Joint Judgment**

Disputes affecting both peoples required representatives from both legal traditions.

### **Limits upon Divine Intervention**

The gods could advise, warn, bless, and protect. They were not to overturn mortal agreements simply because an outcome favored the other court.

These terms were not merely symbolic.

Several early settlements were physically organized around them. Shared courts contained two judgment seats. City gates included separate markings for lawful entry and ancestral passage. River agreements recorded both permanent construction rights and seasonal obligations.

The Concord was built into the first mixed societies.

---

## **III. How Shared Settlements Worked**

Brethren and Beastfolk communities often occupied the same region while maintaining different responsibilities.

The Brethren generally managed permanent infrastructure:

- roads;
    
- walls;
    
- bridges;
    
- storehouses;
    
- written agreements;
    
- and long-distance trade.
    

Beastfolk communities generally managed living and seasonal systems:

- migration routes;
    
- flood cycles;
    
- animal populations;
    
- burial grounds;
    
- grazing rights;
    
- and relations with local spirits.
    

Neither division was absolute. Beastfolk built cities, and Brethren tended forests and rivers. The arrangement reflected emphasis rather than restriction.

At their best, shared settlements benefited from both systems.

A Brethren engineer could construct a dam. Beastfolk river wardens could determine when it should be opened to preserve downstream wetlands. Written records preserved the agreement, while seasonal rites reminded later generations why the terms mattered.

Problems arose when one half of the arrangement was treated as secondary.

A road remained visible.

The migration it disrupted was easier to ignore.

A legal deed remained in an archive.

The spoken obligations attached to it could be forgotten when the witnesses died.

Over generations, Brethren institutions often accumulated authority simply because their records were more durable and easier for distant rulers to enforce.

---

## **IV. The First Major Disputes**

The oldest surviving cases show the same disagreements appearing across several regions.

|Dispute|Brethren Position|Beastfolk Position|
|---|---|---|
|**Land ownership**|A recognized deed established lasting control|Use remained conditional upon duties to land and neighboring peoples|
|**Borders**|Fixed boundaries prevented conflict|Seasonal movement could not always obey permanent borders|
|**Inheritance**|Property and office passed through recognized succession|Obligations passed with property and could limit an heir’s authority|
|**Judgment**|Written law should apply consistently|Context, restitution, and kinship mattered to a just outcome|
|**Burial**|Civic expansion could relocate graves through legal process|Burial grounds remained under protection of the dead and their descendants|
|**Water**|Engineering could improve and secure its use|Rivers remained shared living systems, not possessions|
|**Divine authority**|Recognized temples could certify lawful decisions|No temple could cancel an older sacred relationship merely through proclamation|

None of these disagreements made coexistence impossible.

They required compromise.

The difficulty was that both peoples could appeal to gods who understood the dispute through different principles.

A Dawn deity might see a fixed boundary as a fair solution.

An Old God might see the same boundary as an attempt to bind a living landscape permanently.

Instead of accepting the limits of their own perspective, the divine courts increasingly treated disagreement as evidence of interference.

---

## **V. The Court of Two Measures**

The strongest surviving example of Concord-era cooperation is the **Court of Two Measures** at Talarin Ford.

The settlement stood where a major Brethren trade road crossed a Beastfolk migration route. Disputes were common, particularly during the spring movement of herd kinships.

The court used two standards of judgment.

The **Stone Measure** recorded deeds, contracts, boundaries, and direct material loss.

The **Living Measure** considered seasonal need, inherited duty, environmental consequence, and harm to relationships that could not be restored through payment alone.

A judgment was valid only when both measures agreed.

One surviving case concerns a merchant whose wagons blocked the migration route for six days. The merchant held a lawful permit and argued that no written restriction prohibited stopping there.

Under the Stone Measure, the permit protected him from criminal punishment.

Under the Living Measure, his actions caused the herd to divert through newly planted fields, damaging crops and resulting in several animal deaths.

The court ruled that he had not broken the written law, but had violated the Concord’s shared-passage obligation. He was required to compensate the farmers, fund improvements to the crossing, and travel with the affected Beastfolk community during the next migration.

The purpose was not punishment alone.

He was expected to understand the system his lawful action had disrupted.

The Court of Two Measures operated for nearly two centuries.

Its destruction became one of the earliest clear signs that the Concord was failing.

---

## **VI. The Divine Games**

The gods did not begin by openly attacking one another.

They began by helping their own people gain small advantages.

The period is known in later records as the **Divine Games**, though those who lived through it would not have considered it playful.

Dawn gods granted stronger walls, clearer contracts, more productive fields, and magical protections against natural hazards.

Old Gods strengthened ancestral memory, redirected animals, restored seasonal floods, and caused disputed land to resist permanent settlement.

Each action could be defended as protection.

Together, they undermined the Concord.

Common forms of interference included:

- sending contradictory prophecies to opposing communities;
    
- granting blessings that bypassed shared agreements;
    
- concealing sacred sites from the other people;
    
- changing weather to favor one settlement;
    
- empowering selected judges or rulers;
    
- manipulating fertility, harvests, and animal movement;
    
- and placing divine champions into disputes already close to violence.
    

The gods rarely admitted they were violating the agreement.

They claimed they were correcting the other court’s unfair advantage.

Every exception became justification for another.

---

## **VII. The Broken Harvest at Edras Vale**

The **Broken Harvest at Edras Vale** is the best documented example of divine rivalry causing a mortal disaster.

Edras Vale supported a large Brethren grain settlement and several Beastfolk communities responsible for maintaining pollinating insects, river access, and the movement of grazing herds.

After several poor harvests, the Brethren priesthood asked Chauntea for protection. The resulting blessing caused the grain to grow rapidly and resist pests.

The harvest appeared miraculous.

It also consumed far more water than the valley could naturally provide.

Beastfolk wardens petitioned the Old Court, warning that the river and surrounding grasslands were dying. Sobek’s priests called for the irrigation gates to be opened, while followers of Isis demanded that part of the crop be sacrificed to restore the valley.

The Brethren council refused. They argued that destroying a divinely blessed harvest would condemn thousands to hunger.

An Old God then sent floodwater through the valley.

The flood saved the river and destroyed most of the grain.

Brethren survivors accused the Old Court of attacking civilians. Beastfolk survivors accused the Dawn Court of creating a blessing that fed one people by exhausting the land needed by everyone else.

Both courts claimed the other had acted first.

The following winter killed nearly a third of the valley’s population.

No god accepted responsibility.

---

## **VIII. Mortal Politics Enter the Conflict**

Divine rivalry became more dangerous when mortal rulers realized it could be used for political advantage.

Brethren kings claimed that resistance to civic law was evidence of Old Court interference. Beastfolk leaders accused rivals of carrying Dawn influence when they supported permanent settlements or centralized authority.

Religious loyalty became a test of political trust.

Mixed families faced increasing pressure to choose one tradition. Shared courts were replaced by separate institutions. Settlements began restricting which priests could conduct marriages, funerals, or inheritance rites.

Several rulers deliberately provoked disputes in the hope of receiving divine aid.

A frontier king might settle disputed land, expecting a Dawn god to defend the new city. A Beastfolk war leader might destroy survey markers, expecting the Old Gods to conceal or protect the raiders.

The gods often rewarded such behavior because it appeared loyal.

This encouraged mortals to turn manageable disagreements into tests of pantheonic strength.

By the late Concord period, rulers were no longer merely caught between the divine courts.

They were learning how to manipulate them.

---

## **IX. The Sabotage of the Shared Laws**

The Concord depended upon laws recognized by both pantheons.

As trust collapsed, each court began quietly altering how those laws worked.

Dawn priests revised shared contracts so that written terms took priority over spoken obligations. Old Court ritualists taught that certain ancestral duties could not be changed by any mortal agreement.

Brethren judges were granted magic that exposed false testimony but often failed to recognize statements shaped by Beastfolk communal memory. Beastfolk oath-keepers could identify broken relationships but sometimes treated legal change itself as evidence of betrayal.

Even magic became partisan.

A boundary ward blessed by the Dawn Court might reject Beastfolk who had crossed the land for generations. An ancestral path protected by the Old Court might pass through Brethren walls as though the construction held no lawful place there.

The world began enforcing two different versions of justice in the same locations.

This was more than cultural disagreement.

Divine law was becoming physically incompatible.

The first local reality failures associated with the Concord appear during this period:

- doors opening for one people but not another;
    
- rivers following different courses according to who observed them;
    
- courts producing contradictory magical verdicts;
    
- land recognizing multiple owners simultaneously;
    
- and the dead refusing funerary rites performed outside their ancestral tradition.
    

These incidents remained limited, but they proved the danger of continued divine interference.

---

## **X. The Destruction of the Court of Two Measures**

The Court of Two Measures became a target because it still required both systems to recognize the other.

The final dispute concerned the expansion of Talarin Ford.

Brethren officials approved a new fortified district across part of the migration route. Beastfolk representatives agreed to the expansion on the condition that a broad seasonal passage remain open through the walls.

Construction began under that agreement.

During the work, a Dawn oracle declared that leaving the passage open would allow an approaching enemy to enter the settlement. A Beastfolk dream-reader received a warning that closing it would kill thousands of migrating animals and break an ancestral covenant.

Both warnings appear to have been genuine.

The court ordered a delay while another route was prepared.

Before that work could be completed, Brethren soldiers sealed the passage under royal command. Beastfolk wardens destroyed part of the unfinished wall that same night.

Violence followed.

During the fighting, the two judgment stones at the center of the court cracked simultaneously. Witnesses reported hearing two divine voices, each declaring the other measure invalid.

The courthouse collapsed.

Later investigation found no ordinary structural cause.

The destruction was widely understood as the moment when the divine courts themselves rejected shared judgment.

Talarin Ford was abandoned within a generation.

Its ruins remain unstable. Visitors sometimes find two intact courthouses occupying the same foundation, each preserving a different version of the final dispute.

---

## **XI. The Breaking of the Concord**

The First Concord was never formally revoked.

It simply ceased to govern behavior.

Shared courts closed. Priests stopped recognizing the other court’s rites. Settlements divided along species and religious lines. Old grievances were rewritten as proof that coexistence had always been impossible.

The divine courts increasingly described themselves as protectors against the other.

Dawn theology argued that civilization could not survive while Old Gods disrupted law and construction.

Old Court traditions argued that life itself could not survive if Dawn institutions continued binding land, people, and spirits beneath permanent authority.

Both positions contained legitimate fears.

Both were used to justify greater control.

By the end of the Concord period, even acts of compromise were treated as betrayal. Gods who continued cooperating across pantheonic lines lost influence or were accused of disloyalty. Mortal communities that maintained shared customs became vulnerable to both sides.

The original partnership had become a contest over which divine system had the right to define Aerathon’s future.

Only one final violation was needed to turn that contest into war.

---

## **XII. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as the strongest current reconstruction:

1. The First Concord was a real governing agreement between the two divine courts.
    
2. It established shared protections for Brethren and Beastfolk life.
    
3. The Concord worked best where mortal institutions combined written law with inherited and environmental obligation.
    
4. Its earliest failures arose from practical disputes rather than inherent racial hatred.
    
5. Divine favoritism turned local disagreements into pantheonic contests.
    
6. Both courts violated the spirit and likely the terms of the agreement.
    
7. Mortal rulers, priests, and faction leaders encouraged the escalation for political gain.
    
8. The destruction of shared courts made later conflict far more difficult to resolve peacefully.
    
9. The eventual war was not inevitable, but each act of retaliation made restraint less likely.
    
10. Modern claims that one people alone broke the Concord are unsupported by surviving evidence.
    

The First Concord failed because cooperation required both courts to accept that their laws were incomplete.

Neither was willing to do so for long.

> _“The first peace ended when every compromise began to look like surrender.”_
