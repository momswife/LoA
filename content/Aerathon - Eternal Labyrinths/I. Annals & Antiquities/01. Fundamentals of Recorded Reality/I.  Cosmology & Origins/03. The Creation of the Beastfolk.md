> _“The Beastfolk were not made to resemble animals. They were made to remember what each life owed the world around it.”_  
> — **Fragment from the River Mothers’ Litany**, translated from Old Jackal Script

# **The Creation of the Beastfolk**

### _The Mortal Keepers of Kinship, Continuity, and the Living World_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Cosmological Reconstruction Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Researcher Falyn Onarra  
**Contributing Scholars:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice, Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Warden Hessa Reedstep  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Dominant Origin Reconstruction / Grade-II Authority_  
**Primary Evidence:** Sun-Barrow inscriptions, Jackal mortuary tablets, River Kingdom hymns, ancestral dream records, early mixed-settlement ruins

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The Beastfolk are often described as children of the wild, descendants of sacred animals, or mortals altered by ancient magic.

Each explanation contains part of the truth.

The oldest evidence indicates that the Old Court created the Beastfolk deliberately, shaping their bodies and instincts through the patterns of animals already present in the First Making.

They were not intended to live apart from civilization.

They were created to ensure that civilization remained connected to the living systems around it.

Where the Brethren were designed to preserve knowledge through writing, office, and construction, the Beastfolk were designed to preserve obligations through kinship, custom, instinct, and inherited memory.

Their purpose was not to oppose cities.

It was to prevent cities from forgetting the rivers, forests, migrations, burials, and living creatures upon which they depended.

---

## **II. Why the Old Court Chose Animal Kinships**

The Old Gods rejected the idea of creating one adaptable mortal form for every environment.

They believed that a people capable of living anywhere might eventually believe they belonged everywhere.

Instead, they shaped Beastfolk through distinct animal kinships.

Each kinship carried physical traits suited to particular environments, but also preserved a way of understanding the world.

A cervine people might experience danger through herd awareness and movement. A feline people might understand territory, patience, and sudden action. A corvine people might preserve observation, memory, and adaptation. A jackal people might be drawn toward death, judgment, and the protection of boundaries.

These tendencies were not absolute commands.

A foxfolk was not required to be cunning. An ursine was not born violent. A harefolk was not condemned to fear.

Animal kinship provided an inherited perspective, not a fixed personality.

The Old Court intended those perspectives to keep mortal civilization from developing only one answer to every problem.

---

## **III. The First Kinships**

No complete list of the earliest Beastfolk peoples survives.

Ancient records group them by sacred role more often than by modern species names.

|First Kinship|Preserved Relationship|
|---|---|
|**Herd Kinships**|Cooperation, migration, vigilance, shared survival|
|**Predatory Kinships**|Territory, restraint, necessary violence, balance|
|**Burrowing Kinships**|Shelter, hidden labor, patience, life beneath the surface|
|**River Kinships**|Flood, hunger, fertility, danger, seasonal return|
|**Winged Kinships**|Distance, observation, message-carrying, changing paths|
|**Scavenger Kinships**|Death, cleansing, survival, the reuse of what remains|
|**Nocturnal Kinships**|Secrecy, dream, unseen danger, navigation through darkness|
|**Great-Beast Kinships**|Strength, endurance, guardianship, long memory|

Modern Beastfolk lineages developed from these broad traditions over long periods.

Some retained close physical resemblance to their original sacred forms. Others changed through migration, intermarriage, divine intervention, or exposure to unfamiliar environments.

This is one reason the Ministry avoids treating Beastfolk identity as a simple catalogue of animal traits.

The kinship was a starting point.

History did the rest.

---

## **IV. The Gifts of the Old Court**

The Beastfolk received several gifts intended to help them preserve relationships the gods feared permanent institutions might overlook.

### **Ancestral Memory**

Beastfolk communities could carry knowledge through repeated story, ritual, gesture, and family practice.

This did not mean every Beastfolk remembered the distant past directly. Rather, important knowledge could survive without being written if each generation continued performing and teaching it.

### **Environmental Sensitivity**

Many Beastfolk lineages could recognize changes in weather, soil, animal behavior, spiritual presence, and seasonal patterns before Brethren instruments or records detected them.

### **Kinship Obligation**

Identity was understood through relationships as well as individuality.

A person carried duties to family, clan, community, ancestors, land, and the creatures with which their people shared a sacred connection.

### **Adaptation Through Lineage**

The Beastfolk did not adapt as quickly as the Brethren through tools and institutions. Their changes often occurred more gradually through custom, migration, and inherited physical traits.

### **Ritual Continuity**

The Old Court taught actions that could preserve meaning even when the original explanation was lost.

A burial gesture, migration song, naming rite, or seasonal offering could maintain an ancient relationship for centuries.

These gifts made Beastfolk traditions difficult to erase completely.

They also made old obligations difficult to abandon.

---

## **V. The Jackal Charge**

Among the oldest confirmed Beastfolk duties was the **Jackal Charge**.

The jackal kinships were entrusted with the care of the dead, the protection of tombs, and the judgment of unresolved obligations.

Their role was not based upon fear of death alone.

The Old Court believed that a civilization unable to care for its dead would eventually lose its understanding of responsibility to both past and future generations.

Jackal priests preserved names, prepared bodies, settled final debts, and guarded burial grounds against theft or misuse. They also served as judges in disputes where the dead could no longer speak for themselves.

The earliest version of the Charge states:

> _“No life ends while its promises remain among the living.”_

This principle shaped Beastfolk law.

Inheritance was not merely the transfer of property. It was the acceptance of unfinished obligations.

A child might inherit a field, but also the duty to maintain a riverbank. A clan might inherit a hunting ground, but also responsibility for preserving the animals within it.

Later rulers often accepted the privileges of inheritance while refusing the obligations.

Many early conflicts began there.

---

## **VI. Land Without Ownership**

The Old Court did not teach that land could never be claimed.

Beastfolk communities maintained territories, sacred sites, grazing routes, fishing waters, and defended settlements.

The difference lay in what a claim meant.

Land could be held, used, protected, or inherited. It could not be separated entirely from the obligations that made its use possible.

A river crossing belonged to those who maintained it, but travelers retained rights of passage. A forest might be guarded by one clan, but that clan could not destroy the migration routes passing through it. A fertile valley could support permanent homes, but its floodplain still required room to change.

This system worked well while communities remained small and authority stayed local.

It became difficult when Brethren settlements expanded.

Written deeds granted permanent control over land Beastfolk communities understood as seasonally shared. Roads divided territories that had never required fixed borders. Fields replaced wetlands, burial grounds, and migration paths.

Brethren officials often concluded that Beastfolk law was vague because it was not written.

Beastfolk leaders concluded that Brethren law was dishonest because it recorded rights while omitting duties.

Both peoples believed the other was breaking the Accord.

---

## **VII. The First Beastfolk Councils**

The earliest Beastfolk governments were not uniform.

Some were led by elders. Others by ritual specialists, hunters, war leaders, river wardens, or councils representing several kinships.

Authority was usually tied to a responsibility.

A warden who failed to protect a migration route could be removed. A judge who ignored ancestral law could lose the right to speak in council. A clan leader who endangered neighboring peoples might be abandoned by those they led.

This made Beastfolk leadership flexible, but difficult to centralize.

The Dawn Court often viewed that lack of central authority as disorder.

When Brethren officials wanted an agreement, they expected one ruler to sign for everyone. Beastfolk communities might require several families, ancestral keepers, and neighboring kinships to approve the same decision.

To the Brethren, this process was slow and unreliable.

To the Beastfolk, it was proof that no one person could give away obligations belonging to generations not yet born.

This difference became one of the greatest practical failures of the First Concord.

---

## **VIII. The Problem of Inherited Duty**

The Beastfolk were created to remember relationships across generations.

That gift came with a cost.

An obligation could survive long after its original purpose became unclear.

A clan might continue guarding a ruined shrine because its ancestors had sworn to protect it. A family might maintain hostility toward another lineage without remembering the event that began the feud. A migration route might remain sacred even after environmental change made it dangerous.

The Old Court generally trusted communities to reinterpret these duties carefully.

Some gods resisted any change.

Others strengthened old obligations when they feared Brethren influence was erasing Beastfolk identity.

This created tension within Beastfolk society itself.

Younger generations sometimes questioned whether inherited duties still served the living. Elders feared that abandoning even one obligation might unravel many others.

The oldest surviving debate appears on a set of river stones from the settlement of Iseth’s Bend:

> _“If the promise has become harmful, do we honor the ancestor by keeping it—or by admitting they could not foresee what followed?”_

No agreed answer survives.

The same question continues to divide Beastfolk communities in the modern age.

---

## **IX. The First Interference of the Old Gods**

The Old Court initially expected Beastfolk communities to resolve disputes without constant divine intervention.

That restraint weakened as Dawn institutions expanded.

One of the earliest confirmed interventions occurred at the **Fields of Namar**, where a Brethren settlement diverted a river to support permanent agriculture. The diversion protected the settlement from drought but deprived several Beastfolk communities of seasonal water.

Brethren records state that the land had been lawfully granted and improved.

Beastfolk testimony states that the river was bound without the consent of those downstream.

The Old Goddess Isis is believed to have answered the Beastfolk petition.

During the following flood season, the river abandoned the constructed channel and returned to its previous course. The Brethren dam remained standing, but water flowed around it as though the structure did not exist.

The settlement lost most of its harvest.

The Dawn Court accused the Old Gods of sabotaging a lawful mortal work.

The Old Court answered that the Dawn Gods had permitted their people to claim authority over a river they had not created alone.

The dispute was eventually settled through a shared channel system.

The resentment was not.

Namar became one of the first cases in which divine protection of one people directly harmed the other.

---

## **X. Beastfolk Who Rejected Their Creators**

The Beastfolk were never entirely obedient to the Old Court.

Some communities believed the gods had burdened them with too many inherited duties. Others resented being treated as extensions of animal forms or sacred roles.

A notable early movement known as the **Unmarked Kin** removed divine symbols from homes and refused to identify themselves by ancestral function.

Their surviving declaration reads:

> _“We were given memory so that we might choose what deserves remembrance.”_

The Old Court responded inconsistently.

Some gods condemned the movement as a rejection of sacred identity. Others protected it, arguing that a relationship maintained through force was no relationship at all.

The movement later disappeared from the historical record, though several modern Beastfolk customs may descend from it.

Its existence is important.

Like the Brethren, the Beastfolk were created with enough judgment to challenge the gods who shaped them.

The divine courts would later blame one another for that disobedience.

The evidence suggests it arose naturally from mortal freedom.

---

## **XI. The Beastfolk Legacy**

Modern Beastfolk cultures still reflect the gifts of their creation.

Their greatest strengths remain:

- preserving knowledge through family and ritual;
    
- maintaining strong communal bonds;
    
- recognizing changes in land and spirit;
    
- surviving the collapse of centralized institutions;
    
- carrying identity across migration and displacement;
    
- and remembering obligations that larger governments overlook.
    

Their recurring dangers also remain:

- allowing inherited duties to control the living;
    
- treating ancestral custom as unquestionable;
    
- placing communal expectation above individual choice;
    
- preserving feuds after their causes are forgotten;
    
- resisting necessary change until crisis makes adaptation impossible;
    
- and assuming that old practice is always closer to divine intent.
    

The Beastfolk were not created to remain outside civilization.

They were created to remind civilization that survival depends upon relationships no wall, law, or title can replace.

Their tragedy was that this duty increasingly forced them into opposition with the very people they had been intended to support.

---

## **XII. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as the strongest current reconstruction:

1. The Beastfolk were created primarily by the Old Court.
    
2. Their animal kinships were deliberate expressions of sacred relationships, not signs of lesser development.
    
3. They were intended to live beside the Brethren and preserve parts of the world Brethren institutions might overlook.
    
4. Ancestral memory, ritual continuity, and environmental sensitivity were central divine gifts.
    
5. Beastfolk law recognized possession and territory, but tied them closely to continuing obligation.
    
6. Their decentralized political traditions made cooperation with expanding Brethren states difficult.
    
7. Later claims that Beastfolk were created as servants, experiments, or corrupted Brethren are unsupported by the oldest evidence.
    
8. The Old Gods increasingly intervened when they believed Dawn institutions threatened Beastfolk communities and the living systems entrusted to them.
    
9. Those interventions contributed directly to the breakdown of trust between the two divine courts.
    

The Beastfolk were created to preserve connection.

They remembered rivers after kingdoms renamed them, burial grounds after borders crossed them, and promises after the people who made them were gone.

The Old Gods considered this memory a safeguard against mortal ambition.

The Dawn Gods eventually saw it as resistance to the future they intended to build.

> _“The Brethren asked what could be built upon the land. The Beastfolk asked what the land would lose when they were finished.”_
