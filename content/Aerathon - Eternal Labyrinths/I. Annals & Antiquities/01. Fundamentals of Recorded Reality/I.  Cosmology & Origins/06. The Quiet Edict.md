> _“The gods did not leave because they stopped loving the world. They left because their love had become indistinguishable from possession.”_  
> — **The Merath Testament**, disputed final account of the divine truce

# **The Quiet Edict and the World Left Behind**

### _The Truce of the Two Heavens and the End of Direct Divine Rule_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Cosmological Reconstruction Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice  
**Contributing Scholars:** Priest-Analyst Thera Daan, Researcher Falyn Onarra, Magistrate Talar Shellmark, Professor Arav Dhal-Senn  
**Current Revision:** 3388 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Document Class:** _Dominant Historical Reconstruction / Grade-II Authority_  
**Primary Evidence:** Merath Testament fragments, the Sealed Liturgies, abandoned divine hosts, celestial boundary records, postwar relic studies, early Near- and Far-Heaven traditions

---

## **I. Purpose of This Record**

The Quiet Edict ended the War of the Two Heavens.

It did not reconcile the pantheons. It did not repair the world, restore the dead, or return the divine powers lost during the fighting.

It established limits.

The surviving gods agreed that neither court could remain fully present in Aerathon without eventually renewing the war. Both sides therefore surrendered part of their former authority and withdrew from direct rule.

The Dawn Court retained its temples, names, and organized influence, but gave up the right to freely inhabit mortal hosts.

The Old Court surrendered its earthly seats and withdrew into distant celestial and wild realms, where its powers could continue without directly contesting the cities and institutions of the Dawn Gods.

The result was an unequal peace.

The Dawn Pantheon remained visible in mortal civilization.

The Old Gods became distant, scattered, and increasingly forgotten.

Neither side emerged unchanged.

---

## **II. The Gathering at Merath**

The negotiations began after the **Silence at Merath**, when divine armies discovered that their gods could no longer manifest within or near the city.

The surviving accounts describe Merath as crowded with refugees, wounded soldiers, abandoned divine vessels, and souls unable to complete their passage into death.

Mortal representatives from both peoples demanded an end to the war.

The gods initially attempted to negotiate through their chosen hosts. When those hosts began dying under the strain, the surviving divine courts withdrew from the city and spoke through signs, dreams, sacred fires, animal messengers, and written changes appearing upon the walls of the negotiation hall.

The final agreement took forty-one days.

No complete transcript survives.

The meeting included:

- representatives of the Dawn Court;
    
- representatives of the Old Court;
    
- Brethren rulers and temple authorities;
    
- Beastfolk wardens and ancestral keepers;
    
- guardians of the dead;
    
- and several divine powers whose names were later removed from both pantheons.
    

Mortal testimony was not merely ceremonial.

By the war’s end, the gods lacked the strength to enforce a peace without mortal cooperation. Temples controlled divine vessels and relics. Beastfolk communities guarded many of the sacred routes through which the Old Gods still entered Aerathon.

The Edict therefore bound gods, institutions, and mortal intermediaries together.

---

## **III. Why the Gods Accepted the Edict**

Both courts had reached the limits of what they could lose.

The Dawn Pantheon still controlled many cities and organized churches, but its gods had become dangerously dependent upon hosts, relics, and institutions damaged by the war.

The Old Court retained power over ancestral, natural, and spiritual systems, but had lost many temples, sacred territories, and unified priesthoods.

Continuing the war risked:

- the permanent closure of the paths used by the dead;
    
- the destruction of mortal civilization;
    
- the collapse of several divine realms;
    
- the fragmentation of more gods;
    
- and the possible rejection of all divine presence by Aerathon itself.
    

The Silence at Merath suggested that the world had already begun resisting them.

The gods could no longer assume Aerathon would remain a passive battlefield.

The Edict became both a truce and an act of survival.

---

## **IV. The Core Terms**

The exact language of the Quiet Edict is lost, but its major restrictions are consistently reflected in later divine behavior.

### **No Full Divine Descent**

No god may enter Aerathon in the complete form or power once used during the War.

Limited signs, aspects, dreams, blessings, and temporary manifestations remain possible.

### **No Divine War upon Aerathon**

The pantheons may oppose one another through belief, influence, and mortal institutions, but may not openly deploy their full celestial forces into the mortal world.

### **No Unrestricted Possession**

Gods may not freely seize or inhabit mortal bodies.

Religious traditions distinguish consensual revelation from divine occupation, but even willing hosts are now limited in how much power they can carry.

### **No Exclusive Claim over Mortal Peoples**

Neither pantheon may claim absolute ownership of Brethren, Beastfolk, mixed lineages, or the souls of those who reject their ancestral faith.

### **No Seizure of the Dead**

The passage of souls may not be used as a weapon in divine conflict.

Judgment traditions may differ, but neither court may block the dead merely to deny them to the other.

### **No Reclamation of Embedded Divinity by Force**

Power left within Aerathon may not be removed when doing so would destroy the land, people, species, or institutions now dependent upon it.

This final term prevented the gods from recovering much of what they had sacrificed during the war.

It also ensured that Aerathon would remain filled with abandoned divine power.

---

## **V. What the Dawn Court Surrendered**

The Dawn Pantheon retained the stronger position within mortal civilization.

Its temples survived. Its names remained widespread. Brethren kingdoms continued using Dawn law, calendars, titles, and religious offices.

In exchange, the Dawn Gods surrendered their **direct hold upon mortal hosts**.

Before the Edict, a god could dwell within a priest, king, oracle, paladin, or constructed body for extended periods. Some hosts functioned as living divine offices, speaking with the god’s voice and wielding power far beyond mortal spellcraft.

After the Edict, this relationship was broken.

Clerics and paladins could still receive divine power, but the god could no longer freely occupy the individual granting it.

The surviving channels functioned through:

- sacred oaths;
    
- established priestly offices;
    
- consecrated relics;
    
- temple rites;
    
- inherited covenants;
    
- and divine laws created before the Edict.
    

A cleric might pray to a god and receive magic without ever hearing that god answer directly.

A paladin’s oath might carry power because the divine covenant behind it remained valid, not because a god personally approved each decision.

This distinction transformed Dawn religion.

The churches retained their authority.

The gods became increasingly silent within them.

---

## **VI. The Silence of the Dawn Gods**

The Edict did not forbid every form of divine communication.

Dawn Gods may still act through:

- dreams;
    
- omens;
    
- sacred visions;
    
- brief miracles;
    
- relic responses;
    
- limited aspects;
    
- and rare moments of unmistakable revelation.
    

What they lost was reliable intimacy.

A high priest could no longer expect regular conversation with a patron. A king could not summon a god to settle a dispute. A temple could claim divine approval, but often lacked any way to prove that approval had been given.

Religious institutions adapted by placing greater authority in scripture, doctrine, inherited office, and interpretation.

This preserved order.

It also created opportunity for abuse.

A church could continue speaking in a god’s name long after the god had stopped correcting it.

Some of the most powerful modern Dawn institutions may therefore reflect centuries of mortal policy layered over divine teachings that were never meant to govern every age.

The gods remained in power through their names and systems.

They no longer controlled everything done in those names.

---

## **VII. What the Old Court Surrendered**

The Old Gods made a different sacrifice.

They surrendered their **direct territorial claim over Aerathon**.

During the First Making and the War, the Old Court maintained earthly seats in rivers, deserts, forests, burial complexes, sacred mountains, and living creatures. These were not merely temples. They were places where divine authority existed directly within the mortal world.

Under the Edict, the Old Gods withdrew from most of these seats.

Many passed into the **Far Heaven**, the distant celestial dominion associated with ancestry, death, sacred memory, and the older Beastfolk order.

Others established or expanded their influence through wild adjoining realms, especially regions later identified with the **Feywilds**.

The Feywilds offered the Old Court something Aerathon no longer could:

- living landscapes responsive to divine presence;
    
- fluid borders;
    
- strong ties to animals and spirits;
    
- ancient paths outside Brethren law;
    
- and places where transformation did not require permanent conquest.
    

The Old Gods retained influence through:

- ancestry;
    
- dreams;
    
- instinct;
    
- sacred animals;
    
- natural cycles;
    
- fey crossings;
    
- and inherited Beastfolk rites.
    

They lost the temples, royal courts, and public authority necessary to remain a unified mortal religion.

---

## **VIII. The Fading of the Old Gods**

Withdrawal did not immediately erase the Old Court from Aerathon.

Its priests continued serving temples. Beastfolk rulers still invoked divine law. Sacred animals and ancestral rites remained widespread.

The decline occurred over generations.

Without regular divine appearances:

- priesthoods disagreed over doctrine;
    
- distant temples developed incompatible rites;
    
- Brethren kingdoms absorbed or replaced Old Court sanctuaries;
    
- sacred landscapes were settled, renamed, or destroyed;
    
- and younger Beastfolk communities adopted Dawn worship alongside or instead of ancestral religion.
    

Some Old Gods maintained stronger connections through the Feywilds. Their worshippers received signs through dreams, animal behavior, seasonal anomalies, and fey visitors.

These forms of contact were harder to organize than temple revelation.

They were also easier for later scholars to dismiss as folklore.

The Dawn Pantheon remained visible because its institutions survived the silence.

The Old Court became hidden because its surviving power no longer required those institutions.

---

## **IX. The Division of the Heavens**

Before the War, the divine courts appear to have occupied a more closely connected celestial order.

The Edict formalized their separation.

The Dawn Pantheon withdrew into what became known as the **Near Heaven**.

The Old Court passed into the **Far Heaven** and several connected wild or ancestral realms.

The words “near” and “far” do not describe ordinary distance.

The Near Heaven is closer to mortal institutions. It is reached through temples, oaths, scripture, relics, and organized worship.

The Far Heaven is closer to ancestry, natural cycles, sacred beasts, dreams, death, and inherited memory.

A city priest may find the Near Heaven easier to reach.

A Beastfolk elder walking an ancestral route may find the Far Heaven closer than the temple at the center of the same city.

The two heavens remain part of one celestial creation.

The Edict prevents their courts from meeting in Aerathon as they once did.

---

## **X. The Divinity Left Behind**

The gods could not take all their power with them.

During the War, divine strength had been placed into weapons, hosts, temples, bloodlines, landscapes, and mortal systems.

Much of it had become part of Aerathon.

Removing it would have caused further destruction.

This abandoned power survives in several forms.

|Divine Remnant|Common Result|
|---|---|
|**Relics**|Weapons, crowns, tools, tablets, sacred vessels|
|**Bloodlines**|Inherited magic, physical changes, unusual affinities|
|**Sacred Sites**|Temples, groves, tombs, battlefields, divine seats|
|**Orphaned Aspects**|Portions of gods separated from their greater identity|
|**Living Species**|Animals or plants permanently altered by divine presence|
|**Divine Laws**|Oaths, protections, judgments, and magical customs that still function|
|**Primordial Scars**|Regions damaged or transformed by direct divine conflict|

Modern magic developed in a world already filled with these remains.

Many practices later considered arcane may descend from divine systems whose creators are no longer present to claim them.

This also explains why relics are not evenly distributed.

They are most common where gods fought, manifested, died, or abandoned long-held seats of power.

---

## **XI. The First Mortal Age**

The Quiet Edict ended the age in which the gods personally governed Aerathon.

Mortals inherited the aftermath.

Brethren kingdoms retained roads, archives, temples, and offices, allowing them to rebuild centralized power more quickly.

Beastfolk communities retained kinship, ancestral practice, and environmental knowledge, allowing them to survive in regions where formal institutions had collapsed.

Neither people inherited a complete world.

The Brethren possessed systems whose divine founders no longer spoke clearly.

The Beastfolk possessed sacred duties whose gods had withdrawn beyond ordinary reach.

Both were left to interpret damaged traditions while living among divine ruins.

Later conflicts between Brethren and Beastfolk were therefore shaped by a war neither living people had begun.

The gods made the division.

Mortals inherited its borders.

---

## **XII. Enforcement of the Edict**

No mortal institution enforces the Quiet Edict.

The restriction appears to operate through the structure created by the gods’ surrender.

Attempts at forbidden manifestation have produced:

- the collapse of divine hosts;
    
- destruction of the attempted avatar;
    
- loss of divine power;
    
- violent rejection by the surrounding landscape;
    
- breaches between Aerathon and adjoining realms;
    
- and the creation of unstable divine remnants.
    

Some theologians believe the gods police one another.

Others argue that the Edict was sealed into Aerathon itself, meaning the world now rejects the kind of direct divine presence that nearly destroyed it.

The Silence at Merath supports the latter interpretation.

A third tradition claims the Edict is watched by an unnamed power that stood outside both pantheons and forced them to accept terms they would not have honored willingly.

The Ministry has found no conclusive proof of such an enforcer.

What is certain is that the gods obey the Edict even when doing so costs their followers dearly.

That suggests either fear, necessity, or both.

---

## **XIII. Modern Consequences**

The Quiet Edict remains one of the most important forces shaping modern Aerathon.

It explains why:

- churches possess power without reliable divine instruction;
    
- clerics can perform miracles while disagreeing about their gods’ wishes;
    
- Old Beastfolk rites survive without public temples;
    
- divine relics are extraordinarily valuable;
    
- possession by a true god is rare and dangerous;
    
- gods rely upon mortal institutions and chosen agents;
    
- fey realms retain strong connections to Old Court traditions;
    
- and no pantheon has openly returned to claim Aerathon.
    

It also creates a permanent uncertainty within religion.

When a priest speaks, is the god speaking through them—or is the priest interpreting a silence?

When a Beastfolk dream carries an ancestral warning, is it an Old God, a fey power, a dead relative, or the dreamer’s own fear?

The Edict ended direct divine rule.

It did not end divine influence.

It made that influence harder to recognize and easier to misuse.

---

## **XIV. Ministry Position**

The Ministry recognizes the following conclusions as the strongest current reconstruction:

1. The Quiet Edict was established as a truce after the War of the Two Heavens.
    
2. Both pantheons surrendered powers they had once exercised freely.
    
3. The Dawn Court retained organized mortal authority but lost unrestricted access to divine hosts.
    
4. The Old Court retained ancestral, natural, and fey connections but surrendered most direct earthly seats.
    
5. The division between the Near and Far Heavens became formal under the Edict.
    
6. Clerical and paladin magic may function through surviving covenants without regular divine communication.
    
7. Much of the Old Court’s continued power operates through the Far Heaven, the Feywilds, and inherited Beastfolk traditions.
    
8. Divine power left behind during the War became relics, bloodlines, sacred sites, magical systems, and Primordial Scars.
    
9. The Edict appears capable of resisting or punishing forbidden divine manifestation.
    
10. No modern church, state, or clan possesses verified authority to alter its terms.
    
11. The gods remain active, but none may freely return to the authority they possessed before the War.
    
12. Aerathon’s modern civilizations were built upon power the gods surrendered but never fully abandoned.
    

The Quiet Edict saved Aerathon from its creators.

It also ensured that every later generation would live among their unfinished work.

> _“The gods withdrew into heaven. Their power did not. It remained in our blood, beneath our roads, inside our temples, and behind every sealed door we were warned never to open.”_
