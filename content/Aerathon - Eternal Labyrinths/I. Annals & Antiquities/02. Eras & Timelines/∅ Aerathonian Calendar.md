## **I. Mandate and Authority**

The **Aerathonian Calendar** is the official civil, historical, and operational reckoning recognized throughout Ministry-chartered Aerathon.

It governs the dating of legal contracts, public records, guild licenses, relic transfers, expedition reports, transit schedules, historical findings, and Grand Scrying Network broadcasts. Where a date bears legal or evidentiary consequence, the Ministry reckoning takes precedence over local custom.

The calendar does not claim to be the oldest or most sacred means by which the peoples of Aerathon have understood time. It exists because older systems could no longer reliably agree with one another—or, during the darkest centuries, with themselves.

Regional calendars remain lawful for cultural, religious, and domestic use. Ministry conversion is required only when a record must cross borders, enter an archive, establish ownership, or serve as proof.

The purpose of the standard is not to tell every people how time must be remembered.

It is to ensure that, when lives depend upon the answer, all parties can agree upon when something occurred.

---

## **II. Origins of the Standard Reckoning**

Before the standard calendar, Aerathon possessed hundreds of systems of timekeeping.

The Crown kingdoms dated events by royal succession. The Empire of Law and Light measured years through liturgical reigns and sanctioned miracles. Ember guild-states counted forge generations, master tenures, or the lifespan of great experiments. Beastfolk nations preserved time through migrations, moon passages, ancestral songs, shell records, seasonal roads, and the return of sacred storms.

These systems endured while the lands that created them remained stable.

They failed during the **Age of Collapse**.

Chronometric disturbances caused neighboring settlements to disagree upon the month, the season, and occasionally the year. Islands vanished for decades and returned after what their inhabitants believed had been a single night. Travelers arrived before the messages announcing their departure. Families maintained burial records for relatives who later returned alive, older or younger than when they had left.

The earliest Free Guilds adopted a provisional shared calendar to prevent expedition records from becoming useless. That reckoning was later calibrated by the **Louvain Chronometry Commission**, which compared celestial movement, surviving pre-Collapse records, and the harmonic decay of Orichalcum fragments preserved beneath **Sunwound Crater**.

The resulting system became the foundation of modern Ministry timekeeping.

Its simplicity was intentional.

A world capable of losing centuries required a calendar that could be rebuilt from first principles.

## **III. The Dawning Reckoning**

Official years are recorded in **A.D. — After Dawning**.

The **Dawning** is defined as the first measurable stabilization of the Three Weaves at Sunwound Crater following the disappearance of the Orichalcum civilizations. It marks the boundary between mythic prehistory and the earliest chronology the Ministry believes can be reconstructed with reasonable continuity.

The Dawning Year is designated **0 A.D.**

Events believed to predate it may be recorded as **B.D. — Before Dawning**, though exact B.D. dates are rarely granted high authenticity. Records too ancient, contradictory, or metaphysically unstable for numbered dating are instead classified under:

- **Deep Time**
    
- **Mythic Prehistory**
    
- **Pre-Dawning Antiquity**
    
- or an approved archaeological sequence
    

The Dawning does not mark the creation of Aerathon, the first mortal life, the birth of the gods, or the first use of magic.

It marks only the point at which history ceases to be entirely inseparable from myth.

Even that distinction remains disputed.

## **IV. Structure of the Wreath**

The Aerathonian year is called a **Wreath**.

A single Wreath contains:

|Measure|Quantity|
|---|--:|
|Seasonal Quarters|4|
|Cycles|12|
|Knots|60|
|Spans|360|
|Turns|1,440|
|Marks|8,640|

Each Wreath contains exactly **360 Spans**.

There are no leap years or intercalary days in the standard calendar. Astronomical irregularities, planar interference, and chronometric displacement are recorded as local variance rather than altering the official structure.

Each Cycle contains thirty Spans. Each Knot contains six Spans. Therefore:

- every Cycle begins on **Dawnsday**;
    
- every Cycle ends on **Veilsday**;
    
- each Cycle contains exactly five Knots;
    
- and every new Wreath begins on Dawnsday.
    

This repeating structure was selected for ease of reconstruction, but it has since been observed within several ancient Labyrinth strata. Chambers, mechanisms, and recurring encounters have appeared in groupings of six, thirty, and three hundred and sixty.

The Ministry recognizes this as a verified pattern.

It does not recognize any single explanation for it.

## **V. Seasons and the Twelve Cycles**

The Wreath is divided into four Seasonal Quarters of ninety Spans each.

These quarters describe the traditional climate and aetheric rhythm of the **Louvain Meridian**. They do not guarantee identical weather across every continent, altitude, plane, or Labyrinth zone.

### **Winter Quarter**

|Cycle|Position|Common Significance|
|---|---|---|
|**Glintwane**|Early Winter|Opening of the civil year, appointments, registrations, oath renewals|
|**Frostreach**|Midwinter|Endurance rites, supply audits, defensive preparation, memorial vigils|
|**Embermarch**|Late Winter|Forge observances, thaw feasts, reopening of seasonal routes|

### **Spring Quarter**

|Cycle|Position|Common Significance|
|---|---|---|
|**Bloomtide**|Early Spring|Planting, restoration, fertility rites, civic cleansing|
|**Goldgrove**|Midspring|Youth ceremonies, games, artistic festivals, local gatherings|
|**Brightmoor**|Late Spring|Surveying, invention fairs, cartographic revision, expedition preparation|

### **Summer Quarter**

|Cycle|Position|Common Significance|
|---|---|---|
|**Highsun**|Early Summer|Peak aetherflux, major delves, guild competition, international assemblies|
|**Ashvale**|Midsummer|Combat trials, forge festivals, heat rites, hazard training|
|**Redfall**|Late Summer|Trade fairs, harvest markets, diplomatic bonds, itinerant revels|

### **Autumn Quarter**

|Cycle|Position|Common Significance|
|---|---|---|
|**Duskrend**|Early Autumn|Veil ceremonies, funerary preparation, shadow observances|
|**Hearthwane**|Midautumn|Family reunion, communal feasting, household and charter renewal|
|**Snowturn**|Late Autumn|Annual reckoning, archive closure, burial observances, preparation for the next Wreath|

Cycle names are proper titles and should not be abbreviated in formal prose.

In ledger notation, Glintwane is designated **01** and Snowturn **12**.

## **VI. The Six-Day Knot**

A six-Span week is called a **Knot**.

The term originated among early Collapse-era caravans and Free Guild expeditions. A knot was tied into a record cord after every six completed days of travel, allowing time to be counted even when ink, paper, or reliable memory were unavailable.

Five Knots form one Cycle.

|Day|Traditional Associations|
|---|---|
|**Dawnsday**|Beginnings, departures, contracts, initiations, public openings|
|**Moonsday**|Study, magic, healing, contemplation, limited civic rest|
|**Wardsday**|Protection, governance, inspections, guild and Ministry audits|
|**Firesday**|Training, declarations, forge work, military exercises|
|**Gildsday**|Commerce, wages, auctions, sponsorships, public broadcasts|
|**Veilsday**|Worship, remembrance, funerary rites, household gathering|

These associations are customary rather than universally binding.

Essential Ministry services continue throughout the Knot. Arcane Breach response, emergency healing, active expedition support, rail control, and containment offices observe rotating schedules rather than a shared day of rest.

In some Beastfolk communities, the Knot is treated not as six separate days, but as a single repeating ritual: departure, remembrance, defense, struggle, exchange, and return.

The Ministry recognizes both interpretations.

## **VII. Spans, Turns, and Standard Measurements**

A civil day is called a **Span**.

Each Span contains twenty-four **Marks** and is divided into four six-Mark **Turns**.

|Turn|Marks|General Period|
|---|--:|---|
|**First Turn**|1–6|Deep night into dawn|
|**Second Turn**|7–12|Morning into highsun|
|**Third Turn**|13–18|Afternoon into dusk|
|**Fourth Turn**|19–24|Evening into deep night|

Local names such as **Dawnturn**, **Highturn**, **Duskturn**, and **Veilturn** remain common. Official records use numbered Turns unless regional terminology is itself relevant to the evidence.

### **Standard Units**

|Unit|Official Definition|Common Use|
|---|---|---|
|**Tick**|One-sixtieth of a Mark|Spell timing, relay latency, controlled experiments|
|**Mark**|One-twenty-fourth of a Span|Appointments, transit schedules, watch records|
|**Turn**|Six Marks|Guard rotations, expedition watches, work shifts|
|**Span**|Twenty-four Marks|Ordinary civil day|
|**Knot**|Six Spans|Work schedules, travel estimates, short contracts|
|**Cycle**|Thirty Spans|Licensing, wages, supply rotation|
|**Quarter**|Ninety Spans|Taxation, deployment, seasonal planning|
|**Wreath**|Three hundred and sixty Spans|Civil and historical year|

Words such as **moment**, **breath**, **bell**, and **watch** remain acceptable in ordinary speech but have no fixed legal duration unless explicitly defined.

A **Turn** is a division of a Span.

A **Knot** is the six-day week.

This distinction is mandatory in Ministry records.

## **VIII. Official Dates and the Louvain Meridian**

The full written date follows this order:

> **Weekday, Span of Cycle, Year A.D.**

Example:

> **Dawnsday, 1 Glintwane, 3388 A.D.**

The Ministry ledger format follows:

> **Year.Cycle.Span**

Example:

> **3388.01.01**

A precise time may be appended:

> **3388.01.01 — Second Turn, Mark 08**

For interregional coordination, official time is measured from the **Louvain Meridian**, maintained through a sealed chronometric array beneath the Hall of Charters.

Local time remains acceptable for domestic affairs. A Louvain Meridian conversion is required for:

- interregional contracts;
    
- Ministry summons;
    
- rail and skyship schedules;
    
- military deployment;
    
- Grand Scrying Network broadcasts;
    
- Labyrinth entry windows;
    
- relic transfers;
    
- and emergency breach notices.
    

The word **Cycle** must not be used as an ordinal substitute for a day.

Older documents containing phrases such as “18th Cycle” must preserve their original wording for archival honesty, but should receive a corrected date in brackets where reconstruction is possible.

Example:

> **Original Record:** “18th Cycle, 3098 A.D.”  
> **Ministry Reconstruction:** “18 Embermarch, 3098 A.D.”  
> **Conversion Confidence:** Grade-II

When a precise date cannot be established, the uncertainty must remain visible.

An approximate truth is preferable to a confident falsehood.

## **IX. Labyrinth Time and Chronometric Control**

Time within a Labyrinth is never presumed to match exterior time.

Every licensed expedition must record three separate durations:

1. **Exterior Duration** — time measured by the surface support team.
    
2. **Interior Duration** — time experienced and recorded by the expedition.
    
3. **Biological Duration** — aging, exhaustion, healing, hunger, and physical deterioration observed among returning delvers.
    

The difference between these values is called the **Drift Differential**.

A standard entry may read:

> **Exterior Duration:** 3 Turns  
> **Interior Duration:** 11 Turns  
> **Biological Duration:** approximately 8 Turns  
> **Recorded Differential:** +8 Interior / +5 Biological

A discrepancy does not automatically indicate fraud, enchantment, or false memory. It does require review.

Licensed expeditions entering Drift-active zones must carry at least two independent chronometric instruments. Standard equipment may include:

- synchronized calendar stones;
    
- sealed mechanical timepieces;
    
- resonance pendulums;
    
- paired exterior and interior ledgers;
    
- and an authorized Oculi witness record.
    

**Chronoscribes** authenticate expedition timing and investigate inconsistent reports.

**Timebinders** study celestial, planar, and aetheric recurrence to identify periods of increased temporal instability.

**Divine Oculi** preserve witnessed timestamps and environmental records. They verify evidence but do not independently declare a date lawful.

**Meridian Anchors** are restricted instruments used to restore synchronized time following major chronometric events.

No single device is considered sufficient during a suspected temporal breach.

Grade-I authentication requires agreement between at least two independent measures—or a detailed explanation for why such agreement was impossible.

## **X. Official Observances and Restricted Dates**

The following observances are recognized throughout Ministry-chartered territories.

|Date|Observance|Recognized Practice|
|---|---|---|
|**Glintwane 1**|**Opening of the Wreath**|Public ledgers reopen; guild, civil, and Ministry appointments are renewed|
|**Embermarch 1**|**Day of First Descent**|Commemorates the earliest documented Labyrinth descent accepted under modern evidentiary standards|
|**Highsun 15–20**|**The Gilded Reckoning**|Guild exhibitions, ranked delves, auctions, sponsorship contests, and international assemblies|
|**Duskrend 30**|**Twilight Wake**|Silence in guild halls; lights and offerings are left for those lost beneath the world|
|**Snowturn 30**|**The Veiled Assembly**|The Wreath formally closes; omens, breach reports, and aetheric forecasts are reviewed|

The **Day of First Descent** does not commemorate the earliest known proto-Labyrinth or the first mortal believed to have entered one.

It commemorates the earliest descent for which the Ministry possesses a continuous and defensible record.

Restricted delving dates are not permanently fixed. The Chronometric Inquiry Office and Arcane Breach Division issue annual advisories based upon:

- eclipses;
    
- planar thinning;
    
- resonance tides;
    
- known Drift cycles;
    
- celestial convergence;
    
- and changes in Labyrinth behavior.
    

Such restrictions are operational judgments, not declarations that a date is cursed.

The distinction is often ignored by the public and carefully preserved by the Ministry.

## **XI. Regional Reckonings and Historical Conversion**

The Ministry recognizes that no calendar is culturally neutral.

Different peoples continue to measure time through lunar phases, ancestral reigns, forge generations, monsoon seasons, migrations, sacred roads, storm returns, shell growth, flowering cycles, and oral recitations.

Official archival practice should preserve these reckonings wherever possible.

A converted historical record must include:

1. the original date or temporal description;
    
2. the culture or system from which it came;
    
3. the Ministry conversion;
    
4. and the confidence assigned to that conversion.
    

Example:

> _Third Returning Moon of the Hollow Choir_  
> **Probable Ministry Conversion:** Hearthwane, 2471 A.D.  
> **Conversion Confidence:** Grade-III  
> **Disputed Element:** Exact Knot and Span

Conversion is not correction.

A community that remembers a year through famine, migration, coronation, or storm is not necessarily mistaken because it did not number that year as the Ministry does.

The archive’s duty is to make records comparable without stripping them of meaning.

When a regional date and a Ministry conversion contradict one another, both should remain in the record until stronger evidence justifies preference.

Contradiction is not always error.

Sometimes it is the only surviving evidence that time behaved differently there.

## **XII. Legal Force and Archival Caution**

The Aerathonian Calendar possesses legal force wherever Ministry charter is recognized.

A false or altered date may invalidate:

- a contract;
    
- an expedition claim;
    
- a relic custody record;
    
- a guild license;
    
- a transit order;
    
- a salvage right;
    
- an alibi;
    
- or a historical classification.
    

Deliberate alteration of a temporal record constitutes **Ministerial Time-Tampering**.

Where the alteration conceals an unauthorized delve, death, breach, relic transfer, or disappearance, further charges may be applied under Archive Integrity, Charter Fraud, or Labyrinth Safety law.

Despite this authority, the Ministry cautions against mistaking the calendar for cosmic certainty.

The Wreath is a mortal instrument imposed upon a world where sequence may fracture, repeat, accelerate, or forget itself. Its numerical patterns appear inside ancient Labyrinths. Its beginning rests upon a phenomenon only partially understood. Its stability depends upon instruments, testimony, and shared agreement.

It is possible that the calendar reflects celestial law.

It is possible that it preserves an Orichalcum design.

It is possible that generations of collective belief have impressed its structure upon the Labyrinths.

It is also possible that something beneath Aerathon counted these days long before mortals gave them names.

The Calendar measures the world.

It does not guarantee that the world agrees.
