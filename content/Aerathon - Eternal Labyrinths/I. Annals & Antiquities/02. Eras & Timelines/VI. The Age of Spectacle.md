> _“We used to descend so the world might remember what it had lost. Now we descend because the world refuses to look away.”_  
> — **Archivist Yelna Stonevoice**, _Opening Address to the Contemporary Record Bureau_

# **The Age of Spectacle**

### _Era VI — Memory as Currency, Truth as Performance_

**Filed Division:** Ministry of Delving Operations — Annals & Antiquities  
**Responsible Office:** Contemporary Record Bureau  
**Primary Compiler:** Archivist Yelna Stonevoice, Historian of Echo-Law and Modern-Era Continuities  
**Consulting Authorities:** Lt. Salvia Korrin, Director Halin Drauve, Researcher Falyn Onarra, Magistrate Talar Shellmark, Inspector Jao “Greyglass” Vell  
**Original Predictive Memorandum:** 3297 A.D., late Age of Delving  
**Formal Era Classification:** 3318 A.D., Age of Spectacle  
**Current Consolidated Revision:** 3388 A.D.  
**Document Class:** _Era Dossier / Grade-I Contemporary Chronology_  
**Official Duration:** **3300 A.D.–Present**  
**Current Year:** **3388 A.D.**  
**Preceded By:** The Age of Delving  
**Succeeded By:** No officially recognized era  
**Ministry Classification:** _Ontological-Media and Observational Epoch_

## **I. Overview**

The **Age of Spectacle** is the present age of Aerathon.

It is the era in which observation ceased to be a passive act.

During the Age of Delving, guilds entered Labyrinths to recover food, relics, knowledge, and material necessary for civilization. Expeditions were documented because lives depended upon accurate maps and honest reports.

During the Age of Spectacle, the document became part of the expedition.

Delvers now enter beneath the gaze of Divine Oculi. Their movements are relayed through scrying architecture, narrated across the Grand Scrying Network, judged through public metrics, and shaped by sponsors whose interests may reach deeper than the expedition itself.

A wound suffered beneath the earth can become public property before the blood dries.

A final word can be licensed, replayed, edited, sold, memorialized, or transformed into a guild slogan.

A relic may acquire an auction value before anyone knows what it does.

The defining power of this age is no longer merely the ability to descend.

It is the ability to determine what the descent means.

The Ministry refers to the period in formal theory as the **Era of Witness**.

The public calls it the Age of Spectacle.

The public name endured because witness implies responsibility.

Spectacle requires only an audience.

---

## **II. The Difficulty of Recording the Present**

The Age of Spectacle possesses more records than every preceding era combined.

This does not make it easier to understand.

Modern expeditions may be captured from dozens of angles, accompanied by emotional resonance, commentary, biometric measures, tactical projections, audience response, sponsor records, and later testimony.

Yet the abundance of evidence creates new uncertainties.

A broadcast can be technically authentic while presenting a misleading narrative.

A genuine emotional response can be amplified until it no longer reflects the person who first felt it.

A guild may release every moment of an expedition while concealing the negotiations that determined why the expedition occurred.

The Contemporary Record Bureau therefore distinguishes between:

1. **Event Authenticity**  
    Whether the recorded event actually occurred.
    
2. **Contextual Authenticity**  
    Whether the event is presented with sufficient surrounding information to understand it.
    
3. **Narrative Authenticity**  
    Whether commentary, editing, and framing accurately represent the people and motives involved.
    
4. **Resonant Authenticity**  
    Whether captured emotion originated naturally or was altered through magical influence.
    
5. **Archival Independence**  
    Whether the record remains accessible beyond the control of the guild, sponsor, or broadcaster that produced it.
    

The present age does not suffer from too little evidence.

It suffers from evidence owned by interested parties.

## **III. The Beginning of the Age**

The Age of Spectacle officially begins in **3300 A.D.**

This date does not mark the invention of scrying, the first recorded expedition, or the first observer-responsive Labyrinth.

Public relay halls already existed during the late Age of Delving. Sponsors had begun supplying equipment in exchange for recognition. Guilds appointed heralds and cultivated public reputations.

Observer-responsive architecture had been verified as early as **3197 A.D.**

The boundary was established because, by 3300 A.D., observation had become a dominant social force.

Guild success increasingly depended upon public attention.

Relic prices responded to broadcast popularity.

Political support followed audience sentiment.

Young delvers were recruited for appearance, manner, ancestry, tragedy, or charisma alongside competence.

Labyrinths displaying stage-like behavior became more common around heavily observed teams.

The old Age of Delving question had been:

> _What must be brought back?_

The new question was:

> _Who will be watching when it is found?_

When attention became inseparable from power, the historical logic of the world changed.

A new age had begun.

## **IV. The Louvain Concordat and the Archive-State**

The political foundation of the era was established before its official beginning.

In **3292 A.D.**, the **Louvain Concordat** consolidated the remaining Free Guild Accords beneath a republican structure shared among civic councils, major guild representatives, regional delegates, and the Ministry of Delving Operations.

The Concordat was intended to prevent any single guild, noble house, or Ministry division from controlling Labyrinth access.

Instead, it created a government dependent upon all three.

### **The Ministry as Government**

The MDO had originally governed entrances, relics, guild licenses, and breaches.

By the opening of the Spectacle Age, nearly every major public system depended upon those responsibilities.

Ministry records now affected:

- property ownership;
    
- guild legitimacy;
    
- relic custody;
    
- transit rights;
    
- historical classification;
    
- hazard status;
    
- public identity;
    
- and access to restricted territories.
    

A Ministry record did not merely describe a legal condition.

It often created one.

### **Updates to the Archive**

Many Louvain decrees are issued as **Archive Updates** rather than traditional laws.

Once authenticated and published, an update becomes binding upon Ministry offices and chartered organizations.

Supporters argue that this system keeps law visible, revisable, and connected to evidence.

Critics argue that it permits unelected archivists to change reality through classification.

A forest may become a restricted breach zone.

A relic may become protected cultural property.

A guild may become unlawful.

A person may become officially missing while still standing before the clerk.

The Archive-State governs through the assumption that what is properly recorded can be properly controlled.

The Labyrinths have repeatedly challenged that assumption.

## **V. The Architecture of Observation**

Modern spectacle depends upon a vast network of magical structures collectively known as **Scrying Architecture**.

It connects Divine Oculi, mirror arrays, scryline towers, guild channels, memory vaults, public theatres, and Ministry integrity nodes.

The network possesses three broad layers.

### **The Material Layer**

Mythril-threaded stone, codified obsidian, echo crystal, etched silver, and reinforced conduits provide physical anchoring for the system.

### **The Resonance Layer**

Magical harmonics translate raw perception into sight, sound, emotional pressure, temporal position, and measurable arcane data.

### **The Transmission Layer**

The Arcanet carries these records between Oculi, guild halls, Ministry offices, broadcast studios, and public viewing locations.

### **Divine Oculi**

Divine Oculi are mobile witnessing instruments assigned to official expeditions.

They observe, record, and authenticate.

They do not independently declare law, guilt, rank, or historical truth.

An Oculi record is evidence.

It is not judgment.

### **Reality Anchoring**

Scrying architecture does more than produce entertainment.

Inside unstable Labyrinths, consistent observation can help preserve route position, temporal continuity, and shared memory.

For this reason, destroying an Oculi may threaten not only a broadcast but the physical stability of the expedition itself.

This dependence lies at the heart of the era’s moral contradiction.

The gaze can protect.

The gaze can also become a cage.

---

## **VI. The Grand Scrying Network**

The **Grand Scrying Network**, commonly called the **GSN**, developed from late Delving-era relay halls and regional emergency channels.

Its earliest purpose was coordination.

It carried collapse warnings, rescue requests, entrance failures, guild dispatches, and short expedition reports.

Audiences gathered to watch.

Broadcasters noticed.

Within the first decade of the Spectacle Age, the network evolved into Aerathon’s dominant public medium.

### **Guildstreams**

Major guilds maintain dedicated channels featuring:

- live expeditions;
    
- training demonstrations;
    
- interviews;
    
- memorials;
    
- tactical analysis;
    
- relic auctions;
    
- and controlled access to guild life.
    

### **Public Viewing Halls**

Cities maintain Arcanet theatres where large audiences watch expeditions together.

These halls resemble a mixture of civic chamber, sporting arena, temple, and tavern.

Crowds cheer rescues, debate tactics, mourn deaths, place wagers, and vote upon sanctioned expedition decisions.

### **Narrators and Observers**

Licensed commentators translate magical, tactical, and historical events for the public.

The best become celebrities equal to the delvers they describe.

Poor commentary can destroy reputations.

Deliberately false commentary can trigger Ministry sanction.

### **Control of Attention**

The GSN determines which expeditions receive prominent coverage, which guilds gain narrative focus, and which tragedies become global events.

An expedition omitted from the network may still matter.

It may simply fail to matter publicly.

The GSN does not decide what is true.

It decides which truth the world sees first.

---

## **VII. The Arcane Broadcast Authority**

The first years of public livecasting produced widespread manipulation.

Guilds added glamours to injuries.

Sponsors altered emotional resonance.

Broadcasters concealed civilian deaths.

Commentators invented relic histories in real time.

Audience charms increased aggression, devotion, or spending.

Several low-ranked teams were encouraged into lethal situations because their fear produced exceptional viewer retention.

In response, the Ministry established the **Arcane Broadcast Authority**, or **ABA**.

The Authority regulates:

- Divine Oculi calibration;
    
- broadcast integrity;
    
- commentary licensing;
    
- sponsor disclosure;
    
- audience influence;
    
- emotional coercion;
    
- betting interference;
    
- memory alteration;
    
- and the use of recorded Labyrinth material.
    

### **The Four Principal Offices**

The **Scrytech Regulation Office** inspects equipment and transmission stability.

The **Spectacle Integrity Division** investigates fraud, manipulation, betting interference, and sponsor coercion.

The **Public Influence and Sentiment Control Bureau** monitors mass charms, audience tipping, and emotional feedback.

The **Narrative Authentication Office** verifies historical claims, commentary, relic descriptions, and public summaries.

### **Regulatory Power**

The ABA may suspend a broadcast, freeze a sponsorship, seize illegal scrytech, issue public censure, or refer a case to the Ministerial Tribunal.

Its critics accuse it of protecting the industry rather than the delvers.

Its defenders answer that without regulation, every expedition would become an advertisement written in blood.

The ABA’s official purpose is to protect the truth of the stone.

Its practical task is determining whether truth can survive production.

## **VIII. The Guild Powers of the Modern Age**

Guilds entered the Age of Spectacle as institutions.

They became powers comparable to governments, corporations, religious orders, and noble houses.

Each major guild represents a different answer to what delving should mean.

### **The Free Company of Louvain**

The Free Company remains the Ministry’s sole directly sponsored S-Class guild and its principal civic ally.

Its mission emphasizes:

- public training;
    
- rescue;
    
- rehabilitation;
    
- memorial keeping;
    
- and access to delving regardless of ancestry or social status.
    

The Company trains the majority of Aerathon’s licensed novices through the Hall of Orientation and associated academies.

Its hidden moral center lies within the **Stalwart tradition**, which shelters maimed, disgraced, traumatized, or guildless delvers.

The Free Company’s broadcasts rarely dominate spectacle rankings.

Its rescue feeds nevertheless receive extraordinary public support.

The Company represents the belief that delving remains a service.

Whether the Ministry shares that belief as deeply as it claims remains disputed.

### **The Argent Banner**

The Argent Banner embodies delving as heroic performance.

Its members are selected for ability, discipline, lineage, appearance, charisma, and public appeal. Expeditions are designed as narratives. Relics may be previewed for auction before recovery. Delvers become icons whose personal lives are folded into guild mythology.

The Banner’s creed holds that the story is as important as the relic.

Its supporters argue that inspiration sustains civilization.

Its critics argue that the Banner no longer recognizes a difference between courage and presentation.

### **The Crimson Reavers**

The Crimson Reavers grew from frontier warbands, banned companies, deserters, mercenaries, and delvers who rejected formal authority.

They treat violence, survival, and radical autonomy as proof of worth.

Their expeditions are unpredictable, frequently unlawful, and enormously popular.

The Ministry has censured them repeatedly while continuing to rely upon them for dangerous extraction and violent containment.

The Reavers represent the age’s most uncomfortable truth:

Public condemnation does not reduce an audience.

It often enlarges one.

### **The Iron-Bough Syndicate**

Founded in **3289 A.D.**, the Iron-Bough Syndicate began during the final years of the Age of Delving.

Its full power belongs to the Age of Spectacle.

The Syndicate transformed delving into industrial operation through:

- mechanical excavation;
    
- automated mapping;
    
- large-scale relic processing;
    
- salaried engineering corps;
    
- private transit;
    
- and semi-autonomous constructs.
    

Its machines support Ministry operations across Aerathon.

Its economic independence threatens the Ministry’s ability to regulate it.

Iron-Bough represents the transformation of the guild from fellowship into corporation.

### **The Verdant Collision**

Founded in **3321 A.D.**, the Verdant Collision emerged in response to surface corruption caused by aggressive Labyrinth exploitation.

Its druids, engineers, herbalists, and ecological delvers regard Labyrinths as living wounds rather than mines.

Success is measured not by the relics removed, but by what survives after the guild leaves.

The Collision often works beside the Free Company and against the habits of Iron-Bough, the Banner, and the Reavers.

It has less wealth than its rivals.

It possesses greater moral credibility than nearly all of them.

### **The Emberlight Union**

The Emberlight Union arose during the opening decades of the Age of Spectacle.

Its founders believed that a civilization built entirely around survival had forgotten beauty.

The Union combines lightcraft, illusion, artificing, stage engineering, and public art. Its work powers civic lighting, training simulations, theatres, festivals, broadcast presentation, and much of Louvain’s modern visual identity.

Its guiding belief is that hope is a craft.

Critics call its work distraction.

Citizens continue gathering beneath its lights.

### **The Dawnwright Circe**

The Dawnwright Circe preserves the First Light training traditions of the previous age.

It teaches knowledge, skill, emotional stability, ethical conduct, and team discipline before novices enter full guild service.

Nearly every major guild recruits from its graduates.

In an age that rewards immediate fame, the Circe maintains the unfashionable principle that a living novice is more valuable than a dead prodigy.

### **The Cinderpaw Pact**

The Cinderpaw Pact is not merely a guild.

It is a sovereign Beastfolk community built around memory, ancestral reclamation, and refusal of Brethren control.

The Pact rejects routine livecasting, sponsor ownership, and the treatment of Beastfolk relics as market goods.

Its delvers descend as mourners, guardians, and reclaimers.

The Ministry classifies the Pact as a **Protected Contradiction**: unlawful under ordinary Charter doctrine, culturally invaluable, militarily formidable, and politically unwise to challenge.

The Pact’s refusal of spectacle is itself a powerful public symbol.

## **IX. Rank, Merit, and Public Worth**

Guild classification originated in the Age of Delving as a measure of institutional capacity.

During the Age of Spectacle, rank became identity.

### **Guild Classes**

|Class|Modern Meaning|
|---|---|
|**S-Class**|National or international power with major Labyrinth rights and autonomous command|
|**A-Class**|Regional guild overseeing multiple operations or specialist infrastructure|
|**B-Class**|Established guild with full expedition rights|
|**C-Class**|Apprentice, training, or supervised order|
|**D-Class**|Temporary crews and provisionally licensed delvers|

### **Performance Metrics**

Modern guilds are evaluated through statistics including:

- relic yield;
    
- route discovery;
    
- casualty rate;
    
- rescue success;
    
- containment reliability;
    
- public trust;
    
- audience retention;
    
- and economic contribution.
    

The Ministry’s **Annual Performance Index** includes both operational and public measures.

This has made public favor an administrative resource.

### **UDMI and the Fame Engine**

The **UDMI** evaluates demonstrated delver competence, reliability, and field contribution.

The **Fame Engine** measures captivation.

A delver may rank highly in one and poorly in the other.

The distinction is formally maintained.

In practice, sponsorship, recruitment, media coverage, and political access often favor the more captivating score.

The age claims to reward merit.

It has never agreed upon what merit is.

---

## **X. Sponsorship and the Ownership of Heroes**

A modern sponsorship is a sanctioned pact among a delver or guild and a patron.

Sponsors may include:

- noble houses;
    
- merchant syndicates;
    
- governments;
    
- arcane colleges;
    
- relic manufacturers;
    
- cities;
    
- charitable foundations;
    
- or concealed criminal networks.
    

In exchange for funding, equipment, lodging, transportation, legal support, and broadcast access, sponsors receive association with the delver’s identity.

Their colors appear upon armor.

Their sigils are engraved into equipment.

Their names are spoken during broadcasts.

Their interests enter expedition decisions.

### **Narrative Clauses**

Modern contracts may govern:

- appearance;
    
- public conduct;
    
- approved rivalries;
    
- exclusive interviews;
    
- memory rights;
    
- relic display;
    
- death benefits;
    
- and ownership of final recorded statements.
    

Some contracts continue after death.

A fallen delver’s image may remain commercially active for generations.

### **The Sponsored Self**

Successful delvers increasingly maintain separate identities:

- the private person;
    
- the guild member;
    
- the licensed public character;
    
- and the recorded version preserved by the network.
    

These identities do not always agree.

The law has not fully determined which one owns a life.

---

## **XI. Audience Power**

The audience of the Age of Spectacle is not passive.

Sanctioned systems permit viewers to influence expeditions through magical and financial participation.

### **Tipcasting**

Viewers transmit minor boons supporting stamina, clarity, morale, or magical recovery.

Large audiences can produce significant effects.

### **Labyrinth Polls**

Some regulated formations allow audiences to vote upon routes, challenges, tactical restrictions, or puzzle conditions.

These votes are presented as participation.

Delvers sometimes describe them as remote command by strangers who bear none of the risk.

### **Blessing Tokens**

Rare audience-generated glyphs can reinforce wards, alter probability, or provide emergency protection.

Accounts of tokens reversing death remain disputed and heavily regulated.

### **Live Betting**

Public wagers affect guild value, sponsor behavior, and audience interest.

During highly unstable events, betting may create enormous emotional resonance around a desired outcome.

The ABA requires odds to freeze during major Core pings, Convergence flares, and N-Class anomalies.

### **The Armed Crowd**

The collective attention of millions possesses magical weight.

Excitement strengthens some formations.

Fear intensifies others.

Expectation may influence architecture before an expedition arrives.

The crowd does not merely watch the Labyrinth.

The Labyrinth may be watching the crowd.

---

## **XII. The Memory Market**

The Age of Spectacle trades not only in images but in experience.

Licensed **Memory Binders** extract emotional and sensory impressions from returning delvers.

These memories may be used for:

- training;
    
- therapy;
    
- historical preservation;
    
- entertainment;
    
- religious experience;
    
- tactical analysis;
    
- and commercial consumption.
    

### **Bound Runs**

Certified memories are reviewed for curse contamination, coercion, alteration, and dangerous emotional resonance.

They are released with contextual notes and lawful ownership records.

### **Raw Runs**

Illegal memory recordings circulate without filtration.

They may contain terror, pain, dying impressions, compulsions, Labyrinth echoes, or identity fragments.

Thrillseekers seek them precisely because they are unsafe.

### **Memory Ownership**

Current law has not resolved whether a memory belongs entirely to:

- the person who experienced it;
    
- the guild that funded the expedition;
    
- the Oculi that recorded it;
    
- the sponsor that purchased distribution rights;
    
- the Ministry archive;
    
- or the public whose support enabled the descent.
    

### **The Second Trauma**

Delvers often report that reliving an event through edited broadcasts feels different from remembering it privately.

Some begin recalling the televised version more clearly than their own experience.

This process is called **narrative overwrite**.

It may be psychological.

It may be magical.

In severe cases, the public version of an event becomes more stable than the memory of those who survived it.

## **XIII. Registry Culture and the Right to Forget**

Public Registry Nodes exist throughout major Ministry-chartered cities.

They authenticate declarations, contracts, licenses, civic proceedings, historical testimony, and public transactions.

Many also preserve ambient fragments surrounding legally significant events.

The system was created to prevent falsification.

It has produced a culture terrified of absence.

### **The Recorded Life**

Citizens increasingly maintain public memory threads containing:

- education;
    
- employment;
    
- guild affiliation;
    
- civic testimony;
    
- sponsorship;
    
- public speech;
    
- and authenticated achievements.
    

To lack a record can limit travel, employment, legal protection, and social trust.

### **Deletion Anxiety**

The phrase **“He who deletes is damned”** arose from the belief that removing a record damages not only history but the reality stabilized around it.

Unauthorized deletion is a serious offense.

This makes correction difficult when records contain abuse, humiliation, coercion, or falsehood.

### **Cognitive Autonomy**

Beastfolk unions and privacy advocates petition for a legally protected **right to unrecorded life**.

Their arguments hold that:

- forgetting is necessary for healing;
    
- silence may be culturally sacred;
    
- identity cannot belong permanently to an archive;
    
- and not every truth must become public property.
    

Ministry officials answer that unrecorded gaps permit fraud, disappearance, and historical erasure.

Both sides invoke the horrors of earlier ages.

One fears being forgotten.

The other fears never being allowed to forget.

---

## **XIV. Faith in the Watching Age**

The gods remain quiet.

The public has found new forms of worship.

### **The Eyes of the Many**

The **Eyes of the Many** teach that collective observation grants a form of immortality.

To be witnessed is to remain present within the world.

Their temples resemble amphitheatres lined with mirrors, memory crystals, and recited names.

The faithful chant the deeds of fallen delvers until individual voices become indistinguishable.

### **Celebrity as Demigodhood**

Popular delvers receive offerings, imitation relics, devotional art, and public shrines.

Children memorize their sayings.

Fans reenact their wounds.

Guild insignia appear upon household altars.

Few delvers claim literal divinity.

Some no longer need to.

### **Traditional Faith**

Old gods, Beastfolk pantheons, ancestral practices, Quiet Pantheon sects, and restored regional faiths continue.

Many reject the idea that an audience resembles a god.

Others incorporate broadcasts into worship, treating witnessed descent as communal prayer.

### **The Archive Heresy**

A growing philosophical movement argues that the Archive has become the new divine power of Aerathon.

It sees all, names all, judges all, and preserves the dead.

Ministry doctrine rejects this belief.

Ministry practice occasionally appears less certain.

## **XV. The Labyrinths Reflect Back**

The most dangerous development of the era is the apparent response of Labyrinths to observation.

The first verified observer-responsive architecture appeared in 3197 A.D.

Since 3300 A.D., the phenomenon has intensified.

### **Stage Logic**

Modern formations increasingly produce:

- dramatic entrances;
    
- escalating encounters;
    
- audience-facing chambers;
    
- repeated villains;
    
- symbolic rewards;
    
- reconstructed guild rivalries;
    
- and events resembling narrative conclusions.
    

Labyrinth creatures sometimes imitate famous delvers.

Relics reproduce sponsor sigils or Ministry seals.

Walls display earlier broadcasts.

Rooms appear to wait for a particular decision.

### **Spectral Feedback**

Lt. Salvia Korrin and other Breach analysts use the term **Spectral Feedback** for the cycle through which observation alters the Labyrinth, the altered Labyrinth changes the broadcast, and the broadcast shapes future expectation.

No clear beginning remains within that cycle.

### **Mirror-Class Events**

Mirror-Class anomalies document themselves.

They may display live footage of nearby expeditions, create duplicate Oculi perspectives, or cease existing when no longer recorded.

Some require observation to remain stable.

Others become more dangerous when watched.

The Ministry formally adopted the Mirror-Class designation around **3362 A.D.**

### **The Reflexive Labyrinth**

The leading modern theory proposes that centuries of maps, stories, fear, law, and recorded descent have become part of the material from which Labyrinths shape themselves.

The world entered the Labyrinth to study it.

The Labyrinth has begun studying the world in return.

---

## **XVI. Refusal, Resistance, and the Unwatched**

Not every delver accepts the terms of the age.

### **The Ghostveil Accord**

The Ghostveil movement advocates:

- delayed broadcasts;
    
- consent-based memory release;
    
- protected off-grid expeditions;
    
- and strict separation between field necessity and public entertainment.
    

Its supporters include healers, retired delvers, Beastfolk unions, and some Free Company officers.

### **The Mirrorless**

The Mirrorless destroy or disable Oculi during expeditions.

They believe observation feeds the Labyrinth and that every recorded descent strengthens its ability to imitate mortal thought.

The Ministry classifies them as extremists.

Several Mirrorless predictions have later proven accurate.

### **Stalwart Privacy**

The Free Company’s Stalwart circles often conceal the names and locations of recovering delvers.

This practice conflicts with registry policy but is defended as necessary for rehabilitation.

### **Cinderpaw Refusal**

The Cinderpaw Pact permits little external observation of its descents.

Its members argue that ancestral grief is not entertainment and that recovered Beastfolk memory belongs first to the people from whom it was taken.

### **The Quiet Rooms**

Across Louvain and other cities, informal sanctuaries offer spaces without active recording.

Some are maintained by temples.

Some by healers.

Some by criminals.

Entering one requires trust that silence will not be used as evidence of guilt.

In the Age of Spectacle, privacy is no longer the absence of technology.

It is an actively defended condition.

## **XVII. The Present Year: 3388 A.D.**

The Age of Spectacle has lasted only eighty-eight years.

Many of its defining institutions are younger than living citizens.

Its laws remain unsettled.

Its technologies continue changing faster than the ethical structures intended to govern them.

### **Current Political Tensions**

The Ministry faces growing conflict among:

- Concordant guilds favoring public service;
    
- private S-Class powers controlling relic markets;
    
- industrial guilds seeking weaker restrictions;
    
- regional governments resisting Ministry authority;
    
- Beastfolk unions demanding cognitive and cultural autonomy;
    
- and broadcast consortia whose wealth rivals states.
    

### **Ecological Reckoning**

The Verdant Collision and allied researchers warn that repeated extraction is damaging surface ecosystems and causing Labyrinth traits to spread beyond recognized entrances.

Iron-Bough and other industrial powers argue that modern civilization cannot survive without expanded recovery.

### **Narrative Instability**

Several authenticated expeditions now possess conflicting official versions.

In some cases, footage depicts events no participant remembers.

In others, all participants remember events absent from the recording.

The Narrative Authentication Office has not determined whether these are fraud, Drift recurrence, or Labyrinthal revision.

### **N-Class Teams**

Core-selected **N-Class teams**, including **Unit N-7**, receive heightened protection and scrutiny.

Their expeditions may trigger:

- Convergence pings;
    
- abnormal Oculi clarity;
    
- predictive hazard displays;
    
- narrative stabilization;
    
- or sudden changes in public probability systems.
    

All N-Class records receive triple verification and restricted archival status.

The Ministry publicly describes these measures as precaution.

Internally, several divisions regard N-Class teams as possible indicators that the present age is approaching another threshold.

### **The Unnamed Future**

No Seventh Era has been officially recognized.

Proposed names include:

- the Age of Reversal;
    
- the Age of Witness;
    
- the Living Archive;
    
- the Second Dawning;
    
- and the Age of Unmaking.
    

The Ministry recognizes none.

History does not change eras merely because historians become nervous.

It changes when the old explanation can no longer contain the world.

### **Historical Assessment**

The Age of Spectacle has achieved forms of connection no earlier civilization possessed.

A rescue in Vinyot can be witnessed in Louvain.

A warning from Arneria can cross the world before the breach it describes fully opens.

A forgotten person can be named before millions.

A corrupt guild can be exposed by its own record.

A child who will never descend can learn from the final mistake of someone who did.

These achievements are real.

So are their costs.

### **Does Observation Protect Reality?**

In many Labyrinths, recorded observation provides stability.

In others, attention appears to feed complexity, hostility, or self-awareness.

Both may be true.

### **Who Owns a Delver’s Life?**

Contracts, guild law, sponsorship, public interest, and memory rights provide different answers.

None fully account for the person beneath the recorded identity.

### **Can the Ministry Regulate an Industry It Depends Upon?**

Guilds provide infrastructure, rescue, revenue, and public confidence.

The Ministry governs them while relying upon their cooperation.

The balance is increasingly unstable.

### **Are the Labyrinths Learning Narrative?**

Stage logic, recurring figures, observer response, and self-documentation suggest more than random adaptation.

Whether the Labyrinths understand stories or merely reflect them remains unresolved.

### **Can a Record Become More Real Than an Event?**

Narrative overwrite and conflicting authenticated footage suggest that records may now influence memory, public belief, and perhaps physical history.

This possibility remains under restricted investigation.

### **What Comes After Spectacle?**

The present age cannot see beyond its own reflection.

It has made memory plentiful and privacy scarce.

It has turned courage into a metric, suffering into a commodity, and witnesses into participants.

It has also connected peoples who once would have died unknown and made it more difficult for power to bury its crimes unseen.

The gaze is neither wholly sacred nor wholly corrupt.

It is power.

Like every power Aerathon has inherited, it will be judged by who controls it, who profits from it, and who is permitted to close their eyes.
