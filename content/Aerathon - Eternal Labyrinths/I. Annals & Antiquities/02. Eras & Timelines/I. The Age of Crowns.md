### _Era I — The Reign of Blood, Bell, and Sacred Law_

## **I. Overview**

The **Age of Crowns** was the first recognized historical era after the Dawning and the first age in which mortal civilizations attempted to inherit the authority of vanished wonders.

Those who emerged after the disappearance of the Orichalcum civilizations found a world still crowded with impossible remains: towers that rang before storms, metals that remembered commands, wells that answered only to certain voices, and machines whose functions had outlived the people who built them.

The early kingdoms did not consider these things ruins.

They considered them inheritance.

Across the known world, rulers claimed that their blood carried some surviving fragment of the Dawning. Noble families employed priests, genealogists, and sacred smiths to measure traces of divine resonance within their descendants. Those who glowed before relics were declared favored. Those who survived contact with Orichalcum were crowned.

From these practices arose the first **High Thrones**.

Magic became divine currency.

Faith became administration.

History became the property of those permitted to record it.

Brethren historians later called this period the **First Binding**, referring both to the binding of magic into formal religious institutions and the binding of mortal life beneath sacred law.

Many Beastfolk traditions remember it by another name:

> **The Age of the Closed Eye.**

---

## **II. Historical Sources and Evidentiary Limits**

The Age of Crowns is better documented than the Orichalcum period, though far less reliably than its surviving royal chronicles suggest.

Most written records were produced by courts, temple registries, sanctioned guilds, or noble genealogists. Their authors rarely distinguished political convenience from divine truth.

Military defeats became sacred trials.

Famine became punishment for disobedience.

Failed miracles were blamed upon insufficient faith.

Entire communities vanished from official history when their existence challenged royal theology.

The Ministry’s reconstruction relies upon five principal bodies of evidence:

1. **Royal Annals**  
    Genealogies, coronation records, military declarations, treaties, and court histories.
    
2. **Petition Ledgers**  
    Records of prayers, offerings, requested miracles, temple taxation, and divine response.
    
3. **Orichalcum Resonance Studies**  
    Modern examination of Crown-era regalia, machinery, sacred weapons, and civic relics.
    
4. **Unsanctioned Accounts**  
    Smuggler ledgers, outlawed scripture, workshop marks, private correspondence, and rebel songs.
    
5. **Beastfolk Oral Memory**  
    Stone-song, road chant, shell record, burial recitation, ancestral testimony, and sacred performance.
    

Crown documents frequently exaggerate the reach and sanctity of their rulers. Beastfolk traditions preserve events through symbolic language and generational memory rather than uniform written dates.

Neither source is dismissed.

Where the two contradict one another, the contradiction is preserved.

Where they agree, the result is often more reliable than either alone.

## **III. The World After the Dawning**

The first centuries after 0 A.D. were shaped by settlement around surviving Orichalcum sites.

Communities gathered near warm ruins, singing wells, light-bearing pillars, and sealed chambers that continued functioning without visible fuel. These places became the foundations of villages, temples, cities, and eventually kingdoms.

The first rulers were less monarchs than custodians.

Their authority rested upon their ability to approach dangerous relics, interpret their responses, or convince others that they could. In several regions, the first crowns were functional Orichalcum instruments rather than symbols of office.

They stabilized weather.

They illuminated fields.

They suppressed disease.

They strengthened fortifications.

Some allowed rulers to hear petitions spoken across vast distances.

A monarch’s power was therefore visible and materially useful. When crops flourished within one crown’s influence and failed beyond its protection, divine rule appeared self-evident.

This relationship between authority and survival shaped the whole age.

To reject a throne was not understood merely as treason.

It was believed to endanger the sacred machinery upon which society depended.

---

## **IV. The Three High Thrones**

Although hundreds of lesser crowns rose throughout the era, three Brethren kingdoms dominated the surviving record: **Louvain**, **Dhalraen**, and **Vaelcour**.

Their rulers became known collectively as the **Three High Thrones**.

### **The Bell-Crown of Louvain**

Early Louvain developed around buried resonant towers whose bells could be heard beneath the earth before storms, magical disturbances, and major deaths.

Its earliest reliably attested monarch, **Queen Maelis Bell-Born**, was crowned in 44 A.D. after surviving the opening of the deepest bell tower.

Louvain’s rulers claimed the divine virtue of **Judgment**.

Their court valued records, public petition, measured punishment, and the belief that every grievance could be assigned a lawful place.

The earliest ancestors of the Ministry’s archival traditions emerged within Louvain’s temple courts.

### **The Stone Throne of Dhalraen**

Dhalraen rose among mountain citadels and dwarven forge enclaves. Its rulers claimed the divine virtue of **Endurance** and presented themselves as guardians of physical reality against spiritual disorder.

Its traditional founder, **Othran the Unbroken**, was said to have remained seated upon a molten throne for six days while priests recited the names of his ancestors.

Dhalraen’s strength rested upon mining, fortification, roads, sacred engineering, and disciplined armies.

Its kings governed alongside dwarven master-smiths, though rarely as equals.

### **The Sun Court of Vaelcour**

Vaelcour controlled fertile southern plains and several surviving solar mechanisms believed to predate the Dawning.

Its rulers claimed the virtue of **Illumination**.

The Sun Court became renowned for ceremony, public miracles, elaborate genealogies, and the strongest doctrine of sacred blood.

Vaelcour monarchs presented themselves not merely as servants of divinity, but as living lenses through which the gods entered the world.

Its later obsession with purity, measured lineage, and spiritual inheritance would become one of the foundations of imperial Faith Density law.

---

## **V. Lesser Crowns and Sacred Realms**

Beyond the High Thrones existed dozens of smaller kingdoms, crowned city-states, sacred principalities, itinerant monarchies, and temple domains.

Many endured for only a few generations.

Among the most frequently cited were:

- **Etranor**, whose rulers were crowned beneath a tree that produced metal leaves;
    
- **Khal Veyra**, where the throne was awarded through ritual combat before ancestral spirits;
    
- **Ossimar**, whose monarchs ruled from ships and claimed every coast touched by their bells;
    
- **Thornwake**, where each ruler inherited ritual memories from the previous sovereign;
    
- **Caldris**, whose child-monarchs were believed to hear the gods more clearly before adulthood;
    
- and **Marrow Hall**, a funerary kingdom in which only the dead were considered lawful sovereigns and living regents governed in their names.
    

Some lesser crowns became dependencies of Louvain, Dhalraen, or Vaelcour.

Others vanished through marriage, plague, conquest, failed miracle, or divine dispute.

A number appear only within damaged songs, Labyrinth reconstructions, or isolated ruins. Whether these were true kingdoms, erased polities, symbolic traditions, or histories preserved imperfectly by the Weaves remains unresolved.

The Crown Era was never a single united civilization.

It was a world of competing sacred claims, each certain that heaven had spoken most clearly to them.

---

## **VI. The War of Seven Crowns**

Between approximately **372 and 411 A.D.**, rival monarchies fought a prolonged series of conflicts now collectively known as the **War of Seven Crowns**.

The wars began over a newly uncovered Orichalcum structure called the **Chamber of Measures**.

Surviving inscriptions suggested that the chamber could determine the rightful bearer of sovereign authority.

Seven rulers entered.

All seven emerged bearing different answers.

Each kingdom proclaimed its monarch vindicated.

What followed was nearly four decades of warfare, political marriage, assassination, relic theft, genealogical destruction, and theological accusation. Entire campaigns were fought not for land, but for possession of witnesses who had seen the Chamber open.

The conflict established practices that would define later Crown politics:

- miracle-based declarations of war;
    
- battlefield clerics acting as legal witnesses;
    
- relic seizure as dynastic conquest;
    
- destruction of enemy genealogies;
    
- ritual deposition of defeated rulers;
    
- and the rewriting of ancestry after victory.
    

The wars ended only when the three strongest kingdoms agreed that divine legitimacy could be shared, measured, and ranked.

That agreement became the **Crown Concord** of 420 A.D.

---

## **VII. The Crown Concord**

The Crown Concord established the dominant political order of the middle Crown Era.

It recognized the Three High Thrones as the principal mortal reflections of divine authority and granted lesser rulers legitimacy according to their relationship with those thrones.

The Concord created a formal hierarchy:

|Rank|Designation|Recognized Authority|
|---|---|---|
|**High Throne**|Bearer of a primary divine virtue|Independent sovereignty and authority to recognize lesser crowns|
|**Anointed Crown**|Ruler confirmed by a High Throne|Regional sovereignty under Concord law|
|**Petitionary Crown**|Ruler awaiting confirmation|Temporary authority subject to religious review|
|**Stewarded Seat**|Realm lacking a confirmed monarch|Governed by clergy, council, or appointed regency|
|**Hollow Crown**|Ruler judged divinely illegitimate|Subject to deposition, invasion, or ritual removal|

The Concord reduced open warfare between recognized kingdoms, but transformed theology into international law.

Royal legitimacy could now be audited.

Marriage contracts required resonance review.

Succession disputes were decided by priests, genealogists, relic examiners, and sacred witnesses.

A child could be denied inheritance because a lamp dimmed during their naming.

A distant cousin could become sovereign because an Orichalcum blade sang in their presence.

Power remained hereditary.

Inheritance alone was no longer sufficient.

The blood had to perform.

## **VIII. Crown Society and Daily Life**

Crown society was divided into recognized **Orders**, each believed to occupy a divinely ordained role.

### **The Anointed**

Kings, queens, princely houses, high clergy, and families accepted as bearers of sacred lineage.

They possessed land, magical education, military command, and direct access to powerful relics.

### **The Ordained**

Priests, judges, scribes, physicians, miracle interpreters, astronomers, and sanctioned scholars.

They translated divine intention into law and maintained the machinery of faith.

### **The Crafted**

Smiths, builders, masons, bellwrights, weavers, alchemists, and licensed artisans.

Their labor was respected when it served sacred institutions and feared when it approached independent understanding.

### **The Rooted**

Farmers, fishers, shepherds, miners, household servants, and most laborers.

The Rooted were praised as the foundation of the kingdom while rarely permitted to influence it.

### **The Unplaced**

Wanderers, foreign migrants, hedge mages, unsanctioned craftspeople, outcast scholars, and those without recognized lineage.

The Unplaced possessed few protections and could be expelled, conscripted, or declared heretical with little legal resistance.

Daily life varied greatly.

A prosperous Louvain baker might belong to a temple-chartered guild, employ apprentices, and submit petitions to a Faith Court. A farmer beneath Vaelcour might owe grain, labor, prayer, military duty, and ritual attendance to several overlapping authorities.

Marriage contracts were often witnessed by relics.

Children were registered at temple doors.

Trades required sacred licenses.

Major journeys began with formal petition.

Even common meals could carry ritual obligations depending upon the kingdom, patron deity, season, and household rank.

The Crown Era was orderly when viewed from a palace.

From the road, its laws changed with every bell tower.

---

## **IX. Faith as Administration**

The Crown kingdoms transformed worship into the first great bureaucracy of post-Dawning Aerathon.

Temples did not merely perform rites.

They counted belief.

### **Petition Ledgers**

Citizens submitted formal prayers requesting healing, protection, favorable weather, fertility, legal relief, safe travel, or release from debt.

Petitions were categorized by urgency, social rank, offering, and divine relevance.

A noble’s request might reach an oracle within a Span.

A laborer’s petition could remain unanswered for years.

### **Faith Taxation**

Temples collected coin, grain, labor, crafted goods, and prescribed acts of devotion. These offerings were believed to sustain divine attention and were redistributed through authorized miracles.

In many kingdoms, worship became a civic obligation.

Failure to attend temple could be treated not as impiety, but as theft from the spiritual resources of the community.

### **The Prayer Census**

Beginning around 641 A.D., several Crown kingdoms conducted formal censuses of religious participation.

Households recorded which gods they worshipped, how often they prayed, and whether those prayers produced measurable results.

These censuses became the earliest predecessors of the Faith Registries later imposed by the Empire of Law and Light.

### **Sacred Scheduling**

Coronations, harvests, marriages, campaigns, trials, and executions were scheduled according to divine observance.

A victorious battle begun upon an unlawful date could be condemned after the fact.

The Crown rulers claimed that faith unified society.

In practice, it allowed the state to enter every home without opening the door.

---

## **X. Faith Courts and Sacred Justice**

The first **Faith Courts** arose in Louvain and spread throughout the Concord kingdoms.

Their purpose was to determine not only whether a law had been broken, but whether an act offended the divine structure believed to support that law.

Evidence could include:

- witness testimony;
    
- oath resonance;
    
- relic reaction;
    
- prophetic dreams;
    
- ancestral reputation;
    
- bloodline analysis;
    
- and the accused person’s response to controlled miracles.
    

### **Trial by Relic**

The accused was exposed to an enchanted object believed to reject falsehood, corruption, or spiritual impurity.

Survival, healing, illumination, or calm response might indicate innocence.

Burning, sickness, silence, or death might indicate guilt.

Modern Ministry study has demonstrated that many such relics responded to fear, blood chemistry, magical aptitude, inherited resonance, or unknown factors unrelated to morality.

### **The White Ash Judgment**

One of the age’s most infamous trials occurred in **717 A.D.**, when **Lady Hestra Vael** of Vaelcour was accused of poisoning her elder brother.

She was ordered to walk barefoot across sacred ash.

The ash remained white.

The court declared her innocent.

Within a year, three witnesses disappeared, Hestra inherited the throne, and records of the poison were removed from the royal archive.

Later examination determined that the ash had been chemically treated.

The case is now taught in MDO legal training as an early example of ritual evidence being manipulated to manufacture divine certainty.

---

## **XI. Magic and the Orichalcum Principle**

Crown-era magic was founded upon the belief that all legitimate spellcraft descended from **Orichalcum resonance**.

Theologians taught that Orichalcum retained the first harmony of creation. Through approved striking, heating, inscription, prayer, and song, priests and sacrist engineers could awaken fragments of that harmony.

This process became known as **faith quenching**.

### **Miracle Regulators**

Orichalcum relics stabilized, restricted, or localized magical effects.

A regulator might permit healing within a temple, prevent fire inside a royal archive, suppress unsanctioned magic in a courtroom, or strengthen the walls of a sacred city.

### **Sacrist Engineers**

Dwarven and Brethren craftspeople trained to maintain sacred machinery were called sacrist engineers.

They occupied an uneasy position between priest and artisan.

Their knowledge was essential.

Their understanding threatened the claim that only royalty and clergy possessed sacred authority.

### **Restricted Smelting**

By the eighth century, most High Thrones had declared raw Orichalcum the property of the Crown.

The **Edict of Sealed Forges**, issued in 783 A.D., prohibited private smelting, unauthorized alloy study, and possession of unregistered fragments.

Violations could result in execution and the removal of an entire family from guild recognition.

These restrictions created extensive smuggling networks.

Fragments stolen during the Crown Era would later become vital to dissident artificers, Uncrowned scholars, and the revolutionary schools of the Ember Age.

---

## **XII. Dwarves, Elves, and Sacred Craft**

The Age of Crowns was dominated politically by Brethren monarchies, but those monarchies relied heavily upon dwarven and elven communities.

### **Dwarven Enclaves**

Dwarven smiths understood more about Orichalcum than most Crown priests, though they rarely described their methods in theological terms.

Many enclaves accepted royal protection in exchange for forge access, road construction, and military engineering.

Over time, these agreements became increasingly restrictive.

Royal officials controlled ore shipments, inspected workshops, seized inventions, and claimed ownership of knowledge developed under temple charter.

Some dwarven houses prospered.

Others sealed their halls, concealed their methods, or withdrew into mountain territories beyond Crown control.

Techniques preserved by these communities later resurfaced among the first self-taught artificers.

### **Elven Isolates**

Elven communities often served as astronomers, gardeners, physicians, ritualists, and interpreters of long celestial cycles.

Their lifespans made them valuable witnesses to dynastic history—and dangerous critics of fabricated genealogy.

Several courts employed elven recordkeepers while forbidding them from publishing independent accounts.

The destruction of the **Silver Bough Archive** around 892 A.D. is widely believed to have eliminated records disproving the sacred ancestry of several major noble houses.

No Crown authority accepted responsibility.

---

## **XIII. Beastfolk in the Closed Eye**

Crown-era Brethren records often describe Beastfolk as scattered wilderness populations, spirits in mortal shape, malformed Brethren, divine servants, or dangerous creatures beyond the borders of civilization.

Beastfolk traditions describe something markedly different.

They preserve accounts of established kinships, sacred roads, hunting territories, shrines, burial grounds, trade agreements, and laws extending far deeper into history than Crown scribes acknowledged.

The contradiction is no longer treated as a mystery of Beastfolk development.

It is treated as evidence of Crown-era erasure.

### **The Age of the Closed Eye**

Many Beastfolk cultures remember the Crown Era as the **Age of the Closed Eye**, when the gods of the royal temples saw only those placed before sanctioned altars.

Ursine carvings depict kneeling figures beneath eclipsed circles.

Leonine road-songs describe golden houses that declared the land empty wherever their banners had not reached.

Vulpine memory tales speak of priests who mistook silence for absence.

Relations between Beastfolk kinships and Crown settlements varied. Some communities traded herbs, hides, metal, livestock, and route knowledge. Others were displaced, enslaved, hunted, or displayed within royal menageries as proof of divine dominion over the natural world.

A small number entered Crown society as scouts, servants, bodyguards, laborers, or religious curiosities.

Their personal names were seldom recorded.

Their species was.

### **Tharos, the Seven-Ringed King**

The strongest surviving challenge to the Crown claim that Beastfolk lacked organized civilization is the record of **Tharos**, later remembered as **Tharos Ravelmane**, the Seven-Ringed King.

He rose in western Vinyot during the ninth century A.D., when Crown-aligned settlements, hunting companies, and fortified roads were expanding into the **Fortunata Hills** and the lower approaches of the **Betemere Mountains**.

Brethren accounts called him a beast-lord who “united the feral races.”

The Ministry retains that phrase only when quoting Crown sources. It is not an accepted classification.

Beastfolk traditions remember Tharos as a mighty sovereign who united numerous kinships beneath a common defense while allowing each people to preserve its own customs and ancestral law.

His kingdom is provisionally known to modern historians as the **Seven-Ring Kingdom**, though its original name has not yet been recovered with confidence.

Tharos openly restored worship of the **Old Beastfolk Pantheon**, an ancient divine order associated with kingship, death, protection, knowledge, war, fertility, rivers, and the sacred forms of beasts.

According to surviving traditions, those gods answered him.

Their favor made Tharos the strongest Beastfolk known to have walked the lands of Crown-era Aerathon.

He bore seven rings, each of which altered reality in its bearer’s favor. The rings’ individual powers and origins remain reserved for dedicated relic classification. Crown sources attributed his victories to forbidden sorcery. Beastfolk sources described the rings as covenants between their king and the elder gods.

Under Tharos, western Beastfolk communities raised fortified settlements, reopened ancestral roads, liberated captured kin, and resisted Crown attempts to classify inhabited land as unclaimed wilderness.

His kingdom became a refuge not only for Beastfolk, but for Unplaced Brethren, dissident craftspeople, hedge mages, and escaped servants.

Among the deeds attributed to him are:

- the destruction of the royal menageries of Caldran Vale;
    
- the liberation of settlements along the western bell-road;
    
- the defeat of several Crown expeditions;
    
- the establishment of protected migration routes;
    
- and the creation of the **Oath of Many Voices**, under which distinct kinships retained their own law while answering a shared call to defense.
    

Tharos was not ultimately defeated by a Brethren army.

He was betrayed from within.

Some Beastfolk leaders feared that permanent separation from the Brethren kingdoms would bring eventual destruction. Others believed accommodation would preserve their individual territories. Some resented the authority Tharos exercised over previously independent clans.

Crown agents encouraged these divisions with promises of trade, protection, titles, and regional autonomy.

In **927 A.D.**, during an assembly remembered as the **Night of Open Gates**, members of Tharos’s own alliance admitted his enemies into the royal stronghold.

No reliable account records precisely how he fell.

Some traditions say the seven rings were removed one by one.

Others claim Tharos was bound by sacred oaths that prevented him from striking his own people.

Several insist he could have killed the traitors but refused to preserve his kingdom by slaughtering those he had united.

By dawn, his banners had fallen.

Within a generation, the Seven-Ring Kingdom had fractured into isolated enclaves, refugee roads, Brethren-aligned territories, and hidden communities.

Crown authorities erased Tharos wherever possible.

His roads were renamed.

His shrines were dismantled.

His settlements were reclassified as wilderness.

Later imperial histories claimed that no independent Beastfolk kingdom had existed there at all.

The memory of Tharos survived through song, oath, and warning.

His fall became proof that domination did not always arrive through conquest. Sometimes it entered through an open gate and spoke in the voice of safety.

Centuries later, during the spread of the Labyrinths, the memory of Tharos became powerful enough to shape an entire formation around his lost kingdom.

That phenomenon belongs chronologically to the Age of Collapse.

Its origin belongs here.

## **XIV. Heresy, the Uncrowned, and Resistance**

The Crown Era was never as unified as its official histories suggest.

Resistance appeared in many forms.

### **The Uncrowned Schools**

Unsanctioned scholars argued that relics responded to knowledge, intent, and training rather than sacred blood.

They taught commoners to reproduce simple magical effects without priestly approval.

Most schools survived only briefly before discovery.

Their teachings continued through coded workshop marks, embroidery, children’s games, songs, and fragments hidden within ordinary tools.

### **The Ashen Petitioners**

This movement emerged among families whose prayers had remained unanswered despite years of faithful taxation.

Petitioners burned copies of their rejected prayers outside temple gates and wore the ashes upon their faces.

Their creed was simple:

> _“A silent god has no claim upon the hungry.”_

The movement was repeatedly suppressed but never eliminated.

### **The Bellwright Conspiracy**

In 934 A.D., several Louvain bellwrights were accused of altering temple bells so they carried public grievances across the city during royal ceremonies.

According to court testimony, the bells began reciting the names of citizens whose petitions had vanished from temple ledgers.

Thirty-one artisans were executed.

The bells continued speaking for nine nights.

### **The Uncrowned Rising**

Between approximately 931 and 948 A.D., rural communities, artisans, hedge mages, dissident clergy, and displaced peoples carried out coordinated revolts throughout several Crown territories.

The rising began only years after the betrayal of Tharos.

Some rebel songs invoke a lion beneath seven circles or speak of “the gate opened before dawn.” The Ministry considers these references probable evidence that survivors or sympathizers of the Seven-Ring Kingdom influenced the movement.

The rising failed militarily.

Its ideas survived.

Later imperial historians dismissed it as criminal unrest. Ember philosophers remembered it as one of the first organized declarations that magic, law, and dignity did not belong exclusively to sacred blood.

---

## **XV. Decline and the Binding Concord**

By the final century of the Age of Crowns, the institutions built upon divine certainty had begun to fail.

Oracles contradicted one another.

Relics responded unpredictably.

Sacred lineages produced heirs with no measurable resonance.

Miracles became weaker, rarer, or more costly.

Some relics ceased functioning entirely.

Others continued obeying laws that no living priest understood.

### **The Hollow Masses**

The first widely authenticated **Hollow Mass** occurred in Dhalraen in 1012 A.D.

An entire congregation completed a nine-day prayer for relief from famine.

Nothing answered.

No light appeared.

No relic stirred.

The famine continued.

Similar events spread across the Crown kingdoms.

Temples concealed many of them. Petition ledgers were altered. Failed prayers were blamed upon heresy, insufficient offerings, corrupted bloodlines, or foreign influence.

None of these explanations restored the miracles.

### **The Last Kingly Visitation**

Vaelcour records claim that in 1056 A.D. a divine figure appeared before King Aereth IV and warned that the gods would soon withdraw from mortal rule.

The account survives only in copies produced after Aereth’s death.

Some historians accept it as a genuine warning.

Others consider it propaganda intended to justify expanding royal authority during a period of declining faith.

### **The Binding Concord**

In 1091 A.D., the remaining High Thrones and major Faith Courts signed the **Binding Concord**.

The treaty attempted to preserve divine law despite the absence of reliable divine response.

It established:

- standardized scripture;
    
- permanent Faith Registries;
    
- compulsory miracle licensing;
    
- expanded prayer taxation;
    
- hereditary religious duties;
    
- centralized theological review;
    
- and legal authority for mortal institutions to speak in the gods’ silence.
    

The Concord was presented as preservation.

In practice, it severed sacred law from sacred confirmation.

Faith no longer needed a god to answer.

It needed only an institution willing to declare that the answer had already been given.

By the end of 1099 A.D., the High Thrones remained as ceremonial pillars within a growing centralized theocratic order.

In **1100 A.D.**, that order proclaimed itself the **Empire of Law and Light**.

The Age of Crowns ended.

The Age of Chains began.

## **XVII. Historical Legacy and Unresolved Questions**

The Age of Crowns created many institutions that later ages claimed to reject.

Modern courts descend from Faith Courts.

Ministry filing practices descend from petition ledgers.

Guild licensing descends from sacred trade charters.

Relic regulation descends from Crown monopoly.

Even the MDO’s insistence upon authenticated authority bears an uncomfortable resemblance to the old belief that legitimacy could be measured, recorded, and sealed.

The era also left unresolved questions.

### **Were the First Crowns Truly Divine?**

Some retain functions no modern artificer can reproduce. Others have been exposed as sophisticated relic interfaces mistaken for proof of sacred blood.

### **Did Noble Lineages Carry Measurable Power?**

Certain families displayed unusual resonance, though whether this resulted from inheritance, relic exposure, magical alteration, selective marriage, or fabricated evidence remains disputed.

### **What Was Deliberately Erased?**

Beastfolk testimony, elven archives, hidden workshop records, and the history of Tharos all suggest that Crown scribes systematically removed peoples and events that challenged royal theology.

The scale of that erasure remains unknown.

### **What Became of the Seven Rings?**

No complete Crown-era record describes their fate after the betrayal of Tharos.

Later accounts disagree over whether they were divided, buried, stolen, sealed with their king, or never truly separated from him.

This matter remains under restricted investigation.

### **Why Did the Gods Grow Silent?**

Crown theologians blamed moral decline.

The later Empire blamed insufficient obedience.

Ember philosophers blamed dependence upon failing relic systems.

Some Beastfolk traditions claim the gods never became silent—only unwilling to continue speaking through Brethren thrones.

The eventual formation of a Labyrinth around the memory of Tharos further complicates the question. If ancient Beastfolk gods answered him as tradition claims, some portion of that divine covenant may have endured long after the kingdom itself was erased.

No theory has been proven.

The Age of Crowns is therefore remembered as foundation and warning.

It established law where there had been ruin.

It made large civilization possible across a fractured world.

It also taught rulers that order could be mistaken for holiness—and that a record bearing enough seals could survive long after the truth had been cut from it.

> _“A circle of gold is still a shackle when the hand beneath it is forbidden to open.”_
