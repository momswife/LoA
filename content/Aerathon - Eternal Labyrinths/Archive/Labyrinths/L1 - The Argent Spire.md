## **The First Labyrinth**

> "It was not built. It was revealed."
> **— Archivist Adrastia, 3E 15**

Before the Eternal Labyrinths spread across Aerathon, before Delving became a profession, a spectacle, a way of life, there was one. The first.

The Argent Spire.

It did not appear overnight, nor did it erupt from the land like a wound. It was always there, waiting beneath the surface of the world, buried beneath the heart of what would become Halvask. It was discovered by accident, unearthed when the foundations of the first great towers were laid, when the city’s scholars and masons dug too deep and struck silver.

The stone shattered. The earth trembled.

And below, in the darkness, the first door was waiting.

---

## **A Labyrinth of Silver and Reflection**

Unlike the twisting dungeons, shifting nightmares, or deadly trials that define the other Eternal Labyrinths, the Argent Spire was silent. It was not a place of monsters, nor of war, nor of illusion. Its walls, impossibly smooth and forged from veins of glowing silver, reflected not just those who entered—but something more.

- The corridors stretched longer than they should have, leading back to places already passed.
- Voices whispered—not threats, not warnings, but questions.
- Reflections in the silver did not always match the ones who cast them.

Some who entered found themselves face to face with their past selves, their own eyes staring back, full of judgments never spoken aloud. Others saw people who should not have been there—loved ones long dead, enemies they had never met, figures they had only dreamed of in nightmares.

The Spire did not attack those who entered.

It simply showed them what they did not want to see.

---

## **The First Delvers and the Vanishing**

When the first mage-scholars of Halvask ventured into the Spire, they expected knowledge, relics, secrets of the ancients.

What they found instead was themselves.

The first expedition, led by Archmagister Solvaris the Wise, consisted of twenty of Halvask’s finest minds. They stepped through the doors with confidence, expecting to map its halls, to return with wisdom that would change the world.

None of them returned.

For months, the Spire stood silent, its entrance unchanged, its silver walls gleaming in the torchlight. Then, on the final night of the year’s Reckoning, a single figure emerged—a woman, her robes tattered, her eyes burned silver, her voice a whisper of a thousand echoes.

It was Solvaris.

Or at least, something wearing her name.

She spoke in riddles, in statements that had no meaning, or perhaps had too much meaning. She wrote equations that could not exist, spells that could not function, names that had never been spoken. When asked what she had seen, she only smiled.

Then she stepped back into the Spire.

And the doors sealed behind her.

## **The Attempts to Conquer the Spire**

For the next century, Halvask’s greatest scholars, kings, and heroes attempted to understand the Spire.

- **Warlord Thalric the Resolute** entered with an army. He was found two days later, kneeling before the silver walls, weeping, speaking to someone no one else could see.
- **High Seeress Valmyra of the Veilshroud** attempted to peer into the Spire’s depths through divination. She clawed out her own eyes within the hour.
- **The Lord of Mirrors**, a Delver known for conquering illusions, spent three weeks inside before he walked into a reflection of himself and never re-emerged.

Some who entered returned—but changed. They spoke of questions that could never be answered, choices they had never made, futures that might never happen. Some vanished from history entirely, their names erased from records, their very existence doubted by those who had known them.

It became clear: The Argent Spire was not a Labyrinth to be conquered. It was a question to be answered.

---

## **The Spire Seals Itself**

In 4E 112, after centuries of failed exploration, the Spire changed.

For the first time, it responded.

The doors, once open to all, began to close, slowly, as if time itself was sealing them shut. The silver walls drew inward, the passages folded upon themselves, and the final expedition—a team of Halvask’s finest scholars—barely escaped before the last door vanished entirely.

The Spire was gone.

Not destroyed. Not lost. Simply… removed.

No force—arcane or divine—has been able to reopen it since.

Its ruins remain beneath the capital of Halvask, its presence felt in the dreams of scholars, in the flicker of reflections that last too long, in the questions that have no answers.

For centuries, the world has waited for the Spire to reopen.

And some believe that one day, it will.

The only question is:

When it does, will we be ready for what it has learned?
