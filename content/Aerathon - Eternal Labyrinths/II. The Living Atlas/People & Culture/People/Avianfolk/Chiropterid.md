## I. Lineage Overview

**Chiropterids** are an Avianfolk lineage distinguished by membranous wings, acute hearing, highly expressive ears, and an ancestral connection to the Astral Sea.

They are the only known sapient people believed to have evolved entirely within that plane.

Their recognized sublineages include flying foxes, vampire bats, leaf-nosed bats, horseshoe bats, long-eared bats, fishing bats, ghost bats, and free-tailed bats.

Though physically similar to the nonsapient bats found throughout Aerathon, Chiropterids are not generally believed to descend from them. Whether the resemblance arose through planar convergence, a common forgotten ancestor, or the Astral Sea’s tendency to echo forms from the material world remains unresolved.

Chiropterids are classified as Avianfolk because aerial movement remains essential to their anatomy, identity, architecture, and understanding of space.

Their inclusion remains controversial.

They possess no feathers. Their wings are formed from living membrane stretched across elongated fingers. Outside the Astral Sea, most cannot generate enough lift for sustained powered flight and must instead glide from height.

Within their ancestral plane, however, Chiropterids fly freely.

Gravity there is responsive rather than absolute. Direction may be chosen through will, motion, and familiarity. A Chiropterid born in that environment does not learn to fly in the ordinary sense.

They learn how to decide where falling should lead.

The material world offers no such courtesy.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Chiropterid tradition does not describe a single first settlement.

The oldest surviving accounts speak of numerous floating sanctuaries scattered through the Astral Sea. These communities were not built upon land, for there was no land in the ordinary sense. They were shaped from raw **astra** through intention, imagination, and coordinated effort.

A dwelling might begin as an agreed idea:

A place warm enough for sleeping.

A room where echoes carried clearly.

A garden of edible light.

A hall large enough for everyone to fly through without changing direction.

Through **astralcraft**, that idea could be given form.

Walls, bridges, chambers, food, art, tools, and entire districts were created from mutable astral matter. Their stability depended upon construction, maintenance, shared expectation, and the quality of the will used to shape them.

Chiropterids therefore developed without many conditions considered unavoidable in Aerathon.

Their ancestral environment possessed:

- No fixed gravity
    
- No natural weather
    
- No ordinary agriculture
    
- No permanent shortage of building material
    
- No conventional borders
    
- No stable distinction between road and open space
    
- Few predators recognizable by material standards
    
- Little need to defend land as property
    

This did not create a society without conflict.

Chiropterids argued over attention, beauty, responsibility, memory, communal intent, and whether a creation should be preserved after it ceased serving its original purpose. A person could not starve because another hoarded a field, but an entire sanctuary might dissolve if too few people still cared to sustain it.

Their oldest disputes were therefore rarely about possession.

They were about continuation.

### Creation Over Continuity

Many traditional Chiropterid cultures place greater value upon creation than preservation.

A structure may be remade rather than repaired. A ceremony may change whenever it no longer speaks clearly to those performing it. A name may be altered when its bearer feels it no longer fits.

History is remembered, but not always treated as authority.

An old practice does not become wise merely because it survived. A grievance does not become sacred merely because several generations inherited it. Permanence, to many Chiropterids, is something that must continually justify itself.

This philosophy can appear liberating to outsiders.

It can also appear careless.

Material societies rely upon structures that cannot simply be imagined anew. A bridge cannot be dissolved because its design has become boring. A food store cannot be replaced through communal creativity. Records destroyed in the name of reinvention may contain information that cannot be recovered.

Chiropterids living in Aerathon have therefore developed complicated relationships with continuity.

Some become passionate preservationists precisely because they have learned how easily material things can vanish.

Others view the material world’s attachment to permanence as a fear disguised as virtue.

### The First Weight

Chiropterid communities preserve numerous accounts of the first individuals to cross into Aerathon.

These stories are collectively known as **the First Weight**.

The name refers not only to gravity, but to the accumulated experience of material existence:

- Bodies that tire more quickly
    
- Objects that remain where they are left
    
- Structures that do not respond to thought
    
- Hunger that imagination cannot satisfy
    
- Injuries that do not dissolve when attention shifts
    
- Distance that must be crossed physically
    
- Time that continues whether or not a community agrees
    
- Death that cannot be revised
    

Some First Weight accounts are solemn.

Others are comic.

One early traveler reportedly spent three days attempting to persuade a staircase to become less inconvenient before accepting that it lacked both consciousness and professional pride.

## III. Body and Astral Anatomy

Chiropterids are generally smaller than most feathered Avianfolk.

Most stand between **4 feet 7 inches and 5 feet 7 inches**. Their frames range from slender and lightly built to broad-shouldered forms capable of supporting large wing membranes.

A Chiropterid’s wings extend from elongated fingers and connect along the arms, torso, and often the legs. The exact attachment varies by sublineage.

Their hands remain capable of ordinary tool use, though manipulating objects while the wings are fully extended can be difficult.

### Wing Membranes

Chiropterid wing membranes are thin, living structures containing nerves, blood vessels, muscle fibers, and highly sensitive tissue.

They are not fragile fabric.

Healthy membranes can endure considerable strain, recover from minor tears, and provide precise information about wind, temperature, pressure, and movement.

They are nevertheless vulnerable to:

- Sharp edges
    
- Extreme cold
    
- Fire
    
- Caustic substances
    
- Dehydration
    
- Poorly fitted armor
    
- Repeated overextension
    
- Magical effects that alter bodily shape
    
- Restraints placed across the wing fingers
    

Membrane care commonly includes oils, salves, warm wrapping, gentle stretching, and regular examination for small injuries.

Touching another person’s wing membrane without permission is considered an intimate violation in most Chiropterid communities.

### Hearing and Facial Form

Chiropterid ears vary greatly.

They may be broad, narrow, rounded, pointed, folded, or nearly as long as the person’s forearm. Some possess elaborate nose-leaves or facial structures that assist with the direction of sound.

Many Chiropterids are highly sensitive to:

- Echo
    
- Vibration
    
- Pressure change
    
- High-pitched sound
    
- Sudden magical resonance
    
- Movement through enclosed spaces
    

This sensitivity does not grant flawless perception.

Crowded machinery, alarms, overlapping voices, and hostile magical sound can become disorienting or painful.

A Chiropterid asking for quiet is not necessarily afraid.

They may simply be attempting to perceive anything at all.

### Material Gliding

Outside the Astral Sea, most Chiropterids cannot sustain true powered flight.

Their wings permit controlled gliding, allowing them to descend safely and cross substantial horizontal distance when launched from sufficient height.

This limitation can be deeply distressing to Astral-born individuals.

A person who spent childhood choosing direction freely may arrive in Aerathon and discover that every journey through the air begins with borrowed height.

Some describe the experience as **weight grief**.

Others consider material gliding exhilarating because it introduces risk, speed, and commitment absent from ordinary Astral movement.

### Astral Flight

Within the Astral Sea, Chiropterids regain unrestricted aerial movement.

Their wings interact with raw astra, intention, and the plane’s flexible directionality. They can hover, ascend, turn, and rest in open space without depending upon ordinary lift.

Many report an immediate sensation of relief upon returning.

Others, especially those born in Aerathon, find the absence of fixed gravity disorienting.

Home can become unfamiliar in both directions.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Traditional Chiropterid communities are often called **roosts**, **folds**, or **resonances**.

A roost may refer to a household, neighborhood, sanctuary, artistic collective, or group whose members contribute to maintaining the same astral structure.

A fold is usually smaller and more intimate. It may consist of close family, companions, collaborators, or people who share access to private astral space.

A resonance is defined less by residence and more by shared thought, art, research, purpose, or emotional connection.

A person may belong to several resonances while maintaining only one fold.

### Communal Creation

In the Astral Sea, building is frequently collaborative.

One individual may establish a structure’s shape. Another gives it texture. A third determines how sound travels through it. Others contribute light, scent, temperature, memory, or symbolic meaning.

The resulting creation is not always considered the property of a single maker.

It may belong to:

- Everyone who shaped it
    
- Everyone who maintains it
    
- The purpose for which it was created
    
- The community using it
    
- No one at all
    

This has caused significant legal confusion in Aerathon.

A Chiropterid artist may reproduce a collaborative Astral design without realizing that material law considers it owned by another participant. Conversely, a material patron may attempt to purchase exclusive rights to something its creators believe cannot meaningfully be possessed.

### The Sleeping Fold

Chiropterids commonly sleep near trusted companions.

In the Astral Sea, sleeping groups may drift together within warm chambers, enclosed folds, or slow-moving structures shaped to respond to breath and sound.

In Aerathon, communal sleeping may involve rafters, suspended beds, hanging wraps, high platforms, enclosed lofts, or ordinary rooms adapted for wing comfort.

Sharing a sleeping space does not automatically indicate romance.

It may communicate:

- Trust
    
- Family
    
- Safety
    
- Shared watch
    
- Warmth
    
- Medical need
    
- Familiarity with one another’s breathing and movement
    

Forcing a Chiropterid into isolated sleeping arrangements can be distressing, particularly for recent arrivals.

Others prefer solitude and resent the assumption that all bats require crowded roosts.

### Pocket Etiquette

The **Cheiropocket** carried by each Chiropterid is both practical and personal.

Though it stores only nonliving material, the pocket is tethered directly to the individual’s wing membrane and astral presence.

Opening another person’s pocket, reaching into it, or demanding proof of its contents without lawful cause is treated as a violation comparable to searching their body.

Items placed within a Cheiropocket may include:

- Tools
    
- Clothing
    
- Food
    
- Art
    
- Letters
    
- unfinished creations
    
- Objects too emotionally significant to leave elsewhere
    
- Material reminders of people who cannot enter the pocket themselves
    

Some Chiropterids keep their pockets meticulously organized.

Others appear to maintain a private collapse of causality held together by optimism.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Chiropterid communities commonly admire:

- Imagination
    
- Adaptability
    
- Emotional responsiveness
    
- Creative courage
    
- The ability to revise one’s beliefs
    
- Curiosity about unfamiliar realities
    
- Communal contribution without rigid ownership
    
- The willingness to release what no longer serves a purpose
    

These values can produce inventive thinkers, generous collaborators, flexible leaders, and people capable of adapting when established assumptions collapse.

They can also encourage instability and disregard for consequence.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Creativity|Imagining better possibilities|Treating practical limits as beneath consideration|
|Adaptability|Revising beliefs when evidence changes|Lacking reliable principles|
|Release|Letting go of harmful traditions|Abandoning people or duties too quickly|
|Communal intent|Building through cooperation|Pressuring dissenters to share the group’s vision|
|Curiosity|Exploring unfamiliar worlds|Ignoring danger or consent|
|Improvisation|Responding fluidly to change|Failing to prepare|
|Emotional immediacy|Addressing conflict before it hardens|Dismissing long-term consequences|
|Novelty|Preventing cultural stagnation|Treating continuity as inherently foolish|

A recurring Chiropterid anxiety is the fear of **becoming fixed in a form, role, or history they can no longer change**.

Some respond by reinventing themselves constantly.

Others become intensely protective of personal choice. A few abandon commitments whenever they begin to feel permanent, confusing the possibility of change with the absence of responsibility.

### Material Anxiety

Chiropterids raised in the Astral Sea may struggle with scarcity.

A lost object cannot always be replaced. Food requires labor. Shelter may belong to someone else. A poor decision can continue causing harm long after the person who made it has changed their mind.

This can produce anxiety, fascination, or both.

Some recent arrivals hoard material goods because permanence makes them feel secure. Others remain careless with possessions because they have not internalized their limits.

### The Burden of Novelty

Outsiders often expect Chiropterids to be unusual, whimsical, unpredictable, or entertainingly confused by ordinary life.

This creates pressure to perform strangeness.

A Chiropterid may possess strong routines, conservative beliefs, or little interest in planar philosophy. Those born in Aerathon may know less about astralcraft than scholars expecting them to explain their entire ancestral plane.

Being Astralborn is an inheritance.

It is not a public obligation to remain fascinating.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Chiropterid communities disagree over:

- Whether permanent settlements should be established in Aerathon
    
- Whether material-born Chiropterids remain fully Astralborn
    
- Whether old sanctuaries should be preserved unchanged
    
- Whether Cheiropockets should be subject to customs inspection
    
- Whether astralcraft can be privately owned
    
- Whether material scarcity justifies stronger hierarchy
    
- Whether grudges are warnings or wasted thought
    
- Whether history should guide future creation
    
- Whether true flight in the Astral Sea makes material gliding lesser
    
- Whether regional identities weaken or deepen Chiropterid culture
    

These disputes have intensified as more Chiropterids are born beneath fixed gravity.

## VI. The Astral Sea and the Sky-Reeve Question

No authenticated Sky-Reeve record confirms Chiropterid membership among the ancient clans.

The surviving evidence places the Sky-Reeves primarily among feathered Avianfolk, aerial Beastfolk, and air-kin active within Aerathon’s atmosphere. Chiropterid arrivals became widely documented only after planar boundaries weakened and Astral Gates began opening with greater frequency.

This has not prevented numerous claims to the contrary.

Several old carvings depict membranous-winged figures near Sky-Reeve structures. Some scholars identify them as air-kin hybrids, ceremonial depictions, or stylized feathered pilots. Others argue they show early Chiropterid visitors whose presence was later forgotten.

The evidence remains inconclusive.

### The Fifth Current

Modern Chiropterids sometimes refer to the planar boundary as **the Fifth Current**.

The ancient clans recognized four great atmospheric currents:

- North Gale
    
- South Zephyr
    
- East Tempest
    
- West Cyclone
    

Chiropterid pilots argue that the Sky-Reeves failed to name the current moving not across the world, but between worlds.

Traditional Sky-Reeve descendants generally respond that a planar veil is not a wind.

Chiropterids generally respond that this sounds like a limitation of imagination.

### Modern Stormriders

Chiropterids have entered modern Stormriding traditions with considerable enthusiasm.

They cannot sustain ordinary flight in Aerathon, but specialized harnesses, aerolith engines, elevated launch structures, and controlled storm currents allow them to ride for extended periods.

Their membrane sensitivity makes them highly responsive to subtle changes in pressure.

Their Astral Echoes may also reveal:

- Objects partially phasing from the material world
    
- Planar turbulence near Labyrinth weather
    
- Astral distortions hidden within storm systems
    
- Routes where ordinary air briefly intersects the Fifth Current
    

Chiropterid Stormriders are sometimes called **Veilriders**.

Some treat Stormriding as sport. Others regard it as an attempt to combine material danger with Astral freedom. A smaller number believe they are completing the Sky-Reeve tradition by adding the current its founders failed to perceive.

### The Tithe of Weight

Several modern Chiropterid communities observe a rite known as the **Tithe of Weight**.

Before a dangerous material flight, the rider chooses one object from their Cheiropocket and leaves it behind.

The object must possess personal meaning.

The rite acknowledges that material movement has cost. Not everything can be carried. Not every version of oneself can remain intact through every crossing.

If the rider returns, they reclaim the object.

If they do not, the object remains with their fold.

This practice is not ancient Sky-Reeve tradition.

It is a Chiropterid answer to it.

## VII. Chiropterids in Modern Aerathon

Chiropterids remain uncommon compared with most Avianfolk lineages, but established communities now exist throughout the Five Greater Regions.

Some arrived through sanctioned gates. Others emerged from Labyrinth breaches, failed planar experiments, accidental crossings, or routes they refuse to explain to Ministry officials.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Chiropterids often settle in converted barns, bell towers, communal lofts, old mills, and homes expanded to provide high sleeping spaces.

Allemancian customs of hospitality and shared responsibility can ease the transition into material life.

A newcomer unfamiliar with scarcity may be taught through ordinary household practice:

Food is prepared because someone planted, harvested, and carried it.

A roof remains because someone repairs it.

A place at the table continues because people choose to keep it.

Many Allemancian Chiropterids become deeply attached to objects repaired repeatedly across generations. Such items represent a form of continuity rarely encountered in the Astral Sea.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Chiropterids work in planar engineering, breach analysis, structural acoustics, magitech research, emergency inspection, and astral containment.

Oric culture insists that imagination must survive contact with measurement.

This can frustrate Astral-born Chiropterids, but it also gives their work durable form. Ideas that once depended upon communal intent can become machines, bridges, tools, and stable systems.

Oric Chiropterids frequently develop strong interests in:

- Materials science
    
- Mechanical storage
    
- Structural resonance
    
- Teleportation
    
- Controlled gravity
    
- Emergency power
    
- Artificial flight
    

The most successful collaborations pair Chiropterid conceptual flexibility with Oric verification.

The least successful produce memorable explosions and excellent revisions.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], portable storage, planar craft, novelty, and unusual performance all possess immediate commercial value.

Chiropterids work in art, transport, secure storage, entertainment, trade, fashion, architecture, magical research, and luxury fabrication.

Cheiropockets present constant legal disputes.

Vinyoti authorities argue over:

- Customs declarations
    
- Storage liability
    
- Contraband
    
- Insurance
    
- Search authority
    
- Taxation
    
- Whether pocket contents exist within regional jurisdiction
    
- Whether spilled contents from an unconscious traveler constitute abandoned property
    

Chiropterid advocates maintain that the pocket is part of the person.

Vinyoti officials generally agree until substantial revenue becomes involved.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Chiropterids are often associated with liminal rites, dream interpretation, planar theology, confession, death vigils, and the study of spaces between defined states.

Some Arnerian traditions regard them as living evidence that creation extends beyond the material world.

Others distrust a people whose ancestral society developed with little inherited religious authority.

Chiropterids frequently find Arnerian ritual fascinating. A repeated act given meaning through continuity is nearly the opposite of traditional astralcraft.

Some convert.

Some study.

Some ask enough questions to be removed from the temple.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Chiropterids find cultures already comfortable with improvisation, performance, storms, temporary structures, and rebuilding after change.

Al’Ari settlements often provide elevated walkways, hanging dwellings, flexible interiors, and public spaces adapted for gliding.

Chiropterids participate in aerial performance, Veilriding, storm research, traveling art, salvage, rescue, and the design of temporary cities.

Al’Ar also teaches an important distinction:

A structure may be temporary without being meaningless.

A rebuilt home is not the same home.

It may still belong to the same people.

## VIII. Chiropterids and Delving

Chiropterids are valued as planar sensitives, scouts, gliders, investigators, dream-crafters, storage specialists, and observers of dimensional instability.

Their Astral Echoes allow them to perceive certain creatures and objects phasing through the Astral Plane.

This makes them especially useful in Labyrinths containing:

- Teleportation
    
- Folded space
    
- Ethereal or Astral creatures
    
- Vanishing architecture
    
- Planar doors
    
- Displaced objects
    
- False walls created through dimensional overlap
    
- Chambers that exist in several locations at once
    

Their Cheiropockets also allow them to carry supplies without ordinary packs, though the pocket’s limitations must be understood carefully.

Living creatures cannot be stored.

Oversized objects cannot be forced inside.

If the Chiropterid loses consciousness, the pocket collapses and releases everything it contains.

### The Problem of Down

Chiropterids raised in the Astral Sea can become disoriented in chambers where gravity changes.

They may adapt more quickly than material-born companions when direction becomes flexible, but they may also trust that flexibility too readily.

The Labyrinth can create:

- Several competing directions of gravity
    
- Rooms where gravity responds to thought
    
- Surfaces that become floors only when touched
    
- False falling sensations
    
- Astral drift disguised as ordinary flight
    
- Spaces where choosing a direction causes another person to fall
    

Experienced Chiropterids are taught not to assume that familiar unreality is safe.

### Echo Discipline

Chiropterid delving schools teach **Echo Discipline**.

Before relying upon a sound, the delver considers:

1. What produced it?
    
2. What surface returned it?
    
3. How long did it take to return?
    
4. Did it come back unchanged?
    
5. What else might have heard the original call?
    

A useful echo reveals space.

A repeated voice may also reveal the caller.

### Pocket Failure

Delving parties are instructed to prepare for Cheiropocket collapse.

Critical supplies should not all be stored with one Chiropterid. Fragile objects should be wrapped. Dangerous substances should be secured against sudden release. The location where the bearer falls should be cleared quickly.

The Ministry adopted these rules after several incidents involving alchemical reagents, loose caltrops, ceremonial glass, and one deeply unfortunate collection of live-looking but technically nonliving puppets.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Chiropterid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Charisma score increases by 2, and your Dexterity score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Chiropterids commonly stand between 4 feet 7 inches and 5 feet 7 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 30 feet.

### Cheiropocket

You maintain a tethered pocket of the Astral Sea linked to your wing membrane.

As a bonus action, you can open or close the Cheiropocket. While it is open, you can stow or retrieve one item as an action.

The pocket can contain a total volume in cubic feet equal to:

`12 × your Intelligence modifier`

The minimum capacity is 12 cubic feet.

The pocket can hold only nonliving material. Living creatures and objects too large to fit pass through the opening harmlessly.

If you are reduced to 0 hit points, the Cheiropocket collapses and deposits all its contents into the nearest unoccupied spaces around you.

The pocket also collapses and deposits its contents when you enter the Astral Sea.

### Wings

Outside the Astral Sea, your wings permit controlled gliding rather than sustained flight.

While you are unencumbered and not wearing heavy armor, you can spread your wings when you begin falling. Your descent slows to 30 feet per round, you take no falling damage while your wings remain functional, and you can move 1 foot horizontally for every 1 foot you descend.

While in the Astral Sea, you instead gain a flying speed of 40 feet and can hover.

### Astral Echoes

Your voice resonates across planar boundaries.

As a bonus action, you emit an astral tone that is silent in the Material Plane but thunderous within the Astral Sea.

Until the end of your next turn, any creature or object within 120 feet of you that is currently phasing into, out of, or through the Astral Plane becomes visible to you.

While you are in the Astral Sea, using this trait instead grants you blindsight out to 120 feet until the end of your next turn.

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Chiropterid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Flying Fox Sublineage

Flying Fox Chiropterids are among the largest members of the lineage.

They commonly possess long muzzles, broad wings, large eyes, and dense fur in black, brown, rust, gold, grey, cream, or combinations of several colors.

Many ancestral Flying Fox sanctuaries cultivated astral fruit, flowering structures, and drifting gardens.

Their communities frequently emphasize cultivation, long-distance social bonds, and the creation of beautiful communal spaces.

The name does not indicate any relationship to Vulpine Beastfolk.

Questions regarding this matter are common and rarely interesting.

### Vampire Bat Sublineage

Vampire Bat Chiropterids are generally compact, warm-adapted, and capable of moving with unusual agility along solid surfaces.

Their muzzles are short, their ears sensitive, and their bodies suited to rapid terrestrial movement as well as gliding.

Their Astral ancestors consumed concentrated currents of vitalized astra. This has produced persistent myths that material Vampire Chiropterids must feed upon blood.

Most can eat ordinary food.

Some communities incorporate small amounts of blood into traditional diets or rites. Participation is neither universal nor evidence of predatory intent.

The assumption that every Vampire Chiropterid is dangerous has caused far more harm than their actual dietary practices.

### Leaf-Nosed and Horseshoe Bat Sublineages

Leaf-Nosed and Horseshoe Bat Chiropterids possess elaborate structures around the nose and face that assist with directing sound and astral resonance.

Their forms vary enormously, with fur and membranes appearing in brown, grey, black, cream, gold, orange, or patterned combinations.

Many are highly skilled at distinguishing overlapping echoes.

Their ancestral sanctuaries often contained dense architecture, hanging gardens, and intricately folded chambers through which sound traveled along deliberate paths.

### Long-Eared Bat Sublineage

Long-Eared Chiropterids possess exceptionally large and mobile ears.

They are highly sensitive to subtle sound and commonly develop careful practices concerning noise, privacy, and sensory boundaries.

Their communities often favor soft materials, layered curtains, sound-shaped rooms, and quiet communal spaces.

Outsiders sometimes assume Long-Eared Chiropterids can hear every nearby conversation.

They cannot.

They also do not owe anyone an account of what they did hear.

### Fishing Bat Sublineage

Fishing Bat Chiropterids possess strong feet, long claws, and bodies adapted to movement near water.

Their material communities commonly settle around rivers, lakes, coasts, reservoirs, and cavern pools. Many become capable swimmers despite their lineage’s Astral origins.

Fishing Bat traditions often place emphasis upon timing, surface awareness, and the ability to seize a brief opportunity without losing control of one’s flight.

They possess close relationships with Anatid, Pelagid, and some Aquaticfolk communities.

### Ghost Bat Sublineage

Ghost Bat Chiropterids commonly possess pale grey, cream, white, or silver fur and broad wings.

Their ancestral Astral sanctuaries are believed to have existed near unusually thin planar boundaries, giving rise to strong traditions of veil-listening and dimensional observation.

Their appearance has produced associations with death, spirits, and prophecy.

Some embrace those roles.

Others have no interest in explaining for the hundredth time that being pale and nocturnal does not make one a message from the dead.

### Free-Tailed Bat Sublineage

Free-Tailed Chiropterids possess long, narrow wings and tails extending beyond the flight membrane.

They are often swift gliders and excel at launching from open heights.

Their communities commonly favor large roosts, broad aerial chambers, and coordinated group movement.

Many modern Free-Tailed Chiropterids participate in Veilriding, aerial racing, courier work, and the design of artificial launch towers.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

Choose one Homeland Imprint representing the place or plane that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Astral Sea — Practiced Astralcrafter

You add your proficiency bonus to Intelligence checks made to manipulate raw astra, understand astralcraft, or interact with astral-infused material.

If you are already proficient in the applicable skill, you add twice your proficiency bonus instead.

Astral-born Chiropterids learn that matter is not merely worked.

It is persuaded into becoming useful.

### Allemance — Kept Place

You gain proficiency in the Insight skill.

When a creature you can see within 30 feet of you makes a saving throw against being charmed or frightened, you can use your reaction to add your proficiency bonus to the roll.

You can use this reaction a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

Allemancian Chiropterids learn that belonging is not the absence of change.

It is the choice to keep making room for someone.

### Oria — Fixed Reference

You gain proficiency in the Investigation skill.

You also have advantage on saving throws made to resist being unwillingly teleported or transported to another plane.

Oric Chiropterids are taught to establish what remains reliable before attempting to revise what does not.

### Vinyot — Pocket Appraisal

You gain proficiency in the Investigation or Persuasion skill.

After examining a nonmagical object for 1 minute, you can determine its approximate market value, whether it has been visibly altered to misrepresent that value, and whether it will fit safely within your Cheiropocket.

Vinyoti Chiropterids quickly learn that portable space is valuable.

They learn even faster that someone will attempt to tax it.

### Arneria — Veil Cant

You know the _minor illusion_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _detect magic_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using echo, shadow, or a thin place between planes as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Chiropterids treat the boundary between worlds as both theological question and open door.

They disagree over which is more dangerous.

### Al’Ar — Improvised Descent

When using your Wings to glide, you can move up to 2 feet horizontally for every 1 foot you descend.

In addition, when you begin gliding, you can choose one willing creature within 5 feet of you. If that creature is no larger than you and you have at least one free hand, you can carry it during the glide.

Once you carry another creature in this way, you cannot do so again until you finish a long rest.

Al’Ari Chiropterids learn to make a route from whatever height, wind, rope, roof, and bad decision remain available.

## XIII. Playing a Chiropterid

When creating a Chiropterid character, consider:

- Were they born in the Astral Sea or Aerathon?
    
- How do they feel about fixed gravity?
    
- Do they consider gliding a loss, a challenge, or a new kind of freedom?
    
- What do they keep in their Cheiropocket?
    
- Who is permitted to know its contents?
    
- What part of material scarcity do they struggle to understand?
    
- What ordinary object fascinates them?
    
- What creation do they believe should have been allowed to disappear?
    
- What vanished thing do they wish had been preserved?
    
- Do they value novelty or continuity?
    
- How comfortable are they sleeping alone?
    
- What sounds make them feel safe?
    
- What sounds overwhelm them?
    
- Have they ever heard something through the Astral veil that they could not explain?
    
- Do they regard themselves as Avianfolk?
    
- How do they feel about being grouped with feathered lineages?
    
- Do they believe Chiropterids belong within the Sky-Reeve legacy?
    
- Would they ride the Fifth Current?
    
- What object would they leave behind as a Tithe of Weight?
    
- Which version of themselves are they afraid of becoming permanently?
    

A Chiropterid may be imaginative or rigid, communal or solitary, playful or deeply practical.

Their lineage comes from a world where reality could be revised.

Their choices determine what they preserve when it cannot.
