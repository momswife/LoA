# Avianfolk Overview

**Ministry of Delving Operations — The Living Atlas**  
**Folk Classification:** Avianfolk  
**Registry Scope:** Aerial Peoples, Recognized Lineages, and Cultural Identity  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Determined by Lineage and Individual Capability  
**Date of Revised Filing:** 7th Cycle, 3388 A.D.

---

## I. Classification Overview

**Avianfolk** is the Ministry’s broad classification for sapient peoples whose bodies, histories, settlements, or cultures have been fundamentally shaped by aerial movement.

The name is older than the Ministry’s present understanding of it.

Avianfolk are not simply birdfolk.

The classification includes:

- Feathered peoples capable of sustained flight
    
- Waterfowl and ocean-ranging aerial peoples
    
- Membranous-winged mammalian flyers
    
- Mammalian gliders
    
- Moisture-dependent amphibious and reptilian gliders
    
- Chitinous and insect-like winged peoples
    
- Astralborn peoples whose flight changes between planes
    
- Individuals and communities whose aerial identity persists despite injury, disability, age, or physiological variance
    

These peoples do not share one biological ancestor.

They possess no universal language, faith, government, diet, migration pattern, or relationship with the sky. Some resemble familiar birds. Others resemble bats, gliding mammals, frogs, salamanders, serpents, moths, butterflies, cicadas, and beetles.

Their classification reflects a shared historical truth:

Aerial movement changes how a people understand distance, architecture, privacy, danger, community, and the world beneath them.

Not every Avianfolk person can fly.

Not every Avianfolk person wishes to.

Flight does not determine legitimacy within the classification.

---

## II. The Meaning of Avianfolk

The modern term **Avianfolk** descends from several old Sky-Reeve phrases commonly translated as _those who belong to the currents_.

Early Ministry registries interpreted those phrases too narrowly.

Chiropterids were classified as planar anomalies.

Petaurids were divided between Beastfolk and flight-impaired Avianfolk.

Limnids were alternately recorded as Beastfolk, Aquaticfolk, or biological curiosities.

Pterygotids were separated according to whichever temple, industry, or authority sought to regulate them.

The modern Living Atlas rejects that approach.

Avianfolk classification now considers:

- The role of aerial movement in daily life
    
- Wing, membrane, patagial, webbed, or gliding anatomy
    
- Vertical settlement patterns
    
- Historical participation in aerial societies
    
- Cultural relationships with wind, height, migration, or descent
    
- Access requirements created by aerial physiology
    
- Established kinship with other aerial peoples
    
- The individual or community’s own identity
    

### Aerial Peoples, Not Merely Flying Peoples

A person need not possess sustained powered flight to be Avianfolk.

Petaurids and Limnids glide.

Most Chiropterids glide within Aerathon but fly naturally in the Astral Sea.

Some Avianfolk lineages are equally adapted to water, forest, cliff, or subterranean environments.

Flight may also be limited by:

- Injury
    
- Age
    
- Illness
    
- Disability
    
- Armor
    
- Metamorphic variance
    
- Environmental exposure
    
- Individual anatomy
    
- Personal choice
    

Aerial identity may remain culturally important even when flight is absent.

A person raised among canopy roads, cliff dwellings, migration routes, hanging districts, high roosts, or Sky-Reeve traditions may understand the world through height without ever leaving the ground.

### Self-Identification

Some qualifying peoples reject the Avianfolk label.

They may identify more strongly with:

- Beastfolk
    
- Aquaticfolk
    
- Astralborn communities
    
- Their particular lineage
    
- Their homeland
    
- Their household Wind
    
- A city, village, clan, roost, or nation
    
- No folk classification at all
    

The Ministry recognizes these preferences where possible.

Classification should secure recognition and accommodation.

It should not erase how a person names themselves.

## III. Understanding Avianfolk Identity

Avianfolk identity is best understood through several separate categories.

|Identity|Meaning|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Folk Classification**|The broad registry grouping|Avianfolk|
|**Lineage**|A major physiological and historical people|Corvid|
|**Sublineage**|A hereditary branch within the lineage|Raven|
|**Homeland**|The region that shaped the individual|Allemance|
|**Cardinal Wind**|The household’s inherited Sky-Reeve allegiance|West Cyclone|
|**Community**|Household, clan, guild, roost, chorus, or village|A Glasrún route-house|
|**Profession**|The work performed by the individual|Investigator|

A person might therefore be described as:

> A Raven Corvid of Glasrún, born beneath the West Cyclone.

Or:

> A South Zephyr Moth Pterygotid raised in southeastern Vinyot.

Or:

> A North Gale Swan Anatid serving an Oric House.

Lineage describes inherited physiology and broad cultural history.

Homeland describes the region in which a person was raised.

Wind describes the household, clan, and Sky-Reeve tradition to which they belong.

None of these determines personality, morality, faith, or profession.

## IV. Recognized Avianfolk Lineages

The Living Atlas currently recognizes eleven major Avianfolk lineages.

### [[Raptorid|Raptorid]]

Predatory feathered Avianfolk descended from eagles, hawks, falcons, vultures, ospreys, and related aerial hunters.

Raptorid cultures frequently engage with strength, vigilance, leadership, decisive action, and the responsibility created by physical power.

They are especially numerous beneath the North Gale and East Tempest.

### [[Corvids|Corvid]]

Feathered Avianfolk descended from ravens, crows, rooks, magpies, jays, jackdaws, choughs, nutcrackers, and related peoples.

Corvid communities are strongly associated with memory, information, reciprocity, social intelligence, and adaptation.

They are common beneath the North Gale and West Cyclone.

### [[Passerid|Passerid]]

Light-framed feathered Avianfolk descended from sparrows, finches, swallows, starlings, larks, wrens, thrushes, nightingales, and related perching birds.

Passerid cultures frequently emphasize communication, communal response, music, coordination, and the power of a voice that receives an answer.

They are widespread, with their largest historic populations beneath the South Zephyr and West Cyclone.

### [[Strigid|Strigid]]

Nocturnal feathered Avianfolk descended from owls and related twilight hunters.

Strigid traditions often explore patience, silence, guarded knowledge, observation, memory, and the responsibility to act upon what has been noticed.

They are most heavily concentrated beneath the North Gale.

### [[Psittacid|Psittacid]]

Powerful-beaked Avianfolk descended from parrots, macaws, cockatoos, kea, lories, parakeets, and related peoples.

Psittacid cultures frequently engage with memory, mimicry, curiosity, public presence, reinvention, and the preservation of spoken history.

They are most numerous beneath the South Zephyr and East Tempest.

### [[Anatid|Anatid]]

Water-linked feathered Avianfolk descended from ducks, geese, swans, loons, grebes, and related waterfowl.

Anatid cultures are shaped by migration, layered belonging, communal defense, shore rights, returning routes, and the sharing of burdens within a formation.

They possess major populations beneath the North Gale and South Zephyr, with smaller West Cyclone communities along migration and trade routes.

### [[Pelagid]]

Ocean-ranging feathered Avianfolk descended from albatrosses, gulls, terns, pelicans, cormorants, frigatebirds, puffins, and related seabirds.

Pelagid traditions frequently concern endurance, navigation, refuge, long absence, weather-reading, and the cost of crossing immense distances.

They are most strongly associated with the West Cyclone.

### [[Chiropterid|Chiropterid]]

Membranous-winged Astralborn peoples resembling bats.

Chiropterids originated within the Astral Sea, where they possess true flight and shape raw _astra_. Within Aerathon, most glide rather than sustaining natural powered flight.

Because their permanent Material settlements are comparatively recent, Chiropterid households commonly adopt the Wind of the Aerathian territory, clan, or spouse with whom they settle.

Large communities exist beneath the West Cyclone and within Bat’yan.

### [[Petaurid|Petaurid]]

Mammalian gliders descended from sugar gliders, flying squirrels, colugos, feathertail gliders, greater gliders, anomalures, and related peoples.

Petaurid cultures frequently emphasize preparation, balance, trusted landing places, route maintenance, and the difference between courage and leaping without a plan.

They are most common beneath the South Zephyr and West Cyclone.

### [[Limnid|Limnid]]

Moisture-dependent gliding peoples descended from flying frogs, wandering salamanders, arboreal salamanders, newts, flying snakes, and related canopy peoples.

Limnid traditions concern environmental sensitivity, renewal, controlled retreat, water stewardship, and movement between wet earth and open air.

They are heavily concentrated beneath the South Zephyr and East Tempest.

### [[Pterygotid|Pterygotid]]

Chitinous or insect-like aerial peoples descended from moths, butterflies, cicadas, dragonflies, bees, beetles, and related winged forms.

Pterygotid cultures frequently engage with metamorphosis, seasonal cycles, collective labor, sensory intensity, emergence, and bodily ownership.

Their largest populations live beneath the South Zephyr and East Tempest.

## V. Shared Aerial Experience

Avianfolk possess no single shared culture.

They do, however, encounter several recurring realities.

### Height

Height may function as:

- A road
    
- A workplace
    
- A border
    
- A refuge
    
- A private space
    
- A source of social status
    
- A homeland
    
- A barrier
    

A roof may be a street.

A tower may be a neighborhood.

A branch may be public infrastructure.

A ground-level door may be the rear entrance to an Avianfolk home.

### Airspace and Landing Rights

Open sky does not guarantee a lawful or safe place to land.

Airspace may be:

- Owned
    
- Taxed
    
- Restricted
    
- Patrolled
    
- Militarized
    
- Closed during storms
    
- Denied to particular lineages
    
- Rendered inaccessible through lack of landing sites
    

Modern Avianfolk political movements frequently concern:

- Public landing rights
    
- Emergency refuge
    
- Rooftop access
    
- Migration routes
    
- Vertical property boundaries
    
- Aerial trespass
    
- Artificial wind
    
- Tower construction
    
- Protection of nesting, roosting, and emergence grounds
    
- Access for gliding and flightless peoples
    

An aerial city can reproduce every inequality of a ground city.

Its walls are simply less visible.

### Wing and Membrane Care

Feathers, membranes, patagia, chitinous wings, gliding webbing, antennae, and other aerial structures require different forms of care.

These may include:

- Preening
    
- Oiling
    
- Membrane salves
    
- Chitin treatment
    
- Temperature control
    
- Humidity
    
- Drying
    
- Stretching
    
- Molting assistance
    
- Recovery after emergence
    

Touching another person’s wings without permission is widely considered inappropriate.

In some cultures it is intimate.

In others it is equivalent to seizing an arm.

Consent should be obtained in either case.

### Clothing and Equipment

Conventional clothing and armor may obstruct wings, membranes, tails, antennae, or gliding structures.

Avianfolk equipment may require:

- Open or articulated backs
    
- Wing-root protection
    
- Flexible side panels
    
- Membrane-safe edges
    
- Tail openings
    
- Balanced weight
    
- Reduced drag
    
- Metamorphic resizing
    
- Chitin-compatible straps
    
- Quick-release mechanisms
    

Poorly fitted armor may be more dangerous than no armor at all.

### Disability and Flight Loss

Flight loss may occur through injury, age, illness, developmental variance, environmental exposure, magical harm, or deliberate restraint.

Its emotional significance varies.

For one person, it may be a profound loss.

For another, flight may never have been central to their identity.

The Ministry recognizes both aerial accessibility and ground accessibility as essential to Avianfolk life.

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Legacy

The greatest shared historical influence upon Avianfolk culture is the legacy of the [[The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]].

The Sky-Reeves were a vast aerial civilization composed of numerous lineages, clans, settlements, guilds, pilots, farmers, engineers, navigators, ritualists, and laborers.

Their society was divided among four cardinal currents:

- North Gale
    
- South Zephyr
    
- East Tempest
    
- West Cyclone
    

Each current was both an atmospheric system and a territorial jurisdiction.

Its clans maintained aerial routes, settlements, weather stations, and ritual obligations through offerings known as **Wind-Tithes**.

The Sky-Reeve clans were traditionally matrilineal and led by **Wind-Mothers**, priest-engineers responsible for preserving the balance of their current. Beneath them served **Reeves**, who administered routes, settlements, Tithes, and sky-law.

The most dangerous offerings were carried into storms by oath-pilots who eventually became known as **Stormriders**.

Their surviving oath remains one of the most famous lines in Avianfolk history:

> _“If I fall, let the sky remember my name in thunder.”_

When Labyrinth anomalies began distorting Aerathon’s weather, the Eyrie-Spire of Taellen broke apart within a storm of its own making.

The surviving Sky-Reeve clans scattered across Aerathon.

Their descendants no longer govern the weather.

They still carry the Four Winds through household, ancestry, marriage, ritual, and memory.

## VII. The Four Cardinal Winds

Modern Wind identity descends from the territorial confederations of the ancient Sky-Reeve Clans.

A Wind represents:

- Household ancestry
    
- Regional culture
    
- Clan allegiance
    
- Inherited rites
    
- Marriage and descent
    
- Burial traditions
    
- Historic routes and aeries
    
- Political and familial obligations
    

A Wind does not determine personality, morality, profession, or faith.

Most Avianfolk households formally belong to one Wind. Children ordinarily inherit the Household Wind, while cross-Wind marriages may require one spouse to **Turn the Wing** and join the other’s current.

The Wind territories follow ancient settlement, migration, and aerial routes rather than perfect compass divisions.

### North Gale

The North Gale is strongest throughout Oria and northern Allemance.

Its households descend from cold coasts, fortified passes, mountain aeries, taiga forests, tundra lakes, and northern migration grounds.

North Gale traditions emphasize preparation, continuity, shelter, memory, and the belief that hardship must never be allowed to erase those who endured it.

Other Winds often stereotype North Gale Avianfolk as severe, suspicious, overly traditional, and proud of suffering.

North Gale households answer:

> “Someone must keep the shelter standing.”

### South Zephyr

The South Zephyr is strongest throughout southern and southeastern Vinyot, Bat’yan, and warm portions of Al’Ar.

Its households descend from rainforests, flowering valleys, river deltas, mangrove coasts, tropical islands, fertile wetlands, and cultivated portlands.

South Zephyr traditions emphasize abundance, kinship, hospitality, cultivation, courtship, beauty, and the obligation to share what cannot be preserved forever.

Other Winds often stereotype South Zephyr Avianfolk as extravagant, flirtatious, indirect, and skilled at hiding obligation beneath generosity.

South Zephyr households answer:

> “A guarded hearth is useless if no one is fed beside it.”

### East Tempest

The East Tempest is strongest throughout Arneria and Bat’yan.

Its households descend from scorched plains, volcanic highlands, lightning deserts, monsoon forests, storm-facing mountains, seasonal wetlands, and Astral-thin clearings.

East Tempest traditions emphasize oath, trial, decisive action, personal transformation, and readiness before danger.

Other Winds often stereotype East Tempest Avianfolk as reckless, proud, confrontational, and addicted to public displays of courage.

East Tempest households answer:

> “A warning spoken after the lightning strikes is only an obituary.”

### West Cyclone

The West Cyclone is the most geographically scattered Wind.

Its households appear throughout portions of Allemance, Vinyot, and Al’Ar, with deep cultural authority in Glasrún despite that forest’s northeastern location.

West Cyclone communities follow ports, islands, rivers, trade corridors, migration routes, coastal forests, and settlements founded by traveling Sky-Reeve clans.

Its traditions emphasize movement, independence, adaptability, exploration, and the expectation that departure need not end belonging.

Other Winds often stereotype West Cyclone Avianfolk as rootless, unreliable, opportunistic, and more loyal to routes than people.

West Cyclone households answer:

> “The road home matters only because someone first dared to leave it.”

### Bat’yan

Bat’yan is the principal meeting ground of the South Zephyr and East Tempest.

South and East households may live in neighboring villages, share canopy roads, trade together, and intermarry while retaining distinct Wind allegiances.

The region possesses no single Wind identity.

Each household still formally belongs to one current.

### Glasrún

Glasrún belongs culturally to the West Cyclone despite its northeastern location.

Its communities resist imposed authority, maintain roads collectively, build for adaptation rather than expansion, and place local stewardship above distant claim.

Glasrún lies beneath northern skies, but its oldest Avianfolk households look westward.

For complete territorial history, household customs, rivalries, and Wind stereotypes, see [[The Four Cardinal Winds|The Four Cardinal Winds]].

## VIII. Household Wind and Marriage

Most Avianfolk households formally belong to one Wind.

A person may move across Aerathon without changing that allegiance. A North Gale household living in southern Vinyot remains North Gale unless it formally joins another current.

Children ordinarily inherit the Household Wind.

Under old Sky-Reeve law, this was usually the mother’s Wind because the clans were matrilineal. Modern couples may decide which spouse’s Wind the household will carry, but the established household is ordinarily recognized beneath one current.

Cross-Wind marriages may require one partner to **Turn the Wing**.

The person who Turns formally enters the Wind of their spouse and accepts its household rights, obligations, and inheritance.

A traditional vow reads:

> “The wind that raised me shall always know my name. But yours is the wind beneath which I choose to return.”

The complete rite is recorded in [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/I. Annals & Antiquities/Ancient Peoples & Culture/IV. Shared Customs & Ways/Birth, Bond, & Burial#Turning the Wing|Birth, Bond, & Burial — Turning the Wing]].

## IX. The Fifth Current

Some Chiropterid scholars and Veilriders use the term **Fifth Current** to describe movement between Aerathon and the Astral Sea.

The Ministry does not recognize it as one of the four ancient territorial Winds.

It possesses no authenticated ancient Wind-Mother, territorial jurisdiction, or verified section of the Ledger of Gales.

It is instead recognized as a modern Chiropterid philosophy concerning:

- Planar passage
    
- Liminal identity
    
- Mutable direction
    
- Belonging between worlds
    
- Reality shaped through intention
    

A Chiropterid household may formally belong to one of the Four Winds while privately honoring the Fifth Current.

The debate remains unresolved.

## X. Avianfolk and Delving

Avianfolk are strongly represented among delvers.

Aerial mobility may provide advantages within the Labyrinths, including:

- Crossing gaps
    
- Reaching vertical passages
    
- Scouting from height
    
- Avoiding floor hazards
    
- Navigating flooded chambers
    
- Detecting pressure changes
    
- Using sound and echo
    
- Reaching concealed platforms
    
- Responding quickly to separated companions
    

These advantages do not make aerial delving safe.

The Labyrinth frequently exploits the assumptions of flying and gliding peoples through:

- False skies
    
- Moving horizons
    
- Sudden gravity
    
- Invisible ceilings
    
- Wind without a source
    
- Disappearing landing surfaces
    
- Corridors too narrow to extend wings
    
- Magical storms
    
- Reflections resembling open space
    
- Signals copied by hostile entities
    

A flying delver may bypass one danger and enter another beyond the reach of the party.

A glider may cross a gap without preserving a route back.

A scout may discover danger before anyone can help them.

MDO doctrine therefore requires aerial movement to be integrated into group planning rather than treated as an individual solution to every obstacle.

The Ministry does not assign delver roles according to Wind.

North Gale members are not automatically rear guards.

South Zephyr members are not automatically healers.

East Tempest members are not automatically combatants.

West Cyclone members are not automatically scouts.

Such assumptions confuse regional reputation with individual capability.

## XI. Common Misconceptions

### “All Avianfolk are birds.”

False.

Chiropterids, Petaurids, Limnids, and Pterygotids demonstrate the breadth of the classification.

### “All Avianfolk can fly.”

False.

Some glide. Some fly only in particular environments. Some are flightless through anatomy, disability, injury, age, or choice.

### “The Four Winds divide Aerathon into equal compass regions.”

False.

Their territories follow historic Sky-Reeve settlement, climate, migration, and aerial routes.

### “Every person living in a Wind territory belongs to that Wind.”

False.

Migrant households may preserve another Wind for generations.

### “A household normally follows several Winds.”

False.

Most households formally belong to one current, though their members may possess ancestry from several.

### “Turning the Wing erases someone’s ancestry.”

False.

It changes formal household allegiance. It does not erase birth, family, memory, or cultural inheritance.

### “A person’s Wind determines their personality.”

False.

Wind describes regional and familial identity.

### “Stormriders represent all Avianfolk history.”

False.

They represent its most celebrated pilots.

Sky-Reeve civilization depended equally upon workers, growers, builders, navigators, healers, signalers, and households that maintained the routes beneath them.

## XII. Creating an Avianfolk Character

When creating an Avianfolk character, consider each layer of identity separately.

### Lineage

- What lineage and sublineage are they?
    
- How does their body move through air, water, forest, or vertical space?
    
- How do they care for their wings, membrane, chitin, webbing, or plumage?
    
- How important is flight or gliding to them?
    

### Homeland

- Where were they raised?
    
- Did they grow up in a vertical city, canopy settlement, coastal aerie, or ordinary ground community?
    
- How did the region accommodate or restrict aerial peoples?
    
- What laws governed landing, migration, or airspace?
    

### Wind

- What is their Household Wind?
    
- Does their family still live within its traditional territory?
    
- What reputation does their Wind possess?
    
- What rite, route, aerie, or obligation connects them to the Sky-Reeves?
    
- Are they from Bat’yan, where South and East meet?
    
- Are they from Glasrún, where northern geography carries a Western Wind?
    

### Household

- Who holds authority within their household?
    
- What Wind will their children inherit?
    
- Has anyone in their family Turned the Wing?
    
- Was that choice celebrated, mourned, or condemned?
    
- What would the character lose by leaving their Wind?
    

### Personal Identity

- What stereotype do others apply to them?
    
- What part of their lineage or Wind culture do they resist?
    
- What inherited practice do they preserve?
    
- Who taught them how to move through the air?
    
- Who catches them when they fall?
    
- What does home mean when the road to it can change?
    

An Avianfolk character is not defined merely by wings.

They are shaped by the current that carried their ancestors, the household that waits for their return, and the sky beneath which they choose to build a future.

> [!note] MDO Registry Advisory  
> Avianfolk classification does not indicate universal flight capability, migratory obligation, emotional temperament, professional suitability, religious affiliation, Sky-Reeve descent, or adherence to a Cardinal Wind.
> 
> Cardinal Wind identity is a protected cultural and familial affiliation. It must not be treated as evidence of personality, criminal intent, political reliability, professional aptitude, or delver suitability.
> 
> Ministry personnel are forbidden from assigning aerial scouting, courier, rescue, weather, pollination, performance, hazardous-height, or wing-dependent labor solely on the basis of classification, lineage, or Wind.
> 
> Feathers, wing membranes, patagia, antennae, chitin, gliding webbing, shed material, biological compounds, and developmental casings remain part of the citizen’s body or personal property unless voluntarily transferred through informed consent.
> 
> Turning the Wing must be entered freely. Coerced Wind transfer, fraudulent marriage registration, denial of recognized household status, or punishment imposed solely for cross-Wind marriage constitutes a protected-rights violation.
> 
> Reasonable accommodations may include aerial entrances, ground-accessible routes, landing platforms, humidity control, temperature support, sensory protection, adapted armor, molting or emergence leave, and safe sleeping arrangements.
