# Passerid

**Ministry of Delving Operations — The Living Atlas**  
**Folk Classification:** [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/Avianfolk Overview|Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Passerid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage and Homeland Variance  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Unrestricted  
**Common Names:** Songbirds, Chorusfolk, Perchers  
**Date of Filing:** 1st Cycle, 3387 A.D.

---

## I. Lineage Overview

**Passerids** are an Avianfolk lineage distinguished by lightweight aerial forms, highly developed vocal control, rapid social communication, and a long history of communal song.

Their recognized sublineages include sparrows, finches, canaries, swallows, martins, starlings, larks, wrens, thrushes, nightingales, and many other small or medium-bodied perching birds.

Passerids are often described as cheerful, sociable, musical, and emotionally transparent.

These descriptions are convenient.

They are not complete.

Passerid cultures have developed around the power of a voice to coordinate people separated by distance, obstruction, danger, or darkness. A call can warn of a predator, guide travelers through fog, maintain formation during flight, carry news between rooftops, steady frightened companions, or tell a grieving household that it has not been forgotten.

Song is important to many Passerids.

So are silence, listening, breath, timing, and the choice not to answer.

A Passerid is not required to be pleasant, outgoing, talented, or loud. Some possess beautiful voices. Others speak harshly, sing poorly, communicate through rhythm rather than melody, or prefer long periods of quiet.

What many Passerid communities share is not a love of performance.

It is an understanding that sound can hold people together.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Passerids possess no single accepted place of origin.

Their oldest settlements have been identified in grasslands, temperate forests, river valleys, coastal cliffs, agricultural regions, dense cities, and migratory routes crossing much of Aerathon.

Some Passerid traditions claim their ancestors were the first people to answer the dawn.

Others teach that the first Passerid song was not a song at all, but a warning shouted from one tree to another until an entire forest survived a fire.

A third tradition states that Passerids learned music from the wind but taught the wind how to remember words.

These stories differ in form, but they share a common principle:

A voice becomes meaningful when someone else receives it.

### The Carried History

Passerid history is frequently preserved through repeated performance.

A historical song may encode:

- A migration route
    
- The timing of seasonal winds
    
- The names of lost settlements
    
- Instructions for crossing dangerous terrain
    
- The order of events during a disaster
    
- A family lineage
    
- The terms of an ancient promise
    
- A warning concealed beneath a familiar melody
    

The meaning of such pieces may change depending upon who performs them, where they are sung, and which verses are omitted.

This has occasionally frustrated Ministry historians who expect a single authoritative version.

Passerid scholars argue that variation is not always corruption.

A song carried through five regions may gather the memory of each place that kept it alive.

### Call and Answer

Many Passerid historical traditions use a structure known as **call and answer**.

One person begins the account. Others respond with names, dates, corrections, remembered verses, or affirmations. No single speaker is expected to carry the whole history alone.

In older communities, deliberately refusing to answer a communal call could serve as a public act of protest.

Responding with a changed verse could signal disagreement without interrupting the ceremony outright.

This practice remains common in political gatherings, funerals, festivals, work crews, and military formations.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Passerids are generally slender, lightweight, and agile.

Most stand between **5 feet and 6 feet** tall. Their wings are commonly adapted for rapid takeoff, frequent turns, short bursts of speed, and sustained movement between closely spaced landing places.

Many Passerids are more comfortable navigating streets, forests, rafters, and crowded vertical environments than maintaining long-distance soaring flight.

Their beaks vary greatly by sublineage. Some are narrow and suited to precise work. Others are short, conical, or strong enough to crack hard seeds and materials.

Passerid feet are adapted for gripping branches, rails, ropes, wires, beams, and other narrow surfaces. Many can rest comfortably in places that would require considerable balance from larger peoples.

### Plumage

Passerid plumage displays extraordinary variety.

Common patterns include:

- Brown, grey, cream, or rust mottling
    
- Brilliant yellow, red, orange, or blue markings
    
- Black masks or throat patches
    
- Striped wings
    
- Speckled chests
    
- Iridescent greens, purples, or blues
    
- Seasonal changes in brightness
    
- Distinctive crests, tails, or facial markings
    

Some Passerid cultures treat bright plumage as a source of pride. Others prefer subdued colors and consider highly decorative dress excessive.

Neither approach is universal.

### Voice

Passerids possess finely controlled breathing and vocal anatomy.

Many can sustain complex melodies, shift pitch rapidly, project over long distances, or produce calls that remain recognizable amid noise.

This does not mean every Passerid is a trained singer.

Vocal skill requires practice. Injury, illness, age, anxiety, magical effects, and personal preference all influence how a person uses their voice.

Some Passerids are nonverbal or communicate primarily through sign, percussion, written language, feather position, or magical means.

These individuals remain fully part of Passerid culture.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Passerid communities frequently organize themselves through households, neighborhood circles, work choruses, migration companies, performance troupes, temples, and mutual-aid networks.

The term **chorus** may refer to:

- A singing group
    
- A household alliance
    
- A neighborhood council
    
- A coordinated work crew
    
- A political faction
    
- A group responsible for caring for children or elders
    
- People who have sworn to answer one another in crisis
    

A person may belong to several choruses at once.

Each serves a different purpose.

### The Answering Promise

Many Passerid societies recognize some form of **Answering Promise**.

The precise wording differs, but its meaning is broadly consistent:

When a person calls in true need, those who have accepted the promise must answer if they are able.

Answering does not always mean obeying.

It may mean sending help, acknowledging the danger, warning others, providing shelter, or telling the caller truthfully that assistance cannot come.

Ignoring the call without cause is considered the greater failure.

Answering Promises exist between:

- Family members
    
- Close friends
    
- Military companions
    
- Neighbors
    
- Performers
    
- Travelers
    
- Delving parties
    
- Settlements connected by dangerous routes
    

Some promises last a season.

Others endure for life.

### Communal Rearing

Passerid children are often raised within broad networks of relatives and trusted adults.

Parents may remain central, but instruction in language, flight, music, craft, history, and social responsibility is commonly shared.

Children learn early that different calls require different responses.

They are taught to distinguish:

- Alarm from excitement
    
- Performance from warning
    
- Grief from danger
    
- Private speech from public summons
    
- A call intended for everyone from one meant for a single person
    

These distinctions can be subtle.

Outsiders occasionally mistake ordinary Passerid communication for chaos because several people may speak, sing, click, whistle, or gesture at once.

Within the community, the exchange may be perfectly ordered.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Passerid communities commonly admire:

- Emotional honesty
    
- Attentive listening
    
- Reliability within a group
    
- The courage to speak when silence protects wrongdoing
    
- The restraint to remain quiet when another person needs to be heard
    
- The ability to strengthen others
    
- Adaptability without abandonment of community
    

These values can produce compassionate leaders, gifted coordinators, inspiring artists, and companions who recognize distress before it becomes crisis.

They can also create intense social pressure.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Expressiveness|Communicating needs clearly|Demanding emotional access|
|Community|Mutual protection and support|Conformity and exclusion|
|Responsiveness|Answering when others need help|Inability to establish boundaries|
|Encouragement|Helping others act with confidence|Concealing serious problems beneath optimism|
|Adaptability|Thriving in changing environments|Avoiding difficult commitments|
|Public voice|Challenging silence and injustice|Performing morality for approval|
|Harmony|Coordinating different people|Silencing necessary disagreement|

A recurring Passerid anxiety is the fear of **calling out and receiving no answer**.

For some, this creates deep loyalty and attentiveness.

For others, it produces a constant need for reassurance, approval, or visible acknowledgment. A few become so afraid of being ignored that they dominate every space before silence can settle around them.

### The Burden of Brightness

Passerids are frequently expected to improve the mood of those around them.

A cheerful voice, colorful plumage, or musical talent may cause outsiders to assume that a Passerid is naturally suited to entertainment, encouragement, or emotional care.

Many resent this expectation.

A Passerid may be grieving, angry, tired, private, or simply uninterested in performing comfort for strangers.

Several modern Passerid movements reject what they call **the burden of brightness**: the belief that visible warmth is a duty owed to everyone else.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Passerid communities disagree over:

- Whether traditional songs may be commercially performed
    
- Whether historic verses should be preserved unchanged
    
- Whether silence can be an honorable form of protest
    
- Whether community obligations outweigh individual privacy
    
- Whether Answering Promises should possess legal force
    
- Whether magical amplification weakens or strengthens vocal traditions
    
- Whether nonvocal Passerid practices receive equal cultural recognition
    
- Whether famous performers represent their communities or merely themselves
    

These debates are especially fierce where music, religion, politics, and commerce overlap.

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Passerids held an essential role within the ancient [[The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]].

They served as singers, current-callers, signal relays, ritual witnesses, formation guides, and keepers of the Wind-Tithe hymns.

The surviving Sky-Reeve record frequently celebrates the oath-pilots who carried Crimson Tithes into lightning.

It speaks less often of the voices that guided them home.

### The Wind-Tithe Songs

Each of the four cardinal currents possessed its own ceremonial repertoire.

Passerid singers performed songs intended to match the character of the wind:

- The **North Gale** was answered through low, sustained tones meant to endure across cold distance.
    
- The **South Zephyr** favored layered melodies passed between many voices.
    
- The **East Tempest** used sharp rhythms and rapid calls suited to violent weather.
    
- The **West Cyclone** relied upon repeating phrases that could be altered without breaking the whole.
    

These songs served ritual purposes, but they also carried practical information.

Changes in pitch, timing, or verse could warn pilots of:

- Shifting pressure
    
- Lightning movement
    
- Broken formations
    
- Unsafe approach angles
    
- Missing riders
    
- Failing equipment
    
- The location of sheltered descent
    

To give to the storm was to remain remembered by it.

To sing into the storm was to help the living remember one another.

### Current-Callers

Passerid **Current-Callers** occupied elevated stations throughout the Eyrie-Spire of Taellen and along major flight routes.

They relayed information across distances too great for ordinary speech. In clear conditions, a message could pass from station to station faster than a ground courier could cross a single district.

Current-Callers were selected not merely for volume, but for precision.

A misplaced note could direct pilots into dangerous air.

A delayed answer could be mistaken for a safe route.

Their discipline survives in modern courier codes, emergency whistles, military calls, and aerial traffic signals.

### Passerid Stormriders

Passerid Stormriders were generally lighter than many Raptorid riders and relied upon agility, formation, and coordinated movement rather than raw endurance.

Some flew in linked groups known as **storm choruses**.

Each rider tracked a different aspect of the tempest. One watched lightning. Another judged pressure. Another maintained the route-call. A final rider carried the Tithe.

Modern performers have recreated these formations for racing circuits and festivals.

Traditionalists argue that the performances lack the original danger and purpose.

Performers generally consider the absence of mass electrocution an improvement.

## VII. Passerids in Modern Aerathon

Passerids live throughout the Five Greater Regions.

They are frequently found in roles involving communication, coordination, performance, teaching, public service, and rapid movement. These associations reflect opportunity and cultural history rather than innate professional destiny.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Passerids are closely associated with village bells, roadside songs, seasonal gatherings, harvest calls, and the carrying of news between distant households.

Allemancian Passerid culture places strong value upon familiar voices.

A traveler may know they are near home when they recognize the evening call from a particular hill, mill, or watchtower.

Many communities maintain songs listing local households and the aid each has promised during fire, flood, winter, or illness.

These songs are not considered ceremonial.

They are practical records of who will answer.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Passerids often work in emergency coordination, machinery signaling, structural inspection, communications, and the management of complex work crews.

Oric Passerids tend to value clarity over beauty.

A warning call must be distinguishable through smoke, machinery, storms, and panic. Decorative variation that interferes with comprehension is discouraged in industrial settings.

Several Oric Houses maintain standardized emergency choruses capable of coordinating evacuations even when magical communication fails.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], the voice can become an asset, brand, weapon, or debt.

Passerids are prominent in music, theatre, advertising, auctioneering, journalism, diplomacy, sales, public relations, and political campaigning.

Successful performers may gain immense wealth.

They may also lose control of their own voices through restrictive contracts, illusion rights, magical recordings, or obligations to sponsors.

Passerid-led legal movements have fought extensively over who owns a recorded performance and whether a person’s voice may be reproduced after death.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Passerids serve as ritual singers, oath-responders, chanters, heralds, mourners, and keepers of sacred oral traditions.

Certain rites require a congregation to answer in exact sequence.

A vow spoken alone may express intention.

A vow answered by the gathered faithful becomes a public bond.

Arnerian Passerids often distinguish carefully between sacred song, work song, mourning song, and entertainment. Performing one in the wrong setting can be deeply offensive.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Passerid cultures flourish in dock-cities, cliff settlements, traveling companies, storm shelters, and rebuilding communities.

Songs change quickly in Al’Ar.

New verses are added after disasters, victories, scandals, romances, and spectacular mistakes. A piece may travel from one coast to another and return unrecognizable except for its refrain.

Al’Ari Passerids often treat improvisation as proof that a tradition remains alive.

A song that cannot survive change, they argue, was already dead.

## VIII. Passerids and Delving

Passerids commonly serve as coordinators, scouts, negotiators, support specialists, navigators, signalers, and magical performers.

Their voices are especially valuable in conditions where visibility is poor.

A trained Passerid can use calls to:

- Confirm the location of companions
    
- Mark safe routes
    
- Coordinate movement through darkness
    
- Warn of approaching danger
    
- Maintain morale
    
- Test how sound carries through a chamber
    
- Determine whether a passage bends, opens, or conceals an obstacle
    
- Distinguish a real voice from a repeated echo
    

The Labyrinth is aware of sound.

Some chambers absorb voices entirely. Others repeat calls with altered words. Certain creatures imitate cries for help, while some anomalies make every spoken thought audible.

Experienced Passerids learn that hearing an answer does not mean a friend gave it.

### The Broken Chorus

Passerid delving schools teach a formation known as **the Broken Chorus**.

Rather than having every member answer every call, each person is assigned a particular response. If the wrong voice answers—or if an expected voice remains silent—the group knows something has changed.

The method is particularly useful against mimics, illusions, possession, and magical duplication.

It is less useful when no one remembers which response they were assigned.

## IX. Names, Titles, and Address

Passerid naming traditions vary widely.

Some use short names designed to carry clearly across distance. Others possess long ceremonial names whose rhythm identifies family, chorus, birthplace, or migration history.

A Passerid may also possess a **call-name** used during flight, work, military service, or emergencies.

Call-names are usually brief and difficult to confuse with one another.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Passerid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Charisma score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Passerids commonly stand between 5 feet and 6 feet tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 25 feet.

You have a flying speed of 50 feet.

### Flight

You can fly using your wings. To do so, you cannot be wearing medium or heavy armor.

### Talons

You have talons that you can use to make unarmed strikes.

When you hit with them, the strike deals slashing damage equal to `1d4 + your Strength modifier`.

### Skyward Pulse

As a bonus action, you can beat your wings with explosive force.

Each creature within 5 feet of you must succeed on a Strength saving throw or be pushed 5 feet away from you.

The saving throw DC equals:

`8 + your proficiency bonus + your Strength modifier`

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

### Resonant Call

As a bonus action, you emit a sharp call, spoken encouragement, rhythmic signal, or melodic phrase.

Choose one creature within 30 feet of you that can hear you. That creature gains advantage on its next attack roll or saving throw made before the end of its next turn.

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Passerid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Sparrow Sublineage

Sparrow Passerids are generally compact, quick, and widely distributed.

Their plumage often includes brown, grey, black, rust, or cream markings. Sparrow communities are common in cities, farming settlements, roadside villages, and regions where larger aerial peoples struggle to navigate crowded spaces.

They are frequently associated with humility and survival.

Some Sparrow Passerids embrace that symbolism. Others resent being treated as ordinary or insignificant.

### Finch and Canary Sublineages

Finch and Canary Passerids commonly possess short, strong beaks and highly varied plumage.

Yellow, red, orange, black, white, brown, and patterned coloration are all common. Many communities possess elaborate vocal traditions, though singing skill remains learned rather than guaranteed.

Canary populations have historically been used in mines, ruins, and magical hazard zones because of their sensitivity to changes in air.

Modern Passerids regard involuntary use in such work as a serious historical abuse.

### Swallow and Martin Sublineages

Swallow and Martin Passerids tend toward narrow wings, forked or tapered tails, and agile aerial movement.

They are strongly represented among migratory communities, couriers, sailors, and people who spend much of their lives moving between seasonal homes.

Their arrival is treated as a sign of renewal in many regions.

This symbolism can create pressure upon individuals who would prefer to settle permanently.

### Starling Sublineage

Starling Passerids often possess dark plumage marked by iridescence, speckles, or seasonal patterning.

Many are skilled vocal imitators and comfortable within large, coordinated communities. Starling flight groups can perform complex turns in close formation, creating vast moving patterns in the sky.

Outsiders often interpret this coordination as loss of individuality.

Starling communities generally disagree.

Moving together requires each participant to remain acutely aware of everyone nearby.

### Lark and Wren Sublineages

Lark and Wren Passerids are generally small-framed and known for strong voices relative to their size.

Larks are often associated with open country, high song, and dawn traditions. Wrens are more commonly connected with dense vegetation, narrow spaces, and intricate shelter-building.

Both frequently appear in folklore as examples of strength concealed within small bodies.

The comparison is sometimes appreciated.

It is sometimes extremely tiring.

### Thrush and Nightingale Sublineages

Thrush and Nightingale Passerids commonly possess complex, resonant voices and plumage ranging from subdued earth tones to striking blue, orange, or speckled patterns.

Their sublineages are strongly associated with poetry, mourning, courtship, and artistic performance.

Many individuals find these expectations restrictive, particularly when their interests or voices do not align with inherited reputations.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Answering Road

You gain proficiency in the Insight skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Passerids learn to listen for the traveler who has fallen behind and the household whose familiar voice has gone quiet.

### Oria — Evacuation Call

While flying, you have advantage on saving throws against being charmed or frightened.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to fly up to half your flying speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric Passerids are trained to preserve the signaler. A warning voice that survives can still guide others out.

### Vinyot — Stagefall Recovery

While falling, you can reduce the fall speed of yourself or one willing creature within 5 feet of you by 60 feet per round, potentially negating falling damage.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti Passerids know that every performance, negotiation, and enterprise eventually encounters a failed entrance.

The skilled learn how to turn it into part of the act.

### Arneria — Augury Chorus

You know the _gust_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _feather fall_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using voice, wind, and cloud movement as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Passerids do not merely ask the sky a question.

They listen for what answers between the notes.

### Al’Ar — Thunder Refrain

When you use **Skyward Pulse**, you may release a resonant cry carried by the force of your wings.

Creatures of your choice within 10 feet of you must succeed on a Constitution saving throw against your Skyward Pulse saving throw DC or be deafened until the start of your next turn.

Once you use this trait, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Among Al’Ari Passerids, the proper response to thunder is not always silence.

Sometimes the storm should know it has been heard.

## XIII. Playing a Passerid

When creating a Passerid character, consider:

- What does their voice mean to them?
    
- Are they comfortable being heard?
    
- What kinds of sounds or music do they value?
    
- Who taught them their first important call?
    
- Whose voice can they recognize anywhere?
    
- What Answering Promises have they made?
    
- Have they ever called for help and received no answer?
    
- Do they seek harmony or value disagreement?
    
- How do they behave when frightened?
    
- Do they sing, speak, whistle, hum, or become completely silent under pressure?
    
- Are they burdened by expectations to be cheerful, artistic, or socially available?
    
- What part of their community would answer if they called today?
    
- How do they regard the Sky-Reeve songs: sacred inheritance, practical history, performance material, or something that belongs to the past?
    

A Passerid may be joyful or severe, sociable or solitary, eloquent or quiet.

Their lineage gives weight to the voice.

It does not require them to use it for anyone else.

> Passerid lineage does not indicate musical talent, emotional availability, cheerfulness, willingness to perform, or suitability for diplomatic and entertainment work.
> 
> Ministry personnel are forbidden from assigning vocal signaling, morale support, performance, or public-facing duties solely on the basis of lineage.
> 
> Replication, sale, or magical reproduction of a Passerid citizen’s voice without consent may constitute identity misuse under regional and Ministry law.
> 
> Nonvocal Passerids remain entitled to all lineage recognition, accommodations, and protections.
