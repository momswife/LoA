# Pterygotid

**Ministry of Delving Operations — The Living Atlas**  
**Folk Classification:** [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/Avianfolk Overview|Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Pterygotid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage, Developmental Variance, and Homeland Adaptation  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition

**Common Names:** Insectfolk, Manywings, Chitinfolk

## I. Lineage Overview

**Pterygotids** are an Avianfolk lineage distinguished by insect-like anatomy, articulated wings, sensory antennae, partial or extensive chitinous plating, and developmental cycles that may include molting, emergence, or complete metamorphosis.

Their recognized sublineages include moths, butterflies, cicadas, dragonflies, bees, and beetles.

The Pterygotid classification includes peoples of dramatically different forms.

Some possess two wings.

Others possess four.

Some are covered in fine fur or powder-like wing scales. Others bear smooth chitin, hardened wing casings, translucent membranes, or luminous organs. Some hatch in forms resembling their parents. Others pass through several distinct bodies before reaching their mature winged form.

These differences have caused frequent dispute over whether Pterygotids should be considered one lineage.

The Ministry’s current position is that they share sufficient physiological, cultural, and historical experience to justify a broad classification. This designation is administrative rather than an assertion that all Pterygotids possess one close biological origin.

Pterygotids are commonly described through assumptions about instinct.

Bees are expected to obey.

Butterflies are expected to be beautiful.

Moths are expected to seek light.

Cicadas are expected to be loud.

Dragonflies are expected to hunt.

Beetles are expected to endure.

No sapient person is reducible to the survival patterns of a nonsapient animal bearing similar features.

Pterygotid bodies shape how they encounter the world.

They do not dictate what those encounters must mean.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Pterygotids possess no single accepted ancestral homeland.

Their oldest known communities have been identified in flowering plains, ancient forests, river wetlands, desert bloomlands, subterranean emergence chambers, fungal caverns, orchard valleys, tropical canopies, and settlements built around enormous living plants.

Some traditions claim the first Pterygotids were born from sparks that fell from the sun and learned to hide beneath leaves until their wings cooled.

Others teach that the earth dreamed of flight and formed the first chrysalises so that something buried could answer the sky.

A Cicada tradition claims the first Pterygotids slept beneath the world for so long that the gods forgot them. When they finally emerged, the noise was not celebration.

It was a reminder.

Pterygotid origin stories frequently contain a period of concealment before flight.

The lineage does not generally remember the sky as its first home.

It remembers reaching it.

### Emergence Records

Pterygotid history is frequently organized through **Emergence Records**.

Rather than dividing history only by rulers, wars, or political eras, an Emergence Record may describe events through generations that matured, molted, or returned to public life together.

An entry may read:

- During the Third Red Bloom
    
- Before the Long-Wing Emergence
    
- In the generation that woke beneath black rain
    
- After the Orchard Chrysalises failed
    
- When the eastern brood first saw snow
    
- In the summer when no Cicada voices answered
    
- During the year the Beetle Houses opened their wing casings
    

These records preserve environmental and social conditions that conventional chronicles often overlook.

They may document:

- Flowering cycles
    
- Soil temperature
    
- Rainfall
    
- Failed migrations
    
- Disease among larvae or young
    
- Damage to communal emergence grounds
    
- Changes in available food
    
- The arrival of predators
    
- The construction of safe molting chambers
    
- Laws governing developmental care
    

Pterygotid historians argue that a nation’s true condition can be measured by whether its most vulnerable life stages survived it.

### The Earlier Body

Some Pterygotids pass through forms that differ greatly from their mature appearance.

These earlier bodies are not treated as lesser or incomplete in every culture.

A person may retain:

- A childhood name
    
- Memories from a crawling or burrowing stage
    
- Scars from pupation
    
- A preserved shell or shed
    
- Relationships formed before wing emergence
    
- Skills learned when their body moved differently
    
- Grief for the physical abilities they no longer possess
    

Certain communities speak of the earlier body as an ancestor carried within the self.

Others regard it as a temporary form best released.

Neither belief is universal.

A mature Pterygotid is not obligated to describe metamorphosis as beautiful, spiritual, traumatic, natural, or transformative for the benefit of outsiders.

It was their body.

Its meaning belongs first to them.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Pterygotid bodies display exceptional variation.

Most mature Pterygotids stand between **4 feet 8 inches and 6 feet 6 inches**, though Beetle and Cicada populations may be considerably broader, while some Bee and Butterfly communities possess lighter frames.

Common anatomical features include:

- Two or four wings
    
- Antennae
    
- Compound or multifaceted eyes
    
- Chitinous plates
    
- Segmented limbs
    
- Mandibles or specialized mouth structures
    
- Fine sensory hairs
    
- Wing scales
    
- Hardened wing casings
    
- Luminous organs
    
- Tymbal-like sound structures
    
- Partial exoskeletons protecting the torso and limbs
    

Not every Pterygotid possesses every feature.

### Wings

Pterygotid wings vary by sublineage.

Moth and Butterfly wings are often covered in microscopic scales that create color, pattern, insulation, and protection.

Dragonfly wings are generally transparent or translucent, with visible structural veins and independent control.

Bee wings are smaller relative to body size but capable of rapid, powerful movement.

Cicada wings are commonly long, clear, and held roof-like over the body while resting.

Beetle wings may remain protected beneath hardened coverings called **elytra**.

Wing damage can significantly impair flight.

Scales, membranes, joints, and casings require different treatments. A salve appropriate for one sublineage may cause injury to another.

Ministry medical personnel are therefore instructed not to record “insect wings” as a sufficient anatomical description.

### Chitin

Many Pterygotids possess natural armor formed from chitinous plates.

Coverage may include:

- The chest
    
- Shoulders
    
- Forearms
    
- Shins
    
- Back
    
- Head
    
- Jaw
    
- Portions of the abdomen
    

Chitin varies in hardness, flexibility, and thickness. It may be matte, glossy, translucent, ridged, patterned, or brightly colored.

It grows with the body and may be renewed through molting.

Damage does not always produce ordinary bleeding. A crack may appear minor while exposing vulnerable tissue beneath the plate.

Chitin should never be drilled, engraved, polished, painted, or harvested without the person’s consent.

### Antennae

Pterygotid antennae are highly sensitive organs.

Depending upon the individual, they may assist with:

- Smell
    
- Air movement
    
- Humidity
    
- Temperature
    
- vibration
    
- Balance
    
- Chemical signals
    
- Spatial awareness
    
- Emotional expression
    

Antennae may be straight, clubbed, feathered, hooked, segmented, or longer than the head.

They are not decorative.

Touching them without consent can be painful, overwhelming, or intensely intimate.

### Vision

Many Pterygotids possess compound or partially compound eyes.

Their sight may provide exceptional awareness of movement and broad peripheral vision. Fine detail, distance, color, and low-light capability vary widely.

Some Pterygotids perceive colors or magical wavelengths invisible to most peoples.

Others struggle to focus upon small stationary text unless it is held at a particular distance.

Written accommodations may include:

- Larger lettering
    
- High-contrast materials
    
- Reduced glare
    
- Curved reading surfaces
    
- Colored inks
    
- Motion-based signals
    
- Multidirectional visual markers
    

### Temperature

Many Pterygotids regulate body temperature differently from warm-blooded Avianfolk.

Cold conditions may slow their movement, impair flight, or cause lethargy. Excessive heat may create dehydration or sensory distress.

Sublineage, homeland, equipment, and magical adaptation all affect these limits.

The assumption that a Pterygotid becomes harmless in cold weather has caused several avoidable confrontations.

Reduced speed is not the same as reduced judgment.

## IV. Development and Metamorphosis

Pterygotid development varies more than that of any other recognized Avianfolk lineage.

Some hatch or are born in forms resembling smaller versions of adults.

Others pass through distinct stages such as:

- Larva
    
- Nymph
    
- Grub
    
- Caterpillar
    
- Pupa
    
- Chrysalis
    
- Subadult emergence
    
- Final winged maturation
    

These terms differ between cultures and should not be applied to a person without knowing their preference.

### Metamorphic Pterygotids

Many Moth, Butterfly, Bee, and Beetle communities undergo a protected transitional period during adolescence or early adulthood.

The individual enters a sealed or sheltered state while the body reorganizes.

This period may last days, weeks, months, or longer depending upon lineage, health, environment, and magic.

The person is alive throughout the process.

The extent of awareness varies.

Some remain conscious and capable of limited communication. Others enter deep sleep. A few report vivid dreams, ancestral memories, or sensations of existing in several bodies at once.

No universal spiritual interpretation has been accepted.

### Emergence Care

A person undergoing metamorphosis may require:

- Stable temperature
    
- Humidity control
    
- Protection from vibration
    
- Medical monitoring
    
- Privacy
    
- Ritual attendants
    
- Nutrient support
    
- Magical stabilization
    
- Protection from forced opening
    
- Time for the wings and chitin to harden afterward
    

Opening a chrysalis or pupal chamber from the outside can cause catastrophic injury.

The Ministry classifies unauthorized interference as aggravated bodily violation.

### Gradual Molting

Cicadas, Dragonflies, and some other Pterygotids may mature through repeated molts rather than sealed metamorphosis.

The old outer layer separates and is shed as the body grows.

During and immediately after molting, the new chitin may be soft and vulnerable.

Many Beetles also molt repeatedly during earlier stages, though their final transformation may resemble complete metamorphosis.

### The Right to Emerge

Modern Pterygotid law increasingly recognizes the **Right to Emerge**.

This principle includes:

- The right to safe developmental care
    
- The right not to be displayed during vulnerable stages
    
- The right to choose who may attend
    
- The right to determine what happens to shed material
    
- The right to receive sufficient recovery time
    
- The right not to be assigned a new social identity without consent
    
- The right to retain or change one’s name after transformation
    

In regions without such protections, employers and institutions have sometimes treated metamorphosis as inconvenient leave, elective alteration, or grounds for termination.

Pterygotid advocates consider this equivalent to punishing someone for surviving adolescence.

## V. Society and Kinship

Pterygotid communities may organize themselves through families, broods, flights, hives, houses, guilds, emergence cohorts, and seasonal assemblies.

No single structure applies to the entire lineage.

Terms such as **hive** and **colony** can refer to voluntary communities. They do not imply mindless obedience or reproductive control.

### Emergence Cohorts

Pterygotids who mature or emerge during the same period may form an **Emergence Cohort**.

Cohorts often include people of different families, professions, and social classes who shared the vulnerability of transition.

The bond may resemble:

- Siblinghood
    
- Military fellowship
    
- School companionship
    
- Religious kinship
    
- Mutual-aid association
    
- Lifelong friendship
    

Cohorts commonly celebrate the anniversary of their emergence.

They may also maintain support for members whose wings, chitin, or adult bodies developed differently from expectation.

A cohort is not necessarily united politically.

People can survive the same chrysalis season and disagree about nearly everything afterward.

### Bloom Compacts

Communities dependent upon flowers, orchards, forests, or seasonal food sources often establish **Bloom Compacts**.

A Compact coordinates:

- Planting
    
- Pollination
    
- Harvest
    
- Water use
    
- Protection of wild growth
    
- Migration
    
- Orchard access
    
- Food storage
    
- Emergency support during failed blooms
    

Participants may include Pterygotids, farmers, druids, herbalists, Beastfolk, and regional authorities.

Healthy Compacts treat pollination and cultivation as mutual exchange.

Exploitative agreements reduce Bee and Butterfly populations to agricultural tools while denying them ownership, wages, or access to the harvest they helped sustain.

### The Shared Warmth

Some Pterygotid communities gather closely during cold weather.

This may involve communal halls, heated stone, steam chambers, magical lamps, shared blankets, or clustered rest.

The practice is often called **Shared Warmth**.

Participation does not automatically indicate intimacy.

It is environmental cooperation.

A person who provides warmth during danger may be remembered as kin even when no other bond existed beforehand.

### Individuality and Collective Action

Pterygotids are frequently stereotyped as collectivist.

Many do possess traditions of coordinated labor, synchronous emergence, group migration, or shared defense.

This does not erase individuality.

Coordinated action may require immense personal judgment. A flight of Dragonflies turning together does not share one mind. A Bee work crew maintaining a roof does not lack disagreement. A Cicada chorus contains thousands of distinct voices.

The whole exists because individuals choose, learn, respond, and adjust.

## VI. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Pterygotid communities commonly admire:

- Adaptability
    
- Purposeful labor
    
- Awareness of cycles
    
- Transformation
    
- Cooperation
    
- Sensory attentiveness
    
- Persistence
    
- The courage to emerge visibly after vulnerability
    

These values can produce disciplined communities, inventive artists, careful naturalists, and people capable of rebuilding their lives after profound change.

They can also create intense expectations.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Transformation|Growing beyond an old identity|Treating every difficulty as reason to reinvent oneself|
|Labor|Contributing to communal survival|Measuring worth only through productivity|
|Cooperation|Coordinating toward shared goals|Punishing dissent|
|Persistence|Enduring hardship|Refusing to recognize when a cycle should end|
|Beauty|Communicating through color and form|Valuing appearance above personhood|
|Sensitivity|Perceiving subtle environmental change|Becoming overwhelmed or hypervigilant|
|Emergence|Entering public life with courage|Demanding visibility from private people|
|Cyclical thinking|Understanding decline and renewal|Accepting preventable suffering as “natural”|

A recurring Pterygotid anxiety is the fear of **emerging into a world that has no place prepared for the person they became**.

A family may expect the same child who entered metamorphosis.

An employer may expect identical abilities.

A community may celebrate new wings while ignoring grief for the old body.

Some Pterygotids respond by performing dramatic reinvention.

Others conceal change so thoroughly that no one understands what the transition cost them.

### The Value of Work

Bee, Beetle, and some other Pterygotid cultures possess strong traditions of communal labor.

Outsiders frequently romanticize this dedication.

Pterygotids know that work can become a form of captivity when rest, identity, and belonging depend upon continued usefulness.

Several modern movements reject the belief that a person must produce honey, silk, wax, light, song, beauty, or physical labor to deserve a place within their community.

A body may create valuable material.

That does not make the body communal property.

### Attraction and Danger

Moths are frequently mocked for supposed attraction to dangerous light.

Butterflies are expected to display beauty.

Cicadas are accused of unnecessary noise.

Bees are assumed to obey authority.

Dragonflies are treated as predatory.

Beetles are considered physically durable enough to ignore pain.

These stereotypes confuse inherited senses and anatomy with moral inevitability.

A person can feel drawn toward something and choose not to approach it.

A person can be beautiful and private.

A person can possess a powerful voice and value silence.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Pterygotid communities disagree over:

- Whether metamorphosis should remain private
    
- Whether emergence cohorts possess legal family status
    
- Whether pollination work should be regulated separately from agriculture
    
- Whether shed chitin and wing scales may be sold
    
- Whether traditional hive structures concentrate too much authority
    
- Whether communal labor expectations exploit certain sublineages
    
- Whether artificial light disrupts Pterygotid health and migration
    
- Whether Cicada emergence grounds can be privately owned
    
- Whether transformation justifies abandoning former obligations
    
- Whether Pterygotids belong culturally among Avianfolk or require a separate classification
    

The final dispute remains especially active.

Some embrace Avianfolk solidarity.

Others argue that the classification forces every aerial people to be understood through the history of birds.

## VII. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Pterygotids served throughout the ancient [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]] as season-keepers, cloud gardeners, signalers, pressure listeners, structural workers, and pilots within the lower and middle currents.

Their contributions were most visible in the **Blue Tithes**, through which harvest songs and seasonal offerings were carried upon the winds.

### Season-Keepers

Sky-Reeve **Season-Keepers** observed:

- Flowering cycles
    
- Insect emergence
    
- Temperature
    
- Soil moisture
    
- Cloud height
    
- Wind-carried pollen
    
- Migratory arrivals
    
- Pressure changes
    
- The first and final storms of each growing season
    

Their reports helped determine when Blue Tithes should be performed and whether the weather demanded warning, gratitude, or intervention.

Season-Keepers often worked alongside Passerid singers and Limnid Rainwardens.

The Passerids carried the ritual songs.

The Limnids maintained the water.

The Pterygotids determined whether the season described by the ritual had actually arrived.

### Cloud Gardens

Pterygotid workers maintained the **Cloud Gardens** of the Eyrie-Spire of Taellen.

These suspended growing spaces provided:

- Fruit
    
- Herbs
    
- Flowers
    
- Medicines
    
- Fibers
    
- Wax
    
- Nectar
    
- Seeds
    
- Soil stabilization
    
- Resting places for travelers
    

Bee communities pollinated the gardens.

Butterflies and Moths tracked seasonal and nocturnal flowering.

Beetles maintained soil beds, fungal systems, and structural supports.

Cicadas monitored heat through emergence patterns and acoustic change.

Dragonflies controlled harmful insects and carried rapid messages between garden levels.

The Cloud Gardens were among the first parts of the Eyrie-Spire to fail when weather systems became unstable.

Some records claim their collapse preceded the destruction of the greater structure.

### The Wing-Shed Tithe

Pterygotid traditions preserve an unofficial rite called the **Wing-Shed Tithe**.

After a major molt or emergence, the individual released a fragment of shed scale, chitin, casing, or chrysalis material into the wind.

The offering did not surrender the new body.

It acknowledged the body that had carried them as far as it could.

Some Wind-Mothers incorporated the rite into Blue Tithe ceremonies.

Others rejected it as too personal for public ritual.

The practice survives widely among modern Pterygotids.

### Chorus Signals

Cicada and Bee Pterygotids developed coordinated acoustic signals capable of traveling through crowded aerial settlements.

Different rhythms warned of:

- Fire
    
- Structural collapse
    
- Incoming storms
    
- Failed aerolith engines
    
- Contaminated food
    
- Hostile approach
    
- Missing children
    
- Evacuation
    

Moth and Butterfly signalers supplemented these sounds through wing color, reflective scales, and patterned lantern flight.

At night, entire districts could communicate through light and vibration without using spoken language.

### Pterygotid Stormriders

Pterygotid Stormriders were sometimes called **Manywing Riders**.

Their flight styles varied by sublineage.

Dragonflies excelled at sudden changes in direction.

Moths navigated darkness and cloud.

Butterflies read broad wind patterns and rising warm air.

Bees flew in coordinated protective groups.

Beetles relied upon armored harnesses and powerful short bursts.

Cicadas were uncommon riders but renowned for pressure sensing and acoustic navigation.

Manywing Riders rarely agreed upon what constituted proper Stormriding.

The Sky-Reeves eventually created separate course standards after a Dragonfly champion described endurance racing as “flying slowly until everyone becomes tired of watching.”

## VIII. Pterygotids in Modern Aerathon

Pterygotids live throughout the Five Greater Regions.

Their largest communities remain concentrated around forests, orchards, wetlands, warm cities, caverns, agricultural valleys, and regions where seasonal change strongly affects public life.

They are frequently associated with agriculture, craft, performance, engineering, environmental study, and collective labor. These professions reflect historical opportunity rather than biological obligation.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Pterygotids inhabit orchards, wooded villages, flower farms, mills, gardens, and settlements built around seasonal work.

Bloom Compacts are common.

Successful agreements grant Pterygotid communities access to food, land, shelter, and a voice in agricultural decisions.

Poor agreements treat them as a natural resource.

Allemancian Pterygotids often maintain emergence halls and warm cellars where vulnerable young can mature safely through winter.

Seasonal festivals celebrate first flight, harvest, and return.

Many communities have begun replacing older public emergence displays with private rites chosen by the individual.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Pterygotids work in precision engineering, materials research, communications, climate control, industrial cultivation, structural inspection, and coordinated logistics.

Oric researchers study chitin, wing geometry, vibration, and sensory anatomy.

This work has produced improved armor, lighter machinery, pressure sensors, acoustic warning systems, and advanced artificial wings.

It has also created a history of unethical research.

Modern law requires explicit consent for the study or collection of:

- Chitin
    
- Wing scales
    
- Venom
    
- Wax
    
- Silk
    
- Secretions
    
- Shed material
    
- Developmental casings
    
- Reproductive material
    

Oric Pterygotid unions maintain independent inspectors to ensure these requirements are followed.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], color, transformation, rarity, light, and biological production all possess commercial value.

Pterygotids work throughout:

- Fashion
    
- Theatre
    
- Agriculture
    
- Luxury food
    
- Perfume
    
- Illumination
    
- Architecture
    
- Racing
    
- Advertising
    
- Alchemy
    
- Textile production
    
- Public entertainment
    

Butterfly and Moth performers may command enormous fees.

Bee wax, honey, and royal jelly can become expensive trade goods. Beetle chitin patterns inspire armor and jewelry. Cicada voices are reproduced for music and public signaling.

Commercial success has intensified disputes over bodily ownership.

A company may purchase a performance.

It does not thereby own the performer’s wings, molt, voice, or future transformation.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Pterygotids participate in rites of transformation, burial, awakening, seasonal return, sacred labor, and divine revelation.

The chrysalis is a powerful religious symbol.

It may represent:

- Death
    
- Preparation
    
- Faith
    
- Concealed change
    
- The silence before revelation
    
- A body entrusted to forces beyond conscious control
    

Not all Pterygotids appreciate these interpretations.

For many, metamorphosis is an ordinary biological process made more difficult by everyone insisting it become theology.

Cicada emergence grounds are treated as sacred in several Arnerian traditions. Disturbing them may be considered both ecological destruction and desecration.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Pterygotids flourish in monsoon forests, tropical ports, luminous cities, river districts, storm plains, and settlements where seasonal abundance arrives suddenly.

Al’Ari Pterygotid festivals often coincide with:

- First rain
    
- Mass emergence
    
- Major blooms
    
- Lightning swarms
    
- Firefly nights
    
- Cicada choruses
    
- Butterfly migration
    
- The opening of communal chrysalises
    

These events combine celebration with practical labor.

Food must be stored. Shelters must be prepared. New adults may require assistance. Roads may become unusable. Predators may arrive with the same season.

Al’Ari tradition does not divide survival cleanly from festival.

Sometimes the dancing is how a community counts everyone who survived.

## IX. Pterygotids and Delving

Pterygotids commonly serve as scouts, sensory specialists, alchemists, climbers, engineers, signalers, environmental observers, and aerial combatants.

Their varied bodies allow them to perceive movement, vibration, temperature, light, and chemical changes that other peoples may overlook.

Experienced Pterygotid delvers examine:

- Air temperature
    
- Vibrations through stone
    
- Unnatural light
    
- Repeating scents
    
- Wing movement in still air
    
- Hollow surfaces
    
- Shed material
    
- Chitin damage
    
- Changes in insect activity
    
- Whether a chamber’s apparent season matches its environment
    
- Whether a sound is being produced or remembered
    

The Labyrinth is particularly dangerous to peoples shaped by cycles.

It may create:

- False seasons
    
- Accelerated molting
    
- Artificial emergence calls
    
- Rooms that induce sleep
    
- Light that compels approach
    
- Flowers that consume pollinators
    
- Empty chrysalises containing voices
    
- Swarms that move as a single false body
    
- Chambers that appear only once every several years
    

### The False Bloom

Pterygotid delving schools teach caution around sudden abundance.

A chamber filled with flowers, warmth, light, food, or familiar scent may be a **False Bloom**.

Signs include:

- No fallen petals
    
- No variation in growth
    
- Nectar without insects
    
- Identical flowers facing the same direction
    
- Pollen that does not settle
    
- Warmth without a source
    
- Sweetness concealing decay
    
- Plants reacting to conversation
    

A beautiful environment is not necessarily safe.

A hostile environment is not necessarily honest enough to appear ugly.

### The Sealed Chamber Rule

The Ministry recognizes a Pterygotid delving principle called the **Sealed Chamber Rule**:

> Do not open what appears to be a chrysalis, cocoon, molting shelter, brood chamber, or developmental casing until its contents have been identified and consent has been established.

The casing may contain:

- A living person
    
- A sleeping creature
    
- A preserved corpse
    
- A trap
    
- An imitation
    
- A memory
    
- Something using the form because it knows observers will hesitate
    

The rule remains in effect even when haste is inconvenient.

### Cohort Count

When traveling through environments that affect sleep, memory, or development, Pterygotid parties perform a **Cohort Count**.

Each person confirms:

- Their name
    
- Their current bodily state
    
- Their last remembered molt or transformation
    
- Whether any new marks, wings, plates, or sensations have appeared
    
- Whether they remember entering the chamber
    

A party member insisting they have “always looked like this” should not be accepted as proof.

## XI. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Pterygotid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2.

Your Intelligence, Wisdom, or Charisma score increases by 1. Choose the ability when you select this lineage.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Pterygotids commonly stand between 4 feet 8 inches and 6 feet 6 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 30 feet.

You have a flying speed of 40 feet.

### Articulated Flight

You can fly using your wings.

To do so, you cannot be wearing medium or heavy armor, and your wings must not be restrained.

You can hover while conscious.

### Chitinous Strikes

Your mandibles, claws, horns, or hardened limbs are natural weapons that you can use to make unarmed strikes.

When you hit with one of them, the strike deals piercing or slashing damage equal to `1d4 + your Strength modifier`.

You choose the damage type when you make the attack.

### Faceted Awareness

You gain proficiency in the Perception skill.

When you roll initiative, you can add your proficiency bonus to the roll.

Once you use this initiative bonus, you cannot use it again until you finish a short or long rest.

### Wingbeat Surge

As a bonus action while you are flying, you may move up to half your flying speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

This movement does not count against your speed for the turn.

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

## XII. Recognized Sublineages

Pterygotid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, developmental stages, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Moth Sublineage

Moth Pterygotids commonly possess broad scale-covered wings, feathered or comb-like antennae, and bodies covered in soft fur or fine sensory hairs.

Their coloration may include:

- White
    
- Grey
    
- Brown
    
- Black
    
- Gold
    
- Green
    
- Rose
    
- Crimson
    
- Complex eye-like markings
    
- Patterns resembling bark, leaves, stars, or flames
    

Many Moths are adapted to twilight or darkness.

Their communities possess strong traditions of night navigation, textile work, dream interpretation, astronomy, and quiet seasonal gathering.

The stereotype that Moths cannot resist dangerous light is inaccurate.

Some are sensitive to particular magical or natural wavelengths. This may create distraction, disorientation, comfort, pain, or attraction depending upon the individual.

It does not remove choice.

### Butterfly Sublineage

Butterfly Pterygotids possess large, often brightly colored wings covered in fine scales.

Their patterns may include eyespots, bands, gradients, metallic colors, or markings unique to a household or regional population.

Many Butterfly communities undertake seasonal migrations or maintain strong metamorphic traditions.

They are frequently associated with beauty, elegance, and personal transformation.

These associations can become restrictive.

A Butterfly may be severe, private, physically powerful, scarred, unfashionable, or uninterested in serving as a symbol of anyone else’s growth.

### Cicada Sublineage

Cicada Pterygotids commonly possess broad heads, clear-veined wings, strong climbing limbs, and sound-producing structures capable of tremendous volume.

Many spend long developmental periods beneath the ground or within protected subterranean settlements before emerging into public life.

Aerathian Cicadas do not possess universally brief mature lifespans.

However, their cultures often preserve a strong division between hidden preparation and visible participation.

Some Cicada cohorts emerge together after years apart from surface society. Their arrival can reshape local politics, labor, population, and ritual life almost overnight.

Cicada voices may be musical, rhythmic, harsh, layered, or physically overwhelming at close range.

Loudness is a capability.

It is not a requirement.

### Dragonfly Sublineage

Dragonfly Pterygotids possess long bodies, four independently controlled wings, broad eyes, and exceptional aerial maneuverability.

Their wings are typically transparent or translucent and may reflect blue, green, gold, violet, or silver.

Dragonfly communities are common around rivers, wetlands, lakes, canals, and open sunlit spaces.

Many are capable of sudden turns, hovering, rapid acceleration, and controlled backward movement.

Their hunting ancestry has produced stereotypes of aggression and impatience.

In reality, Dragonflies display the same range of temperaments as any other people.

They are simply capable of leaving a tedious conversation with unusual efficiency.

### Bee Sublineage

Bee Pterygotids possess furred or partially furred bodies, compact wings, strong sensory antennae, and coloration that commonly includes black, gold, brown, orange, white, blue, or green.

Some possess defensive stingers.

In many populations, a sting can be used safely multiple times. In others, serious injury may result. No Bee should be expected to use this defense casually.

Bee communities possess strong traditions of architecture, pollination, cultivation, waxwork, food preservation, coordinated labor, and mutual aid.

They are frequently assumed to obey queens or central authorities instinctively.

Sapient Bee societies include monarchies, councils, republics, guilds, family houses, independent settlements, and solitary travelers.

A biological reproductive role does not automatically grant political authority.

### Beetle Sublineage

Beetle Pterygotids are among the most physically diverse members of the lineage.

They commonly possess hardened wing casings, sturdy chitin, and powerful limbs.

Beetle forms may resemble:

- Scarabs
    
- Stag beetles
    
- Rhinoceros beetles
    
- Jewel beetles
    
- Fireflies
    
- Ladybirds
    
- Ground beetles
    
- Diving beetles
    
- Longhorn beetles
    

Their coloration ranges from black, brown, and rust to brilliant metallic green, blue, purple, gold, and red.

Some possess horns, heavy mandibles, luminous organs, or bodies adapted to digging, climbing, swimming, or carrying weight.

Beetles are often stereotyped as stoic laborers.

Many do value endurance.

Others are artists, scholars, performers, mystics, traders, and people who become extremely irritated when asked to lift furniture solely because their shoulders appear dependable.

## XIII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Bloom Compact

You gain proficiency in the Nature skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Pterygotids learn to notice when the orchard has fallen silent, when a familiar flower opens too early, and when one missing worker means the season is beginning to fail.

### Oria — Hardened Emergence

You have advantage on saving throws against poison and disease.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to move up to half your flying speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric Pterygotids are taught that resilience is not the refusal to crack.

It is knowing how to move before the damaged structure breaks completely.

### Vinyot — Display Flight

When you fall while conscious and your wings are functional, you can reduce your falling speed by 60 feet per round, potentially negating falling damage.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti Pterygotids learn that every entrance is a negotiation between spectacle, control, and the audience’s willingness to pay attention.

### Arneria — Rite of Emergence

You know the _light_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _faerie fire_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using shed chitin, wing scales, a chrysalis casing, or the observation of seasonal movement as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Pterygotids ask what the hidden form is becoming.

Their wisest rites also ask whether it wishes to be seen.

### Al’Ar — Manywing Burst

When you use **Wingbeat Surge**, you may move up to your full flying speed instead of half your flying speed.

During this movement, you may pass through the spaces of creatures larger than you, though you cannot end your movement there.

Once you enhance Wingbeat Surge in this way, you cannot do so again until you finish a long rest.

Al’Ari Pterygotids know that emergence rarely waits for the world to become orderly.

Sometimes the wings must open in the middle of the storm.

## XIV. Playing a Pterygotid

When creating a Pterygotid character, consider:

- What was their earliest bodily form?
    
- Do they remember it clearly?
    
- Have they undergone metamorphosis or a major molt?
    
- Who protected them during that transition?
    
- Did they emerge into the life others expected?
    
- Do they retain their childhood name?
    
- What does transformation mean to them?
    
- Are they comfortable with their wings being visible?
    
- What environmental sensations do they notice first?
    
- What kinds of sound, light, scent, or motion overwhelm them?
    
- Do they belong to an Emergence Cohort?
    
- What does their community expect them to produce?
    
- Do they value collective work?
    
- When does cooperation become control?
    
- What part of their body do outsiders treat as a commodity?
    
- Have they ever been praised for beauty while being ignored as a person?
    
- What season feels like home?
    
- How do they react to cold?
    
- Do they believe decline is part of every necessary cycle?
    
- How do they regard the Sky-Reeves: ancestors, agricultural governors, daring pilots, exploiters of forgotten labor, or preservers of valuable seasonal knowledge?
    
- Would they offer a Wing-Shed Tithe?
    
- What former body, identity, or obligation do they still carry?
    
- What will be waiting when they next emerge?
    

A Pterygotid may be industrious or idle, radiant or understated, communal or fiercely independent.

Their lineage teaches that bodies and seasons change.

It does not decide what must be abandoned when they do.
