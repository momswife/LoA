# Strigid

**Ministry of Delving Operations — The Living Atlas**  
**Folk Classification:** [[‎ Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Strigid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage and Homeland Variance  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Unrestricted  
**Common Names:** Owls, Nightwings, Hushfolk  
**Date of Filing:** 2nd Cycle, 3387 A.D.

---

## I. Lineage Overview

**Strigids** are an Avianfolk lineage distinguished by silent flight, exceptional low-light vision, highly sensitive hearing, flexible neck movement, and an ancestral affinity for nocturnal environments.

Their recognized sublineages include barn owls, horned owls, snowy owls, screech owls, burrowing owls, fish owls, hawk owls, and numerous other nocturnal or twilight-adapted peoples.

Strigids are often described as patient, wise, secretive, and solemn.

Such descriptions are usually offered by people who have met very few Strigids.

Many Strigid communities do admire patience and careful observation. Their bodies allow them to move quietly, perceive distant sounds, and remain attentive through long periods of darkness. These qualities have shaped traditions of guardianship, scholarship, hunting, navigation, and watchkeeping.

They have also shaped a persistent outsider expectation that every Strigid must possess unusual wisdom.

A Strigid may be thoughtful.

They may also be impulsive, foolish, talkative, impatient, easily distracted, or entirely uninterested in being treated as the nearest available oracle.

The defining lesson of many Strigid traditions is not that silence always contains wisdom.

It is that silence creates room to notice what noise conceals.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Strigids possess no single accepted homeland.

Their oldest known settlements have been found in ancient forests, mountain valleys, grasslands, deserts, frozen tundra, cliff regions, ruined cities, underground approaches, and towers abandoned by other peoples.

Some Strigid traditions claim their ancestors emerged when the first sunset divided sight from sound.

Others teach that the earliest Strigid was appointed to watch the world while the gods slept.

A darker account says the first Strigids were once ordinary Avianfolk who followed a fallen moon into the wilderness and returned with eyes that could see what daylight had hidden.

No surviving record verifies these accounts.

Strigid historians often regard origin stories as lenses rather than maps. Their purpose is to reveal what a people values, not necessarily where their first footprints stood.

### The Memory of Absence

Many Strigid historical traditions preserve what is missing alongside what remains.

A ruined settlement may be remembered through the spaces where homes once stood. A dead person may be honored through an intentional pause in a familiar story. A stolen object may remain listed in a family inventory for generations—not because anyone expects its return, but because removing the entry would complete the thief’s work.

This practice is called **keeping the hollow**.

To keep the hollow is to acknowledge that absence possesses shape.

Strigid records may therefore include:

- Empty lines where destroyed names once appeared
    
- Unoccupied seats at memorial gatherings
    
- Silent measures within ritual music
    
- Maps marking settlements that no longer exist
    
- Family titles retained after the last holder’s death
    
- Shelves reserved for lost or stolen objects
    
- Watches maintained for threats believed long vanished
    

Outsiders sometimes interpret these customs as an inability to move beyond grief.

Strigids generally answer that forgetting a wound does not heal it.

It merely makes the scar harder to explain.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Strigids are generally light to medium in build, with broad wings adapted for controlled, quiet movement.

Most stand between **5 feet 2 inches and 6 feet 5 inches**. Their height, wingspan, and build vary considerably between sublineages.

Their feathers possess specialized edges that soften the turbulence created during flight. This allows many Strigids to move with remarkably little sound, especially at slower speeds.

Their eyes are typically large and forward-facing, providing strong depth perception. Eye colors include black, brown, gold, amber, orange, red, grey, pale blue, and near-white.

Strigid eyes do not move freely within their sockets. Instead, flexible neck anatomy allows them to turn their heads through a much wider range than most peoples.

The common claim that Strigids can rotate their heads entirely around is false.

Repeated attempts to test this claim have resulted in disciplinary action and several avoidable medical reports.

### Hearing

Strigid hearing is exceptionally sensitive.

Many can distinguish subtle differences in distance, direction, movement, and surface texture through sound alone. Some possess asymmetrical ear placement that improves their ability to locate concealed movement.

This sensitivity can become uncomfortable in crowded workshops, festivals, industrial districts, or magically amplified spaces.

Strigid accommodations may include:

- Quiet rooms
    
- Sound-dampening cloth
    
- Protective ear coverings
    
- Soft-soled flooring
    
- Restricted bell use
    
- Warning before magical amplification
    
- Separate sleeping areas away from machinery
    

### Plumage

Strigid plumage commonly includes:

- White, grey, cream, brown, black, or rust
    
- Fine speckling or mottling
    
- Facial discs surrounding the eyes
    
- Ear-like feather tufts
    
- Dark markings around the face
    
- Pale undersides
    
- Banded wings and tails
    
- Camouflage suited to bark, stone, snow, grass, or shadow
    

The feather tufts of horned and screech sublineages are not ears.

Touching them to confirm this remains impolite.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Strigid communities are often organized around **watches**.

A watch may be a period of duty, a household, a neighborhood association, a professional circle, or a group entrusted with protecting a particular body of knowledge.

The same community may maintain several watches:

- The dusk watch
    
- The midnight watch
    
- The fire watch
    
- The child watch
    
- The archive watch
    
- The mourning watch
    
- The outer watch
    
- The watch that listens for returning travelers
    

Responsibility rotates according to need, ability, age, and tradition.

A person who cannot fly may still serve as an accomplished listener, archivist, healer, teacher, or keeper of doors.

### The Kept Hour

Many Strigid cultures recognize a period known as the **Kept Hour**.

During this time, members of a household or community reduce unnecessary noise. Work may continue, but conversation becomes limited and deliberate.

The Kept Hour may occur:

- Before sleep
    
- Before an important decision
    
- At the beginning of a night watch
    
- Following a death
    
- During dangerous weather
    
- When a traveler is overdue
    
- When the community believes it has failed to notice something important
    

It is not always solemn.

Children may draw, elders may mend clothing, partners may share food, and craftspeople may work beside one another without speaking.

The purpose is not enforced silence.

It is shared attention.

### Counsel by Watching

Traditional Strigid councils often begin with testimony rather than debate.

Each person describes what they observed, heard, or experienced. Interpretation is delayed until the available accounts have been gathered.

This practice attempts to separate:

- What happened
    
- What was perceived
    
- What was assumed
    
- What is feared
    
- What action is now necessary
    

The method can produce careful judgments.

It can also become unbearably slow when participants use procedure to avoid making an unpopular decision.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Strigid communities commonly admire:

- Patience
    
- Careful observation
    
- Restraint
    
- Protection without spectacle
    
- Respect for privacy
    
- The ability to remain calm in uncertainty
    
- The courage to name what others prefer not to notice
    

These values can produce thoughtful leaders, reliable guardians, perceptive healers, and companions who listen without immediately attempting to control the conversation.

They can also create emotional distance.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Patience|Waiting for the proper moment|Delaying until action is impossible|
|Observation|Noticing overlooked dangers|Watching others without consent|
|Privacy|Respecting personal boundaries|Withholding necessary truth|
|Calm|Remaining steady during crisis|Appearing indifferent to suffering|
|Restraint|Avoiding reckless action|Refusing to take meaningful risks|
|Independence|Functioning confidently alone|Isolation and distrust|
|Memory|Preserving warnings and losses|Refusing to release old fear|

A recurring Strigid anxiety is the fear of **failing to notice danger before it reaches someone under their care**.

Some respond by becoming vigilant and dependable.

Others become controlling, suspicious, or incapable of resting. They may treat every unexplained sound as evidence of approaching disaster and every private decision as a threat they were not permitted to assess.

### The Expectation of Wisdom

Strigids are frequently expected to provide calm answers during difficult situations.

This expectation can be burdensome.

A young Strigid may be treated as an elder simply because they speak quietly. A frightened Strigid may feel unable to admit uncertainty because everyone around them has already decided that they understand.

Several modern Strigid scholars refer to this as **the borrowed mantle**: authority placed upon someone who neither requested nor earned it.

Some embrace the role.

Others respond to requests for ancient wisdom with increasingly absurd advice.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Strigid communities disagree over:

- Whether observation without intervention is ever ethical
    
- Whether sacred knowledge should remain restricted
    
- Whether the Kept Hour belongs in public institutions
    
- Whether old dangers deserve continued watch
    
- Whether silence protects victims or protects abusers
    
- Whether night-adapted communities should reshape daytime regional schedules
    
- Whether Strigid archives place too much value upon loss
    
- Whether young Strigids are taught caution at the expense of joy
    

These arguments frequently turn upon the distinction between guarding knowledge and imprisoning it.

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Strigids served throughout the ancient [[The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]] as night navigators, weather observers, signal listeners, rescue pilots, and keepers of the hours between the formal Wind-Tithes.

The oldest surviving Sky-Reeve songs celebrate thunder, lightning, and riders visible against the storm.

Strigid records preserve what happened after those riders disappeared into darkness.

### Nightwardens

Sky-Reeve **Nightwardens** maintained watch when cloud, distance, or darkness made ordinary visual signals unreliable.

They listened for:

- The change in pitch produced by a damaged harness
    
- Wingbeats falling out of formation
    
- Lightning striking too near an aerolith engine
    
- Calls reflected from the wrong direction
    
- Riders descending without control
    
- Pressure shifts announcing a collapsing current
    
- The silence that followed a missing oath-pilot
    

Nightwardens were stationed along the Eyrie-Spire of Taellen and at remote landing shelters throughout the cardinal currents.

Their responsibility was not merely to observe.

They were expected to launch when observation became insufficient.

### The Quiet Tithe

Certain Strigid traditions describe a fifth ritual known as the **Quiet Tithe**.

Unlike the recognized Blue, Silver, and Crimson Tithes, the Quiet Tithe involved no public offering. A Nightwarden remained alone at altitude from sunset until dawn, listening to the current without song, instrument, or conductive relic.

What the storm received from this rite remains disputed.

Some traditions say the offering was a night of fear endured without retreat.

Others claim the Nightwarden surrendered a secret to the wind.

The official Ledger of Gales contains no confirmed reference to the Quiet Tithe.

Strigid historians consider this suspicious rather than conclusive.

### Stormriders of the Blind Sky

Strigid Stormriders were uncommon but highly respected.

They specialized in weather conditions where visibility had become nearly useless. Rather than following distant landmarks, they judged space through pressure, echo, lightning delay, and the sound of other riders.

Some were called **Blind-Sky Riders**, though most were not blind.

Modern descendants often consider the title misleading. It referred to the condition of the sky, not the pilot.

Strigid racing traditions still include night courses in which competitors navigate by sound, signal, and brief flashes of controlled light.

Ministry-approved versions of these races include more safety measures than the originals.

Unofficial versions include approximately the same number.

## VII. Strigids in Modern Aerathon

Strigids live throughout the Five Greater Regions.

They are commonly associated with watchkeeping, scholarship, investigation, medicine, navigation, and nocturnal labor. These professions reflect historical opportunity rather than inherent obligation.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Strigids often maintain night watches over villages, roads, fields, barns, and isolated households.

Allemancian culture values the person who keeps the lamp lit for someone expected home.

Strigid households frequently become gathering places for late travelers, sleepless neighbors, and workers returning after dark. Their watches are commonly tied to mutual obligation rather than formal authority.

A Strigid who hears trouble in the night is expected to wake others.

They are not expected to face it alone.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Strigids work in inspection, diagnostics, security, medicine, structural monitoring, and night-shift operations.

Their hearing allows them to identify subtle changes in machinery, stress fractures, pressure systems, and unstable structures.

Oric training emphasizes verification.

A strange sound is evidence that something changed. It is not proof of what changed.

Strigid inspectors are therefore expected to record the sound, isolate its source, and avoid dramatic conclusions until the mechanism can be examined.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], the night is an economy of its own.

Strigids work throughout security, finance, transport, entertainment, private investigation, hospitality, communication, and markets that operate while the rest of the city sleeps.

Vinyoti Strigids often become skilled at controlling access to information. A quiet room, private conversation, or undisclosed meeting can be more valuable than a public performance.

This has produced both respected confidentiality professions and notorious networks of surveillance.

The distinction often depends upon who was asked for consent.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Strigids serve as vigil keepers, temple guardians, dream recorders, confession witnesses, astronomers, and custodians of restricted rites.

Many Arnerian ceremonies continue through the night.

Participants may be expected to remain awake, silent, or attentive until a flame extinguishes, a star reaches a particular position, or an answer arrives through dream or omen.

Strigids are often trusted to determine whether a vigil was completed properly.

This authority can become politically significant when the legitimacy of an oath, succession, or religious claim depends upon the rite.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Strigids inhabit storm coasts, cliff settlements, crowded docks, traveling companies, and cities where rebuilding continues through the night.

Al’Ari Strigids tend to possess a more improvisational relationship with silence.

Quiet is not treated as the absence of life. It is the pause before the next change.

Many become navigators, stage technicians, night performers, storm listeners, healers, and rescue workers. Their ability to hear structural movement has saved countless people from buildings beginning to fail under wind or flood.

## VIII. Strigids and Delving

Strigids are frequently recruited as scouts, watchers, investigators, navigators, sentries, and specialists in low-light environments.

Their vision and hearing allow them to operate effectively where ordinary light is unreliable or dangerous.

A trained Strigid delver may notice:

- Breathing behind a wall
    
- A creature moving beneath loose stone
    
- The different echo of an open passage
    
- A mechanism continuing after its visible parts stop
    
- A companion whose footsteps have changed
    
- Air moving through a concealed opening
    
- A voice that comes from the proper direction but the wrong distance
    
- Silence too complete to be natural
    

The Labyrinth often exploits heightened perception.

Some chambers produce sounds too faint for most creatures but painfully clear to Strigids. Others create false echoes, delayed movement, or whispers that resemble familiar voices.

A Strigid who can hear more may also be given more lies to sort through.

### The Three Silences

Strigid delving schools commonly teach that silence has three forms:

1. **Empty Silence** — nothing is making a sound.
    
2. **Listening Silence** — something is deliberately remaining quiet.
    
3. **Taken Silence** — sound is being prevented, consumed, or removed.
    

Confusing one for another can prove fatal.

Experienced Strigids therefore test silence through controlled movement, thrown objects, spoken signals, or changes in airflow before entering an apparently quiet chamber.

## IX. Names, Titles, and Address

Strigid naming traditions vary by region.

Many names contain soft consonants, long vowels, or pauses easily distinguished during quiet conversation. Some communities use separate day names and night names, while others grant a new name after a person completes their first independent watch.

Examples include:

- Aeva Stillbranch
    
- Cael Hushwing
    
- Dorran Hollow-Keeper
    
- Eira Snowwatch
    
- Hessa Softflight
    
- Ivara Moon-Eye
    
- Kael of the Kept Hour
    
- Maevra Pale-Eye
    
- Norrik Bell-Listener
    
- Orra Lowbranch
    
- Sella Duskward
    
- Teren Quiet-Talon
    
- Vael Last-Watch
    
- Yssa Reedshadow
    

Historic and professional titles include:

- **Nightwarden**
    
- **Hollow-Keeper**
    
- **Vigil-Bearer**
    
- **Dusk-Reeve**
    
- **Moon-Watcher**
    
- **Quiet-Tithe Keeper**

- **Last Listener**
    

Calling a Strigid **wise one** without familiarity is often considered patronizing.

Calling one **all-seeing** is both inaccurate and likely to provoke a demonstration of everything they failed to notice about you.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Strigid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Wisdom score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Strigids commonly stand between 5 feet 2 inches and 6 feet 5 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 25 feet.

You have a flying speed of 50 feet.

### Flight

You can fly using your wings. To do so, you cannot be wearing medium or heavy armor.

### Talons

You have talons that you can use to make unarmed strikes.

When you hit with them, the strike deals slashing damage equal to `1d4 + your Strength modifier`.

### Skyward Pulse

As a bonus action, you can beat your wings with explosive force.

Each creature within 5 feet of you must succeed on a Strength saving throw or be pushed 5 feet away from you.

The saving throw DC equals:

`8 + your proficiency bonus + your Strength modifier`

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

### Night Sight

You have darkvision out to a range of 120 feet.

Within that range, you can see in dim light as though it were bright light and in darkness as though it were dim light. You discern colors in darkness only as shades of grey.

### Silent Approach

The structure of your feathers allows you to fly with remarkably little sound.

When you move at least 20 feet while flying and end that movement in dim light or darkness, you can take the Hide action as a bonus action.

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Strigid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Barn Owl Sublineage

Barn Owl Strigids commonly possess pale plumage, long legs, heart-shaped facial discs, and dark eyes.

Their feathers often include white, cream, gold, grey, and fine speckling. Their facial structure grants them exceptional directional hearing.

Barn Owl communities are common in agricultural regions, old towers, grasslands, and settlements where high rafters provide secure shelter.

Their association with abandoned buildings and silent flight has made them figures of fear in some local folklore.

### Horned Owl Sublineage

Horned Owl Strigids are generally broad-bodied and powerfully built, with prominent feather tufts above the eyes.

Their plumage often includes brown, black, grey, rust, and cream barring.

Many are adapted to forests, mountains, and cold regions. Their size and appearance have produced a reputation for severity that individuals may either cultivate or find deeply inconvenient.

### Snowy Owl Sublineage

Snowy Owl Strigids possess dense white plumage marked by varying degrees of black, grey, or brown barring.

They are adapted to cold climates, open tundra, highlands, and regions where visual cover is scarce.

Many Snowy communities maintain traditions of long-distance observation and seasonal movement.

The assumption that they are naturally unemotional is largely the result of outsiders struggling to read subtle expression through dense facial plumage.

### Screech Owl Sublineage

Screech Owl Strigids are generally smaller in stature and may possess grey, brown, or rust-colored plumage.

Despite their name, many are capable of exceptionally controlled and varied calls.

Their sublineages are common in forests, towns, and urban regions where compact wings and careful movement are advantageous.

They are often stereotyped as irritable.

Several Screech communities consider the stereotype useful because it shortens unwanted conversations.

### Burrowing Owl Sublineage

Burrowing Owl Strigids possess long legs, compact bodies, and strong terrestrial adaptations.

They are comfortable living near or beneath the ground and often inhabit grasslands, deserts, tunnels, low settlements, and reclaimed ruins.

Many spend more time walking or running than other Strigid sublineages.

Their relationship with underground space has made them particularly valuable in delving and mining communities.

### Fish Owl Sublineage

Fish Owl Strigids are commonly large-framed, with powerful feet and plumage adapted to rivers, lakes, coasts, and wetlands.

Their feathers may be less completely sound-dampening than those of other Strigids due to frequent exposure to water.

Fish Owl communities often maintain close relationships with maritime peoples and [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Aquaticfolk/Aquaticfolk Overview|Aquaticfolk]] settlements.

### Hawk Owl Sublineage

Hawk Owl Strigids possess longer tails, narrower wings, and more visibly raptor-like silhouettes than many other Strigids.

They are frequently active during daylight or twilight as well as at night.

Their existence has complicated numerous Ministry attempts to draw a simple distinction between Strigid and Raptorid anatomy.

Hawk Owl Strigids generally find this bureaucratic confusion amusing until it delays their paperwork.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Lantern Watch

You gain proficiency in the Insight skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Strigids are taught to notice the empty chair, the unlit window, and the traveler whose footsteps should have reached the road by now.

### Oria — Controlled Extraction

While flying, you have advantage on saving throws against being charmed or frightened.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to fly up to half your flying speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric Strigids are trained to leave failing structures before observation becomes burial.

### Vinyot — Quiet Descent

While falling, you can reduce the fall speed of yourself or one willing creature within 5 feet of you by 60 feet per round, potentially negating falling damage.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti Strigids know that the best exit is often the one no one noticed being prepared.

### Arneria — Vigil of the Open Sky

You know the _gust_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _feather fall_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using wind, cloud movement, and a period of silent observation as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Strigids understand that an answer may arrive as a sound, an absence, or the moment a flame refuses to move.

### Al’Ar — Hush Before Thunder

When you use **Skyward Pulse**, you may release a sudden cry carried by the force of your wings.

Creatures of your choice within 10 feet of you must succeed on a Constitution saving throw against your Skyward Pulse saving throw DC or be deafened until the start of your next turn.

Once you use this trait, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Al’Ari Strigids know that silence does not oppose the storm.

It gives the thunder somewhere to arrive.

## XIII. Playing a Strigid

When creating a Strigid character, consider:

- What do they notice that others routinely miss?
    
- Are they comfortable with silence?
    
- What kinds of noise overwhelm or irritate them?
    
- What responsibility are they currently watching over?
    
- What absence have they chosen to preserve?
    
- Have they ever noticed danger but waited too long to act?
    
- Do they enjoy being treated as wise?
    
- What question are they afraid they cannot answer?
    
- How do they behave when they do not understand something?
    
- Do they protect privacy, or use it to avoid vulnerability?
    
- What keeps them awake when they should be resting?
    
- Who would they trust to take over their watch?
    
- How do they regard the Sky-Reeves: ancestors, fellow watchkeepers, reckless riders, or subjects of historical study?
    
- Would they undertake the Quiet Tithe?
    
- What secret would they be willing to surrender to the wind?
    

A Strigid may be solemn or ridiculous, guarded or affectionate, patient or impulsive.

Their lineage teaches them to listen.

It does not require them to remain silent.

>

> 
> Reasonable sound accommodations must be provided where machinery, magical amplification, alarms, or repeated high-volume signals create a documented risk of sensory injury.
