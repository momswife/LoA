**Folk Classification:** [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/Avianfolk Overview|Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Limnid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage and Homeland Variance  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Unrestricted with Environmental Accommodations  
**Common Names:** Rainfolk, Mistkin, Slickwings  
**Date of Filing:** 4th Cycle, 3388 A.D.

## I. Lineage Overview

**Limnids** are a moisture-dependent Avianfolk lineage distinguished by gliding membranes, climbing adaptations, sensitive skin, and bodies shaped by humid forests, rain-soaked cliffs, seasonal wetlands, and waterways surrounded by dense vegetation.

Their recognized sublineages include flying frogs, wandering salamanders, arboreal salamanders, newts, and flying snakes.

Limnids are not primarily aquatic people.

Most spend the greater part of their lives upon land, within trees, along wet cliffs, or among elevated settlements. Some enter water regularly. Others return to it only during particular seasons, for reproduction, healing, religious rites, or protection from extreme heat.

A few avoid deep water almost entirely.

What unites Limnids is not a permanent life beneath the surface.

It is dependence upon moisture and the ability to move between layers of an environment that other peoples often treat as separate:

- Water and air
    
- Branch and ground
    
- Rain and shelter
    
- Stillness and sudden motion
    
- Concealment and exposure
    

Limnids rarely possess true powered flight.

They glide through webbing, flattened bodies, expanded skin, flexible tails, or membranes stretched between the limbs. Some leap from great height. Others launch through explosive strength from lower positions.

Their aerial movement is usually brief.

It is also precise.

A Limnid does not need to remain in the sky.

They need only reach the next wet branch before the thing behind them does.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Limnids possess no single accepted origin.

Their oldest communities have been found in rainforests, cloud forests, moss-covered highlands, river canopies, seasonal marshes, humid caverns, waterfall cliffs, and groves where mist remains long after the surrounding land has dried.

Some traditions claim the first Limnids were born when rain struck leaves but refused to fall the final distance to the ground.

Others teach that their ancestors lived beneath an endless flood until one among them climbed the tallest drowned tree and discovered the sky.

A Flying Snake account claims that the earliest Limnid simply watched winged peoples argue over ownership of the air and crossed it without asking.

These stories disagree on whether Limnids began in water, earth, or canopy.

Most agree that remaining in one place was never the point.

### Rain Paths

Limnid history is often preserved through **Rain Paths**.

A Rain Path is a route defined by the availability of moisture rather than distance alone. It may connect:

- Seasonal pools
    
- Mist-heavy valleys
    
- Sheltered cliff faces
    
- Trees that collect water within broad leaves
    
- Rain barrels maintained by allied settlements
    
- Cavern entrances where condensation gathers
    
- Healing springs
    
- Breeding pools
    
- Shrines opened only during the wet season
    
- Structures where travelers may safely rehydrate their skin
    

A route that is safe during the rainy season may become deadly during drought.

A direct path may be abandoned in favor of a longer journey through shade.

Limnid travelers therefore record temperature, humidity, water quality, fungal growth, prevailing wind, and the condition of sheltered resting places.

To outsiders, a Rain Path may appear inefficient.

To a Limnid, a shorter road that dries the body before arrival is not a road.

### The First Pool

Many Limnid communities maintain a tradition known as the **First Pool**.

The First Pool may be:

- The water where a child first entered the world
    
- The spring where a household was founded
    
- A basin used for healing
    
- A seasonal gathering place
    
- A pool where travelers prepare before migration
    
- A symbolic vessel carried by a community that has lost its homeland
    

Not every Limnid is born in water.

Wandering and Arboreal Salamander communities may hatch from eggs kept in damp hollows or moss beds without an aquatic stage. Flying Snakes generally possess no biological need for a breeding pool at all.

Even so, many participate in First Pool traditions as cultural inheritance.

The pool represents the place where life was first entrusted to an environment beyond the self.

### Shedding and Renewal

Limnid cultures often treat the shedding, renewal, or restoration of skin as a meaningful event.

The practice varies by sublineage.

Some shed visibly. Others renew damaged outer layers gradually. Many require periods of rest, moisture, and reduced exposure while healing.

A discarded skin may be:

- Buried
    
- Burned
    
- Preserved
    
- Used in medicine
    
- Placed in running water
    
- Woven into family records
    
- Left where rain can dissolve it
    

The symbolism is not always rebirth.

Sometimes a discarded skin simply represents something that once protected a person but no longer fits.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Limnid anatomy varies more dramatically than that of most Avianfolk lineages.

Most stand between **4 feet 8 inches and 6 feet 3 inches**, though Flying Snakes may possess elongated lower bodies and unusual upright postures.

Their forms commonly include:

- Broad webbing between the fingers and toes
    
- Skin flaps along the arms, legs, or torso
    
- Flattened tails
    
- Flexible ribs
    
- Adhesive pads
    
- Strong jumping muscles
    
- Low-profile bodies suited to clinging against surfaces
    
- Smooth, granular, scaled, or lightly textured skin
    

### Gliding Structures

Limnids do not share one universal wing structure.

Flying Frogs rely primarily upon extensive webbing and lateral skin flaps.

Salamanders spread their limbs, flatten their bodies, and use their tails to control descent.

Newt sublineages possess broader tail membranes and webbing than their ancestral forms, allowing brief glides through wet forest and cavern environments.

Flying Snakes extend and flatten the body by spreading flexible ribs, creating a broad surface capable of generating lift during aerial undulation.

These adaptations produce different styles of movement.

Some Limnids descend slowly.

Others cross horizontal distance quickly.

Most can make sharp adjustments shortly before landing.

### Skin

Limnid skin is sensitive to moisture, heat, chemicals, and environmental contamination.

Depending upon sublineage, it may be:

- Smooth
    
- Damp
    
- Softly scaled
    
- Granular
    
- Covered in fine ridges
    
- Protected by a thin mucous layer
    
- Capable of limited color change
    
- Marked by bright warning patterns
    
- Naturally camouflaged against bark, moss, leaves, or stone
    

Healthy skin should not be assumed to feel wet at all times.

Many Limnids maintain moisture through oils, salves, wraps, magical treatments, or carefully regulated environments.

Prolonged exposure to dry heat may cause pain, fatigue, cracking, impaired breathing, or loss of gliding control.

### Adhesion

Many Limnids possess fingers, toes, scales, or body structures capable of gripping wet and vertical surfaces.

They may climb:

- Bark
    
- Mossy stone
    
- Glass
    
- Plaster
    
- Damp cavern walls
    
- Narrow branches
    
- Hanging roots
    
- Ceilings with sufficient texture
    

Oil, dust, frost, magical residue, and polished metal may interfere with this ability.

A surface being wet does not automatically make it safe.

### Poison and Venom

Some Limnid populations possess defensive toxins, venom, or irritating skin secretions.

These features are neither universal nor inherently lethal.

Where present, they may serve as protection against predators, infection, or parasites.

Ministry law treats weaponization, harvesting, or forced extraction of Limnid toxins as bodily exploitation.

A brightly colored Limnid should not automatically be assumed poisonous.

A dull-colored Limnid should not automatically be assumed safe to lick.

The Ministry regrets that this sentence became necessary.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Limnid communities are frequently organized around **rainhouses**, **pools**, **canopy paths**, and seasonal gatherings.

A settlement may remain occupied throughout the year or become active only when rain returns.

Some Limnid households migrate vertically rather than across great distance. They descend toward rivers during drought and return to the upper canopy when the wet season restores water to the leaves.

### Rainhouses

A **Rainhouse** is a dwelling or public structure designed to retain humidity and provide safe shelter during dry weather.

Rainhouses may include:

- Shaded pools
    
- Moss walls
    
- Condensation channels
    
- Covered landing branches
    
- Damp sleeping chambers
    
- Clay reservoirs
    
- Mist-producing devices
    
- Medicinal gardens
    
- Separate dry rooms for visitors
    
- Ventilation designed to prevent harmful mold
    

A well-maintained Rainhouse balances moisture with cleanliness.

Too little humidity injures the residents.

Too much stagnant moisture encourages disease.

The distinction between a Rainhouse and a poorly ventilated basement is maintenance.

### Pool-Kin

Many Limnids recognize **pool-kin**: people connected by shared care of a water source, hatchery, healing basin, Rainhouse, or seasonal refuge.

Pool-kin need not be biologically related.

The relationship may include obligations to:

- Maintain water quality
    
- Shelter travelers during drought
    
- Protect eggs or young
    
- Carry news between seasonal settlements
    
- Share medicine
    
- Repair canopy routes
    
- Search for missing members
    
- Defend the pool from contamination
    

Damaging a community’s protected pool is often treated as an attack upon the entire lineage group residing there.

### Stillness

Limnids frequently spend long periods remaining nearly motionless.

This may be practical. Sudden movement reveals position, wastes energy, disturbs a hunting ground, or makes a gliding route less predictable.

Stillness may also be social.

Limnid companions may sit together without speaking, communicating through posture, breath, skin pattern, eye movement, or subtle touch.

Outsiders sometimes interpret this as discomfort or emotional distance.

Limnids may instead regard constant speech as evidence that no one is observing what is actually happening.

### The Shared Leap

Some Limnid communities recognize a rite called the **Shared Leap**.

Two or more people climb to an elevated position and descend together, each responsible for tracking the others’ route.

The rite may mark:

- Adulthood
    
- Partnership
    
- Reconciliation
    
- Membership in a household
    
- The beginning of a journey
    
- Acceptance into a delving company
    
- Trust after betrayal
    

The purpose is not to land in the same place.

It is to arrive knowing that someone watched the entire crossing.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Limnid communities commonly admire:

- Adaptability
    
- Patience
    
- Environmental awareness
    
- Emotional sensitivity
    
- Precision
    
- Respect for necessary retreat
    
- The ability to remain still without becoming passive
    
- Renewal after hardship
    

These values can produce careful observers, capable survivors, compassionate healers, and companions who understand that withdrawal can sometimes preserve the strength needed to return.

They can also encourage avoidance.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Patience|Waiting for the right moment|Delaying necessary action|
|Sensitivity|Recognizing subtle changes|Becoming overwhelmed by discomfort|
|Adaptability|Changing with the environment|Lacking stable commitments|
|Retreat|Withdrawing from unwinnable danger|Avoiding every confrontation|
|Renewal|Allowing oneself to change|Discarding responsibility with the old self|
|Stillness|Observing before acting|Appearing indifferent|
|Precision|Choosing the safest route|Freezing when certainty is impossible|
|Environmental awareness|Protecting shared resources|Excluding outsiders from essential water|

A recurring Limnid anxiety is the fear of **remaining exposed after the environment has changed**.

A branch dries. A pool recedes. A trusted shelter becomes contaminated. A relationship that once protected someone begins to harm them.

Limnids are often taught to recognize these changes early.

Some become exceptionally good at leaving danger.

Others leave whenever comfort shifts, abandoning situations that might have been repaired.

### Softness and Survival

Limnids are frequently perceived as physically delicate because many possess soft skin, small frames, or strong environmental needs.

This assumption ignores the environments from which they come.

Limnids survive:

- Predators
    
- Seasonal drought
    
- violent rain
    
- Flooded forests
    
- Slippery cliffs
    
- Unstable branches
    
- Disease-rich wetlands
    
- Long falls
    
- Sudden changes in temperature
    

Their survival rarely depends upon overpowering the environment.

It depends upon feeling the change before others notice it.

### The Burden of Transformation

Frog- and newt-like Limnids are often expected to possess strong cultural ties to transformation, rebirth, or personal reinvention.

Some do.

Others dislike the expectation that every change in appearance, profession, identity, or relationship must become a symbolic metamorphosis.

A person is permitted to change without turning the experience into a lesson for everyone else.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Limnid communities disagree over:

- Whether seasonal pools should remain under local control
    
- Whether dry-region settlements are sustainable
    
- Whether toxic defenses should be registered with regional authorities
    
- Whether sacred pools should accept outsiders during drought
    
- Whether old Rain Paths should be abandoned as climates change
    
- Whether artificial humidity weakens traditional life
    
- Whether Flying Snakes belong within the lineage
    
- Whether skin shed during rites can be preserved by museums
    
- Whether retreat is a form of wisdom or avoidance
    
- Whether environmental need has become too central to Limnid identity
    

These debates often become most intense during drought, when cultural principles must compete with limited water.

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Limnids occupied the wet lower reaches, cloud gardens, and rain-fed infrastructure of the ancient [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]].

They were rarely counted among the great high-altitude riders.

Their importance lay elsewhere.

They maintained the places where cloud became water.

### Rainwardens

Sky-Reeve **Rainwardens** tended reservoirs, condensation gardens, mist channels, and living structures designed to collect moisture from passing clouds.

The Eyrie-Spire of Taellen required enormous quantities of water.

Its elevated position made ordinary wells impossible.

Rainwardens therefore managed systems of:

- Cloud nets
    
- Condensation glass
    
- Moss cisterns
    
- Hanging gardens
    
- Rain channels
    
- Emergency reservoirs
    
- Living roots carrying water between levels
    
- Purification pools
    
- Mist chambers used for healing
    

Limnids moved easily through the wet surfaces and narrow spaces surrounding these systems.

Their work provided drinking water, irrigation, cooling, medical care, and emergency protection during fires.

The great Sky-Reeve records gave more attention to those who rode lightning.

The people living there were more immediately grateful to those who ensured they could drink afterward.

### Mistways

Limnid routekeepers maintained aerial paths known as **Mistways**.

These routes passed through cloud gardens, waterfalls, rain curtains, forested cliffs, and the humid lower regions beneath major aeries.

Mistways were used by:

- Young flyers
    
- Gliders
    
- injured pilots
    
- Water carriers
    
- Healers
    
- Nonfeathered aerial peoples
    
- Travelers seeking cover from violent upper winds
    

The routes often depended upon living vegetation and seasonal water.

When the Sky-Reeve clans scattered, many Mistways withered, collapsed, or became isolated.

Some survive as overgrown ruins hidden behind waterfalls or within cloud forests.

### The Dew Tithe

Limnid tradition preserves an unofficial rite known as the **Dew Tithe**.

Before sunrise, a Rainwarden gathered water condensed upon the highest safe leaves, cables, or structures beneath the Eyrie-Spire.

The first drops were returned to the garden rather than consumed.

The offering recognized that every great storm eventually became small enough to rest upon a leaf.

Unlike the dramatic Crimson Tithes, the Dew Tithe demanded no danger.

Its importance lay in repetition.

It was performed every morning that water appeared.

### Mist-Riders

A small number of Limnids participated in Stormriding through gliding harnesses, launch towers, and carefully chosen descending currents.

They were known as **Mist-Riders**.

Most avoided the violent hearts of storms. Instead, they moved along rain bands, cloud edges, and the lower pressure currents beneath the main tempest.

Mist-Riders were exceptionally skilled at:

- Entering cloud without losing orientation
    
- Landing upon wet structures
    
- Navigating waterfalls and heavy rain
    
- Detecting temperature changes
    
- Locating emergency water
    
- Descending through narrow terrain
    

High-altitude riders sometimes dismissed them as cautious.

The Mist-Riders noted that the purpose of a flight was usually to reach somewhere.

## VII. Limnids in Modern Aerathon

Limnids live throughout the Five Greater Regions, though their largest communities remain concentrated around humid forests, rivers, wetlands, caverns, and regions with reliable rainfall.

They are frequently associated with medicine, environmental care, climbing, scouting, water management, and biological research. These professions reflect cultural history rather than compulsory ability.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Limnids inhabit river woods, orchard ponds, mill streams, shaded valleys, mossy ruins, and household Rainhouses.

Allemancian communities often integrate Limnid water needs into ordinary village maintenance.

Rain barrels, shaded gardens, springhouses, and clean roadside basins may serve both residents and travelers.

Limnids frequently participate in monitoring wells, treating crop disease, caring for orchard ecosystems, and locating contamination before it spreads.

The health of one pool is understood as part of the health of the entire settlement.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Limnids work in water treatment, environmental monitoring, industrial inspection, medical research, condensation systems, and access to wet or vertical machinery.

Oric facilities employ Limnid specialists to detect:

- Chemical leakage
    
- Changes in humidity
    
- Polluted runoff
    
- Mold growth
    
- Heat imbalance
    
- Damage within cooling systems
    
- Contaminants that affect living tissue before ordinary instruments register them
    

This work can be hazardous.

Oric law requires protective equipment suited to permeable or highly sensitive skin, though enforcement remains inconsistent outside major Houses.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], water access, climate control, and vertical urban space are all commodities.

Limnids work in horticulture, medicine, performance, environmental design, luxury baths, architecture, courier services, toxin research, and urban water law.

Wealthy Vinyoti districts may maintain elaborate mist gardens and artificial waterfalls while poorer Limnid neighborhoods struggle to secure clean communal pools.

This inequality has produced strong Limnid political movements centered upon water as a public necessity rather than private luxury.

Contracts involving naturally produced toxins, shed skin, or biological compounds remain especially controversial.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Limnids participate in purification rites, rain vigils, healing traditions, funerary washing, seasonal pilgrimages, and ceremonies of renewal.

Certain temples maintain sacred pools that are emptied and refilled according to ritual cycles.

Some Limnid traditions teach that water should never remain sacred merely because it once was. A pool must be tested, cleaned, and renewed like any other living trust.

This belief has brought Limnid reformers into conflict with authorities who consider ancient ritual waters beyond ordinary inspection.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Limnids flourish in monsoon forests, storm coasts, waterfall towns, mangrove districts, cliff settlements, and communities rebuilt repeatedly after floods.

Al’Ari Limnids are accustomed to abundance arriving violently and disappearing quickly.

Their Rainhouses are often designed to collect excess water during storms and preserve it through dry periods.

Routes shift with every season.

A broken bridge may become a pool wall. A fallen tree may become a new glide path. A flooded street may serve as a temporary gathering place.

Al’Ari Limnids do not assume the environment will remain stable.

They build communities that can move with it.

## VIII. Limnids and Delving

Limnids are commonly recruited as scouts, climbers, environmental observers, alchemical specialists, healers, and explorers of vertical or water-damaged chambers.

Their bodies allow them to cross gaps, cling to wet surfaces, and detect subtle changes in humidity or contamination.

Experienced Limnid delvers examine:

- Condensation
    
- Water taste and smell
    
- Fungal growth
    
- Skin irritation
    
- Air temperature
    
- Changes in stone moisture
    
- Whether a wall remains climbable
    
- Whether a pool is natural, magical, or alive
    
- Whether the chamber is becoming drier
    
- Whether rain falls from a real ceiling
    

The Labyrinth frequently creates environments that appear safe to moisture-dependent peoples.

A pool may soothe the skin while slowly altering memory. Rain may fall upward. A damp wall may absorb whatever touches it. A humid chamber may be the breath of something enormous.

### The Skin Test

Limnid delving schools teach that unknown substances should never be tested directly against the body.

This rule is known as **the Skin Test**, despite explicitly forbidding the use of skin.

A sample should instead be examined using:

- Cloth
    
- Paper
    
- Treated wood
    
- Alchemical strips
    
- Glass
    
- Plant material
    
- Disposable tools
    
- Magic
    

Limnids who can detect irritants rapidly are often pressured to test them personally.

The Ministry classifies this practice as negligent exposure.

### Wet Stone Doctrine

Limnid delvers follow a principle called the **Wet Stone Doctrine**:

> A surface that held you once has proven only that it held you once.

Water, slime, magic, shifting gravity, and living stone can alter a climbing route between crossings.

Handholds should be tested again during the return.

### The Drying Chamber

Some Labyrinth environments gradually remove moisture from everything within them.

The process may be too slow for other peoples to notice.

For Limnids, the danger can become immediate.

Parties traveling with Limnid members are instructed to carry:

- Clean water
    
- Protective wraps
    
- Skin salves
    
- Portable shade
    
- Mist-producing magic or equipment
    
- A planned retreat threshold
    

Waiting until the Limnid collapses is not evidence that the chamber was safe until that moment.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Limnid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Wisdom score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Limnids commonly stand between 4 feet 8 inches and 6 feet 3 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 30 feet.

You have a climbing speed of 30 feet and a swimming speed of 20 feet.

### Adhesive Grip

You can climb difficult surfaces, including wet walls and ceilings with sufficient texture, without needing to make an ability check.

To remain attached to a ceiling, you must have at least two limbs free.

You have advantage on ability checks and saving throws made to avoid falling or losing your grip.

### Gliding Form

When you begin falling and are not wearing medium or heavy armor, you can spread your webbing, flatten your body, or extend your gliding membranes without using an action.

While gliding:

- You descend no more than 60 feet during each of your turns.
    
- You take no falling damage.
    
- For every 1 foot you descend, you may move up to 1 foot horizontally.
    
- You may turn freely during the glide if sufficient space is available.
    

You cannot gain altitude through this trait alone.

If you are incapacitated, restrained, or unable to extend your gliding structures, you cannot use this trait.

### Sudden Spring

As a bonus action, you leap to an unoccupied space you can see within 20 feet.

This movement:

- Does not count against your speed
    
- Does not provoke opportunity attacks
    
- Can cross gaps and difficult terrain
    
- May move you up to 10 feet vertically
    
- Must end upon a surface capable of supporting you or transition immediately into your Gliding Form
    

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

### Moisture Sense

You gain proficiency in the Survival skill.

By spending 1 minute examining the air, soil, vegetation, or nearby surfaces, you can determine:

- Whether natural water is present within 1 mile
    
- The general direction in which it lies
    
- Whether the immediate environment is becoming significantly wetter or drier
    
- Whether visible water shows obvious signs of natural contamination
    

This trait does not identify magical poisons, invisible creatures, or substances that perfectly imitate water.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Limnid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Flying Frog Sublineage

Flying Frog Limnids possess broad webbing between the fingers and toes, adhesive pads, powerful legs, and skin flaps that assist with gliding.

Their skin may display:

- Green
    
- Blue
    
- Yellow
    
- Orange
    
- Brown
    
- Black
    
- Cream
    
- Brilliantly contrasting warning patterns
    

Flying Frog communities are common in rainforest canopies, flooded forests, hanging gardens, and settlements built around broad-leafed plants.

They are capable of launching from relatively low positions compared with many gliding peoples.

Their strong jumping ability has produced a reputation for impulsiveness.

Most Flying Frogs prefer to inspect the landing first.

### Wandering Salamander Sublineage

Wandering Salamander Limnids possess long limbs, gripping feet, flattened bodies, and flexible tails capable of precise aerial adjustment.

Their coloration often resembles redwood bark, moss, lichen, wet stone, or dark leaves.

Many spend almost their entire lives above the ground and possess no aquatic childhood stage.

Wandering Salamander communities frequently inhabit enormous trees, cliff forests, and elevated settlements where a fall may extend for hundreds of feet.

They are among the lineage’s most precise controlled gliders.

### Arboreal Salamander Sublineage

Arboreal Salamander Limnids tend to be heavier and more powerfully built than Wandering Salamanders.

They possess strong jaws, muscular tails, broad gripping feet, and bodies suited to climbing through dense branches, hollows, and wet stone.

Their coloration commonly includes brown, black, cream, grey, rust, and irregular mottling.

Arboreal Salamander communities often construct homes within living trees, cliff cracks, and enclosed canopy spaces.

Their heavier bodies make them less efficient over long glides but highly capable during steep, controlled descents.

### Newt Sublineage

Newt Limnids possess smooth or textured skin, long tails, webbed extremities, and expanded membranes along the limbs and tail.

Their coloration may include:

- Black
    
- Brown
    
- Olive
    
- Orange
    
- Red
    
- Blue
    
- Yellow
    
- White spotting
    
- Brightly colored undersides
    

Many Newt communities spend most of the year in forests, meadows, humid ruins, or cavern systems and return to water only during particular seasons.

Their Aerathian anatomy permits short glides and controlled descents through webbing and tail movement.

Some Newt populations can regenerate damaged tissue more effectively than other Limnids, though no individual should be assumed capable of surviving catastrophic injury.

### Flying Snake Sublineage

Flying Snake Limnids possess elongated bodies, flexible ribs, scaled skin, and serpentine lower forms.

To glide, they flatten and widen the body while moving through the air in controlled lateral waves.

Their scales may display:

- Green
    
- Black
    
- Gold
    
- Red
    
- Orange
    
- Cream
    
- Blue
    
- Complex bands or diamond patterns
    

Flying Snake communities inhabit humid forests, cliff settlements, temple groves, and dense cities with closely spaced towers.

They are the primary reptilian branch of the Limnid classification.

Some scholars argue that they should possess a separate lineage designation. Flying Snake communities generally consider administrative discomfort insufficient reason to sever established cultural relationships.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Springhouse Knowledge

You gain proficiency in the Nature skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Limnids learn the location of shaded wells, clear streams, orchard pools, and the household that keeps clean water ready for travelers.

### Oria — Emergency Adhesion

You have advantage on saving throws made to resist being pushed, pulled, or knocked prone.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to move up to half your climbing speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric Limnids are trained to reach a stable surface before the machinery, scaffolding, or water system fails completely.

### Vinyot — Awning Descent

While using your Gliding Form, you may move up to 2 feet horizontally for every 1 foot you descend.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti Limnids learn to cross balconies, signs, market awnings, and privately owned roofs without landing long enough to receive an invoice.

### Arneria — Rite of First Rain

You know the _druidcraft_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _create or destroy water_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using rainwater, shed skin, leaves, or the reflection within a sacred pool as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Limnids understand that renewal does not erase what came before.

It determines what can continue afterward.

### Al’Ar — Monsoon Spring

When you use **Sudden Spring**, you may increase its distance to 35 feet and its vertical movement to 20 feet.

Once you enhance Sudden Spring in this way, you cannot do so again until you finish a long rest.

Al’Ari Limnids know that when the rain changes the route, hesitation is simply another surface becoming slippery.

## XIII. Playing a Limnid

When creating a Limnid character, consider:

- What level of humidity feels comfortable to them?
    
- How do they protect their skin while traveling?
    
- Were they raised near water, within a canopy, or among wet cliffs?
    
- Does their community maintain a First Pool?
    
- What does water symbolize to them?
    
- Do they enjoy swimming?
    
- Are they patient, or merely difficult to read?
    
- When do they retreat?
    
- Have they ever left too early?
    
- Have they ever remained exposed too long?
    
- What part of themselves have they shed?
    
- What former protection no longer fits them?
    
- Who watched their first Shared Leap?
    
- What environmental change frightens them most?
    
- How do they react to dry cities or open plains?
    
- Do they feel kinship with other Avianfolk?
    
- How do they regard Flying Snakes within the lineage?
    
- What do they believe the Sky-Reeves owed the Rainwardens?
    
- Would they perform the Dew Tithe?
    
- What place would they preserve through a drought?
    
- What would they abandon to save the water that keeps their community alive?
    

A Limnid may be quiet or exuberant, cautious or sudden, deeply rooted or seasonally wandering.

Their lineage teaches them to survive between environments.

It does not require them to belong fully to either.

> [!note] MDO Registry Advisory  
> Limnid lineage does not indicate aquatic residence, poisonous physiology, emotional passivity, regenerative ability, willingness to handle hazardous substances, or suitability for water-management labor.
> 
> Ministry personnel are forbidden from assigning contamination testing, toxin extraction, flooded-chamber entry, or hazardous climbing duties solely on the basis of lineage.
> 
> Limnid skin, mucus, venom, shed tissue, eggs, and biological compounds may not be harvested, purchased, replicated, or used as arcane components without informed consent.
> 
> Environmental accommodations may include clean water access, shade, humidity control, protective skin treatments, and relief from prolonged dry heat.
> 
> Deliberate dehydration, contamination of protected pools, or denial of necessary moisture constitutes a protected-rights violation under Ministry charter.
