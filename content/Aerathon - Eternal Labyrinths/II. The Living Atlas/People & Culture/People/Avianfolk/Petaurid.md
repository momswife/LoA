> — Sera Many-Landings, Span-Keeper of Vaelwood

# Petaurid

**Ministry of Delving Operations — The Living Atlas**  
**Folk Classification:** [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/Avianfolk Overview|Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Petaurid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage and Homeland Variance  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Unrestricted  
**Common Names:** Gliderfolk, Sailkin, Canopyfolk  
**Date of Filing:** 3rd Cycle, 3388 A.D.

## I. Lineage Overview

**Petaurids** are a mammalian Avianfolk lineage distinguished by climbing adaptations, highly flexible bodies, balancing tails, and a broad membrane of skin called a **patagium** stretched between the arms and legs.

Their recognized sublineages include sugar gliders, flying squirrels, colugos, feathertail gliders, greater gliders, and anomalures.

Petaurids cannot achieve sustained powered flight through their natural anatomy.

They glide.

To an observer accustomed to feathered Avianfolk, the distinction may appear limiting. A Petaurid cannot ordinarily rise from an open field, hover above the ground, or continue climbing through empty air by strength of wing alone.

Yet gliding is not failed flight.

It is a different relationship with height.

A Petaurid crosses distance by reading where they stand, what waits ahead, how the air is moving, and which surfaces can be trusted. Their movement depends upon trees, towers, cliffs, rafters, bridges, ropes, elevated roads, and the labor of those who maintain them.

This has shaped cultures in which routes matter as much as destinations.

A single branch is temporary.

A network of branches can become a homeland.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Petaurids possess no single accepted ancestral homeland.

Their oldest communities have been identified in humid forests, temperate woodlands, high-canopy jungles, cliffside groves, enormous fungal forests, densely built cities, and ancient settlements connected by towers and elevated roads.

Some traditions claim that the first Petaurid climbed a tree to escape a great flood.

When the waters continued rising, they leapt toward another tree. The wind caught the loose skin beneath their arms and carried them farther than fear had allowed them to imagine.

Another tradition teaches that Petaurids were once ordinary ground-dwelling people who built their homes higher with each generation until the earth forgot how to call them back.

A Colugo account claims the first Petaurid was created when the moon grew tired of watching creatures fall and stretched a cloak between their limbs.

The myths differ, but most contain the same central truth:

A crossing begins before the leap.

### Branch Maps

Petaurid history is frequently preserved through **Branch Maps**.

A Branch Map records a network of connected elevations rather than a conventional ground route. It may identify:

- Trees capable of supporting a full household
    
- Rooftops that permit safe landing
    
- Towers aligned with favorable winds
    
- Cliffs with climbable stone
    
- Public ropes and suspended bridges
    
- Seasonal foliage
    
- Structures weakened by age
    
- Private landings requiring permission
    
- Places where a person can rest without descending to the ground
    
- Alternate routes used when storms close the primary crossing
    

Branch Maps may be carved into wood, embroidered into gliding cloaks, represented through knotted cords, or memorized through movement.

Some are understood only when viewed from above.

Others must be touched in sequence.

A map may remain geographically accurate while becoming useless because a property owner has barred access to one critical roof. Petaurid navigators therefore record social conditions alongside physical ones.

A route is not open merely because the distance can be crossed.

Someone must still permit the landing.

### The First and Second Landing

Many Petaurid histories distinguish between a **first landing** and a **second landing**.

The first landing is the place where a traveler expected to arrive.

The second is the place that saved them when the first failed.

A second landing might be:

- A lower branch
    
- A stranger’s balcony
    
- A roof not designed to bear weight
    
- A market awning
    
- A rope caught at the last moment
    
- Another person who braced themselves against the impact
    
- A body of water beneath a broken span
    

Petaurid families often preserve the names of those who provided second landings.

Such names may remain honored for generations.

The lesson is not that preparation is useless.

It is that no plan survives alone.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Petaurids are generally light to medium in build.

Most stand between **4 feet 6 inches and 5 feet 10 inches**, though Colugo and Greater Glider populations may be taller or broader.

Their bodies are adapted for climbing, leaping, balancing, and controlled descent. Strong hands and feet allow them to grip bark, stone, ropes, beams, and rough walls.

Many possess curved claws suited to climbing. These claws are useful tools but less effective as weapons than the talons of Raptorids or Strigids.

### The Patagium

The defining feature of Petaurid anatomy is the **patagium**.

This membrane generally extends between the wrists, arms, sides, legs, and ankles. Its exact size and attachment vary by sublineage.

When the limbs are spread, the patagium forms a broad surface capable of catching air and converting a fall into forward movement.

A Petaurid controls their glide through:

- Limb position
    
- Tail movement
    
- Changes in body angle
    
- Tension across the membrane
    
- Subtle shifts of weight
    
- Reading wind before and during the crossing
    

The patagium is living tissue.

It contains nerves, blood vessels, and muscles capable of adjusting its shape. It may be covered in fine fur, bare skin, or a combination of both.

Injury can severely impair movement.

Armor and clothing must be designed so the membrane can extend freely. Ordinary sleeves, tight belts, or rigid side plates may prevent safe gliding.

### Tails

Most Petaurids possess long tails used for balance and aerial steering.

Tail forms include:

- Broad and furred
    
- Flat and rudder-like
    
- Long and narrow
    
- Feather-edged
    
- Partially prehensile
    
- Covered in overlapping scales near the base
    

A Petaurid’s tail may reveal changes in balance, focus, agitation, or intent before these emotions become visible elsewhere.

Touching or pulling it without permission is dangerous and insulting.

### Eyes and Senses

Many Petaurids possess large eyes adapted to twilight, nighttime, or shadowed forest canopies.

Their hearing and sense of balance are often highly developed. Some are capable of judging distance through tiny changes in airflow, branch movement, or sound.

Bright open environments may feel exposed to Petaurids accustomed to layered canopy cover.

Dense architecture often feels safer than an empty plain, even when the plain contains no visible danger.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Petaurid communities are often organized through **spans**, **landings**, and **route-houses**.

A span is a group of people connected by mutual access and responsibility. Members may maintain different homes but share routes, platforms, bridges, and emergency shelters.

A landing is any place where a traveler may safely arrive.

It may be no more than a reinforced branch.

It may also be a household, inn, temple, tower, public platform, or entire settlement.

### Landing Houses

A **Landing House** is a household or institution that maintains a recognized refuge along a travel route.

Landing Houses commonly provide:

- Water
    
- Basic food
    
- Membrane salves
    
- Rope
    
- Shelter from weather
    
- Route updates
    
- Repairs
    
- Medical assistance
    
- A safe platform from which to resume travel
    

Some operate commercially.

Others are maintained through community obligation.

A Landing House is not required to provide indefinite shelter, but refusing a traveler in immediate danger is considered deeply shameful in most Petaurid traditions.

### The Catch

Petaurid communities often designate people to serve as **Catchers** during construction, migration, ceremonies, and difficult crossings.

A Catcher waits beneath or beyond the intended landing. Their role is to guide, brace, signal, or physically assist if the traveler loses control.

Serving as Catcher is not considered a lesser role.

The person making the crossing receives the attention.

The Catcher accepts the impact.

A common Petaurid saying holds:

> The leap is remembered. The Catcher is trusted.

### Route Kinship

Kinship may form through repeated travel.

People who cross the same spans, maintain the same shelters, or routinely act as one another’s Catchers may become **route-kin**.

Route-kin obligations can include:

- Warning one another about damaged crossings
    
- Providing temporary shelter
    
- Carrying messages
    
- Repairing shared infrastructure
    
- Searching for overdue travelers
    
- Maintaining alternate routes
    
- Teaching children the safe sequence of landings
    

A route-kin bond may last longer than the route that created it.

### Ground Settlements

Not all Petaurids live above the ground.

Some inhabit ordinary towns, burrows, caves, low forests, or open regions where gliding is used only occasionally.

These communities may build artificial launch towers, raised paths, and climbing walls. Others adapt to predominantly terrestrial movement.

Petaurids who rarely glide remain fully part of the lineage.

Aerial ability is not a measure of cultural legitimacy.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Petaurid communities commonly admire:

- Foresight
    
- Adaptability
    
- Balance
    
- Trustworthiness
    
- Practical courage
    
- Awareness of one’s surroundings
    
- Maintenance of shared routes
    
- The ability to commit after preparation is complete
    

These values can produce reliable planners, careful scouts, skilled builders, and companions who understand that safety is created collectively.

They can also encourage hesitation and excessive caution.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Preparation|Anticipating danger before acting|Refusing to act without certainty|
|Adaptability|Choosing a new route when needed|Avoiding direct confrontation|
|Independence|Crossing difficult ground alone|Rejecting support|
|Mutual reliance|Maintaining safe routes for others|Enforcing conformity through access|
|Caution|Respecting genuine risk|Treating unfamiliarity as danger|
|Commitment|Following through once a choice is made|Remaining on a failing trajectory|
|Balance|Considering several possible outcomes|Avoiding strong convictions|
|Resourcefulness|Using available structures creatively|Taking liberties with others’ property|

A recurring Petaurid anxiety is the fear of **committing to a crossing without a safe place to land**.

This may appear in literal travel, relationships, careers, politics, faith, and personal identity.

Some Petaurids refuse to leave one situation until another has been fully secured.

Others maintain several alternatives and commit to none of them.

A few leap recklessly to prove they are not afraid, then resent anyone who fails to catch them.

### The Visible Exit

Petaurids often feel more comfortable when they understand how to leave a space.

A meeting room with only one door, a contract without a termination clause, a relationship that demands immediate permanence, or an oath with no provision for changed circumstances may all create unease.

Outsiders sometimes interpret this as disloyalty.

Petaurids may instead regard visible exits as the foundation of genuine commitment.

A person who could leave but chooses to remain has made a meaningful decision.

A person trapped in place has not.

### The Myth of Timidity

Petaurids are frequently portrayed as nervous or cowardly because they inspect routes before crossing them.

This misunderstands their movement.

A powered flyer can correct direction, gain height, or retreat after takeoff.

A Petaurid has fewer opportunities to revise the decision once the leap begins.

Caution is not the absence of courage.

It is the recognition that some choices become real before they become reversible.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Petaurid communities disagree over:

- Whether private property can justly sever ancestral routes
    
- Whether all settlements must maintain emergency landings
    
- Whether powered flight has become culturally overvalued
    
- Whether young Petaurids are taught caution at the expense of ambition
    
- Whether route knowledge should remain within local communities
    
- Whether artificial launch towers weaken traditional canopy life
    
- Whether ground-settled Petaurids are losing their heritage
    
- Whether Catchers receive sufficient recognition
    
- Whether unsafe historic crossings should be preserved
    
- Whether a community owes refuge to travelers who ignored clear warnings
    

These disputes usually return to a single question:

Who is responsible for the landing—the traveler, the routekeeper, or everyone who knew the route was failing?

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Petaurids were present within the lower infrastructure of the ancient [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]], though surviving histories rarely grant them the prominence given to Stormriders, Wind-Mothers, or high-altitude pilots.

They served as span builders, platform keepers, harness workers, rescue gliders, current observers, and maintainers of the structures that allowed others to reach the upper winds.

### Span-Keepers

Sky-Reeve **Span-Keepers** maintained:

- Suspended bridges
    
- Launching platforms
    
- Emergency nets
    
- Harness lines
    
- Landing shelves
    
- Lower-current markers
    
- Rope lifts
    
- Supply crossings
    
- Evacuation routes beneath the Eyrie-Spire of Taellen
    

Petaurids were particularly skilled at inspecting structures from below and reaching damaged sections inaccessible to larger flyers.

Their work was essential.

It was also rarely celebrated.

A Stormrider who survived the East Tempest might receive a ceremonial title.

The Span-Keeper who repaired the failing launch platform before the flight usually received another repair assignment.

### The Low Wind

Petaurid records describe a system of sheltered air routes called the **Low Wind**.

These routes passed beneath the most violent currents, through cliffs, forests, towers, valleys, and artificial spans. They allowed young flyers, injured riders, nonflying personnel, and supply carriers to move between Sky-Reeve holdings.

The Low Wind was not a single atmospheric current.

It was an infrastructure network.

Its routes depended upon countless maintained landing points. When the Eyrie-Spire fell and the Sky-Reeve clans scattered, much of the Low Wind collapsed through neglect.

Modern Petaurid expeditions continue searching for surviving spans and hidden stations.

### The Catcher’s Tithe

Petaurid tradition preserves an unofficial Wind-Tithe known as the **Catcher’s Tithe**.

Before a dangerous flight, a rider gave a length of cord, a clasp, or a repaired piece of harness to the person responsible for their safe return.

The offering did not honor the storm.

It honored the person waiting beneath it.

Some Wind-Mothers rejected the rite because it shifted attention away from the cardinal currents.

Petaurids continued practicing it quietly.

Modern Catcher’s Tithes may be exchanged between delvers, pilots, climbers, soldiers, or anyone entering danger while another person maintains the route home.

### Drift-Riders

Petaurids occasionally participated in Stormriding through specialized harnesses and enlarged sail membranes.

These pilots were known as **Drift-Riders**.

They could not climb freely through the storm. Instead, they launched from great height and descended along carefully selected currents, converting loss of altitude into astonishing distance.

A Drift-Rider had little ability to reverse course once committed.

Their races therefore emphasized planning, wind knowledge, and precise landings.

Some Sky-Reeves dismissed Drift-Riding as less courageous than powered flight.

The Petaurid answer survives in several records:

> Then begin without wings and show us how easy it is.

## VII. Petaurids in Modern Aerathon

Petaurids live throughout the Five Greater Regions, particularly in forests, cliff settlements, dense cities, old towers, and communities with substantial vertical infrastructure.

They are frequently associated with construction, courier work, scouting, repair, route maintenance, and urban navigation. These professions reflect cultural opportunity rather than biological obligation.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Petaurids inhabit forests, orchard villages, mill towns, hillside settlements, and households built among old trees.

Public routes often include:

- Marked branches
    
- Reinforced barn rafters
    
- Rope bridges
    
- Raised platforms
    
- Painted landing signs
    
- Open loft windows
    
- Emergency lines stretched across rivers
    

Allemancian Petaurids place strong value upon maintaining modest crossings that benefit an entire community.

A repaired footbridge, cleared tree route, or accessible loft may be considered a more meaningful inheritance than an impressive monument.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Petaurids work throughout industrial maintenance, structural inspection, evacuation planning, tower construction, bridge engineering, and vertical transport.

Oric facilities frequently include Petaurid access routes through rafters, maintenance shafts, cable networks, and elevated platforms.

Their presence has significantly influenced workplace safety law.

A route suitable for a trained Petaurid inspector is not automatically safe for an untrained worker. Likewise, a narrow maintenance span cannot substitute for a proper evacuation path.

Oric Petaurids tend to view every crossing as a problem of load, angle, material, wind, and contingency.

Confidence is welcome.

Measurements are better.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], access to rooftops, towers, balconies, and aerial corridors carries substantial economic value.

Petaurids work in courier services, construction, performance, private security, urban planning, salvage, advertising, and property assessment.

Vinyoti law contains extensive rules concerning:

- Landing rights
    
- Rooftop access
    
- Aerial trespass
    
- Damage caused by emergency landings
    
- Maintenance of public launch points
    
- Liability for failed structures
    
- Ownership of routes passing above private property
    
- Commercial use of ancestral Branch Maps
    

Some wealthy districts maintain immaculate private spans while barring public passage.

Petaurid organizers argue that a city cannot call its air open while every safe landing has an owner.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Petaurids participate in elevated pilgrimages, shrine routes, cliff monasteries, ritual descents, and ceremonies centered upon trust.

Certain pilgrimages require the faithful to travel from one sacred landing to another without touching the ground.

The journey symbolizes commitment made in stages.

The pilgrim cannot see the entire route from its beginning. They must trust each station long enough to reach the next.

Arnerian Petaurids often serve as route witnesses, shrine keepers, and Catchers.

Some reject these traditions, arguing that faith should not be measured through physical aptitude.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Petaurids flourish in storm forests, cliff towns, dense dock-cities, traveling settlements, and regions where roads must be rebuilt after every major season.

Al’Ari routes are often temporary.

Ropes shift. Platforms are replaced. A fallen tree becomes part of the new crossing. A damaged sail becomes a bridge. A roof abandoned after one storm may serve as a public landing after the next.

Petaurids in Al’Ar commonly value improvisation over perfect preparation.

A route does not need to endure forever.

It needs to remain intact until everyone has crossed.

## VIII. Petaurids and Delving

Petaurids are commonly recruited as scouts, climbers, navigators, trap inspectors, rescue specialists, and explorers of vertical chambers.

Their ability to climb and glide allows them to cross gaps, descend shafts, and reach platforms without expending magical resources.

Experienced Petaurid delvers examine:

- Whether a landing surface can support their weight
    
- Whether stone is real or illusory
    
- The direction of air movement
    
- Alternate landing points
    
- Handholds along the intended route
    
- Whether a platform will remain after being touched
    
- Whether a visible branch belongs to a living creature
    
- What lies beneath the primary landing
    
- Whether the return route still exists
    

The Labyrinth often attacks certainty.

A surface may retreat as the traveler approaches. A branch may soften into smoke. A wall may become smooth after the Petaurid leaves it. A route crossed safely once may lead somewhere different on the return.

### The Two-Landing Rule

Petaurid delving schools teach the **Two-Landing Rule**:

> Never leap with only one acceptable ending.

Before crossing, the traveler identifies:

1. The intended landing.
    
2. The best survivable alternative.
    

If neither can be found, another route should be considered.

The rule is frequently ignored by adventurers with teleportation magic.

It becomes relevant again immediately after the magic fails.

### Catcher Placement

When a party must cross a dangerous gap, Petaurid procedure places the most reliable Catcher near the landing rather than sending every agile member ahead.

This may require the Petaurid to cross first and secure a rope, brace, or signal point for the others.

A delver who reaches safety and immediately continues onward has not completed the crossing.

The route is not finished until the group can follow.

### False Height

Certain Labyrinth chambers manipulate the perception of distance.

A floor may appear hundreds of feet below while actually being close enough to touch. A nearby platform may require hours of descent. A glide may continue without losing altitude because the chamber is moving with the traveler.

Petaurids test height through:

- Dropped objects
    
- Rope
    
- Echoes
    
- Dust
    
- Light
    
- Air pressure
    
- Observation from multiple positions
    

A visible landing is not proof of a reachable landing.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Petaurid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Wisdom score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Petaurids commonly stand between 4 feet 6 inches and 5 feet 10 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 30 feet.

You have a climbing speed of 30 feet.

### Climbing Claws

Your claws are natural weapons that you can use to make unarmed strikes.

When you hit with them, the strike deals slashing damage equal to `1d4 + your Strength modifier`.

### Gliding Membrane

When you begin falling and are not wearing medium or heavy armor, you can spread your patagium without using an action.

While your membrane remains extended:

- You descend no more than 60 feet during each of your turns.
    
- You take no falling damage.
    
- For every 1 foot you descend, you may move up to 2 feet horizontally.
    
- You may change direction during the glide, provided you have sufficient space to maneuver.
    

You cannot gain altitude through this trait alone.

If you are incapacitated, restrained, or unable to extend both arms and legs, you cannot use your Gliding Membrane.

### Sure Grip

You have advantage on ability checks and saving throws made to avoid falling, losing your grip, or being knocked from a climbable surface.

### Canopy Bound

As a bonus action, you leap and briefly spread your patagium.

Move up to 15 feet to an unoccupied space you can see. This movement:

- Does not count against your speed
    
- Does not provoke opportunity attacks
    
- Can cross gaps and difficult terrain
    
- Must end upon a surface capable of supporting you
    

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Petaurid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Sugar Glider Sublineage

Sugar Glider Petaurids are generally compact, agile, and highly social.

Their fur commonly includes grey, cream, brown, black, or white, often marked by dark stripes running from the head along the back. Their eyes are large and adapted to nocturnal movement.

Many Sugar Glider communities organize themselves through extensive route-kin networks and communal sleeping groups.

Their name reflects traditional diets rich in nectar, sap, fruit, and other sweet foods.

It does not mean every Sugar Glider is enthusiastic about pastries, though many consider the misunderstanding harmless.

### Flying Squirrel Sublineage

Flying Squirrel Petaurids are among the most widespread members of the lineage.

Their fur may be grey, brown, rust, black, white, or patterned. Their tails are generally broad and heavily furred, providing considerable aerial control.

Flying Squirrel communities inhabit forests, cities, mountain groves, and temperate regions throughout Aerathon.

They are known for adapting established architecture into connected aerial routes.

Property owners do not always appreciate the improvements.

### Colugo Sublineage

Colugo Petaurids possess some of the broadest patagia within the lineage.

Their membrane may extend around the neck, between the fingers and toes, and along much of the tail, creating an extensive gliding surface.

Their fur commonly displays mottled patterns in grey, green-brown, cream, rust, or charcoal, providing camouflage against bark and lichen.

Colugo communities often favor long, efficient glides and maintain settlements with widely spaced but carefully aligned landing points.

Their unusual appearance has led outsiders to describe them as unfinished combinations of several animals.

Colugos generally regard the observer as an unfinished combination of poor manners and limited imagination.

### Feathertail Glider Sublineage

Feathertail Glider Petaurids are typically light-framed and distinguished by flattened tails edged with stiff hair resembling a feather.

Their tails provide exceptional control during short, precise glides.

Feathertail communities are frequently found in dense forests, gardens, urban rafters, and other environments where landing spaces are narrow and closely spaced.

They are often talented at changing direction quickly.

Their small frames can lead others to underestimate their endurance or treat adults as children.

### Greater Glider Sublineage

Greater Glider Petaurids are generally taller, heavier, and more thickly furred than most members of the lineage.

Their coloration may include black, brown, grey, cream, white, or mottled combinations. Their ears are often large and rounded.

Greater Glider communities frequently inhabit cold high forests, mountain woodlands, and settlements built around enormous old trees.

They excel at long, stable glides but may require broader launch and landing spaces than smaller sublineages.

### Anomalure Sublineage

Anomalure Petaurids are distinguished by strong climbing claws, broad membranes, and rows of stiff scales beneath portions of the tail.

These scales assist with gripping trunks, supporting weight, and controlling rapid climbs.

Their fur commonly includes brown, grey, rust, black, or cream.

Anomalure communities often maintain steep vertical settlements and routes involving repeated climbing followed by short, forceful glides.

Their tail scales can damage delicate furnishings.

This has become a recurring point of negotiation in Vinyoti rental agreements.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Familiar Rafters

You gain proficiency in the Insight skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Petaurids learn which barn lofts remain open, which branches were repaired, and which household will leave a lamp beside the landing.

### Oria — Emergency Traverse

You have advantage on saving throws made to resist being pushed, pulled, or knocked prone.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to move up to half your climbing speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric Petaurids are trained to leave a failing structure before the final route disappears.

### Vinyot — Privileged Airway

While using your Gliding Membrane, you may move up to 3 feet horizontally for every 1 foot you descend.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti Petaurids learn to extract every possible foot of movement from a route whose landing rights have already been paid for.

### Arneria — Stations of Descent

You know the _gust_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _feather fall_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using wind, suspended cords, and the selection of two possible landing points as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Petaurids ask the divine not whether they will fall.

They ask which landing will teach them what the other cannot.

### Al’Ar — Stormbound Leap

When you use **Canopy Bound**, you may increase the distance moved to 30 feet and may move up to 10 feet vertically during that movement.

Once you enhance Canopy Bound in this way, you cannot do so again until you finish a long rest.

Al’Ari Petaurids know that waiting for the perfect route is another way of giving the storm time to remove every route.

## XIII. Playing a Petaurid

When creating a Petaurid character, consider:

- What kinds of height make them feel safe?
    
- What kinds make them feel exposed?
    
- Who taught them to glide?
    
- Who served as their first Catcher?
    
- Have they ever missed an intended landing?
    
- Who provided their second landing?
    
- Do they inspect every possible exit?
    
- Are they cautious, or do they resent being expected to be?
    
- What commitment feels like a leap with no visible landing?
    
- Do they trust others to maintain the route behind them?
    
- Are they comfortable allowing someone else to lead?
    
- What structure, tree, or route do they help maintain?
    
- Have they ever been barred from an ancestral landing?
    
- Do they prefer natural forests or constructed vertical cities?
    
- How do they feel about powered flyers?
    
- Do they consider gliding equal to flight, separate from it, or something better?
    
- How do they regard the Sky-Reeves: ancestors, exploitative elites, daring pilots, or people who depended upon forgotten labor?
    
- Would they accept the role of Catcher while someone else received the glory?
    
- What would make them leap before they were ready?
    

A Petaurid may be cautious or reckless, settled or wandering, practical or visionary.

Their lineage teaches them to identify the landing.

It does not promise that the landing will remain.
