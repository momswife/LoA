# Anatid

**Ministry of Delving Operations — The Living Atlas**  
**Folk Classification:** [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/Avianfolk Overview|Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Anatid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage and Homeland Variance  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Unrestricted  
**Common Names:** Waterfowl, Reedwings, Shorefolk  
**Date of Filing:** 4th Cycle, 3387 A.D.

---

## I. Lineage Overview

**Anatids** are an Avianfolk lineage distinguished by waterproof plumage, webbed feet, strong migratory instincts, and bodies capable of moving comfortably through air, water, and along the shore between them.

Their recognized sublineages include ducks, geese, swans, loons, grebes, and related waterfowl peoples.

Anatids are often described through contradiction.

They are called graceful in the water and awkward upon land. Peaceful until threatened. Social, yet territorial. Restless travelers who return obsessively to the same places.

These observations touch upon genuine parts of Anatid life, but they are often reduced into caricature.

Many Anatid cultures developed around environments that change constantly. Rivers flood and withdraw. Wetlands appear and vanish with the season. Familiar shores freeze, dry, or become dangerous. A settlement suitable during one part of the year may become unlivable during another.

Anatids therefore learned to carry belonging across distance.

To many of them, home is not one place.

It is the trusted sequence of places to which one knows how to return.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Anatids possess no single accepted ancestral homeland.

Their oldest communities have been found along rivers, lakes, marshlands, coastal inlets, flooded forests, tundra pools, highland lochs, and the seasonal wetlands that form between established regions.

Some traditions claim that the first Anatids emerged when the sky descended to drink from the world.

Others say their ancestors were born from feathers shed by a forgotten wind-god upon the surface of a still lake. Each feather became a traveler who could choose between the world above and the world reflected below.

A common Goose tradition claims that the first Anatid simply became tired of being told whether they belonged to the water or the sky and chose both out of spite.

This account has not received formal theological recognition.

It has nevertheless remained popular.

### Flyways

Anatid history is often preserved through **flyways**: inherited routes connecting seasonal homes, resting places, feeding grounds, temples, trade settlements, and safe shelters.

A flyway is more than a path through the air.

It may include:

- The river used when storms make flight impossible
    
- The household that offers shelter during winter
    
- The hill from which the next lake becomes visible
    
- A market where supplies can be obtained without exploitation
    
- A marsh where the dead are remembered
    
- A region that must be crossed only after thaw
    
- A dangerous stretch where travelers gather before proceeding together
    
- A settlement that once existed and remains named despite its destruction
    

These routes may pass through several political borders.

Anatid communities frequently argue that a flyway predates the nations now attempting to regulate it. Regional governments generally answer that age does not exempt travelers from customs law.

The disagreement has persisted for centuries.

### The Returning Map

Many Anatid families preserve a **Returning Map**.

It may be written, sung, embroidered, carved into a traveling chest, or taught through repeated journeys. The map records not merely where to travel, but how a route has changed over time.

A mark may indicate that:

- A bridge no longer exists
    
- A family has moved
    
- A wetland has become poisoned
    
- A ruler now demands payment
    
- A new community offers shelter
    
- A resting place should no longer be trusted
    
- Someone failed to return from that location
    

Returning Maps are rarely considered complete.

A route is alive for as long as people continue traveling it.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Anatids vary considerably in height and build.

Most stand between **5 feet 2 inches and 6 feet 7 inches**. Ducks and grebes are often compact and agile, while geese and swans may possess taller, more powerful frames. Loon populations frequently have long bodies and strong legs adapted for diving.

Their wings range from narrow, rapidly beating forms to broad structures capable of carrying substantial weight across long distances.

Most Anatids possess webbed feet suited to swimming. Their movement upon land varies by sublineage, individual anatomy, and upbringing. The familiar Anatid side-to-side gait is neither universal nor evidence of poor balance.

### Plumage

Anatid plumage may include:

- White, cream, grey, brown, or black
    
- Iridescent green, blue, violet, or bronze
    
- Distinctive facial masks
    
- Banded or speckled chests
    
- Bright wing patches visible primarily during flight
    
- Seasonal changes in color
    
- Contrasting head, neck, and body plumage
    
- Elaborate crests or decorative feathers among some populations
    

Waterproofing is maintained through careful grooming and natural oils distributed across the feathers.

Damage, illness, contamination, or neglect may compromise this protection. An Anatid whose waterproofing has failed can become dangerously cold even when remaining afloat.

Assisting with preening or oiling difficult-to-reach feathers is often an act of familiarity and trust.

### Beaks, Spurs, and Feet

Anatid beaks vary in shape according to inherited diet and environment.

Some are broad and sensitive. Others are narrow, serrated, hooked, or adapted for catching fish. Their feet and lower legs may also possess hard claws or defensive spurs.

Although most Anatids do not possess the curved hunting talons associated with Raptorids, their beaks, wings, and feet can deliver painful strikes when necessary.

### Water and Breath

Anatids are capable swimmers.

Many can remain underwater considerably longer than most terrestrial peoples, though their abilities vary. Loons and grebes are especially accomplished divers, while some geese and swans are more comfortable remaining near the surface.

Anatid settlements often include entrances accessible from both air and water.

A visitor may arrive through the roof, the front door, or beneath the dock.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Anatid communities often form around **routes**, **shores**, and **returning households**.

A household may remain in one location throughout the year. Another may divide itself between several seasonal homes. A third may travel continuously while maintaining long-standing obligations to settlements along its flyway.

Kinship is frequently measured through return.

A relative may be absent for months and remain deeply involved in family life. A person who repeatedly arrives during difficult seasons may become family regardless of ancestry.

### The Return Promise

Many Anatid communities recognize a tradition known as the **Return Promise**.

A person making the promise names:

- Where they intend to travel
    
- When they expect to return
    
- Who should be told if they do not
    
- What responsibilities must be carried in their absence
    
- How long others should wait before searching
    

The promise is not always formal.

It may be spoken before leaving home, written beside a door, or communicated through a familiar phrase.

Failing to return is not considered a moral failure.

Leaving without giving others the knowledge needed to respond may be.

### Formation

Anatid migration is frequently communal.

Travelers take turns facing difficult winds while others conserve strength behind them. The leader is not necessarily the person of greatest authority. It is simply the person currently accepting the greatest resistance.

When they tire, another takes their place.

This practice has influenced Anatid ideas of leadership. Authority is often understood as a position of temporary burden rather than permanent elevation.

A leader who never yields the front may be admired for endurance.

They may also be judged incapable of trusting anyone else.

### Shore Rights

Traditional Anatid law commonly recognizes **shore rights**.

A traveler in genuine danger may land, rest, and request water or temporary shelter even upon privately controlled ground. The host may establish reasonable boundaries but is expected not to drive a weakened traveler back into unsafe conditions.

Modern regional law does not recognize these rights consistently.

Conflicts are especially common in Vinyot, where privately controlled docks, airspace, and shorelines may leave exhausted migrants with no legal place to land.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Anatid communities commonly admire:

- Loyalty across distance
    
- Endurance
    
- Practical cooperation
    
- Hospitality toward travelers
    
- Courage in defense of home and family
    
- The wisdom to leave before conditions become impossible
    
- The reliability to return when others are expecting them
    

These values can produce devoted companions, resilient communities, capable travelers, and defenders who stand firmly between danger and those under their care.

They can also create possessiveness, exclusion, and suspicion.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Loyalty|Maintaining relationships across distance|Refusing to release harmful bonds|
|Protectiveness|Defending vulnerable people|Territorial aggression|
|Endurance|Continuing through hardship|Remaining in hardship unnecessarily|
|Cooperation|Sharing burdens and leadership|Punishing independence|
|Hospitality|Sheltering travelers in need|Expecting lifelong obligation in return|
|Tradition|Preserving routes and returning places|Rejecting necessary change|
|Adaptability|Moving when circumstances demand it|Never allowing oneself to settle|

A recurring Anatid anxiety is the fear of **returning to find that one no longer belongs**.

A route may change. A familiar household may be gone. A homeland may close its borders. A community may resent those who left or distrust those who came back changed.

Some Anatids respond by preserving tradition fiercely.

Others refuse to call any place home, believing that belonging cannot be lost if it is never claimed.

### Territoriality

Many Anatid communities possess strong customs concerning personal, household, and communal space.

This does not mean Anatids are universally aggressive.

Territorial behavior may involve clearly marking boundaries, announcing one’s approach, requesting permission before entering nesting or bathing areas, and recognizing that hospitality does not erase privacy.

Conflicts arise when boundaries become tools of exclusion.

A community that once defended vulnerable travelers may begin deciding that only familiar travelers deserve protection. A family may use concern as justification for controlling those who wish to leave.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Anatid communities disagree over:

- Whether ancestral flyways should supersede regional borders
    
- Whether migration remains a cultural duty
    
- Whether permanent settlement weakens Anatid identity
    
- Whether shore rights should become enforceable law
    
- Whether defensive traditions encourage unnecessary aggression
    
- Whether Returning Maps should be shared with the Ministry
    
- Whether dangerous routes should be preserved or abandoned
    
- Whether travelers who leave repeatedly owe more to those who remain
    

These disputes often concern the same question:

Does belonging come from staying, returning, or being welcomed when one does?

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Anatids served throughout the ancient [[The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]] as route guides, weather observers, water-landing specialists, rescue flyers, and keepers of the lower currents.

The most famous Sky-Reeve accounts concern high-altitude offerings and Stormriders passing through lightning.

Anatid records preserve the lakes, rivers, and marshes where those riders landed when the sky rejected them.

### Reedwardens

Sky-Reeve **Reedwardens** maintained emergency landing sites beneath major aerial routes.

These havens included:

- Mountain lakes
    
- Sheltered river bends
    
- Marsh platforms
    
- Floating supply stations
    
- Cliffside pools
    
- Reservoirs beneath the Eyrie-Spire
    
- Coastal inlets protected from crosswinds
    

Reedwardens monitored water depth, wind direction, contamination, and the condition of emergency supplies. They also recovered riders whose injuries made ordinary landings impossible.

A Stormrider might earn glory by entering a tempest.

A Reedwarden decided whether that rider survived long enough to enjoy it.

### The Downwind Havens

The Sky-Reeves established a network of refuge sites known as the **Downwind Havens**.

Their locations were marked in old flyway songs and encoded into some Sky-Script Feathers. Riders separated from formation could follow rivers or descending currents toward the nearest haven.

Several modern settlements are believed to have grown from these stations.

Others have disappeared beneath silt, construction, or altered waterways.

Anatid historians continue to search for them because the havens may contain:

- Stormrider equipment
    
- Wind-Tithe records
    
- Lost route markers
    
- Aerolith fragments
    
- Final messages
    
- Evidence of riders officially recorded as missing
    

### The Open-Water Tithe

Some Anatid traditions describe an unofficial rite called the **Open-Water Tithe**.

During severe storms, a pilot carried a crafted feather or conductive token over a lake and released it where the sky’s reflection had been broken by wind.

The rite acknowledged that the storm belonged not only to the heights.

What fell from the sky entered rivers, wetlands, fields, homes, and the lives of those who had never chosen to challenge it.

The Wind-Mothers never formally recognized the Open-Water Tithe.

Reedwardens continued performing it.

### Anatid Stormriders

Anatid Stormriders were known for endurance, formation flying, and dangerous low-altitude routes above water.

Some deliberately skimmed storm-driven waves to avoid the most violent upper winds. Others used the surface of lakes and seas as visual reference when cloud cover erased the horizon.

Their rivals sometimes dismissed these methods as cautious.

Those rivals were generally quieter after needing rescue.

Modern Anatid Stormriders remain common in relay races, long-distance courses, and events requiring repeated water landings.

## VII. Anatids in Modern Aerathon

Anatids live throughout all Five Greater Regions.

They are especially common near rivers, lakes, wetlands, coasts, reservoirs, canals, and migration routes. Their relationship with water frequently shapes their opportunities, but does not determine their profession.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Anatids are found among farming communities, river settlements, mill towns, roadside households, and seasonal markets.

Many maintain routes connecting several small communities rather than one major city.

Allemancian Anatid traditions emphasize return, hospitality, and the responsibility to notice when a familiar traveler has not arrived.

During floods, Anatids often organize evacuation, transport supplies, recover livestock, and maintain communication between isolated households.

A returning Anatid is commonly greeted first with food and only afterward with questions.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Anatids work around reservoirs, flood-control systems, industrial canals, emergency services, structural inspection, and long-distance transport.

Oric Anatids often view migration as logistics rather than romance.

Routes must be measured. Rest sites must be maintained. Weather must be respected. A formation that ignores the weakest flyer is considered badly designed.

Several Oric Houses employ Anatid specialists to inspect submerged foundations and machinery positioned beneath artificial lakes.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], rivers, docks, air routes, and migration permissions are all matters of commerce.

Anatids work throughout shipping, ferry services, trade, security, water management, entertainment, competitive racing, and transport law.

Vinyoti Anatids frequently become skilled in contracts governing:

- Landing rights
    
- Dock access
    
- Water passage
    
- Airspace
    
- Cargo liability
    
- Seasonal migration
    
- Emergency refuge
    
- Ownership of artificial shorelines
    

Some have built successful businesses controlling key resting points.

Others lead campaigns arguing that no exhausted traveler should be charged for the right not to drown.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Anatids participate in ritual crossings, pilgrimages, funerary processions, oath journeys, and the tending of sacred pools.

Water often serves as a boundary between one state of life and another.

An oath may begin upon one shore and become binding only after the speaker reaches the opposite side. Mourners may release feathers onto a river so that memory continues traveling after the body has been laid to rest.

Some Arnerian Anatids undertake seasonal migrations as religious obligations.

Others reject the idea that faith should determine where they are permitted to live.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Anatids thrive among monsoon wetlands, mangrove settlements, storm coasts, dock-cities, flooded districts, and communities repeatedly reshaped by weather.

Al’Ari Anatids are often skilled at moving between temporary forms of settlement.

A market may operate from boats during flood season and return to the shore afterward. A household may possess walls designed to be removed before storms. A community may rebuild on higher ground without treating the previous site as abandoned.

Their flyways change frequently.

The tradition lies not in following the same path forever, but in ensuring that a path remains.

## VIII. Anatids and Delving

Anatids are commonly recruited as scouts, rescue specialists, navigators, flooded-chamber explorers, transporters, and defensive support.

Their ability to fly and swim allows them to operate effectively in chambers where water divides the party or conceals additional routes.

Experienced Anatid delvers examine:

- The direction and speed of current
    
- Whether a surface is safe to land upon
    
- Changes in water level
    
- Air pockets and submerged passages
    
- Contamination
    
- Reflections that do not match the chamber
    
- Creatures moving beneath apparently shallow water
    
- Whether the water is actually water
    

The Labyrinth frequently uses familiar surfaces deceptively.

A pool may possess no bottom. A reflected ceiling may be another chamber. A river may flow upward once entered. A calm lake may preserve every sound made above it and repeat them later.

### The Returning Count

Anatid delving schools commonly teach a procedure known as **the Returning Count**.

Before crossing water, descending into a shaft, or entering a chamber with several exits, one member identifies every person present.

The count is repeated when the group gathers again.

The procedure includes names rather than numbers alone.

A party that enters with six members and returns with six shapes has not necessarily returned intact.

### Formation Discipline

Anatid delvers are often trained to rotate exposed positions.

The strongest swimmer does not remain at the front until exhaustion. The most capable flyer does not scout alone merely because they can.

Formation is treated as a method of distributing danger.

The Labyrinth is adept at punishing the person who mistakes carrying the burden for owning it.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Anatid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Constitution score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Anatids commonly stand between 5 feet 2 inches and 6 feet 7 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 25 feet.

You have a flying speed of 50 feet and a swimming speed of 30 feet.

### Flight

You can fly using your wings. To do so, you cannot be wearing medium or heavy armor.

### Beak and Spurs

Your beak, claws, or defensive spurs are natural weapons that you can use to make unarmed strikes.

When you hit with one of them, the strike deals slashing or piercing damage equal to `1d4 + your Strength modifier`. You choose the damage type when you make the attack.

### Skyward Pulse

As a bonus action, you can beat your wings with explosive force.

Each creature within 5 feet of you must succeed on a Strength saving throw or be pushed 5 feet away from you.

The saving throw DC equals:

`8 + your proficiency bonus + your Strength modifier`

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

### Waterborne

You can hold your breath for up to 15 minutes.

You also have advantage on Strength (Athletics) checks made to swim against a current, remain afloat, or pull another creature through water.

### Formation Guard

When a creature you can see attacks a target other than you within 5 feet of you, you can use your reaction to impose disadvantage on the attack roll. You interpose a wing, strike at the attacker, or otherwise disrupt the assault.

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Anatid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Duck Sublineage

Duck Anatids are among the most varied and widespread members of the lineage.

Their plumage may be subdued and mottled or marked by brilliant green, blue, violet, white, rust, and black. Many possess compact frames and strong maneuverability in air and water.

Duck communities are common around rivers, marshes, city ponds, agricultural regions, forest lakes, and sheltered coastlines.

They are frequently underestimated because of their familiarity.

Duck Anatids generally consider surviving nearly everywhere a sufficient response.

### Goose Sublineage

Goose Anatids are commonly tall, powerfully built, and adapted for long migrations.

Their plumage often includes grey, white, brown, black, or barred patterns. Many possess strong voices capable of carrying across great distances.

Goose communities frequently maintain detailed formation traditions and long-standing flyways.

They are often stereotyped as aggressive.

Most distinguish carefully between unprovoked aggression and ensuring that a clearly announced boundary remains respected.

### Swan Sublineage

Swan Anatids are generally large-bodied, long-necked, and physically powerful.

Their plumage may be white, black, grey, or marked by contrasting coloration. Their movements upon water are often described as graceful, leading outsiders to assume equal calm beneath the surface.

Swan communities possess traditions of ceremony, territorial guardianship, partnership, and public dignity.

Individuals may find the expectation of elegance restrictive—particularly when angry, frightened, injured, or simply uninterested in appearing composed.

### Loon Sublineage

Loon Anatids possess long bodies, strong legs, and exceptional diving ability.

Their plumage commonly includes black, white, grey, and intricate checked or striped patterns. Many have vivid red or dark eyes and powerful voices capable of carrying across lakes at night.

Loons are less comfortable walking upon land than many other Anatids.

Their communities are often concentrated around deep lakes, cold coasts, and isolated waterways.

Their calls have become associated with loneliness and omens in outsider folklore. Loons themselves use them for communication, territorial announcement, navigation, and locating family across darkness or fog.

### Grebe Sublineage

Grebe Anatids are highly accomplished swimmers and divers, often possessing compact wings, strong legs, and distinctive head or neck plumage.

Some Grebes have reduced long-distance flight ability compared with other Anatids, while others migrate normally.

Grebe communities frequently build floating structures, reed platforms, and settlements designed to rise and fall with changing water levels.

Elaborate courtship dances and ceremonial movements are common in several Grebe cultures, though no individual is obligated to enjoy public ritual.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Returning Road

You gain proficiency in the Insight skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Anatids learn every familiar light along the road, river, and shore—and notice when one has gone dark.

### Oria — Formation Withdrawal

While flying, you have advantage on saving throws against being charmed or frightened.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to fly up to half your flying speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric Anatids understand that a formation survives only when exhausted members are permitted to leave the front.

### Vinyot — Contracted Landing

While falling, you can reduce the fall speed of yourself or one willing creature within 5 feet of you by 60 feet per round, potentially negating falling damage.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti Anatids learn early that the right to land safely is often written in smaller letters than the price of taking flight.

### Arneria — Rite of Returning Waters

You know the _gust_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _feather fall_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using wind, water, and reflected cloud movement as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Anatids ask not only whether a journey should begin.

They ask what must be carried back.

### Al’Ar — Stormwake Cry

When you use **Skyward Pulse**, you may release a forceful call carried across the air and water.

Creatures of your choice within 10 feet of you must succeed on a Constitution saving throw against your Skyward Pulse saving throw DC or be deafened until the start of your next turn.

Once you use this trait, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Among Al’Ari Anatids, the storm is measured by what it scatters.

The community is measured by what it gathers again.

## XIII. Playing an Anatid

When creating an Anatid character, consider:

- What places do they call home?
    
- What route connects those places?
    
- Who expects them to return?
    
- Have they ever failed to keep a Return Promise?
    
- What does hospitality mean to them?
    
- What boundary will they defend without hesitation?
    
- Are they comfortable relying upon a formation?
    
- Do they willingly let others take the lead?
    
- Have they remained somewhere long after it became harmful?
    
- Have they ever returned to find that their place had changed?
    
- Are they migratory, permanently settled, or uncertain?
    
- What object, song, or landmark guides them home?
    
- How do they regard the Sky-Reeves: ancestors, reckless pilots, maintainers of useful routes, or distant history?
    
- Would they rather enter the storm or wait below to recover those who did?
    
- Which shore would they choose if they could return to only one?
    

An Anatid may be gentle or fierce, wandering or deeply rooted, ceremonious or practical.

Their lineage teaches them how to return.

It does not decide where they belong.
