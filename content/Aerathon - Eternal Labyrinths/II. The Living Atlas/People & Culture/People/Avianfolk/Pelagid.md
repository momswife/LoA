# Pelagid

**Ministry of Delving Operations — The Living Atlas**  
**Folk Classification:** [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Races of Aerathon/Avianfolk/Avianfolk Overview|Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Pelagid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage and Homeland Variance  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Unrestricted  
**Common Names:** Seabirds, Farwings, Horizonfolk  
**Date of Filing:** 1st Cycle, 3388 A.D.

---

## I. Lineage Overview

**Pelagids** are an Avianfolk lineage shaped by coastlines, saltwater, open wind, and journeys measured not in roads but in the distance between safe shores.

Their recognized sublineages include albatrosses, gulls, terns, pelicans, cormorants, frigatebirds, puffins, and other ocean-ranging avian peoples.

Pelagids are often described as wanderers.

The description is incomplete.

Many Pelagids travel extraordinary distances, but movement alone does not define them. Their cultures developed around knowing when to depart, where to rest, which storms can be crossed, and which journeys demand more strength than pride can provide.

They are navigators of exposure.

An inland road may offer trees, settlements, bridges, and visible landmarks. The open sea offers wind, cloud, stars, current, and a horizon that appears unchanged no matter how long one travels toward it.

A Pelagid may fly for hours without seeing a place upon which they can safely land.

That reality has shaped traditions of endurance, mutual obligation, weather-reading, and careful preparation. It has also produced generations of people who appear self-sufficient because admitting exhaustion while far from shore can feel dangerously close to surrender.

Pelagids do not belong only to the sea.

They belong to the long distance between one refuge and the next.

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Pelagids possess no single accepted origin.

Their oldest settlements have been found upon sea cliffs, island chains, reefs, tidal flats, storm coasts, floating structures, harbor cities, and remote rocks where few other peoples would choose to build.

Some Pelagid traditions claim that their ancestors were created from scraps of cloud torn loose during the first ocean storm.

Others teach that the first Pelagid followed the setting sun beyond every known shore, discovered that the world continued after certainty ended, and returned carrying a feather darkened by night.

A common Gull account claims that the first Pelagid simply noticed that everyone else had abandoned a perfectly usable stretch of coastline and moved in.

Theological authorities have not formally disputed this version.

### Horizon Records

Pelagid history is frequently preserved through **Horizon Records**.

A Horizon Record describes a journey by what could be perceived from each stage of travel:

- The angle of the sun
    
- The shape of distant clouds
    
- The smell of rain
    
- The color of shallow water
    
- The appearance of birds, fish, or drifting plants
    
- The direction of waves
    
- The stars visible before dawn
    
- The sound of wind passing through the traveler’s feathers
    
- The moment a known landmark first appeared
    

These records may be written, sung, knotted into cords, carved into bone, or taught through repeated travel.

They are rarely exact maps.

A coastline changes. An island sinks. A tower is rebuilt. A storm alters a reef. A route that once took three days may become impossible when the wind turns against it.

Pelagid navigators therefore preserve methods of recognition rather than relying only upon fixed measurements.

A chart tells a traveler where a place was.

A Horizon Record teaches them how to know whether it remains.

### The Names of Lost Shores

Pelagids commonly preserve the names of islands, settlements, and landing sites that no longer exist.

Some vanished beneath rising water. Others were destroyed by storms, war, Labyrinth anomalies, or the slow collapse of cliffs. A few were abandoned for reasons their descendants no longer remember.

These names may remain in navigational chants long after the shore itself has disappeared.

A young traveler might ask why a route-song warns them not to land upon an island that cannot be found.

The traditional answer is:

> Because the sea remembers where it took something.

Some Pelagid scholars regard these lost names as cultural memorials.

Others believe several supposed ruins still exist, concealed by weather, magic, or altered currents.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Pelagids vary greatly in size and build.

Most stand between **5 feet 1 inch and 6 feet 8 inches**. Albatross and pelican sublineages often possess broad wingspans and large frames, while terns, gulls, and puffins tend toward more compact bodies.

Pelagid wings are commonly adapted for efficient travel over open water.

Some possess long, narrow wings capable of using shifting air currents to remain aloft with minimal effort. Others are built for rapid diving, abrupt turns, or repeated takeoff from cliffs and water.

### Plumage

Pelagid plumage often includes:

- White
    
- Grey
    
- Black
    
- Brown
    
- Cream
    
- Dark blue
    
- Charcoal
    
- Rust or gold accents
    
- Contrasting wing tips
    
- Dark caps, masks, or eye markings
    
- Bright beaks, feet, or throat pouches
    
- Seasonal breeding colors
    

Many Pelagids possess dense, water-resistant feathers.

This protection requires regular care. Salt, oil, ash, magical residue, and industrial contamination can damage plumage, reduce insulation, and make flight or swimming dangerous.

A Pelagid may spend considerable time cleaning themselves after travel.

This is maintenance, not vanity.

### Salt and Thirst

Pelagid bodies are generally more tolerant of salt exposure than those of most terrestrial peoples.

Many possess glands that help remove excess salt from the body. These adaptations do not allow them to survive indefinitely without fresh water.

The belief that Pelagids can drink seawater without consequence has caused repeated cases of dehydration among poorly supplied crews.

Pelagids are capable of surviving difficult conditions.

They remain people with physical limits.

### Water Movement

Most Pelagids are capable swimmers, though their ability varies by sublineage.

Cormorants, puffins, and certain diving populations are especially comfortable beneath the surface. Frigatebirds and some albatrosses are far less suited to extended swimming despite their close association with the ocean.

Pelagid settlements therefore distinguish carefully between:

- A safe landing
    
- A survivable water landing
    
- A place from which one can take flight again
    

These are not always the same location.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Pelagid communities commonly form around **harbors**, **cliffholds**, **island houses**, **voyaging companies**, and networks of distant settlements connected by regular passage.

A Pelagid may consider several communities part of one extended social world even when weeks of travel separate them.

Their relationships often endure through absence.

A friend may not be seen for years and still be expected to receive shelter upon return. A family may scatter across several coasts while maintaining shared property, obligations, and ritual gatherings.

### Harbor-Kin

Many Pelagid traditions recognize **Harbor-Kin**.

Harbor-Kin are people who have agreed to provide one another with refuge, supplies, information, or aid during travel. The relationship may exist between individuals, families, guilds, ships, or entire settlements.

Harbor-Kin need not be Pelagids.

A coastal Aquaticfolk enclave, Anatid wetland community, human lighthouse household, or dwarven cliff fortress may all belong to the same network.

The obligation is practical:

When distance has exhausted every other resource, the harbor must remain.

Abusing Harbor-Kin hospitality is considered deeply shameful. So is refusing aid because a returning traveler has changed during their absence.

### Long Absence

Pelagid relationships are often built to endure long separations.

Partners may maintain different travel routes. Parents may entrust children to extended households while undertaking seasonal work. Friends may exchange messages through merchants, skyships, passing flocks, or travelers who know only part of the journey.

This does not mean absence is painless.

Pelagid culture contains many songs, rituals, and sayings about those who do not return. The outward calm of a household accustomed to departure may conceal generations of unresolved fear.

### The Empty Place

Before a major journey, some Pelagid households set aside an **Empty Place**.

This may be a chair, perch, sleeping space, bowl, lamp, or mark upon a wall. It remains reserved for the traveler until they return or the household formally accepts that they will not.

No one is forbidden from using the physical space in an emergency.

Its meaning is symbolic.

The traveler remains part of the household even while absent.

Removing the Empty Place too quickly is considered an act of abandonment. Refusing to release it after all hope has passed can prevent a household from grieving.

Deciding when the place should be reclaimed is rarely simple.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Pelagid communities commonly admire:

- Endurance without needless complaint
    
- Reliable navigation
    
- Respect for weather and distance
    
- Hospitality to exhausted travelers
    
- Independence
    
- Loyalty maintained through absence
    
- The judgment to abandon an unsafe route
    
- The courage to continue when no refuge is visible
    

These values can produce resilient travelers, patient navigators, steadfast companions, and communities capable of surviving isolation.

They can also encourage emotional withdrawal.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Endurance|Persisting through hardship|Concealing dangerous exhaustion|
|Independence|Functioning far from support|Refusing needed assistance|
|Caution|Respecting weather and distance|Avoiding all uncertain journeys|
|Wanderlust|Remaining open to distant places|Inability to build lasting roots|
|Loyalty|Maintaining bonds through absence|Expecting others to wait indefinitely|
|Adaptability|Changing course when conditions shift|Abandoning difficult commitments|
|Self-reliance|Carrying one’s own burden|Judging those who cannot do the same|
|Hospitality|Providing refuge to travelers|Neglecting the needs of permanent residents|

A recurring Pelagid anxiety is the fear of **being too far away when someone needs them**.

A Pelagid may cross an ocean successfully and still feel they failed because hardship reached home during their absence.

Some respond by refusing long journeys.

Others never stop traveling, believing that remaining in motion will somehow place them where they are needed next.

### The Romance of Distance

Outsiders often romanticize Pelagid travel.

They speak of freedom, endless horizons, and lives without borders.

Pelagids know the other side of those stories:

- Hunger between supply points
    
- Wings strained by contrary winds
    
- Salt wounds
    
- Isolation
    
- Missing weather reports
    
- Dead reckoning beneath clouded stars
    
- The knowledge that turning back may be as dangerous as continuing
    
- Arriving too late
    

Many Pelagids love travel.

They simply reject the assumption that love makes it easy.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Pelagid communities disagree over:

- Whether dangerous ancestral routes should remain in use
    
- Whether Harbor-Kin obligations should be legally enforceable
    
- Whether long-distance voyaging remains culturally necessary
    
- Whether settlements should be permanent or seasonally abandoned
    
- Whether new skyship routes preserve or destroy Pelagid traditions
    
- Whether weather knowledge should remain within navigator families
    
- Whether rescue is owed to those who ignored clear warnings
    
- Whether travelers possess greater prestige than those who maintain the harbor
    
- Whether lost shores should be sought or allowed to remain memorials
    

These disputes often reveal an old imbalance.

Those who leave are remembered in stories.

Those who keep the light burning are sometimes remembered only when it goes out.

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Pelagids played a vital role within the ancient [[The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]] as far-route navigators, storm observers, current chartkeepers, supply carriers, and messengers between isolated aerial settlements.

Where other Sky-Reeves maintained the winds near mountains, cities, and inland aeries, Pelagids carried the clan network across open water.

### Far-Reeves

Sky-Reeve navigators assigned to ocean routes were commonly called **Far-Reeves**.

They maintained records of:

- Upper coastal currents
    
- Seasonal storm tracks
    
- Island refuges
    
- Safe descent paths
    
- Waterspout regions
    
- Migratory crossings
    
- Skyship approaches
    
- Rescue distances
    
- Lightning patterns over open sea
    

Far-Reeves were expected to revise their charts continually.

A route copied without recent observation could be more dangerous than no route at all.

Some Far-Reeves spent years away from the Eyrie-Spire of Taellen, returning only for major Tithe gatherings or changes in authority. Their distance from the central clans granted them considerable independence.

It also meant their warnings were sometimes ignored by officials who had never crossed the waters being discussed.

### The Horizon Tithe

Pelagid traditions preserve an unofficial ritual known as the **Horizon Tithe**.

At the furthest safe point of a recognized route, the pilot released a feather, cord, or fragment of sail into the wind.

The offering marked the boundary between mapped passage and uncertainty.

The pilot then chose whether to turn back.

Those who continued did so without claiming the storm owed them safe return.

The Horizon Tithe was not a celebration of recklessness. It was a formal admission that skill had reached the edge of knowledge.

Modern Pelagid navigators sometimes perform a symbolic version before entering uncharted airspace, Labyrinth weather, or a region altered by magical catastrophe.

### Stormglass Charts

Pelagid Sky-Reeves helped develop **Stormglass Charts**, layered maps produced using treated glass, salt residue, metallic dust, and pressure-reactive runes.

When exposed to moisture and changing air pressure, the charts revealed different routes.

A path safe during calm weather might disappear entirely as a storm approached. Another would emerge along the edge of the dangerous current.

Only fragments survive.

Modern weather towers and skyship navigation systems still use principles derived from their design.

### Pelagid Stormriders

Pelagid Stormriders specialized in endurance courses, open-water tempests, and conditions where no emergency landing site existed.

Their harnesses were lighter than many inland designs and often included flotation bladders, salt-resistant conductors, signal mirrors, and cords intended to keep injured riders from sinking.

Pelagid riders rarely dominated short racing events.

They became legendary in trials where surviving the entire route mattered more than crossing one point first.

Several All-Wind Riders are believed to have been Pelagids.

The evidence remains incomplete, partly because the ocean claimed many of their records and partly because Far-Reeves were notoriously indifferent to filing paperwork on time.

## VII. Pelagids in Modern Aerathon

Pelagids live throughout the Five Greater Regions, though their largest populations remain concentrated along coastlines, islands, major rivers, and skyship routes.

They are frequently associated with sailing, navigation, rescue, trade, weather work, and long-distance delivery. These professions reflect inherited opportunity and physical adaptation rather than compulsory vocation.

### Allemance

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Pelagids are most common around broad rivers, western ports, lakeside settlements, and trade routes leading toward the coast.

Allemancian Pelagids often serve as links between distant communities and the larger world beyond them.

Their journeys carry:

- Letters
    
- Medicine
    
- Tools
    
- News
    
- Seeds
    
- Family gifts
    
- The names of those who died far from home
    

Allemancian culture places special honor upon Pelagids who return reliably rather than those who merely travel far.

Distance is impressive.

Coming back with what others entrusted to you matters more.

### Oria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Pelagids work in weather analysis, long-range logistics, skyship inspection, coastal engineering, hazard response, and communication between isolated Houses.

Oric Pelagids tend to treat endurance as a measurable resource.

Food, sleep, wing strain, temperature, visibility, and emergency landing distance are all calculated before a route begins.

A pilot who boasts of surviving through poor preparation is generally considered less competent than one who planned well enough to avoid the danger.

Several Oric Houses maintain Pelagid-led storm stations positioned where conventional structures cannot safely operate.

### Vinyot

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], Pelagid travel supports trade, communication, private transport, racing, maritime insurance, salvage, and speculation.

Information from a distant port may be worth enormous sums if it arrives before competing reports.

Pelagid couriers therefore occupy an important place within markets that profit from speed and distance.

The work is lucrative.

It is also dangerous.

Contracts may penalize delays caused by storms, injury, territorial disputes, or impossible routes. Some firms maintain excellent Harbor-Kin networks. Others treat pilots as replaceable components of the delivery system.

Pelagid labor guilds have repeatedly fought for weather-delay protections and the right to refuse routes judged suicidally unsafe.

### Arneria

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Pelagids participate in pilgrimages, ocean vigils, funerary passages, sacred navigation, and rituals performed beyond sight of land.

Certain Arnerian traditions hold that an oath spoken where no shore can hear it belongs directly to the divine.

Pelagid witnesses carry these vows back to the community.

Their testimony may determine whether a pilgrimage, succession rite, or act of atonement was completed properly.

Some Pelagids accept this responsibility as sacred service.

Others object that no mortal traveler should become the sole judge of what occurred beyond the horizon.

### Al’Ar

In [[Aerathon - Eternal Labyrinths/II. The Living Atlas/Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Pelagids thrive among storm coasts, dock-cities, archipelagos, floating markets, cliff settlements, and communities connected more easily by air and water than by land.

Al’Ari Pelagids frequently combine navigation with performance, trade, rescue, and weather craft.

Routes change after every major storm. Islands may become isolated, newly connected, or temporarily uninhabitable. A capable navigator is expected to improvise without pretending uncertainty has disappeared.

Many Al’Ari Pelagids still observe some form of Horizon Tithe before entering an unfamiliar storm.

The offering may be solemn.

It may also be a cheap ribbon thrown into the wind while the pilot insults the weather.

Both are considered acceptable.

## VIII. Pelagids and Delving

Pelagids commonly serve as navigators, scouts, long-range messengers, rescue specialists, flooded-chamber explorers, and observers of environmental anomalies.

Their experience with open distance makes them comfortable in vast chambers where walls and ceilings may be difficult to judge.

It also creates vulnerabilities.

A Labyrinth chamber may appear to contain an open sky or limitless sea while possessing boundaries that cannot be seen. A horizon may remain fixed no matter how far the delver travels. A distant light may retreat at precisely the same speed as the person approaching it.

Experienced Pelagid delvers watch for:

- Wind that repeats at exact intervals
    
- Waves unaffected by nearby movement
    
- Clouds that never change shape
    
- Stars that remain fixed despite travel
    
- Saltwater without a source
    
- Distant land that casts no reflection
    
- Echoes arriving too quickly for the apparent distance
    
- Fatigue disproportionate to movement
    
- Routes that return to their beginning without turning
    

### The False Horizon

Pelagid delving schools teach that a horizon inside a Labyrinth should never be trusted merely because it appears distant.

A **False Horizon** may be:

- An illusion
    
- A folded space
    
- A boundary that moves with the viewer
    
- A lure
    
- A memory imposed upon the chamber
    
- A living entity attempting to appear harmless
    
- A passage visible only from one direction
    

Pelagids test such horizons through thrown objects, timed flight, controlled signals, changes in elevation, and observation from several angles.

### Reserve for Return

Many Pelagid delvers follow a rule known as **Reserve for Return**.

No journey should consume more than half the resources required to retrace it unless a new refuge has been confirmed.

Resources include:

- Food
    
- Water
    
- spells
    
- light
    
- physical endurance
    
- climbing equipment
    
- healing supplies
    
- trust between party members
    

Adventurers frequently ignore this rule when treasure becomes visible.

The Labyrinth appears to account for that tendency.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Pelagid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Constitution score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Pelagids commonly stand between 5 feet 1 inch and 6 feet 8 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 25 feet.

You have a flying speed of 50 feet and a swimming speed of 30 feet.

### Flight

You can fly using your wings. To do so, you cannot be wearing medium or heavy armor.

### Beak and Talons

Your beak, claws, or feet are natural weapons that you can use to make unarmed strikes.

When you hit with one of them, the strike deals slashing or piercing damage equal to `1d4 + your Strength modifier`. You choose the damage type when you make the attack.

### Skyward Pulse

As a bonus action, you can beat your wings with explosive force.

Each creature within 5 feet of you must succeed on a Strength saving throw or be pushed 5 feet away from you.

The saving throw DC equals:

`8 + your proficiency bonus + your Strength modifier`

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

### Saltwind Endurance

You can hold your breath for up to 10 minutes.

You also have advantage on Constitution saving throws made to resist exhaustion caused by forced travel, strong natural winds, extreme cold, or extended exposure during a journey.

### Weather Eye

You gain proficiency in the Survival skill.

When you finish a long rest, you can accurately determine the natural weather expected within your immediate region for the next 24 hours, provided no magical effect conceals or alters it.

In addition, when you or a creature you can see within 30 feet of you makes a saving throw against being pushed, knocked prone, or forcibly moved by wind or water, you can use your reaction to add your proficiency bonus to the roll.

You may use this reaction after the roll is made but before its result is declared.

You can use this reaction a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Pelagid sublineages represent distinct hereditary and physiological branches within the broader lineage. They may influence appearance, anatomy, inherited adaptations, and cultural history, but do not determine personality, morality, profession, or homeland.

### Albatross Sublineage

Albatross Pelagids commonly possess enormous wingspans, long bodies, and pale plumage marked by black, grey, brown, or gold.

They are exceptionally well adapted to long-distance soaring and can travel vast stretches of ocean while conserving energy.

Albatross communities maintain some of the most extensive Horizon Records in Aerathon.

Their endurance has produced a reputation for emotional stoicism. Many individuals find this expectation burdensome, especially when exhaustion or grief is interpreted as personal failure.

### Gull Sublineage

Gull Pelagids are highly adaptable and widely distributed.

Their plumage commonly includes white, grey, black, and brown, with bright beaks, feet, or eye markings. They inhabit coasts, rivers, islands, cities, ports, and inland settlements connected to water.

Gulls are frequently stereotyped as noisy, opportunistic, or thieving.

Their communities generally answer that survival in crowded ports requires recognizing resources before someone else wastes them.

This explanation does not excuse theft.

It does explain why Gulls are often excellent salvagers, negotiators, and urban navigators.

### Tern Sublineage

Tern Pelagids tend toward slender bodies, narrow wings, forked tails, and swift, precise flight.

Many undertake extraordinary migrations between distant seasonal homes. Their plumage often includes white, grey, black, and occasional red or orange markings.

Tern communities frequently maintain relationships across several regions and consider movement an ordinary part of family life.

Individuals who choose permanent settlement may face assumptions that they have abandoned tradition.

### Pelican Sublineage

Pelican Pelagids are commonly large-framed, broad-winged, and distinguished by expandable throat pouches.

Their plumage may be white, grey, brown, black, or marked by vivid seasonal colors.

Pelican communities possess strong traditions of transport, fishing, communal provisioning, and rescue. The throat pouch may be used to carry food, water, tools, or small protected objects.

It is not an unrestricted public storage space.

Requests to “hold something for a moment” should be made politely and accepted only after explicit consent.

### Cormorant Sublineage

Cormorant Pelagids possess dense, dark plumage, long necks, powerful feet, and exceptional underwater mobility.

Unlike many water-resistant Avianfolk, their feathers may absorb a controlled amount of water, reducing buoyancy and improving diving ability.

After swimming, they often rest with wings spread to dry.

Cormorant communities are common around rocky coasts, rivers, island settlements, and maritime industries.

Their comfort beneath the surface makes them especially valuable in salvage and rescue work, though many resent being treated as natural diving equipment.

### Frigatebird Sublineage

Frigatebird Pelagids possess long wings, forked tails, and extremely lightweight bodies suited to sustained flight.

Many are poor swimmers and avoid landing directly upon open water. Their aerial skill allows them to remain aloft for extraordinary periods.

Some possess expandable throat displays in red, orange, or dark coloration.

Frigatebird cultures frequently emphasize timing, aerial observation, and the careful selection of landing sites.

Their association with taking food from other flyers has produced an unfair reputation for piracy, though actual maritime raiding traditions do exist among a small number of historic communities.

### Puffin Sublineage

Puffin Pelagids are compact, cold-adapted, and capable swimmers.

They commonly possess black-and-white plumage with brightly colored beaks and facial markings. Their wings are suited both to rapid flight and underwater propulsion.

Puffin communities often inhabit cliffs, rocky islands, cold coasts, and burrow-like settlements protected from severe weather.

Outsiders frequently describe them as charming.

Puffins generally tolerate the observation until it becomes an excuse to treat them as harmless, childish, or decorative.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Harbor Memory

You gain proficiency in the Insight skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Pelagids learn the harbor lamps, river bends, household roofs, and familiar faces that mark the final miles of a long return.

### Oria — Crosswind Recovery

While flying, you have advantage on saving throws against being charmed or frightened.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to fly up to half your flying speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric Pelagids are taught that endurance includes recognizing when the route must change.

### Vinyot — Salvager’s Descent

While falling, you can reduce the fall speed of yourself or one willing creature within 5 feet of you by 60 feet per round, potentially negating falling damage.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti Pelagids know that a failed approach may still become a profitable landing—provided the contract does not claim the wreckage first.

### Arneria — Rite of the Far Horizon

You know the _gust_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _feather fall_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using wind, saltwater, cloud movement, or the distant horizon as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Pelagids ask what waits beyond sight.

Their rites remind them that not every answer is an invitation to travel there.

### Al’Ar — Gale-Wake Cry

When you use **Skyward Pulse**, you may release a piercing cry carried across wind and water.

Creatures of your choice within 10 feet of you must succeed on a Constitution saving throw against your Skyward Pulse saving throw DC or be deafened until the start of your next turn.

Once you use this trait, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Among Al’Ari Pelagids, a voice cast into the storm is both challenge and signal:

I remain here.

Find me if you can.

## XIII. Playing a Pelagid

When creating a Pelagid character, consider:

- What shore do they consider home?
    
- How long have they been away from it?
    
- Who maintains an Empty Place for them?
    
- Whom do they consider Harbor-Kin?
    
- What is the longest journey they have completed?
    
- What journey did they abandon?
    
- Are they proud of their endurance or trapped by others’ expectations of it?
    
- When do they ask for help?
    
- What weather frightens them?
    
- What landmark tells them they are nearly safe?
    
- Do they prefer open distance or sheltered communities?
    
- What obligation waits for them elsewhere?
    
- Have they ever returned too late?
    
- Do they romanticize lost shores or believe some places should remain lost?
    
- How do they regard the Sky-Reeves: ancestors, navigators, reckless pilots, obsolete officials, or keepers of knowledge still needed today?
    
- Would they cross beyond a Horizon Tithe without knowing what waited ahead?
    
- What would convince them to turn back?
    

A Pelagid may be restless or rooted, quiet or boisterous, cautious or daring.

Their lineage prepares them to cross great distances.

It does not require them to leave.
