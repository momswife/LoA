# Raptorid

**Folk Classification:** [[‎ Avianfolk|Avianfolk]]  
**Lineage Classification:** Raptorid  
**Registry Scope:** Sapient Lineage and Homeland Variance  
**Recognition Status:** Full Sapient Recognition  
**Delver Eligibility:** Unrestricted  
**Common Names:** Raptors, Highwings, Keen-Eyes  
**Date of Filing:** 3rd Cycle, 3386 A.D.

---

## I. Lineage Overview

**Raptorids** are an Avianfolk lineage distinguished by powerful flight anatomy, hooked beaks, grasping talons, exceptional distance judgment, and an ancestral aptitude for rapid aerial descent.

Their recognized sublineages include eagles, hawks, falcons, kestrels, kites, vultures, condors, ospreys, and other winged peoples whose bodies are adapted for soaring, pursuit, scavenging, or the sudden commitment of a dive.

Raptorid cultures are often associated with decisiveness, vigilance, and martial discipline. These associations are not entirely without foundation, but outsiders frequently mistake cultural admiration for biological certainty.

A Raptorid is not born impatient, territorial, or warlike.

Many traditional Raptorid communities simply place great value upon preparing carefully and acting completely. Flight at great height allows time for observation. A dive does not. Once begun, hesitation can be more dangerous than a poor decision.

That understanding has shaped generations of Raptorid philosophy:

> Consider from above.  
> Commit on the descent.  
> Answer for where you land.

---

## II. Origins and Collective Memory

Raptorids possess no single accepted homeland or origin.

Their oldest settlements have been found among mountain aeries, highland forests, desert escarpments, coastal cliffs, and open plains where broad thermal currents allow long periods of soaring. Some communities claim descent from elemental winds. Others describe themselves as children of the first creatures to look down upon the world without despising it.

Neither claim has been proven.

Raptorid history survives through flight routes, recited landmarks, feather records, memorial cairns, and the names of currents passed between generations. A route may remain culturally important long after the settlement it once connected has vanished.

Among many old Raptorid families, history is remembered vertically.

The summit marks triumph. The sheltered ledge marks survival. The valley marks loss. The place where an ancestor fell may be remembered more clearly than the town in which they were born.

This tradition has made Raptorids exceptionally capable custodians of geographic memory. It has also created disputes when ancestral routes cross borders established centuries after the journeys themselves.

## III. Body and Aerial Form

Raptorids are generally lean, muscular, and built around the demands of powered flight.

Most stand between **5 feet 6 inches and 6 feet 6 inches**, though vultures, condors, and certain eagle lineage may exceed this range. Their wings vary considerably in breadth and shape.

Broad-winged Raptorids excel at soaring and conserving energy. Narrow-winged expressions are often suited to speed, pursuit, and rapid changes in direction. Forest-dwelling populations may possess shorter wings that allow sharper movement through confined spaces.

Their hands and feet end in curved talons. The degree to which these affect tool use varies by individual. Most Raptorid settlements produce handles, grips, writing implements, armor fittings, and climbing surfaces adapted to their anatomy.

Raptorid vision is often exceptionally sensitive to distant motion. This does not mean every Raptorid possesses perfect eyesight. Injury, inheritance, age, illness, and magical exposure affect them as they would any other people.

### Plumage

Plumage differs by lineage, ancestry, age, and region.

Natural patterns may include:

- Dark flight feathers with pale heads or shoulders
    
- Rust, gold, brown, grey, black, or white plumage
    
- Facial markings around the eyes
    
- Banded wings and tails
    
- Mottled patterns adapted to forests, cliffs, or deserts
    
- Bare or lightly feathered heads among some vulture lineage
    

Plumage is not a reliable measure of temperament, status, or moral character.

The persistent belief that pale Raptorids are noble while dark-feathered or carrion-adapted Raptorids are ominous has no basis beyond old heraldry and poor storytelling.

## IV. Society and Kinship

Raptorid communities may be called **aeries**, **holds**, **circles**, or **watches**, though these terms describe settlements and affiliations rather than a universal form of government.

Many traditional communities place importance upon mentorship.

Young Raptorids are commonly taught by several adults rather than by parents alone. One mentor may teach flight, another craft, another history, and another the responsibilities of adulthood. A person who saves a young flyer during their first dangerous fall may be welcomed into the family regardless of blood.

Respect is often earned through reliability rather than seniority alone.

An elder who can no longer fly may retain considerable authority if their judgment remains sound. Conversely, physical prowess without discipline is rarely regarded as sufficient qualification for leadership.

### The Circle Before the Dive

A common Raptorid custom requires serious disagreements to be discussed while walking or circling a shared space.

The participants are expected to examine the dispute from several perspectives before anyone proposes action. Interrupting this process with an immediate demand is considered reckless in traditional communities.

Once a decision is reached, however, those involved are expected to support it fully or formally withdraw from it.

This custom is frequently misunderstood by outsiders. Raptorids may appear slow to make a choice and then startlingly swift once that choice has been made.

## V. Inner Life, Virtue, and Shadow

Raptorid communities commonly admire:

- Clarity of purpose
    
- Controlled courage
    
- Reliability under pressure
    
- Vigilance without panic
    
- Protection of those unable to see danger approaching
    
- The willingness to accept responsibility for one’s choices
    

These values can produce skilled guardians, patient observers, and leaders who remain composed in crisis.

They also cast long shadows.

|Admired Quality|Constructive Expression|Potential Shadow|
|---|---|---|
|Decisiveness|Acting when others freeze|Refusing to reconsider|
|Vigilance|Recognizing danger early|Treating everyone as a threat|
|Independence|Remaining capable in isolation|Rejecting necessary help|
|Protectiveness|Guarding people and places|Possessiveness and control|
|High standards|Pursuing mastery|Cruelty toward failure|
|Courage|Accepting meaningful risk|Mistaking recklessness for honor|

A recurring Raptorid anxiety is the fear of **failing after others have trusted their judgment**.

Some respond by becoming meticulous. Others avoid leadership entirely. A few become controlling, believing that responsibility grants them the right to make every decision themselves.

### Internal Divisions

Modern Raptorid communities disagree over several major questions:

- Whether the old Sky-Reeve traditions should be restored
    
- Whether Stormriding is sacred practice, dangerous spectacle, or both
    
- Whether ancestral flight routes should carry modern legal authority
    
- Whether settled city life weakens or merely changes Raptorid culture
    
- Whether vigilance is a communal responsibility or an excuse for surveillance
    
- Whether young Raptorids should be expected to learn flight before pursuing other callings
    

These arguments prevent Raptorid culture from becoming singular or stagnant.

## VI. The Sky-Reeve Inheritance

Of all modern Avianfolk lineages, Raptorids maintain one of the strongest cultural connections to the ancient [[The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]].

Surviving records indicate that many Reeves, oath-pilots, and Stormriders were Raptorids. Their endurance at altitude, sensitivity to distant weather, and aptitude for controlled descent made them natural participants in the Wind-Tithe traditions.

They were not the only people among the clans.

The Sky-Reeves also included other feathered skyfolk, air-kin, and allied aerial peoples. Raptorid participation should therefore be understood as prominent rather than exclusive.

### Raptorids Among the Four Currents

Several Raptorid traditions preserve associations with the four cardinal currents:

- **North Gale** traditions emphasize endurance, restraint, and survival in bitter air.
    
- **South Zephyr** traditions favor navigation, diplomacy, and the protection of migratory routes.
    
- **East Tempest** traditions celebrate speed, challenge, and the dangerous rites of Stormriding.
    
- **West Cyclone** traditions emphasize adaptability, rescue, and the ability to escape collapsing winds.
    

These divisions no longer represent a unified political order. They endure through family customs, racing colors, old oaths, guild insignia, and ceremonial markings.

Claims of direct descent from an ancient Wind-Mother or All-Wind Rider are common.

Verifiable claims are considerably rarer.

### Stormriding

Modern Raptorids remain heavily represented among Stormriders.

Some regard the practice as a sacred continuation of the Wind-Tithe: the rider enters the storm not to conquer it, but to give courage, motion, and risk in exchange for passage.

Others treat it as sport.

Professional circuits in Vinyot and Al’Ar have transformed Stormriding into a lucrative spectacle involving sponsors, contracts, engineered harnesses, and carefully measured danger. Traditionalists condemn these races as empty performance, though many still attend them.

The contradiction is an old one.

Even the ancient Wind-Mothers who denounced the first riders are believed to have watched from hidden balconies.

## VII. Raptorids in Modern Aerathon

Raptorids live throughout the Five Greater Regions.

Their professions are no more predetermined than those of any other people, though their aerial capabilities often shape the opportunities and burdens placed before them.

### Allemance

In [[Five Greater Regions/Allemance|Allemance]], Raptorids are frequently found among roadside communities, watch towers, farming settlements, and the courier routes that connect distant households.

Allemancian culture places less value upon dramatic displays of flight than upon returning when promised. A Raptorid who notices danger and brings their neighbors safely home is often admired more than one who defeats that danger alone.

Many serve as weather-watchers, shepherds, wardens, messengers, and craftspeople who maintain towers, roofs, mills, and high bridges.

### Oria

In [[Five Greater Regions/Oria|Oria]], Raptorids are valued for structural inspection, emergency response, hazard observation, and communication between isolated Houses.

Oric Raptorids often reject romantic ideas about flight. Wings are treated as tools that require care, maintenance, and disciplined use. A pilot who risks injury for spectacle may be considered less impressive than one who recognizes unsafe conditions and refuses to launch.

Several Oric Houses maintain dedicated aerial recovery teams trained to extract workers from damaged towers, mines, and storm-battered machinery.

### Vinyot

In [[Five Greater Regions/Vinyot|Vinyot]], flight is opportunity—and nearly every opportunity carries a contract.

Raptorids work in aerial commerce, rapid transport, private security, racing, surveying, advertising, and high-value delivery. Wealthy firms compete fiercely for pilots capable of crossing dangerous routes ahead of their rivals.

Vinyoti Raptorids are often skilled negotiators because aerial work creates complicated questions of liability. Wind, injury, cargo loss, magical interference, and territorial restrictions are all matters of contract.

Stormriders may become celebrated athletes, corporate symbols, or heavily indebted performers whose public glory conceals the cost of their harness.

### Arneria

In [[Five Greater Regions/Arneria|Arneria]], Raptorid flight often carries ritual significance.

Temple ascents, military observations, funeral circles, and oath-flights may all require trained Raptorid participants. Some guard sacred towers or carry flame between distant sanctuaries without allowing it to touch the ground.

Arnerian Raptorids frequently distinguish between flight undertaken for personal desire and flight undertaken under vow. The latter is treated with considerable gravity.

Breaking an aerial oath before landing is believed in some traditions to leave the promise suspended in the sky, where it may trouble the oathbreaker through dreams and ill winds.

### Al’Ar

In [[Five Greater Regions/Al'Ar|Al’Ar]], Raptorids live among storm coasts, cliff settlements, dock-cities, traveling performances, and communities rebuilt repeatedly after violent weather.

Al’Ari flight culture values improvisation. Currents change, structures fail, and plans rarely survive the first true storm. A capable pilot is therefore not one who follows a route perfectly, but one who can abandon it without losing the purpose of the journey.

Stormriding is widely practiced, though not always formally. Many Al’Ari Raptorids regard every severe crossing as a conversation with the sky.

## VIII. Raptorids and Delving

Raptorids are frequently recruited as scouts, breach observers, rescue specialists, and aerial combatants.

Their ability to gain height and commit quickly to a target can be decisive within large chambers. Their distance vision and sensitivity to movement also make them valuable when examining unstable ceilings, distant mechanisms, or hidden ledges.

These advantages create their own dangers.

Raptorid delvers are especially vulnerable to overcommitting. A dive that would be safe in open air may become fatal when a chamber changes shape, reverses gravity, conceals a barrier, or closes behind the flyer.

Experienced Raptorids are taught to identify:

- The space required to turn or arrest a descent
    
- Possible false floors and false horizons
    
- Crosswinds produced by hidden passages
    
- Surfaces capable of supporting a landing
    
- Threats positioned above the apparent ceiling
    
- Whether a companion can reach them if they fall
    

A common delver maxim warns:

> The Labyrinth does not fear the eagle.  
> It merely builds a room where the eagle forgets to turn.

## IX. Names, Titles, and Address

Raptorid naming traditions vary by region.

Many carry an ordinary given name followed by a family name, settlement name, professional title, or earned by-name. Older aerial communities may use route names or current names instead of hereditary surnames.

Examples include:

- Avara Highwatch
    
- Cael of the Third Current
    
- Dren Ashwing
    
- Eshra Far-Circle
    
- Ilyr Red-Talon
    
- Kaith Gale-Marked
    
- Maela of Northwatch
    
- Orren Stormbound
    
- Sava Broadwing
    
- Tael Duskward
    
- Veyra Cliffborn
    
- Ysran of the Long Descent
    

Historic titles include:

- **Wind-Mother**
    
- **Reeve**
    
- **Current-Keeper**
    
- **Tithe-Bearer**
    
- **Oath-Pilot**
    
- **Stormrider**
    
- **All-Wind Rider**
    

These titles should not be used casually.

Calling an accomplished flyer a **Stormrider** may be complimentary. Addressing someone as **Reeve** implies legal or inherited authority and may be interpreted as mockery when used incorrectly.

## X. D&D Lineage Traits

Your Raptorid character has the following traits.

### Creature Type

You are a Humanoid.

### Ability Score Increase

Your Dexterity score increases by 2, and your Wisdom score increases by 1.

### Size

Your size is Medium.

Raptorids commonly stand between 5 feet 6 inches and 6 feet 6 inches tall.

### Speed

Your walking speed is 25 feet.

You have a flying speed of 50 feet.

### Flight

You can fly using your wings. To do so, you cannot be wearing medium or heavy armor.

### Talons

You have talons that you can use to make unarmed strikes.

When you hit with them, the strike deals slashing damage equal to `1d4 + your Strength modifier`.

### Skyward Pulse

As a bonus action, you can beat your wings with explosive force.

Each creature within 5 feet of you must succeed on a Strength saving throw or be pushed 5 feet away from you.

The saving throw DC equals:

`8 + your proficiency bonus + your Strength modifier`

You can use this trait a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus. You regain all expended uses when you finish a long rest.

### Predator’s Dive

If you move at least 20 feet straight downward while flying toward a creature and hit it with a melee weapon attack on the same turn, you deal extra damage equal to your proficiency bonus.

The target must also succeed on a Strength saving throw against your **Skyward Pulse** saving throw DC or be knocked prone.

## XI. Recognized Sublineages

Raptorid sublineages describe broad physiological inheritances. They do not determine personality, culture, profession, or homeland.

### Eagle Lineage

Eagle Raptorids are commonly broad-winged and powerfully built. Many possess exceptional soaring endurance and plumage marked by strong contrasts around the head, shoulders, or tail.

They are often associated with leadership because of old heraldic traditions, though many Eagle Raptorids consider this expectation burdensome.

### Hawk and Kite Lineage

Hawk and Kite Raptorids possess highly varied builds adapted to forests, plains, cities, or open hills.

They are often skilled at changing altitude quickly and navigating irregular terrain. Urban populations have become particularly adept at moving through towers, bridges, cranes, and crowded rooflines.

### Falcon and Kestrel Lineage

Falcon and Kestrel Raptorids tend toward narrow wings, light frames, and rapid aerial acceleration.

They are heavily represented in racing and courier traditions, though the assumption that every Falcon desires speed has become a common source of irritation.

### Vulture and Condor Lineage

Vulture and Condor Raptorids are adapted traditions for endurance, high-altitude soaring, and survival across harsh distances.

Many possess bare or lightly feathered heads and necks. Their communities have historically performed essential work involving sanitation, death rites, battlefield recovery, and the safe disposal of corrupted creatures.

Despite this, outsiders frequently associate them with death or misfortune. Most Vulture Raptorids regard such superstition with exhausted contempt.

### Osprey Lineage

Osprey Raptorids possess adaptations suited to coastal, river, and lake environments.

Their feet are often especially capable of gripping wet or shifting surfaces. Osprey communities commonly maintain close relationships with [[Races of Aerathon/Aquaticfolk/Aquaticfolk Overview|Aquaticfolk]] settlements and maritime cultures.

## XII. Homeland Imprints

If the campaign uses Homeland Imprints, choose one trait representing the region that most strongly shaped your character.

A homeland represents upbringing and cultural experience. It does not need to match your birthplace or ancestry.

### Allemance — Hearthline Vigil

You gain proficiency in the Insight skill.

When you take the Dash or Disengage action on your turn, you can also make a Wisdom (Perception) check as a bonus action.

Allemancian Raptorids are taught that speed matters most when it brings warning, aid, or a missing traveler home.

### Oria — Measured Withdrawal

While flying, you have advantage on saving throws against being charmed or frightened.

When you are reduced to half your hit points or fewer, you can use your reaction to fly up to half your flying speed without provoking opportunity attacks.

Once you use this reaction, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Oric training emphasizes survival over pride. A controlled retreat preserves the pilot, the knowledge they carry, and the possibility of another attempt.

### Vinyot — Spiral Descent

While falling, you can reduce the fall speed of yourself or one willing creature within 5 feet of you by 60 feet per round, potentially negating falling damage.

You also have advantage on Dexterity (Acrobatics) checks made while airborne.

Vinyoti pilots learn to turn a failing contract, collapsing platform, or ruined approach into a survivable landing.

### Arneria — Rite of the Open Sky

You know the _gust_ cantrip.

When you reach 3rd level, you can cast _feather fall_ once with this trait. You regain the ability to do so when you finish a long rest.

When you reach 5th level, you can cast _augury_ as a ritual, using the movement of wind and cloud as your ritual focus.

Arnerian Raptorids are taught to read the sky as both witness and warning.

### Al’Ar — Storm Laugh

When you use **Skyward Pulse**, you may release a defiant cry carried by the force of your wings.

Creatures of your choice within 10 feet of you must succeed on a Constitution saving throw against your Skyward Pulse saving throw DC or be deafened until the start of your next turn.

Once you use this trait, you cannot use it again until you finish a long rest.

Among Al’Ari Raptorids, laughter in the heart of a storm is not denial of danger.

It is an announcement that danger has not yet won.

## XIII. Playing a Raptorid

When creating a Raptorid character, consider:

- What does decisive action mean to them?
    
- Do they trust their own judgment?
    
- Who taught them to fly, and what remains unresolved between them?
    
- Do they regard vigilance as protection, duty, habit, or fear?
    
- Have they ever committed to a choice they later regretted?
    
- What responsibility do they carry for others?
    
- Are they connected to the Sky-Reeve legacy?
    
- Do they consider Stormriding sacred, foolish, exhilarating, or exploitative?
    
- What would make them abandon a plan after beginning the descent?
    
- Where do they feel most at home: above the world, among a community, or somewhere solid beneath a roof?
    

A Raptorid character may be bold or cautious, severe or gentle, disciplined or impulsive.

Their lineage gives them wings.

It does not decide where they fly.
