## **I. Political Identity of Allemance**

Allemance is often mistaken for a monarchy.

It is not.

What remains of royal authority in Allemance is **heritage rather than command**. Old noble houses possess land, wealth, titles, and deep-rooted social capital—but their ability to impose will beyond their immediate spheres has eroded over generations.

Power in Allemance is **distributed**, negotiated between baronies, guild coalitions, urban councils, and the Ministry of Delving Operations.

The crown still exists.  
It simply does not close its fist anymore.

---

## **II. The Queen & the Crown**

The Queen of Allemance remains a unifying symbol, not an autocrat.

Her authority functions through:

- Charter ratification
    
- Dispute mediation between baronies
    
- Ceremonial oversight of national crises
    

Direct intervention in local governance is rare and politically costly. Baronies that resent interference have proven capable of economic resistance and legal obstruction.

The crown governs by **consensus or consequence**, not decree.

---

## **III. Baronies as Semi-Sovereign States**

Allemance’s true political units are its **baronies**.

Each barony:

- Sets local law
    
- Raises its own guard
    
- Manages taxation and land use
    

Barons are wealthy and powerful within their territory—but their influence weakens sharply beyond it.

Attempts at consolidation typically fail, undone by:

- Guild resistance
    
- Neighboring baronial alliances
    
- MDO arbitration
    

The land belongs to those who work it.  
The title belongs to those who remember.

---

## **IV. Old Royalty & Fading Authority**

Ancient noble houses remain prominent, particularly in cities like **Dole**, **Patrie**, and older river baronies.

They control:

- Historic estates
    
- Long-standing trade privileges
    
- Cultural institutions
    

They do not control:

- Delver access
    
- Modern guild networks
    
- Urban populations
    

Their power is real—but **localized and conditional**.

> _“The blood is old.”_  
> _“The leverage is new.”_

---

## **V. Guilds, Cities, and Economic Counterweight**

Guild coalitions and city councils increasingly dictate policy.

- Trade guilds influence taxation
    
- Urban councils set labor standards
    
- Merchant houses can freeze baronial income
    

The MDO’s licensing authority over delvers and Labyrinth access has further diluted noble dominance.

Allemance’s politics are increasingly **transactional**, not hereditary.

---

## **VI. The Role of the MDO**

The **Ministry of Delving Operations** is the single greatest stabilizing force in Allemance.

It:

- Arbitrates baronial disputes
    
- Regulates delver activity
    
- Enforces cross-baronial charters
    

While nobles often resent the MDO’s influence, they rely on it to prevent escalation into private wars.

---

## **VII. Delvers & Social Disruption**

Delvers destabilize traditional hierarchies.

- They accumulate wealth rapidly
    
- They ignore inherited status
    
- They move freely between baronies
    

Old houses cannot easily control them—and attempts to do so often backfire.

Delvers are tolerated because they are **too useful to suppress**.

---

## **VIII. External Relations**

### **Vinyot**

Viewed with wary respect. Corporate governance challenges Allemagnian tradition.

### **Al’Ar**

Admired for autonomy, envied for mobility.

### **Arneria**

Seen as ideologically alien; Causeway politics resist noble influence.

### **Oria**

Respected for discipline and restraint.

---

## **IX. Conflict Resolution & Power Balance**

Allemance avoids civil war through:

- Charter law
    
- Economic pressure
    
- MDO mediation
    

Violence between baronies is rare and heavily sanctioned.

Power struggles play out in courts, contracts, and coin—not on battlefields.

---

## **X. Political Character of Allemance**

Allemance is a land where:

- History still matters
    
- Titles still open doors
    
- But no one rules alone
    

The crown endures by bending.  
The nobles endure by adapting.

> _“A lord may own the field.”_  
> _“The harvest answers to the many.”_
