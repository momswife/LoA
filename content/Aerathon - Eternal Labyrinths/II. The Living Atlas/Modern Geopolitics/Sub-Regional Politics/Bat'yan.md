## **I. Political Identity of the Bat’yan**

The Bat’yan is not a state.

It has no capital, no crown, no unified banner, and no appetite for them.

What binds the Bat’yan together is not law, but **rhythm**—shared customs, seasonal labor, oral tradition, and a deeply ingrained belief that **authority must remain close enough to be spoken to face-to-face**.

To outsiders, Bat’yan politics appear disorganized.  
To those who live there, they are simply **human-scaled**.

---

## **II. Barangays & Local Authority**

The foundation of Bat’yan governance is the **barangay**.

Each barangay:

- Governs itself entirely
    
- Maintains its own laws and customs
    
- Collects its own taxes or tribute
    
- Resolves disputes internally
    

At the center of each barangay is the **datu**, chosen through local tradition—often from respected families, elders, or figures associated with the Pirhouan church.

The datu’s authority is personal, conditional, and revocable. A datu who loses the trust of their people does not rule for long.

---

## **III. The Raja: Voice Without a Throne**

Above the barangays stands the **raja**—and even then, only barely.

The raja is:

- A diviner capable of communing mentally with the datus
    
- A coordinator for external diplomacy and crisis response
    
- A spokesperson, not a sovereign
    

The raja serves **at the pleasure of the barangays**. If they act against the collective will, they are removed—sometimes quietly, sometimes not.

> _“The raja speaks because we listen.”_  
> _“They rule only while they remember that.”_

---

## **IV. Relationship with the Beylik**

The greatest external pressure on the Bat’yan comes from **Arneria’s Beylik**.

- The Beylik seeks order, registration, and control
    
- The Bat’yan values flexibility, privacy, and adaptation
    

This creates constant low-grade tension. Beylik patrols rarely venture deep into Bat’yan territory, knowing that enforcement without cooperation would be costly and ineffective.

The Bat’yan resists not with rebellion, but with **inertia**.

---

## **V. Law, Justice & Social Enforcement**

Bat’yan law is unwritten but well understood.

- Justice emphasizes restitution over punishment
    
- Shame is more powerful than imprisonment
    
- Repeat offenders often find themselves quietly excluded
    

There are no grand courts—only circles of witnesses.

---

## **VI. Faith & Political Culture**

Faith in the Bat’yan is inseparable from governance.

- Religious leaders often guide civic decisions
    
- Ritual calendars shape labor and travel
    
- Faith is communal, not institutional
    

Under the Quiet Edict, the Bat’yan did not change much. The gods were never distant here, but neither were they rulers.

---

## **VII. Delvers, the Labyrinths, and Necessity**

The Labyrinths have struck Bat’yan lands hard.

- Incursions damage farmland and villages
    
- Monsters disrupt carefully balanced ecosystems
    
- Delvers are both saviors and destabilizers
    

Barangays welcome delvers pragmatically—but never blindly. Aid is accepted. Authority is not surrendered.

---

## **VIII. External Perception**

### **Arneria**

Sees the Bat’yan as inefficient and unruly.

### **Vinyot**

Views the region as underdeveloped but culturally rich.

### **Allemance**

Finds the Bat’yan familiar in its love of local rule.

### **Oria**

Respects Bat’yan resilience and self-sufficiency.

---

## **IX. The MDO’s Role**

The **Ministry of Delving Operations** is one of the few external bodies tolerated widely.

- It negotiates access rather than imposes it
    
- It respects barangay autonomy
    
- It provides resources without demanding restructuring
    

This earns it cautious trust.

---

## **X. Political Character of the Bat’yan**

The Bat’yan endures because it refuses to harden.

It bends, adapts, and absorbs pressure rather than confronting it directly. No single blow can shatter it, because there is no single center to strike.

> _“You cannot conquer a place that never agreed to be one thing.”_
