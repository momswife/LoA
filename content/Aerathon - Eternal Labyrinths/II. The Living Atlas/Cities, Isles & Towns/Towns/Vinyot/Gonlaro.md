### _Abandoned Settlement of the Cristalmonte Lowlands_

**Region:** **Vinyot**  
**Nearest Authority:** **Dole** (project sponsor, records sealed)  
**Settlement Status:** Abandoned / Restricted  
**Local Designation:** _Uninhabited. Unsafe._

---

## **I. Overview**

**Gonlaro** was the largest and most ambitious attempt to settle **Kelvonostro**, founded between the **Cristalmonte River** and miles of surrounding swamp. Its purpose was simple and profitable: harvest insects at industrial scale and ship them downstream to be processed into fertilizer and animal feed for Vinyot’s expanding markets.

The plan failed faster than anyone expected.

Floodwaters rose. The ground shifted. And within a single season, the town collapsed inward—its people fleeing so suddenly that homes were left stocked, locked, and lit.

Gonlaro was never resettled.

---

## **II. Geography & Layout**

The remains of Gonlaro sit half-sunken in mud and reed.

- **Central Causeway:** once elevated, now broken and partially submerged
    
- **Processing Yards:** rusted drying racks and insect presses, overgrown
    
- **Worker Rows:** stilted housing collapsed at uneven angles
    
- **Foreman’s House:** stone-built, still standing, thick with algae
    

A **perpetual black fog of insect swarms** hangs over the site, densest at dawn and dusk. Visibility drops sharply without warning.

---

## **III. Economy & Work (Former)**

Gonlaro existed entirely as an extraction site.

**Primary output:**

- Processed swamp insects for fertilizer
    
- Protein mash for animal feed
    

Labor was grueling but well paid—by Vinyot standards. Many workers planned to stay only a year.

Most did not get the chance.

---

## **IV. Governance & Authority**

The town was governed by a **single project authority**, appointed directly from Dole.

- **Foreman:** an armadillo beastfolk industrial overseer
    
- Authority absolute within the settlement
    
- No civilian council
    

When the collapse began, no evacuation orders came from above. Workers left on their own initiative.

Official responsibility for Gonlaro is still **disputed on paper**.

---

## **V. Labyrinth Pressure (Unspoken)**

The mines beneath Gonlaro connected to a known subterranean route later identified as part of **the Loamlink**.

When floodwaters surged, the lower tunnels were **completely inundated with swamp water**, collapsing access and sealing off a long stretch of the route.

No public Labyrinth declaration was ever made.  
The Quiet Edict remains in effect.

The area is now classified as **structurally unstable and spiritually active**.

---

## **VI. People & Tone**

Gonlaro is not quiet.

Insect noise never stops. Water shifts where there is no wind. Structures creak as if settling, though they have not moved in years.

Locals downstream report:

- Lights flickering in windows at night
    
- Figures moving through the fog
    
- Voices carrying across the marsh
    

It is widely believed that **not everyone escaped**.

---

## **VII. Rumors & Narrative Use**

Stories associated with Gonlaro include:

- The **foreman’s fortune** still sealed inside his flooded home
    
- Ghosts of workers bound to unfinished labor
    
- A submerged mine entrance leading into the Loamlink
    

**Gonlaro is best used as:**

- A haunted ruin tied to industrial failure
    
- A Quiet Edict cover-up site
    
- A dangerous obstacle blocking subterranean travel
