## **I. Overview**

**Malarthain Stronghold** is a reclusive walled city in **northwestern Vinyot**, set among the **Fortunata Hills** near the **Betemere Mountains**. Built of dark mountain stone and iron-banded gates, the stronghold stands where harsh plains rise toward coal seams, ore veins, and old beastfolk memory.

The city is almost entirely beastfolk and maintains a population of **under five thousand**. Its wealth comes not from fertile land, but from what can be cut, quarried, trapped, dried, forged, and defended.

**Primary Exports:** coal, stone, metal, worked arms, furs, dried meats

**Primary Imports:** grain, fine textiles, medicines, luxury goods, arcane components

**City Type:** Walled Stronghold / Mining City / Beastfolk Cultural Redoubt

**Primary Faith:** Malar, the Beastlord

**Civic Saying:** __“What is tempered lasts.”__

Malarthain is not large enough to dominate Vinyot by population, but it is difficult to ignore. Its mines feed regional industry, its metalwork is respected across northern trade roads, and its people have survived by treating isolation not as weakness, but as a form of armor.

Despite its reclusive culture, Malarthain serves as the primary hub for the **western sect of Vinyot**. This importance comes less from hospitality than infrastructure: the stronghold controls the safest rail-linked passage through the Betemere Mountains, connecting western Vinyot’s mines, forge towns, and hill settlements to **Louvain** and the broader continental trade network.

Malarthain also functions as western Vinyot’s most reliable security provider. Its knights and guards are contracted, requested, or politically expected to protect trade roads, rail-linked supply lines, mining routes, and frontier caravans throughout the region. This gives the city influence far beyond its population, even among settlements that dislike its insularity.

## **II. Geography & Architecture**

Malarthain sits on a hard stone shelf overlooking cold plains and broken hill country. The land surrounding it is difficult to farm. A few small farms cling to the outside of the walls, but they produce barely enough to supplement the city’s needs. Most food security comes from preserved meats, trade agreements, and disciplined rationing during winter and siege seasons.

### **The Fortunata Hills**

The surrounding hills are rich in coal, iron, stone, and deeper ore pockets. Mine roads wind through guarded passes toward the Betemere Mountains, with outposts watching old emergency shafts and sealed tunnels.

### **The Betemere Rail Link**

Malarthain’s strategic value is magnified by the rail line that cuts through the Betemere Mountains toward Louvain. The route is difficult to maintain, but it allows western Vinyot to move ore, stone, coal, forged goods, and military supplies without relying entirely on slower wagon roads through harsh hill country.

Because of this rail connection, merchants, MDO officials, guild factors, and neighboring settlements all treat Malarthain as the practical gateway of western Vinyot, even when the stronghold itself would prefer fewer outsiders at its gates.

### **The Walls**

Malarthain’s walls are tall, severe, and practical.

- Basalt and mountain-stone ramparts reinforced with iron bands

- Narrow gates designed to slow wagons and expose attackers

- Guard towers positioned toward mines, trade roads, and farmsteads

- Ritual markings worked into defensive stonework by old Malarite hands

The walls are not ornamental. They are a declaration: Malarthain expects danger to come eventually.

### **The Inner Stronghold**

The oldest district contains the lord’s manor, council chambers, ancestral halls, sanctuaries of Malar, and the most traditional beastfolk households. Buildings are low, heat-retentive, and deliberately plain. Public art favors carved stone, trophies of the hunt, lineage marks, and ironwork rather than color or grandeur.

### **The Outer Shell**

Outside the oldest civic core is the industrial shell that grew around the original walls: forge lanes, smokehouses, tanneries, rail yards, supply depots, miner barracks, worker housing, cheap taverns, and workshops that keep the city alive. These districts are more practical than sacred, though nearly every forge still keeps a token mark to the Beastlord.

The Outer Shell is poorer, smokier, and more crowded than the Inner Stronghold, but it is not merely a slum. It is where Malarthain’s laboring families live, argue, drink, organize, and keep the city’s economy breathing. The Inner Stronghold often claims to preserve Malarthain; the Outer Shell believes it **is** Malarthain.

## **III. History**

Malarthain began as a fortified beastfolk redoubt built around mine access, hunting routes, and sacred devotion to Malar. In its earliest form, the stronghold was less a city than a vow: a place where beastfolk could hold stone, bloodline, craft, and instinct together against a world that often misunderstood them.

The modern history of Malarthain is inseparable from **House Cardwell**, a ruling line marked by contested succession, military defense, weakened faith, restored tradition, and grief.

### **House Cardwell & the Malarthain Line**

**Blaez Cardwell**, a wolffolk bastard son of a knight and a royal lady, proved his claim during a tumultuous succession dispute and took the throne in **3329 A.D.** Deeply faithful to Malar, Blaez fortified the stronghold, strengthened its defenses, and brought new blood into the old court.

His marriage to **Margerie du Perron**, an elkfolk noblewoman from another Vinyot city, secured trade but marked the beginning of a slow cultural shift. Margerie found the city’s deep Malarite devotion distasteful, and later historians often cite her influence as the first visible weakening of the faith’s place at the lord’s table.

Blaez’s eldest son, **Rainer Cardwell**, inherited the throne in **3363 A.D.** and married **Rosalie Cardwell-Kollek**, a stoatfolk guildmaster’s daughter, as a gesture of goodwill toward the city’s merchant and industrial families. Rainer was killed during the **Siege of 3371**, before producing an heir. In the aftermath, Blaez’s second son, **Edouard Cardwell**, assumed rule and continued the family’s emphasis on fortification, trade, and knightly defense.

Edouard’s eldest son, **Cecil Cardwell**, later took the throne after Edouard’s death in **3375 A.D.** Cecil tried to embody his grandfather’s ideal of the lord-knight, often placing himself directly in danger to defend the city. He was beloved for it, but the habit became fatal. After grievous injuries sustained during the **Battle of 3377**, Cecil died in **3378 A.D.**, leaving the rule of Malarthain to his younger brother.

### **Lord Elias Cardwell**

**Lord Elias Cardwell**, a cervine elk, is the current ruler of Malarthain. Unlike his brother Cecil, Elias was never naturally drawn to militant pride or conquest. He was gentler, more administrative, and more comfortable with ledgers, supply, and political balance than battlefield glory.

After his father’s illness and death, Elias prepared to serve on his brother’s council rather than rule. During this period, he sought guidance from **Lady Neve Malarthain**, a young vulpine woman intended to inherit leadership of the city’s old Malarite priesthood. Neve carried a sacred connection to the ancient beastfolk lineage of the fort, and through her, Elias found not only faith but identity.

Elias married Neve in **3377 A.D.**, only months before Cecil’s fatal injury. Through his council seat and marriage, he began restoring Malarite faith to the political heart of the city after generations of decline.

When Neve died in childbirth in **3381 A.D.**, leaving behind **Princess Lyla Cardwell**, Elias withdrew from public life. He now rules primarily through council, guards, and advisors, devoting much of his private life to Lyla’s education and upbringing.

To Elias, Lyla is not merely his daughter. She is the living bridge between House Cardwell, Lady Neve, and Malarthain’s ancient beastfolk foundation. His love for her is genuine, but it has hardened into obsession. He raises her in intensely traditional ways, refusing much of the modern world for fear that it will sever the past from the future.

## **IV. Key Historical Events**

Malarthain records its history carefully, but not generously. Public chronologies favor turning points over genealogical detail, preserving enough to explain the present without exposing every weakness of House Cardwell.

| Year | Event |

|---|---|

| **3329** | Blaez Cardwell proves his line of succession and takes the throne, beginning the modern Cardwell hold over Malarthain. |

| **3371** | The siege of Malarthain kills Lord Rainer and permanently hardens the city’s defenses around mine access and emergency tunnels. |

| **3377–3378** | Elias marries Lady Neve; the sinkhole disaster devastates the knights; Cecil dies from his wounds; Elias ascends. |

| **3379** | The Inner/Outer city divide is formalized and the Council of Rings is founded to stabilize mine, forge, and trade interests. |

| **3381** | Princess Lyla is born and Lady Neve dies in childbirth, pushing Elias into seclusion and intensifying his traditionalism. |

| **3388** | The royal council elects to send knights of its order to become delvers and investigate the nearby Nameless Labyrinth. |

| **3509** | The Under-Crucible is publicly opened, contained, and later marked as resolved. The full Cardwell record of what remained active is sealed. |

### **The Siege of 3371**

In **3371 A.D.**, neighboring powers attempted to gain control of the ore wealth beneath Malarthain. Rogue agents entered through emergency mine shafts outside the city, catching Lord Rainer and his knights off guard. The attack began with Rainer’s death and escalated into a violent siege that lasted several difficult weeks.

An older but still formidable Blaez helped lead the defense. Malarthain eventually broke the siege, then negotiated with the attackers from a position of survival rather than mercy alone: improved trade, expanded protection agreements, and heavy investment in outposts around emergency mine entrances.

The lesson became permanent. Malarthain would trade, but never trust an unguarded tunnel again.

### **The Battle of 3377**

In **3377 A.D.**, construction on a new mineshaft caused a sinkhole to open, swallowing one of Malarthain’s few farms whole. The collapse exposed old subterranean pressure, buried passages, and something deeper that historians now suspect brushed against the veil of what would later be classified as the **Under-Crucible**.

The disaster weakened the city at the worst possible moment. Monsters surged through damaged mine systems while bandits struck exposed trade routes. Umber hulks and ankhegs tore through the tunnels, but the greatest threat was a juvenile purple worm that devastated the city’s defenders.

Cecil Cardwell was gravely injured. Many knights died, and several veteran defenders were maimed or elevated afterward for valor during the disaster. While monsters tore through damaged mine systems, younger route-guard units fought to keep bandits from exploiting Malarthain’s exposed trade roads.

The threat was eventually contained, but at severe cost. Malarthain chose to cave in the affected tunnels, sacrificing profitable ore rather than risk another breach. Whether to revisit the sinkhole site became one of the most contentious debates in the city’s modern political history.

## **V. Governance**

Malarthain is ruled by **Lord Elias Cardwell**, though his reclusive habits have made the council the true day-to-day instrument of government.

### **The Lord’s Council**

Lord Cardwell communicates primarily through a council of roughly eight guards, advisors, and civic representatives. This body handles matters of defense, trade pressure, religious observance, mine safety, and external relations.

The council is not a parliament in the modern sense. It exists because Elias trusts a small circle more than crowds, guild delegates, or foreign officials.

### **The Lord’s Guard**

Cardwell keeps in his direct employ a unit of elite guards charged with:

- mobilizing the city watch

- assessing security threats

- defending the mines and trade roads

- guarding the lord, Princess Lyla, and sacred civic spaces

- responding to monster activity near sealed tunnels

The most prominent figures in this structure are veteran captains, sworn protectors, and trusted council defenders whose authority comes from service rather than popularity.

### **The Council of Rings**

Founded in **3379 A.D.**, the **Council of Rings** arose from the formal divide between Malarthain’s inner traditional authority and its outer industrial interests. Its members represent forge, mine, trade, and supply factions whose work keeps the stronghold economically viable.

The Council of Rings does not replace Lord Cardwell. It exists because Malarthain can no longer function by noble command alone. The mines, forges, and trade houses require structured representation, especially after the devastation of 3377.

Tension between the Lord’s Council and the Council of Rings remains one of Malarthain’s defining political pressures.

## **VI. Notable Figures**

### **Lord Elias Cardwell**

Current lord of Malarthain. A cervine elk of Cardwell blood, Elias is gentle by temperament but rigid in grief. He adopted Lady Neve’s culture and Malarite faith wholeheartedly and now clings to tradition as a means of preserving both her memory and the city’s ancient identity.

He makes few public appearances and devotes most of his private life to Princess Lyla’s education.

### **Lady Neve Malarthain-Cardwell**

Deceased wife of Elias and mother of Lyla. A vulpine woman tied to the ancient beastfolk lineage of the stronghold and intended to become a leading figure in the city’s Malarite faith. Her marriage to Elias restored sacred authority to the ruling house. Her death in childbirth reshaped Malarthain’s politics, faith, and future.

### **Princess Lyla Cardwell**

Six-year-old daughter of Elias and Neve. Lyla is the city’s most important living symbol: Cardwell heir, Malarthain bloodline bearer, and the fragile future of a reclusive people. She is highly educated, intensely sheltered, and rarely seen beyond manor walls.

### **Cardwell Attention to Jackal-Fey Lineages**

Lord Cardwell pays unusual religious and political attention to the jackal-fey race as a whole. Within his traditionalist reading of Malarite omen-lore, jackal-fey are treated as liminal figures: wanderers between instinct and judgment, hunt and grave, beast-pride and old divine memory.

This does not mean every jackal-fey visitor is automatically trusted, but Cardwell considers their arrival, counsel, and symbolism worth serious attention. To his court, this fascination is one of the clearest signs of how deeply grief, faith, and ancient beastfolk lineage have fused in his rule.

## **VII. Economy**

Malarthain survives through mountain industry, disciplined trade, and control of the western rail passage through the Betemere Mountains.

### **Primary Exports**

- Coal

- Iron and worked metal

- Stone and cut masonry

- Durable arms and armor

- Furs

- Dried meats

- Mining tools and forge components

### **Primary Imports**

- Grain and softer produce

- Medicines

- Arcane reagents

- Fine cloth

- Books and instructional materials

- Luxury items for noble and council households

The city’s farms are culturally important but economically insufficient. Everyone understands that a failed trade season can be more dangerous than a failed harvest.

### **The Unspoken Surplus**

By public accounting, Malarthain’s prosperity comes from disciplined extraction, careful trade, and old mountain veins that have not yet run dry. This is only partly true.

The Cardwell family keeps a deeper secret: the Under-Crucible was never truly exhausted. Even after it was declared resolved, the Labyrinth remained active beneath the sealed works, quietly producing ore at an impossible rate. The city’s “fortunate veins” are not natural deposits alone, but a managed miracle hidden behind collapsed tunnels, restricted ledgers, and generations of Cardwell silence.

This secret is the foundation of Malarthain’s modern stability. It is also the thing that could ruin it. If the MDO, rival Vinyot cities, delver guilds, or the Council of Rings ever proved the ore was Labyrinth-born and still renewing, Malarthain would face legal seizure, economic invasion, religious crisis, or all three at once.

## **VIII. Faith & Culture**

Malarthain worships **Malar, the Beastlord**, but local understanding of Malar differs sharply from the common outsider view.

To many non-beastfolk, Malar is a god of bestial savagery, bloodlust, and animal violence. This reputation is not helped by zealots who have committed atrocities in his name.

In Malarthain, however, Malar is revered as:

- the god of the hunt

- the keeper of ancient instinct

- the patron of resilience

- the spirit of conquest through survival

- the reminder that beastfolk nature is not shameful

- the symbol of peoples misunderstood, feared, and vilified by outsiders

Some worshippers treat him as almost druidic. Others honor him as a war god. Many see him as the divine expression of beastfolk pride and endurance.

Malarthain’s old faith weakened after Blaez’s generation as trade, finance, and secular metalwork displaced clerical authority. Enchantment once tied directly to Malarite rites shifted into the hands of wizards, alchemists, and commercial forge-practitioners. Elias and Neve’s marriage began reversing that decline by restoring faith to the lord’s table.

> __“The hunt teaches. The forge proves.”__

## **IX. Law & Defense**

Malarthain law is severe, practical, and survival-minded.

Commonly punished offenses include:

- mine sabotage

- theft of ore or worked metal

- relic smuggling

- endangering farms or food stores

- unauthorized entry into sealed tunnels

- desecration of Malarite rites

- threats against House Cardwell

Defense is organized through the Lord’s Guard, city watch, mine outposts, and forge-trained militias. The city does not seek war, but it prepares for siege as a matter of civic instinct.

### **Western Vinyot Route Guard**

Malarthain’s knight and guard system is one of the city’s most important exports. Because the stronghold sits near the Betemere rail connection and the mountain roads that feed western Vinyot’s economy, neighboring settlements rely on Malarthian patrols to guard trade routes, escort ore shipments, defend caravans, and respond to bandit or monster activity along the hills.

These deployments are framed by Malarthain as duty rather than mercenary work. The stronghold protects the routes because the routes feed the region, and because any threat to western Vinyot’s trade eventually becomes a threat at Malarthain’s gates. In practice, this gives House Cardwell political leverage across nearby mining towns, forge villages, rail stops, and merchant houses.

## **X. Relationships to Delvers & the Labyrinths**

For most of its recent history, Malarthain has kept itself almost entirely out of delver business. Lord Cardwell’s grief, traditionalism, and suspicion of spectacle have discouraged official involvement, though some townsfolk quietly watch delver broadcasts from afar.

This begins to change as the **Nameless Labyrinth** opens near Malarthain and older omen traditions regain political weight. Cardwell’s attention to jackal-fey symbolism, combined with pressure from practical council voices, allows delving to be reframed not as modern vanity, but as a sacred hunt into the unknown.

Under this interpretation, Cardwell encourages two of his most adept knights to become delvers—not for fame, not for wealth, but to defend Malarthain and investigate possible links between the Labyrinth, Malar, and the city’s buried past.

### **The Under-Crucible: Public Resolution, Private Secret**

In the party-era crisis, the Under-Crucible is not yet a public dungeon. It exists as the sealed 3377 sinkhole site: a buried wound associated with monster emergence, Cecil’s death, the maiming of several defenders, and the loss of profitable mine access.

Later public Ministry chronology records the Under-Crucible as officially opened in **3509 A.D.**, brought under regulation, and ultimately **resolved**. To most of Aerathon, it becomes an example of Malarthain’s discipline: the stronghold faced the Labyrinth beneath its feet, contained it, and returned to industry without allowing delver spectacle to swallow its identity.

That is the public story.

The Cardwell family knows the truth is more dangerous. The Under-Crucible remains active beneath Malarthain, but not in the way the public fears. It no longer behaves like an open monster wound. Instead, it continues to generate ore, replenishing sealed veins and feeding the city’s mines with a supply that should be geologically impossible.

House Cardwell keeps this secret for several reasons. Revealing it would invite MDO intervention, rival claims, guild exploitation, and theological panic over whether Malar has blessed the city, tested it, or trapped it. The Council of Rings suspects irregularities in the city’s production, but only the Cardwell inner line knows the full truth: Malarthain’s prosperity is built atop a Labyrinth that never stopped giving.

To the public, the Under-Crucible is resolved.

To House Cardwell, it is contained.

To the Labyrinth, it may simply be waiting.

## **XII. Cultural Identity & Legacy**

Malarthain believes survival comes from remembering what the world tried to make you forget.

Its people do not rush to modernize. They refine. They temper. They distrust novelty until it proves it can endure. To outsiders, this can look like fear, stubbornness, or backwardness. To Malarthain, it is discipline.

The city is wounded by grief, protected by stone, fed by mines, and guided by a faith the wider world rarely understands correctly. Beneath all of it is the Cardwell secret: Malarthain survives because something under the mountain still answers when the city cuts into the dark.

Lord Cardwell wants to preserve the past, the Outer Shell wants recognition for the labor that keeps Malarthain alive, and the Council of Rings wants enough access to the truth to protect its own interests. Cardwell’s growing attention to jackal-fey omen traditions only deepens the uncertainty over whether the city is being guided, tested, or manipulated by forces older than its walls.

> __“Steel forgets nothing.”__

## **XIII. Quirky Natures**

- Smiths greet trusted peers by clasping forearms rather than hands.

- No forge fire is allowed to go fully cold without a spoken reason.

- Children learn the names of local beasts before they learn distant capitals.

- Broken mirrors are buried face-down so they cannot “remember the eyes that looked into them.”

- Princess Lyla is spoken of warmly but rarely seen.

- Delver broadcasts are officially dismissed and privately watched.

- A guest who wastes meat is judged more harshly than one who wastes coin.

- The Inner Stronghold grows quieter each year.
