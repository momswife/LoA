## **I. Overview**

**Linang** is a broad **Causeway ring-region** in the Bat’yan, notable for enclosing and defending one of the most fertile agricultural basins along the road. Unlike most Causeway cities, Linang was designed not to dominate trade or traffic, but to **protect land worth keeping**.

The Causeway splits at Linang’s entrance, rising to the top of a continuous wall before rejoining on the southern side. This elevated road forms a complete ring, sealing the farmland within from encroaching wildlife and—more recently—far worse threats.

Linang is not one city, but many **Ring-Towns**, bound together by habit, harvest, and shared risk.

**Population:** ~52,000 (distributed across ring settlements)  
**City Type:** Ring-Region / Agrarian Causeway City  
**Primary Renown:** Protected agriculture, resilient communities  
**Regional Saying:** _“The road stands so the fields may live.”_

---

## **II. Geography & Architecture**

Linang occupies a shallow basin of exceptionally rich soil, surrounded by rainforest and rolling Bat’yan terrain.

### **The Ring Wall**

- A solid stone wall supporting the Causeway road above
    
- Continuous around the entire basin
    
- Designed to block large wildlife and wandering herds
    

The wall is functional rather than ornamental, thick enough to support both traffic and patrols.

### **The Ring-Towns**

Settlement is sparse but constant along both sides of the wall:

- Small homes, storehouses, lifts, and gathering halls
    
- Buildings spaced with intention, not density
    
- Elevators descend daily into the basin below
    

Architecture favors durability and ease of repair over grandeur.

---

## **III. History**

Linang was founded on foresight.

Early Bat’yan settlers recognized the basin’s fertility—and its vulnerability. Rather than fence the land piecemeal, they raised the road itself into a barrier, ensuring the Causeway would serve both travelers and those who stayed.

For generations, Linang prospered quietly, exporting grain and produce while maintaining its small-town rhythm. That balance fractured with the arrival of **repeated Labyrinth incursions** along the Causeway corridor.

Entire stretches of the ring were abandoned as monsters claimed ground faster than it could be reclaimed. Families relocated inward or outward, leaving behind a visible scar: miles of empty buildings where laughter once carried.

### **Key Historical Events**

- **1048 A.D.** — Construction of the **Linang Causeway Ring**, elevating the road to protect farmland.
    
- **1783 A.D.** — Formal recognition of the **Ring-Town Charter**, decentralizing governance.
    
- **2897 A.D.** — First recorded **Labyrinth incursion** into Linang’s outer farmland.
    
- **3112 A.D.** — Establishment of the **Dead Stretch**, following repeated evacuations along the eastern ring.
    

Linang has not given up its land—only stepped back to breathe.

---

## **IV. Governance**

Linang has no single ruler.

- Each Ring-Town governs itself through local councils
    
- Basin-wide decisions are made through seasonal assemblies
    
- Emergency authority is temporarily granted during incursions
    

This loose structure favors consensus and rapid adaptation.

---

## **V. Economy**

Linang’s economy is rooted—literally.

**Primary Outputs**

- Grain, root crops, and hardy fruits
    
- Seed stock adapted to Bat’yan conditions
    
- Preserved foods for Causeway travelers
    

Trade is steady but secondary to subsistence. The fields feed Linang first.

---

## **VI. Faith & Culture**

Linang’s culture is shaped by routine.

- Early mornings and shared lifts
    
- Communal meals after harvest
    
- Seasonal festivals tied to planting and protection
    

Colorful rainforest birds are the only wildlife allowed within the basin and have become beloved symbols—painted on walls, carved into lifts, and stitched into clothing.

> _“If it flies, it’s welcome.”_

---

## **VII. Law & Defense**

Defense in Linang is layered and local.

- Wall patrols coordinated between Ring-Towns
    
- Rapid response teams for incursion alerts
    
- Evacuation routes drilled regularly
    

Weapons are tools, not status symbols.

---

## **VIII. Relationships to the Labyrinths**

Linang has suffered deeply from Labyrinth proximity.

- Multiple localized incursions over decades
    
- Monsters claim territory but do not advance evenly
    
- Ongoing efforts aim to **contain rather than eradicate**
    

Linang’s long-term goal is reclamation—returning stolen land once the threat can be held.

## **X. Cultural Identity & Legacy**

Linang is defined by patience.

It does not rush to reclaim what was lost, nor does it abandon hope. Its people believe land remembers care, and that one day the dead stretches will be planted again.

> _“The field waits.”_

---

## **XI. Quirky Natures**

- Elevators are named and argued over
    
- Birds are counted as good-luck omens
    
- Children learn evacuation routes before arithmetic
    
- Empty houses are kept clean, just in case
