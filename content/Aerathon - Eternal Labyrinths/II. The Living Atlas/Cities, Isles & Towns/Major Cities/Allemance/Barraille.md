## **I. Overview**

**Barraille** (BAR-all) is a cliffbound mining city perched high in the mountains of **Glasrún**, where forest thins into stone and gravity is treated as an adversary rather than a law. Founded as a frontier extraction outpost, the city has grown outward beyond the cliff’s original edge into a tiered sprawl of stone platforms, lift towers, and reinforced overhangs.

Massive counterweighted elevators—each capable of lifting **three fully laden delver wagons**—connect Barraille’s vertical districts through the dense forest canopy below. From the **Upper District**, Glasrún spreads endlessly outward, a reminder that Barraille survives only because it has earned the mountain’s tolerance.

---

## **II. Geography & Architecture**

Barraille is built **against and over** a sheer mountainside.

- **The Upper District:**  
    The original settlement carved directly into cliff stone, housing council halls, communal hearths, and lift controls. Considered the civic heart of the city.
    
- **The Overhangs:**  
    Later expansions built outward from the cliff face, supported by massive stone pylons and iron braces. These residential and workshop tiers sway subtly in high wind—something locals insist visitors “learn to trust.”
    
- **The Liftworks:**  
    A network of sacred vertical elevators, counterweighted by stone and ore. Maintained communally; sabotage is punished by permanent exile.
    
- **The Mantle Roads:**  
    A web of mining paths leading into the **Mantle Mountains**—some active, many abandoned, none clearly marked.
    

---

## **III. History**

Barraille began as a **temporary extraction camp**, established centuries ago to supply Allemance with mountain ore without violating the Glasrún Pact. When noble sponsors deemed the peaks too hostile and the yields too slow, oversight was quietly withdrawn.

The workers stayed.

What followed was not abandonment, but adaptation. Miners, beastfolk, and free settlers refused to descend, slowly transforming the camp into a permanent city through labor alone. No charter was granted. No lord claimed it. Barraille was not founded—it was **insisted upon**.

Over generations, the city’s defining trait became refusal: refusal of noble authority, refusal of inherited power, refusal to leave stone that had already accepted their weight.

**Key Historical Events:**

- **602 A.D.** — Establishment of the **First Extraction Camp** under Allemantic charter, intended as a temporary mining operation.
    
- **771 A.D.** — The **Silent Withdrawal**, when noble oversight ceases without formal decree; the remaining workers choose to stay.
    
- **1038 A.D.** — Completion of the **Great Liftworks**, enabling permanent vertical expansion and marking Barraille’s transition from camp to city.
    
- **1662 A.D.** — The **Stone Council Compact**, formalizing consensus rule and explicitly rejecting hereditary authority.
    
- **2879 A.D.** — The **Three-Wagon Trial**, proving the modern lift systems’ capacity and cementing Barraille’s role as Allemance’s primary mountain ore supplier.
    

---

## **IV. Governance**

Barraille rejects noble governance outright.

Authority rests with the **Stone Council**, a rotating body of senior miners, liftwardens, and elders. There are no permanent leaders, no titles of inheritance, and no formal hierarchy beyond reputation and proven labor.

Decisions are made by open debate and consensus. Those who speak without having worked are rarely heard twice.

---

## **V. Economy**

Barraille supplies Allemance with critical materials:

- Iron ore
    
- Structural stone
    
- Reinforcement metals
    

Trade is conducted through scheduled lift convoys and pre-agreed exchanges. Coin is accepted, but **labor barter** remains culturally preferred.

Barraille does not compete on volume—it competes on reliability.

---

## **VI. Faith & Culture**

Faith in Barraille is **functional rather than ceremonial**.

- Old Allemantic hearth rites persist
    
- Beastfolk ancestral reverence is common
    
- The mountain itself is treated as a judge
    

Prayers are brief. Rituals mark beginnings, survivals, and safe returns—not dominion.

> _“The mountain hears effort louder than words.”_

---

## **VII. Law & Defense**

Law in Barraille is enforced socially rather than institutionally.

- Serious crimes are rare
    
- Endangerment of lifts or workers carries the harshest penalties
    
- Permanent exile is the ultimate sentence
    

Defense relies on terrain, vigilance, and the simple fact that attackers must climb to reach anything of value.

---

## **VIII. Relationships to the Labyrinths**

Barraille maintains strict distance from the Labyrinths.

- No known entrances within city bounds
    
- Deep mining tunnels are regularly collapsed or sealed
    
- Delvers are tolerated only if they respect local boundaries
    

The mountain is not treated as something to be conquered—and neither are the depths beneath it.

---

|Region|Relationship|Summary|

|**Allemance**|Economic|Essential ore supply|

|**Vinyot**|Distrusted|Merchants unwelcome|

Barraille influences Allemance not through politics, but through **necessity**.

---

## **X. Cultural Identity & Legacy**

Barraille does not advertise itself.  
It endures visibly, carved into stone where retreat is impossible.

The city is proof that Glasrún is not only forest and silence—it is also **height, weight, and refusal**.

> _“If you wish to rule Barraille,”_ one Ministry note concludes,  
> _“first convince the mountain.”_

---

## **XI. Quirky Natures**

- Locals can identify outsiders by how tightly they grip railings
    
- Children learn lift signals before letters
    
- Heights are described in “stories,” not feet
    
- Visitors are warned never to insult the mountain—even jokingly
