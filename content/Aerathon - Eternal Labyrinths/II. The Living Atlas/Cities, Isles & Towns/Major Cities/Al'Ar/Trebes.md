## **I. Overview**

**Trebes**, known collectively as **the Three Babes**, is an offshore dock-city spread across the outer coasts of three closely clustered islands in **Al’Ar**. Unlike most island settlements, Trebes does not cling to its shoreline. Instead, it reaches outward—its docks and platforms extending far into deep water along winding, ever-changing routes.

Each year, the city dismantles and rebuilds its platforms in a **new configuration**, ensuring that no two dock maps of Trebes are ever the same. This practice grants access to deep-water fishing, creates space between households, and—most importantly—keeps residents away from the islands’ interiors.

Few Trebeans willingly walk inland.  
Fewer still return unchanged.

---

## **II. Geography & Architecture**

Trebes is defined by **avoidance as design**.

### **The Outer Platforms**

- Vast wooden and stone dockworks reaching far beyond the coast
    
- Zig-zagging routes that change annually
    
- Modular construction meant to be dismantled and rebuilt
    

The result is a city that resists mapping and refuses permanence.

### **The Inner Shores**

The islands themselves are sparsely developed:

- Emergency shelters and storage huts only
    
- No permanent districts
    
- Inland travel discouraged without cause
    

Children are taught early: _“The shore is enough.”_

---

## **III. History**

Trebes began as a conventional island port, but early settlers quickly learned that the islands’ interiors were **unwelcoming in ways that defied explanation**. Initial inland expansion attempts ended quietly—no great disasters, just enough loss and unease to discourage repetition.

Over time, the city adapted not by fortifying inward, but by **retreating outward**. Platforms grew longer, homes spaced farther apart, and the habit of rebuilding routes annually emerged as both practical fishing strategy and cultural ward. Trebes became a city that survived by refusing to linger too close to what unsettled it.

In recent centuries, the stories have grown stranger. Where once there were vague warnings, now there are shared symbols, repeated dreams, and consistent sightings. The city still does not investigate officially—but it builds farther away than ever before.

**Key Historical Events:**

- **512 A.D.** — The **First Withdrawal**, when multiple inland homesteads are abandoned following unexplained disappearances and recurring night phenomena.
    
- **889 A.D.** — Adoption of the **Wandering Platforms**, formalizing the annual dismantling and reconfiguration of Trebes’ dock routes.
    
- **1374 A.D.** — The **Rootwalk Incident**, in which explorers traverse the mangrove shallows without touching water and report identical visions despite traveling separately.
    
- **2016 A.D.** — The **Silent Cartography**, when every known inland map of Trebes is destroyed by communal agreement after a season of shared dreams.
    
- **3142 A.D.** — Increase in **Symbol Reports**, with repeated markings found carved into mangrove bark across all three islands, matching descriptions from unrelated households.
    

---

## **IV. Governance**

Trebes has no central authority.

- Platform clusters govern themselves
    
- Seasonal councils coordinate rebuild routes and safety zones
    
- No permanent inland jurisdiction exists
    

Any proposal involving inland expansion is rejected immediately—often without discussion.

---

## **V. Economy**

Trebes survives on what lies **beyond** and **below**.

**Primary Exports**

- Deep-water fish and preserved seafood
    
- Rare mangrove resins
    
- Expertise navigating shallow-rooted waters
    

**Primary Imports**

- Timber and metal for platform construction
    
- Dry goods
    
- Ritual and warding materials
    

Trade vessels are required to dock only along approved outer routes.

---

## **VI. Faith & Culture**

Faith in Trebes is **preventative rather than devotional**.

- Shrines face outward, never inland
    
- Offerings are made to calm, not summon
    
- Silence is considered respectful, not empty
    

Many families maintain private warding traditions passed down through generations.

> _“You don’t thank what watches you,”_  
> _“you make sure it stays watching.”_

---

## **VII. Law & Defense**

Trebes enforces law through **collective refusal**.

- No standing guard force
    
- Dangerous behavior results in exclusion or loss of platform access
    
- Inland violations carry the harshest penalties
    

Defense is achieved by distance, dispersion, and awareness.

---

## **VIII. Relationships to the Labyrinths**

Trebes does not seek the Labyrinths.

- Mangrove-adjacent anomalies are treated as potential breaches
    
- Delving inland is strongly discouraged
    
- Survivors of unsanctioned expeditions rarely provide coherent accounts
    

The city treats unknown spaces as **warnings, not invitations**.

---

Trebes influences others primarily through **what it refuses to exploit**.

---

## **X. Cultural Identity & Legacy**

Trebes endures by **not demanding answers**.

Whether the inner islands are haunted, cursed, or misunderstood matters less than the simple truth that the city still stands—afloat, adaptable, and wary. Trebes is proof that survival sometimes means knowing when not to look closer.

---

## **XI. Quirky Natures**

- Dock routes are renamed yearly after bad dreams
    
- Inland wind is considered a warning sign
    
- Children are forbidden from playing hide-and-seek toward the mangroves
    
- Some residents claim the platforms drift farther each year on their own
