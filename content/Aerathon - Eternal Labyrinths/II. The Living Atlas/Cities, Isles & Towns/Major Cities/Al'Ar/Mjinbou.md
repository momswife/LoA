## **I. Overview**

**Mjinbou** (MEEN-bau) is a coastal city stretched along the northern shore of **Aurica**, within **Al’Ar**. It is renowned as the **Single-Shore City**, a settlement that deliberately disperses itself for half of every year.

For six months annually, the population fractures into fleets and households that sail outward—some along established trade routes, others embedding themselves temporarily in distant dock cities. When the season turns, Mjinbou reconvenes, reforming its civic body through shared stories, spoils, and experience.

Mjínbou is not defined by permanence.  
It is defined by **return**.

---

## **II. Geography & Architecture**

Mjínbou occupies a **single, continuous shoreline**, with minimal inland expansion.

- Long docks designed for repeated departure and return
    
- Communal halls used for seasonal assemblies
    
- Homes constructed to be sealed, abandoned, and reopened cyclically
    

During the reassembly season, the shore is vibrant and dense. During dispersal, it becomes quiet but never deserted—fires remain lit as signals to those at sea.

Maps of Mjinbou must account for **time**, not just terrain.

---

## **III. History**

Mjínbou began as a modest coastal settlement along Aurica’s northern shore, founded by Al’Ari sailors who rejected the dangers of fixed Storm Voyages. Rather than committing to permanent routes, early Mjínbouans learned to survive by **leaving together and returning changed**, embedding flexibility into the city’s very bones.

Over generations, this practice transformed from necessity into philosophy. While other ports grew powerful through accumulation, Mjínbou grew influential through **absence**—its people carrying knowledge, culture, and warning signs back from every corner of the world. The city’s shoreline became a place of reunion rather than departure, where stories mattered as much as cargo.

Today, Mjínbou stands as Al’Ar’s great listener: a city that knows the world not by ruling it, but by visiting it—and coming home.

**Key Historical Events:**

- **412 A.D.** — The **First Dispersal** occurs after an extended storm season; multiple dock-quarters scatter independently and successfully reunite months later, establishing the Single-Shore tradition.
    
- **891 A.D.** — The **Season Without Return**, when nearly a third of the city fails to reconvene; mourning rites and the modern remembrance customs are established.
    
- **1437 A.D.** — Formal rise of the **Birdkeepers of Mjínbou**, as trained avian navigation becomes essential to long-distance dispersal and intelligence gathering.
    
- **2284 A.D.** — The **Moot of Seven Horizons** ends with the decree that no single journey grants civic authority, cementing collective experience as the basis of governance.
    
- **3172 A.D.** — The **Quiet Return**, when an entire fleet comes back without song or account; their silence becomes a lasting cultural taboo.
    

Mjínbou endures not because it stands firm, but because it never forgets how to leave—and why it must return.

---

## **IV. Governance**

Governance in Mjinbou is **seasonal and narrative-driven**.

- During dispersal, authority rests with fleet captains and household elders
    
- Upon return, a **Reassembly Moot** is convened
    
- Civic decisions are made only after journeys are recounted
    

Leadership favors those who:

- Traveled widely
    
- Returned safely
    
- Brought back knowledge, not just goods
    

Those who do not return are mourned collectively and remembered by name.

---

## **V. Economy**

Mjínbou’s economy is based on **circulation rather than accumulation**.

**Primary Exports**

- Navigational expertise
    
- Trade intelligence
    
- Cultural mediation
    
- Trained avian companions
    

**Primary Imports**

- Goods gathered abroad
    
- Foreign crafts
    
- Techniques, stories, and songs
    

Coin matters less than usefulness and novelty; stagnation is the only true poverty.

---

## **VI. Faith & Culture**

Faith in Mjinbou is **plural, portable, and adaptive**.

- Shrines travel with households
    
- Sea spirits are honored before departure
    
- Hearth gods are thanked upon return
    

Belief is expected to change with experience.

> _“What you believe should survive the journey.”_

Storytelling is sacred. Silence is respected. Curiosity is mandatory.

---

## **VII. Law & Defense**

Mjínbou maintains minimal permanent defense.

- No standing military
    
- Defense handled by fleet crews during dispersal
    
- Conflict resolution occurs during reassembly
    

Crimes committed abroad are judged at home, with context considered as heavily as consequence.

---

## **VIII. Relationships to the Labyrinths**

Mjínbou has no fixed relationship with any single Labyrinth.

- Delving contracts are often taken opportunistically while abroad
    
- Artifacts are assessed communally upon return
    
- Dangerous finds may be voluntarily surrendered to the MDO
    

The city treats the Labyrinths as **places to learn from, not settle near**.

---

## **IX. Inter-Regional Influence**

|Region|Relationship|Summary|
|---|---|---|
|Tempestat|Complementary|Movement vs gathering|
|Muraya|Respectful|Information exchange|
|Vinyot|Embedded|Seasonal integration|
|Allemance|Observational|Cultural curiosity|
|Arneria|Variable|Route-dependent|
|MDO|Cooperative|Intelligence sharing|

Mjínbou’s greatest influence is informational—it knows what the world is doing before most cities realize it has changed.

---

## **X. Cultural Identity & Legacy**

Mjínbou proves that a city does not need walls, banners, or permanence to endure.

By dispersing together and returning changed, it ensures that no single narrative dominates and no single failure defines it. The city remembers the world so Al’Ar does not have to relearn it every season.

The MDO classifies Mjinbou as **culturally adaptive, informationally rich, and structurally unconventional**.

---

## **XI. Quirky Natures**

- Birds are considered valid witnesses in civic disputes
    
- Dock names change yearly based on notable journeys
    
- It is rude to ask someone where they are going—but polite to ask where they returned from
    
- Some families refuse to sleep indoors during reassembly, claiming it “confuses the tide”
