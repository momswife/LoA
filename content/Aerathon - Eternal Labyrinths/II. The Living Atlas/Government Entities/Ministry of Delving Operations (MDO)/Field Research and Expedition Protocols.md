# Field Research and Expedition Protocols

**Issuing Authority:** Ministry of Delving Operations  
**Administering Bodies:** Directorate of Labyrinth Operations and Office of Ministerial Inquiry and Public Record  
**Oversight Authority:** Office of Ethics, Review, and Record Integrity  
**Document Classification:** Field Operations and Research Standard  
**Application:** All Ministerial surveys, excavations, expeditions, recovery missions, and authorized field investigations  
**Current Standard:** Seventh Expedition Protocol  
**Date of Revised Filing:** 7th Cycle, 3388 A.D.

---

## I. Purpose of This Protocol

The **Field Research and Expedition Protocols** establish the standards by which Ministry personnel prepare, conduct, document, and conclude research beyond controlled Ministerial facilities.

These protocols apply to:

- Geographic surveys
    
- Cultural fieldwork
    
- Archaeological excavations
    
- Bestiary observation
    
- Environmental studies
    
- Relic recovery
    
- Missing-person investigations
    
- Ruin exploration
    
- Planar research
    
- Labyrinth expeditions
    
- Breach assessment
    
- Post-disaster documentation
    
- Joint operations with delver companies
    
- Evidence recovery following containment incidents
    

Field research places personnel in environments where knowledge and danger are often inseparable.

A researcher may be required to preserve evidence while the structure containing it collapses.

A delver may become the sole surviving witness to an event no instrument recorded.

A cartographer may discover that the road used during entry no longer exists.

A cultural specialist may possess the authority to halt an excavation that an operations commander considers urgent.

A relic handler may be required to destroy the very object an historian was sent to preserve.

These protocols do not eliminate such conflicts.

They establish how decisions should be prepared, recorded, reviewed, and made answerable afterward.

The purpose of an expedition is not merely to return with information.

It is to return without sacrificing lives, communities, evidence, or judgment needlessly in the process.

## III. Classes of Field Operation

Field operations are classified according to purpose and expected danger.

### Survey Mission

A mission intended primarily to record:

- Geography
    
- Routes
    
- Settlement conditions
    
- Environmental change
    
- Infrastructure
    
- Public hazards
    

Survey missions ordinarily avoid excavation, specimen removal, or entry into restricted sites unless separately authorized.

### Research Expedition

A planned mission intended to investigate a defined question through observation, interview, measurement, sampling, or controlled testing.

### Archaeological Expedition

A mission involving ruins, buried structures, funerary sites, ancient settlements, inscriptions, relic contexts, or material remains.

Archaeological expeditions require preservation planning and review of possible descendant-community claims.

### Recovery Mission

A mission intended to retrieve:

- Missing persons
    
- Bodies
    
- Records
    
- Relics
    
- Evidence
    
- Equipment
    
- Dangerous material
    

Recovery does not automatically authorize broad exploration beyond the stated objective.

### Containment Operation

A mission intended to isolate, stabilize, suppress, remove, or destroy an active hazard.

Containment priorities may supersede ordinary research goals where delay presents imminent danger.

### First-Contact Mission

A mission involving a previously undocumented sapient people, isolated settlement, surviving population, or intelligent Labyrinth-associated community.

First-contact missions require cultural, linguistic, medical, and contamination planning.

### Labyrinth Expedition

Any authorized entry into a confirmed or suspected Labyrinth environment.

Labyrinth expeditions require additional safeguards because architecture, causality, memory, time, and observation may be unreliable.

### Joint Ministerial Operation

A mission involving several directorates, regional governments, guilds, faiths, or external institutions under a single written mandate.

### Emergency Deployment

A mission initiated before ordinary preparation is possible because delay would create unacceptable risk.

Emergency deployment reduces preparation time.

It does not eliminate the requirement to document authority, decisions, casualties, and evidence afterward.

## IV. Expedition Authorization

No formal expedition may proceed without a recognized authority.

### Expedition Mandate

The mandate should identify:

- Mission title
    
- Purpose
    
- Lead office
    
- Field commander
    
- Research lead
    
- Participating offices
    
- Regional jurisdiction
    
- Expected duration
    
- Access permissions
    
- Known hazards
    
- Evidence authority
    
- Collection limits
    
- Emergency powers
    
- Communication plan
    
- Conditions for withdrawal
    
- Review requirements
    

### Scope

The mandate should define what the expedition may and may not do.

Examples include:

- Observe but not collect
    
- Map but not enter sealed chambers
    
- Recover persons but not relics
    
- Excavate one designated area only
    
- Interview willing witnesses
    
- Destroy specified hazardous material
    
- Establish temporary containment
    
- Record but not publicly broadcast
    

A mission may expand its scope only through:

- New written authorization
    
- Immediate necessity
    
- A defensible emergency decision recorded by the field commander
    

Convenience is not necessity.

### Regional Permission

Where the operation occurs within recognized regional jurisdiction, the Ministry must determine:

- Applicable charters
    
- Local authority
    
- Required permits
    
- Cultural custodians
    
- Land or site ownership
    
- Military restrictions
    
- Community rights
    
- Emergency exceptions
    

Ministerial standing does not erase local law merely because the field team considers the mission important.

### Denial of Authorization

Authorization may be denied because:

- The research question is too vague
    
- Risk exceeds likely value
    
- Required specialists are unavailable
    
- Consent has not been secured
    
- The site is culturally restricted
    
- Evacuation is not credible
    
- Evidence custody is unresolved
    
- The sponsor demands improper secrecy
    
- The team lacks necessary accommodations
    
- The mission duplicates existing work without justification
    
- Political pressure has compromised independence

## V. Expedition Leadership

Field expeditions commonly divide authority among several roles.

### Field Commander

The Field Commander is responsible for:

- Immediate safety
    
- Movement
    
- Withdrawal
    
- Perimeter discipline
    
- Emergency decisions
    
- Operational coordination
    
- Use of force
    
- Rescue priorities
    

The Field Commander may halt research where continued activity presents unacceptable immediate danger.

### Research Lead

The Research Lead is responsible for:

- Research purpose
    
- Method
    
- Observation standards
    
- Sampling
    
- Interpretation
    
- Documentation quality
    
- Preservation priorities
    
- Scholarly review
    

The Research Lead may halt collection or excavation where methods would destroy evidence or violate authorization.

### Chief Scribe

The Chief Scribe maintains:

- Field log
    
- Decision record
    
- Witness register
    
- Evidence register
    
- Time marks
    
- Casualty record
    
- Communication record
    
- Amendments to mission scope
    

The Chief Scribe should not be placed under unnecessary pressure to alter the record for morale, prestige, or political convenience.

### Chief Handler

The Chief Handler controls:

- Relic custody
    
- Dangerous specimens
    
- Containment tools
    
- Hazard packaging
    
- Transport security
    
- Evidence transfer
    

### Medical Lead

The Medical Lead possesses authority concerning:

- Fitness to continue
    
- Quarantine
    
- Contamination
    
- Evacuation
    
- Incapacity
    
- Exposure
    
- Immediate treatment
    
- Survivor stabilization
    

### Cultural Liaison

The Cultural Liaison advises on:

- Consent
    
- Sacred restriction
    
- Community authority
    
- Translation
    
- Local expectations
    
- Cultural hazards
    
- Burial rights
    
- Publication limits
    

### Cartographic Lead

The Cartographic Lead records:

- Routes
    
- Elevation
    
- distance
    
- landmarks
    
- hazards
    
- altered terrain
    
- exit paths
    
- route certainty
    

### Delver Captain

Where licensed delvers form the primary mobile party, the Delver Captain coordinates party-level movement, combat readiness, formation, and tactical response.

The Delver Captain does not automatically possess authority over research, relic custody, or cultural access.

## VI. Division of Authority

Field authority should be divided rather than concentrated.

### Immediate Danger

The Field Commander possesses final authority over movement and withdrawal during immediate danger.

### Medical Incapacity

The Medical Lead may remove a person from active duty when continued participation creates serious risk.

### Evidence Preservation

The Research Lead may order temporary suspension of excavation, collection, or destructive testing.

### Dangerous Materials

The Chief Handler may prohibit contact with an object, specimen, or material pending containment review.

### Cultural Restriction

The Cultural Liaison may halt entry into a sacred, private, or community-restricted area until authority is clarified.

### Record Integrity

The Chief Scribe may enter formal objection where a decision, alteration, or omission threatens the integrity of the field record.

### Irreconcilable Dispute

Where time permits, unresolved disputes should be referred to the written mandate or remote command.

Where delay is impossible, the Field Commander may decide the immediate course of action.

That decision must be recorded with:

- Objections
    
- Alternatives considered
    
- Reason for urgency
    
- Resulting consequences
    

Emergency command does not erase later review.

## VII. Team Composition

Expedition teams should be assembled according to mission need rather than prestige or tradition.

Possible personnel include:

- Field commander
    
- Research lead
    
- Delvers
    
- Healers
    
- Cartographers
    
- Field scribes
    
- Relic handlers
    
- Archaeologists
    
- Engineers
    
- Ecologists
    
- Linguists
    
- Translators
    
- Cultural liaisons
    
- Scrytech operators
    
- Guards
    
- Porters
    
- Guides
    
- Accessibility specialists
    
- Local experts
    
- Animal handlers
    
- Planar scholars
    
- Clergy or ritual custodians
    

### Minimum Competence

Every member should understand:

- Mission purpose
    
- Chain of authority
    
- Known hazards
    
- Withdrawal signal
    
- Emergency rally points
    
- Communication method
    
- Evidence restrictions
    
- Cultural restrictions
    
- Medical procedure
    
- What they are not authorized to do
    

### Local Expertise

Where the site lies within an inhabited or culturally claimed region, local expertise should be treated as operational necessity rather than ceremonial consultation.

### Team Size

Larger teams possess greater capability and create greater burden.

Large expeditions require more:

- Supplies
    
- Movement space
    
- Secrecy
    
- Coordination
    
- Evacuation capacity
    
- Sanitation
    
- Community support
    
- Environmental impact
    

The smallest credible team is often preferable.

## VIII. Risk Assessment

Every planned operation requires a written risk assessment.

### Hazard Categories

Risks may include:

- Structural collapse
    
- Hostile entities
    
- Magical exposure
    
- Planar contamination
    
- Disease
    
- Poison
    
- Environmental extremes
    
- Flood
    
- Fire
    
- Altitude
    
- Confined space
    
- Temporal distortion
    
- Memory alteration
    
- Illusion
    
- Political conflict
    
- Cultural violation
    
- Relic activation
    
- Public interference
    
- Broadcast exposure
    
- Equipment failure
    
- Psychological harm
    
- Team conflict
    

### Risk Ratings

Each significant hazard should be assessed by:

- Likelihood
    
- Severity
    
- Detectability
    
- Speed of escalation
    
- Reversibility
    
- Required expertise
    
- Effect on evacuation
    

### Unknown Risk

Unknown conditions must be treated as risk rather than omitted from the assessment.

A blank portion of a map is not evidence of safety.

### Risk Acceptance

The person approving the mission must identify:

- Why the risk is justified
    
- Who bears it
    
- Who benefits
    
- What alternatives were considered
    
- What conditions require cancellation
    

Researchers may not expose local guides, laborers, or contracted delvers to risks that would be rejected for permanent Ministry staff merely because those persons possess less institutional power.

## X. Field Records

Field records are the foundation of later interpretation.

### The Master Field Log

Every formal expedition should maintain one Master Field Log containing:

- Date and time
    
- Location
    
- Personnel present
    
- Environmental conditions
    
- Route
    
- Observations
    
- Decisions
    
- Orders
    
- Incidents
    
- Evidence collected
    
- Communications
    
- Casualties
    
- Changes in mission scope
    
- Withdrawal
    

### Observation and Interpretation

Field notes must distinguish:

- Direct observation
    
- Instrument reading
    
- Witness statement
    
- Team inference
    
- Rumor
    
- Prior expectation
    
- Emotional response
    
- Unknown condition
    

### Contemporaneous Recording

Notes should be made as close to the event as safety allows.

Later reconstruction should be labeled.

### Multiple Logs

Independent logs are encouraged where:

- Memory effects are possible
    
- Political disagreement exists
    
- Several disciplines are involved
    
- Labyrinth mimicry is suspected
    
- Translation is uncertain
    

Agreement among copied notes is not independent verification.

### Erasures and Corrections

Original errors should remain traceable.

Field notes should be corrected by:

- Striking through without destruction
    
- Adding a dated amendment
    
- Recording the reason for correction
    

Evidence of uncertainty must not be cleaned away for appearance.

## XI. Evidence Collection and Chain of Custody

Evidence is useful only when its history can be established.

### Evidence Register

Every collected item should receive:

- Identification mark
    
- Collection time
    
- Collection location
    
- Collector
    
- Witness
    
- Condition
    
- Method of collection
    
- Containment method
    
- Transfer history
    
- Known hazards
    
- Access restrictions
    

### Categories of Evidence

Evidence may include:

- Relics
    
- Documents
    
- Biological specimens
    
- Soil
    
- Water
    
- Magical residue
    
- Remains
    
- Images
    
- Scry-records
    
- Testimony
    
- Tools
    
- Clothing
    
- Structural fragments
    
- Environmental readings
    

### Collection Limits

The team should collect only what is:

- Authorized
    
- Necessary
    
- Safe
    
- Ethically defensible
    
- Capable of being preserved
    

A specimen should not be removed merely because it is rare.

### Contamination

Collectors must consider:

- Biological contamination
    
- Magical contamination
    
- Cross-sample contamination
    
- Planar instability
    
- Memory effects
    
- Observer-linked changes
    
- False duplication
    

### Transfer

Each transfer of custody should be recorded.

An item without a reliable chain may still possess research value, but its evidentiary status must be reduced accordingly.

## XII. Archaeological and Ruin Protocols

Ruins are not empty merely because their builders are gone.

They may remain:

- Sacred
    
- Occupied
    
- Claimed
    
- Funerary
    
- Politically significant
    
- Structurally dangerous
    
- Environmentally active
    

### Site Assessment

Before excavation, researchers should determine:

- Known ownership
    
- Descendant communities
    
- Local law
    
- Sacred status
    
- Prior disturbance
    
- Structural condition
    
- Looting risk
    
- Environmental impact
    
- Relic hazard
    
- Research value
    

### Excavation

Excavation should proceed in documented layers.

The position of an object may be more informative than the object itself.

Rapid removal without context may destroy the evidence the expedition was intended to recover.

### Funerary Sites

Human or sapient remains require:

- Legal review
    
- Cultural consultation
    
- Medical precautions
    
- Respectful handling
    
- Clear storage
    
- Return or reburial planning
    

### Publicity

Exact site locations may be withheld where publication would encourage:

- Looting
    
- Relic hunting
    
- Tourism
    
- Grave disturbance
    
- Political seizure
    
- Ecological damage
    

---

## XIII. Bestiary and Ecological Fieldwork

Living organisms should not be treated solely as specimens.

### Observation Priority

Observation should be preferred over capture where possible.

### Sapience Assessment

Where an organism displays possible language, social structure, ritual, tool use, negotiation, memory, or self-recognition, the team must reassess whether bestiary protocols remain appropriate.

When sapience is plausible, contact standards take precedence.

### Capture

Capture requires justification based on:

- Public danger
    
- Medical necessity
    
- Research necessity
    
- Conservation
    
- Containment
    
- Rescue
    

### Lethal Collection

Killing an organism for research requires explicit authorization unless immediate defense is necessary.

### Ecological Impact

Researchers must consider whether sampling, baiting, tracking, or observation alters behavior or habitat.

---

## XIV. Labyrinth Expedition Protocols

Labyrinths require additional rules because ordinary assumptions may fail.

### Entry Record

Before entry, the team should record:

- Entry time
    
- Entrance condition
    
- Exterior weather
    
- Personnel
    
- Equipment
    
- Physical health
    
- Memory baseline
    
- Known map
    
- Withdrawal schedule
    

### Mapping

Labyrinth maps should identify:

- Confirmed route
    
- Unconfirmed route
    
- Changed route
    
- One-way passage
    
- Hazard
    
- False landmark
    
- Unstable chamber
    
- Observed exit
    
- Suspected exit
    

No route should be called permanent without repeated confirmation.

### Marking

Physical markers may be:

- Moved
    
- Copied
    
- Consumed
    
- Reversed
    
- Replaced
    
- Used by hostile entities
    

Teams should use layered verification rather than one marking system.

### Memory Protocol

Where memory alteration is possible, personnel should maintain:

- Individual logs
    
- Shared passphrases
    
- Identity questions
    
- Known personal facts
    
- Timed check-ins
    
- External memory seals where available
    

A person who fails a memory check should not automatically be treated as hostile.

### Temporal Distortion

Teams should compare:

- Personal timepieces
    
- Biological signs
    
- external relays
    
- repeated events
    
- environmental cycles
    

No one measure should be trusted alone.

### False Survivors and Mimicry

Recovered persons should be treated with compassion and caution.

Verification may include:

- Medical examination
    
- Personal history
    
- Behavioral comparison
    
- magical screening
    
- witness confirmation
    

Verification must not become punishment of genuine survivors.

### Observer Effects

Where observation appears to change the environment, the team should record:

- Who observed
    
- Method of observation
    
- Sequence
    
- Emotional state
    
- Instruments used
    
- Result
    

### Withdrawal

A Labyrinth expedition should withdraw when:

- Exit certainty is deteriorating
    
- Casualty capacity is exceeded
    
- Leadership is compromised
    
- Maps become unreliable beyond recovery
    
- Containment fails
    
- Mission scope is no longer achievable
    
- Team identity is uncertain
    
- Environmental behavior changes beyond the mandate
    

Continuing merely because the objective appears close is not courage.

It is often the final stage of preventable loss.

## XV. Relic Recovery

Relics may be:

- Valuable
    
- Dangerous
    
- Sacred
    
- Sentient
    
- Politically contested
    
- Structurally integrated
    
- Environmentally necessary
    

### Before Removal

The team should determine:

- Is the relic supporting the structure?
    
- Is it containing another threat?
    
- Does a community claim it?
    
- Is it active?
    
- Can it be transported?
    
- Will removal alter the site?
    
- Is its function understood?
    
- Is destruction safer than recovery?
    

### Handling Authority

Only authorized handlers should direct contact.

Delver possession during discovery does not establish permanent ownership.

### Emergency Neutralization

A relic may be damaged or destroyed where necessary to prevent imminent harm.

The decision must be documented with:

- Threat
    
- Alternatives
    
- Authority
    
- Witnesses
    
- Outcome
    

### Commercial Claims

Commercial sponsorship does not override safety, cultural ownership, or Ministerial custody law.

---

## XVI. Communication and Scrytech

### Scheduled Contact

Expeditions should maintain scheduled contact where possible.

Failure to report should trigger a predefined response rather than improvised panic.

### Communication Loss

The team should know:

- How long to wait
    
- Whether to continue
    
- When to withdraw
    
- When rescue launches
    
- What signals may be false
    

### Public Broadcast

Broadcast expeditions require additional safeguards.

Public transmission must not reveal:

- Vulnerable communities
    
- Restricted routes
    
- Rescue positions
    
- Private medical events
    
- Sacred rites
    
- Tactical weaknesses
    
- Unsecured relic procedures
    

### Audience Influence

Public attention must not alter mission priorities.

The desire for spectacle, donations, wagers, fame, or viewer approval does not justify:

- Extended exposure
    
- Unnecessary combat
    
- Entry into prohibited areas
    
- Delayed rescue
    
- Disclosure of restricted information
    

---

## XVII. Emergency Authority

Emergencies may require deviation from ordinary protocol.

### Permitted Deviations

Possible deviations include:

- Immediate evacuation
    
- Unplanned entry
    
- Relic seizure
    
- Destructive containment
    
- Temporary detention
    
- Restricted communication
    
- Abandonment of equipment
    
- Unverified route use
    

### Documentation

Where possible, the decision should be recorded at the time.

Where impossible, it must be reconstructed afterward through:

- Survivor testimony
    
- field logs
    
- scry-records
    
- instrument data
    
- external communications
    
- physical evidence
    

### Limits

Emergency authority may not be used to justify:

- Personal enrichment
    
- Concealment of misconduct
    
- Punishment of critics
    
- Unrelated research
    
- Permanent occupation
    
- Unnecessary destruction
    
- Coerced participation after danger has passed
    

---

## XVIII. Casualty and Missing-Person Protocol

### Immediate Priority

The team should prioritize:

1. Prevention of further casualties
    
2. Stabilization
    
3. Evacuation
    
4. Reliable location marking
    
5. Communication
    
6. Preservation of relevant evidence
    

### Missing Personnel

A missing person record should include:

- Last confirmed location
    
- Time
    
- Condition
    
- Equipment
    
- companions
    
- route
    
- hazards
    
- search actions
    
- signs recovered
    

### Presumed Death

No person should be declared dead solely because recovery is difficult.

Presumption should follow applicable law and evidence.

### Remains

Remains should be handled with regard to:

- Identity
    
- family
    
- faith
    
- culture
    
- contamination
    
- legal investigation
    
- return
    

### Abandonment

Where remains cannot be recovered, the decision should be documented.

The Ministry should not disguise an impossible recovery as an unattempted one, nor sacrifice living personnel solely to avoid admitting loss.

## XIX. Withdrawal and Mission Termination

An expedition may end through:

- Objective completion
    
- Planned withdrawal
    
- Emergency withdrawal
    
- Loss of authorization
    
- Regional order
    
- Cultural refusal
    
- Safety failure
    
- Environmental change
    
- Insufficient resources
    
- Ethical suspension
    
- command compromise
    

### Withdrawal Is Not Failure

A mission that returns without completing its objective may still preserve:

- Lives
    
- evidence
    
- warnings
    
- corrected assumptions
    
- future routes
    
- proof that a method was unsafe
    

### Destruction Before Withdrawal

Evidence or equipment should be destroyed only where abandonment would create a greater danger.

The decision must identify what was destroyed and why.

## XX. Return and Quarantine

The expedition does not end at the entrance.

### Initial Processing

Returning personnel may require:

- Medical examination
    
- Magical screening
    
- contamination control
    
- memory assessment
    
- identity verification
    
- psychological stabilization
    
- equipment isolation
    

### Quarantine

Quarantine must be:

- Necessary
    
- Proportionate
    
- Reviewed
    
- Humane
    
- Properly supplied
    

Quarantine should not become punishment for survival.

### Private Recovery

Personnel should not be required to give public interviews immediately after return.

Operational debrief and medical care take priority over publicity.

---

## XXI. Debriefing

Debriefing should occur in stages.

### Immediate Debrief

Covers:

- Threats
    
- missing persons
    
- active hazards
    
- exit conditions
    
- urgent warnings
    
- evidence custody
    

### Technical Debrief

Covers:

- Route
    
- method
    
- equipment
    
- observations
    
- failures
    
- anomalous behavior
    
- recommendations
    

### Individual Debrief

Conducted separately where:

- Memory alteration is possible
    
- misconduct is alleged
    
- accounts conflict
    
- coercion occurred
    
- witnesses require privacy
    

### Cultural Debrief

Where communities were involved, the team should review:

- Consent
    
- promises
    
- restricted knowledge
    
- local concerns
    
- materials borrowed
    
- publication expectations
    
- obligations still outstanding
    

### Psychological Debrief

Psychological care should not be confused with interrogation.

Participation in clinical support should remain confidential except where immediate safety requires limited disclosure.

---

## XXII. Evidence Review and Publication

Field evidence should not enter public reference without review.

### Verification

Reviewers should assess:

- Chain of custody
    
- methods
    
- contradictions
    
- contamination
    
- translation
    
- missing context
    
- team bias
    
- environmental instability
    
- competing explanations
    

### Preliminary Findings

Preliminary findings should be labeled clearly.

### Public Claims

The Ministry should not announce:

- A new people
    
- A new species
    
- A solved historical mystery
    
- A secure route
    
- A contained threat
    
- A safe relic
    

before evidence supports the claim.

### Community Review

Where publication concerns living communities, the Standards of Observation and Cultural Contact remain applicable after return.

---

## XXIII. Expedition Failure and Internal Review

Failure may result from:

- Poor planning
    
- bad intelligence
    
- inadequate equipment
    
- misconduct
    
- unforeseeable change
    
- command error
    
- sponsor pressure
    
- communication loss
    
- Labyrinth behavior
    
- ordinary chance
    

The purpose of review is not merely to assign blame.

It is to understand what must change.

### Mandatory Review

Formal review is required following:

- Death
    
- disappearance
    
- major injury

- emergency destruction

### Protected Testimony

Personnel should be able to report:

- unsafe orders
    
- falsified logs

- coerced consent
    
- evidence theft

Retaliation is prohibited.

Sponsors may provide:

- Funding
    
- equipment
    
- personnel
    
- transport
    
- publication
    
- recovery support
    

They may not control:

- Safety conclusions
    
- evidence custody
    
- casualty reporting
    
- legal findings
    
- research results
    
- cultural restrictions
    
- public warnings
    
- withdrawal decisions
    

### Commercial Confidentiality

Commercial secrecy may delay release of minor proprietary methods.

It may not conceal:

- Public danger
    
- misconduct
    
- contamination
    
- casualty
    
- illegal relic activity
    
- false mapping
    
- breach instability

## XXV. Field Conduct

Personnel should conduct themselves as representatives of the Ministry.

They must not:

- Loot
    
- falsify records
    
- conceal discoveries
    
- harass local residents
    
- remove sacred materials
    
- abuse emergency authority
    
- provoke danger for spectacle
    
- enter prohibited sites for personal curiosity
    
- collect trophies
    
- abandon contracted personnel without cause
    
- use research access for private trade
    
- alter maps for competitive advantage
    

### Respect Among Personnel

Rank does not permit:

- humiliation
    
- discriminatory assignment
    
- reckless exposure
    
- denial of accommodation
    
- retaliation
    
- coerced silence
    

A team that cannot protect trust within itself is unlikely to preserve it in the field.

## XXVII. Pre-Entry Checklist

Before departure, the team should answer:

### Authority

- Who authorized the mission?
    
- Where does the mandate end?
    
- What local permission is required?
    

### People

- Who commands movement?
    
- Who controls research?
    
- Who controls evidence?
    
- Who may halt the mission?
    

### Risk

- What can kill or alter us?
    
- How will we detect it?
    
- What triggers withdrawal?
    

### Route

- How do we enter?
    
- How do we leave?
    
- What happens if the route changes?
    

### Evidence

- What may be collected?
    
- Who records custody?
    
- What must remain untouched?
    

### Communication

- When do we report?
    
- What happens when we do not?
    

### Responsibility

- Who bears the risk?
    
- Who benefits from the mission?
    
- Have local personnel received equal protection?
    

A mission unable to answer these questions is not ready to depart.

## XXVIII. Summary Protocol

Every Ministerial field operation should uphold seven obligations:

1. **Enter with lawful and clearly limited authority.**
    
2. **Assign responsibility before danger begins.**
    
3. **Plan withdrawal as carefully as entry.**
    
4. **Distinguish observation from interpretation.**
    
5. **Preserve evidence without sacrificing people needlessly.**
    
6. **Respect communities, sites, remains, and local knowledge.**
    
7. **Return prepared to account for every decision made beneath Ministerial seal.**
    

An expedition is not judged solely by what it discovers.

It is judged by what it risked, what it preserved, whom it harmed, and whether those choices can withstand the scrutiny of those who were not present to make them.

> Possession of a delver license, research seal, regional appointment, guild charter, or expedition contract does not grant unrestricted authority to enter private sites, remove materials, compel participation, seize relics, or expand the purpose of a mission.
> 
> Field personnel may refuse an order they reasonably believe to be unlawful, outside mandate, deliberately falsified, or immediately suicidal without defensible necessity.
> 
> Emergency deviations must be documented and remain subject to review.
> 
> Withdrawal from an expedition does not by itself constitute cowardice, breach of contract, or professional failure where continued participation would exceed authorized or reasonable risk.
> 
> No sponsor, audience, guild, commander, or public official may lawfully require personnel to remain in the field solely for reputation, entertainment, wagering, or commercial advantage.
