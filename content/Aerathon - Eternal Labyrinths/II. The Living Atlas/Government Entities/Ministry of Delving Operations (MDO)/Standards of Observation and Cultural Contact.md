> — Archivist Thelen Yarrow, _Lectures on the Ethics of Witness_

# Standards of Observation and Cultural Contact

**Issuing Authority:** Ministry of Delving Operations  
**Administering Body:** Office of Folk Anthropology and Community Consultation  
**Oversight Authority:** Office of Ethics, Review, and Record Integrity  
**Document Classification:** Research Conduct and Cultural Contact Standard  
**Application:** All Ministerial personnel, contractors, delvers, translators, and partner institutions conducting research among living communities  
**Current Standard:** Seventh Contact Standard  
**Date of Revised Filing:** 7th Cycle, 3388 A.D.

---

## I. Purpose of This Standard

The **Standards of Observation and Cultural Contact** govern how representatives of the Ministry of Delving Operations enter, observe, interview, document, and publish information concerning living peoples and communities.

These standards apply wherever Ministerial work involves:

- Cultural research
    
- Oral history
    
- Linguistic study
    
- Community interviews
    
- Religious observation
    
- Kinship and household records
    
- Traditional crafts
    
- Regional custom
    
- Sacred or funerary practice
    
- Medical or biological knowledge
    
- Migration
    
- Displacement
    
- Ancestral sites
    
- Community-held artifacts
    
- Local responses to Labyrinth activity
    
- Living claimants to ancient histories
    

The Ministry recognizes that research is not a neutral act merely because it is conducted peacefully.

The arrival of researchers may alter:

- Local authority
    
- Public reputation
    
- Access to land
    
- Commercial interest
    
- Religious privacy
    
- Political claims
    
- Internal community disputes
    
- The value placed upon particular traditions
    
- The treatment of individuals described in official records
    

A Ministry seal carries influence.

An observer who asks a question beneath that seal does not ask as an ordinary traveler.

These standards exist to ensure that inquiry does not become extraction, that documentation does not become possession, and that official curiosity does not override the dignity of those being studied.

---

## II. Scope of Application

These standards bind:

- Living Atlas researchers
    
- Annals and Antiquities personnel working with living communities
    
- Field scribes
    
- Cartographers
    
- Translators
    
- Bestiary researchers studying sapient or culturally protected beings
    
- Relic specialists examining community-held objects
    
- Licensed delvers acting as Ministerial witnesses
    
- Contracted scholars
    
- Regional consultants
    
- Scry-recording personnel
    
- Public Observation officers
    
- Partner institutions operating beneath Ministerial seal
    

They apply whether research is conducted:

- In person
    
- Through correspondence
    
- Through scrytech
    
- Through public broadcast
    
- Within an archive
    
- At a sacred site
    
- During an expedition
    
- During an emergency
    
- Through recovered testimony
    
- Through material collected by others
    

A researcher may not avoid these standards by claiming that the information was gathered informally.

## III. Foundational Principles of Cultural Contact

All cultural contact should be governed by seven principles.

### Presence Must Be Declared

Researchers must identify:

- Who they are
    
- Which office they represent
    
- Why they have come
    
- What they intend to observe
    
- What they intend to record
    
- How the information may be used
    
- Whether publication is expected
    
- What restrictions may apply
    

A researcher must not disguise an official inquiry as casual conversation when the information will later enter a Ministerial record.

### Consent Must Be Meaningful

Consent is valid only when the participant understands the nature and foreseeable consequences of participation.

Consent must not be presumed from:

- Hospitality
    
- Silence
    
- Lack of resistance
    
- Social rank
    
- Existing regional treaties
    
- Presence in a public place
    
- Participation by another household member
    
- Prior cooperation with the Ministry
    
- Economic dependence
    
- Emergency aid received from the Ministry
    

A person may welcome a researcher without agreeing to be studied.

### Communities Are Participants, Not Sources

Living communities possess interpretive authority concerning their own customs, language, history, and self-identification.

This does not mean every claim must be accepted without examination.

It means the community’s own understanding must be treated as evidence rather than merely as material for an external scholar to reinterpret.

### Observation Requires Context

No observed practice should be described without considering:

- Region
    
- Season
    
- Social class
    
- Age
    
- Gender
    
- Lineage
    
- Household
    
- Faith
    
- Historical circumstance
    
- Political pressure
    
- War
    
- Famine
    
- Displacement
    
- Local disagreement
    

A single ceremony is not necessarily universal tradition.

A crisis response is not necessarily ordinary culture.

### Knowledge May Carry Obligations

Some knowledge is protected by:

- Initiation
    
- Kinship
    
- Sacred office
    
- Burial duty
    
- Professional oath
    
- Community trust
    
- Ecological stewardship
    
- Personal privacy
    

A researcher who receives such knowledge may inherit restrictions upon its use.

Recording information does not nullify those restrictions.

### Refusal Must Be Respected

Individuals and communities may refuse:

- Interview
    
- Observation
    
- Recording
    
- Photography or illustration
    
- Scry-recording
    
- Specimen collection
    
- Entry into private sites
    
- Publication
    
- Attribution
    
- Continued participation
    

Refusal should be documented without punishment or hostile interpretation.

### Research Must Remain Answerable

Communities materially represented within a Ministerial record must possess a reasonable means to:

- Review relevant passages
    
- Challenge errors
    
- Request clarification
    
- Contest terminology
    
- Seek restricted handling
    
- Add dissent
    
- Correct attribution
    
- Report misconduct
    

A record should not close merely because it has been filed.

---

## IV. Preparing for Contact

Cultural contact begins before the researcher arrives.

### Preliminary Research

Researchers must review available information concerning:

- Local government
    
- Community structure
    
- Languages
    
- Forms of address
    
- Religious restrictions
    
- Recent conflict
    
- Known Ministry disputes
    
- Existing research
    
- Local scholars
    
- Access needs
    
- Seasonal movement
    
- Hospitality customs
    
- Burial practices
    
- Relevant regional law
    

Ignorance that could reasonably have been corrected before arrival is not an acceptable excuse for harmful conduct.

### Contact Assessment

Before initiating formal research, the lead researcher should determine:

- Whether the Ministry has lawful standing
    
- Whether community permission is required
    
- Which local authorities possess relevant legitimacy
    
- Whether those authorities represent the whole community
    
- Whether marginalized groups require separate consultation
    
- Whether the research risks worsening internal conflict
    
- Whether the inquiry can be performed without entering restricted spaces
    
- Whether an external researcher is necessary at all
    

### Local Partners

Where possible, research teams should include or consult:

- Local scholars
    
- Community historians
    
- Translators
    
- Cultural custodians
    
- Regional researchers
    
- Accessibility advisers
    
- Persons familiar with local law
    
- Members whose perspectives differ from official leadership
    

The most politically powerful local contact should not automatically become the sole interpreter of the community.

### Research Notice

For substantial projects, a research notice should explain:

- Purpose
    
- Sponsoring office
    
- Expected duration
    
- Methods
    
- Intended publication
    
- Compensation
    
- Privacy protections
    
- Complaint process
    
- Contact information
    
- Known risks
    

The notice should be provided in a form accessible to the affected community.

---

## V. Entry and First Contact

The first formal approach often determines whether later consent is genuine.

### Proper Introduction

Researchers should present:

- Name
    
- Office
    
- Rank or role
    
- Research purpose
    
- Expected length of stay
    
- Immediate requests
    
- Known sponsorship
    
- Any operational or legal authority being exercised
    

Researchers must distinguish between:

- A request
    
- A recommendation
    
- A legal order
    
- An emergency directive
    

Ministerial personnel may not present a request as though refusal would be unlawful.

### Hospitality

Many communities possess traditions requiring them to shelter, feed, or receive travelers.

Researchers must not interpret customary hospitality as permission to:

- Begin interviews
    
- Record private conversations
    
- Enter restricted spaces
    
- Inspect household objects
    
- Photograph residents
    
- Collect stories
    
- Publish what was seen
    

A guest remains a guest until another relationship is explicitly established.

### Gifts and Offerings

Gifts may be culturally appropriate.

They may also create pressure or implied obligation.

Researchers should determine whether an offered gift functions as:

- Courtesy
    
- Payment
    
- Contract
    
- Tribute
    
- Religious offering
    
- Apology
    
- Bribe
    
- Kinship gesture
    

No gift should be used to create the appearance of consent.

---

## VI. Individual Consent

### Informed Consent

Before an interview or personal observation, the participant should understand:

- Who is conducting the research
    
- What subjects may be discussed
    
- Whether notes will be taken
    
- Whether scry-recording will occur
    
- Whether their name will appear
    
- Whether quotations may be published
    
- Who may access the record
    
- Whether they may stop
    
- Whether compensation is offered
    
- Whether the information may influence policy or classification
    

Consent should be renewed if the purpose of the interaction changes materially.

### Continuing Consent

Consent is not a single permanent event.

A participant may:

- Decline a question
    
- Pause an interview
    
- Withdraw from the project
    
- Change attribution preference
    
- Request private handling
    
- Reconsider publication
    
- Clarify an earlier statement
    

Withdrawal does not always require destruction of all prior material, particularly where the information has already entered legal proceedings or public record.

The limits of withdrawal should be explained honestly before participation.

### Capacity

Researchers must take additional care where participants may have limited ability to consent because of:

- Age
    
- Illness
    
- Injury
    
- Intoxication
    
- Curse
    
- Magical influence
    
- Memory alteration
    
- Severe distress
    
- Captivity
    
- Dependency
    
- Language barrier
    
- Emergency conditions
    

A person’s vulnerability must not be treated as an opportunity for easier access.

### Power Imbalance

Consent may be compromised when the researcher controls or appears able to control:

- Medical care
    
- Food
    
- Shelter
    
- Delver licensing
    
- Relic access
    
- Legal protection
    
- Evacuation
    
- Employment
    
- Compensation
    
- Political recognition
    

Where such imbalance exists, the research role should be separated from the authority providing essential services whenever practical.

---

## VII. Community Consent and Internal Diversity

Individual consent does not always settle community-level access.

A person may consent to discuss their own experience without possessing authority to disclose:

- Sacred rites
    
- Clan knowledge
    
- Burial locations
    
- Protected routes
    
- Community-held relics
    
- Initiatory teachings
    
- Collective claims
    

### Community Authority

Researchers should determine who possesses legitimate authority concerning the matter under study.

This authority may rest with:

- Elders
    
- Councils
    
- Households
    
- Priests
    
- Guilds
    
- Custodians
    
- Elected officials
    
- Clan leaders
    
- Seasonal assemblies
    
- No single centralized body
    

The absence of a familiar hierarchy does not mean the community lacks governance.

### Multiple Voices

No community is internally uniform.

Researchers should seek perspectives across relevant differences, including:

- Age
    
- Gender
    
- Lineage
    
- Household
    
- Wealth
    
- Profession
    
- Faith
    
- Disability
    
- Region
    
- Political allegiance
    
- Migration status
    

Official leadership may describe what the community wishes to be.

Ordinary residents may describe how it is lived.

Both are evidence.

### Internal Dispute

Where customs, history, or identity are contested within a community, the record should preserve the disagreement rather than selecting one faction as the authentic voice without explanation.

The Ministry should not resolve internal cultural disputes merely for easier classification.

---

## VIII. Observation

### Public Observation

Public behavior may generally be observed, but recording and publication may still require caution.

A market is public.

An individual’s grief within that market is not automatically public property.

A procession may be visible.

Its sacred meaning may remain restricted.

### Participant Observation

A researcher who joins daily labor, ceremony, travel, or communal life must disclose the research purpose unless doing so would create a narrow and formally approved exception.

Participation should not be used to gain access that would otherwise be refused.

### Private Spaces

Entry into homes, shrines, workshops, burial grounds, archives, nesting areas, emergence chambers, medical spaces, or restricted ecological sites requires appropriate permission.

Permission to enter does not automatically include permission to record.

### Emergencies

During emergencies, observation necessary for rescue, diagnosis, containment, or public safety may occur without ordinary research consent.

Information gathered under emergency authority must not later be repurposed for unrelated publication without appropriate review.

---

## IX. Interviews and Testimony

### Interview Method

Researchers should:

- Ask clear questions
    
- Avoid unnecessary leading language
    
- Distinguish memory from inference
    
- Allow silence
    
- Respect refusal
    
- Record uncertainty
    
- Preserve the participant’s phrasing where practical
    
- Avoid correcting cultural interpretation during testimony
    
- Clarify whether statements are personal, communal, or official
    

### Quotation

Direct quotations should be verified with the speaker where feasible, especially when:

- The wording is politically sensitive
    
- Translation is difficult
    
- The statement concerns sacred practice
    
- The quote may affect public reputation
    
- The speaker may face retaliation
    

### Anonymous Testimony

Anonymity may be appropriate where identification would create:

- Political retaliation
    
- Family conflict
    
- Religious punishment
    
- Commercial harm
    
- Criminal danger
    
- Social stigma
    

Researchers must explain that perfect anonymity may be impossible where context itself reveals identity.

### Confession and Self-Incrimination

Researchers are not automatically protected as legal confessors.

If testimony concerns serious danger or crime, the researcher should not promise secrecy beyond what law and ethics allow.

### Trauma

Interviewing survivors of catastrophe, violence, Labyrinth exposure, displacement, or loss requires care.

Repeated testimony can become another form of harm.

Researchers should avoid compelling survivors to retell events merely because separate Ministry offices failed to share lawful records.

---

## X. Translation and Interpretation

Translation is interpretation.

The Ministry therefore treats translators as research partners rather than invisible instruments.

### Translator Qualifications

A translator should possess:

- Language competence
    
- Cultural competence
    
- Understanding of research purpose
    
- Familiarity with relevant terminology
    
- Ability to identify ambiguity
    
- Freedom to state when no reliable translation exists
    

### Translator Disclosure

Formal records should identify translators unless safety or privacy requires otherwise.

Where a translator has a meaningful political, familial, religious, or commercial interest in the subject, that relationship should be disclosed.

### Difficult Terms

Researchers must not force exact equivalents where none exist.

An untranslated term may be preferable when the alternative would erase:

- Social rank
    
- Ritual significance
    
- Gender
    
- Kinship
    
- Sacred meaning
    
- Legal nuance
    
- Metaphysical distinction
    

### Multiple Translations

Where interpretations differ materially, the record should preserve the alternatives.

### Children and Dependents as Translators

Children, servants, captives, or dependent persons should not be used as primary translators for sensitive matters merely because they are available.

Such arrangements may expose them to danger, inappropriate knowledge, or divided loyalty.

---

## XI. Scry-Recording, Illustration, and Image Capture

Visual preservation can be more intrusive than written notes.

### Prior Notice

Participants should be told:

- What is being recorded
    
- When recording begins
    
- Who controls the record
    
- Where it will be stored
    
- Who may view it
    
- Whether it may be broadcast
    
- Whether copies may exist
    

### Hidden Recording

Hidden scry-recording is prohibited for ordinary cultural research.

Exceptions require formal legal and ethical authorization for matters involving substantial public danger, criminal investigation, or comparable necessity.

### Ceremonies

Permission to attend a ceremony does not imply permission to record it.

Some communities permit description but prohibit image capture.

Others permit recording but prohibit public reproduction.

### Portraits

A person’s likeness should not be published without consent where the image may expose identity, location, status, disability, religious role, or political affiliation.

### Illustrative Reconstruction

Artists must distinguish between:

- Direct depiction
    
- Memory-based depiction
    
- Composite representation
    
- Symbolic illustration
    
- Speculative reconstruction
    

A reconstructed image must not be presented as documentary evidence.

---

## XII. Sacred, Funerary, and Restricted Knowledge

Some knowledge is protected not because it is dangerous in a technical sense, but because public release would violate the relationships that give it meaning.

### Sacred Knowledge

Sacred information may include:

- Divine names
    
- Initiatory teachings
    
- Ritual sequence
    
- Sacred routes
    
- Prayer forms
    
- Relic locations
    
- Temple architecture
    
- Ceremonial identities
    
- Restricted songs
    
- Transformative practices
    

Researchers should ask:

- Who may know this?
    
- Who may speak it?
    
- Who may record it?
    
- Who may hear it?
    
- When may it be repeated?
    
- Does translation itself violate the restriction?
    

### Funerary Knowledge

Burial sites, remains, death names, mourning rites, and ancestral materials require particular caution.

The dead are not unowned merely because they cannot refuse.

Research involving remains should consider:

- Descendant communities
    
- Religious custodians
    
- Local law
    
- Reburial
    
- Display
    
- Sampling
    
- Return
    
- Naming
    
- Publication
    

### Protective Secrecy

Some communities conceal locations or practices to protect:

- Rare species
    
- Emergence grounds
    
- Refuge routes
    
- Sacred springs
    
- Vulnerable settlements
    
- Relics
    
- Medicinal resources
    
- Children
    
- Survivors
    

Publication should not convert community knowledge into a guide for exploitation.

---

## XIII. Cultural Materials and Artifacts

Objects held by living communities are not ordinary antiquities merely because they are old.

They may remain:

- Sacred
    
- Functional
    
- Inherited
    
- Politically significant
    
- Legally owned
    
- Ceremonially alive
    

### Inspection

Researchers should request permission before:

- Handling
    
- Opening
    
- Measuring
    
- Sampling
    
- Disassembling
    
- Translating
    
- Scrying
    
- Copying
    
- Testing
    

### Removal

No cultural material should be removed without documented authority.

Removal may require:

- Owner consent
    
- Community consent
    
- Custodial approval
    
- Regional legal authorization
    
- Conservation necessity
    
- Emergency justification
    

### Loans

Loan agreements should specify:

- Duration
    
- Custody
    
- Storage
    
- Testing
    
- Publication
    
- Reproduction
    
- Damage
    
- Return
    
- Compensation
    

### Improperly Acquired Materials

Where the Ministry possesses materials obtained through coercion, deception, looting, or invalid authority, the Office of Ethics, Review, and Record Integrity should review:

- Return
    
- Restitution
    
- Shared custody
    
- Restricted access
    
- Public acknowledgment
    
- Continued preservation needs
    

---

## XIV. Compensation and Exchange

Research often relies upon local labor.

That labor should not be treated as free merely because it takes the form of knowledge.

Compensation may be appropriate for:

- Translation
    
- Guiding
    
- Interview time
    
- Craft demonstration
    
- Community review
    
- Site access
    
- Archival assistance
    
- Ecological expertise
    
- Ritual consultation
    
- Lodging
    
- Transport
    

### Appropriate Compensation

Compensation should be:

- Agreed in advance
    
- Proportionate
    
- Culturally appropriate
    
- Free from coercive conditions
    
- Recorded
    
- Distinct from gifts where possible
    

### Unequal Payment

Researchers should avoid compensation practices that create hostility, empower one faction, or distort testimony.

The person most willing to speak is not always the person with the most legitimate knowledge.

### Community Benefit

For substantial projects, the Ministry should consider benefits beyond individual payment, including:

- Local archive copies
    
- Preservation grants
    
- Training
    
- Infrastructure support
    
- Translation resources
    
- Restoration assistance
    
- Community-controlled research
    
- Public education materials
    
- Return of collected knowledge in accessible form
    

Research should not leave a community poorer in knowledge than before the researchers arrived.

---

## XV. Naming, Classification, and Self-Identification

Names are not minor filing choices.

They influence recognition, law, and public treatment.

### Preferred Names

Researchers should record:

- Community self-name
    
- Local variants
    
- External names
    
- Historical names
    
- Derogatory or obsolete names where relevant
    
- Ministerial filing term
    

The official filing term should not erase the preferred self-name.

### Classification

Where the Ministry places a people within a broader classification, the entry should explain:

- Why the category is used
    
- What practical purpose it serves
    
- Its limits
    
- Whether the community accepts it
    
- Which identities it may obscure
    

### Renaming

A community may petition for revised terminology.

The Ministry may preserve older names in historical records while changing modern usage.

### Personal Identity

Researchers must not assume that:

- Lineage determines culture
    
- Homeland determines citizenship
    
- Religion determines politics
    
- Biology determines profession
    
- Household determines personal belief
    
- Ministry classification overrides self-identification
    

---

## XVI. Publication Review

Before publication, cultural records should be reviewed for:

- Accuracy
    
- Scope
    
- Translation
    
- Consent
    
- Attribution
    
- Privacy
    
- Sacred restriction
    
- Political harm
    
- Exploitation risk
    
- Accessibility
    
- Community objection
    
- Internal diversity
    
- Stereotyping
    
- Overgeneralization
    

### Community Review

Affected communities should receive a meaningful opportunity to review materially descriptive sections where practical.

The Ministry is not required to surrender editorial independence.

It is required to take correction seriously.

### Disagreement

Where the Ministry and community disagree, the record may include:

- Ministry interpretation
    
- Community interpretation
    
- Supporting evidence
    
- Dissent notice
    
- Pending review
    

The Ministry must not conceal disagreement merely to produce a cleaner entry.

### Urgent Publication

During immediate danger, public-safety information may be released before ordinary cultural review.

Such publication should be narrowly limited to what the emergency requires.

---

## XVII. Research During Crisis and Displacement

Communities experiencing disaster, war, famine, epidemic, breach, or displacement may possess urgent knowledge.

They are also especially vulnerable to exploitation.

### Priority of Aid

Essential aid must not depend upon research participation.

Food, shelter, healing, evacuation, and legal protection may not be withheld because a person or community refuses interview or observation.

### Crisis Testimony

Testimony gathered during crisis should be treated as:

- Potentially incomplete
    
- Emotionally immediate
    
- Vulnerable to confusion
    
- Worth preserving
    
- In need of later verification
    

### Displaced Communities

Researchers should not treat displacement as evidence that cultural authority has vanished.

A people does not lose ownership of its history because it has lost possession of its land.

### Vanishing Knowledge

Urgency may justify rapid preservation efforts where a language, practice, elder tradition, or archive faces imminent loss.

Urgency does not remove the need for consent.

It increases the responsibility to proceed carefully.

---

## XVIII. Delvers as Cultural Intermediaries

Delvers often become the first outsiders to contact isolated communities, hidden settlements, or surviving populations within Labyrinth-associated sites.

They may therefore become accidental ethnographers.

### Delver Responsibilities

Licensed delvers acting beneath Ministerial authority should:

- Identify themselves
    
- Avoid promising recognition or rescue beyond their authority
    
- Record names carefully
    
- Respect restricted spaces
    
- Avoid collecting cultural objects as trophies
    
- Distinguish hostility from fear
    
- Report requests for protected contact
    
- Preserve testimony without embellishment
    

### First Contact

Where a previously undocumented sapient people is encountered, delvers should prioritize:

1. Immediate safety
    
2. Non-hostile communication
    
3. Avoidance of contamination
    
4. Respect for boundaries
    
5. Withdrawal where appropriate
    
6. Notification of qualified contact personnel
    

Discovery does not create ownership.

### Public Broadcast

Delvers must not broadcast the location, image, language, or vulnerability of an undocumented community merely for public fame, entertainment, or wagering.

---

## XIX. Misconduct

Violations of cultural contact standards may include:

- Coercive interview
    
- False consent
    
- Hidden recording
    
- Theft of cultural material
    
- Sacred trespass
    
- Deliberate mistranslation
    
- Misrepresentation of research purpose
    
- Publishing protected knowledge
    
- Retaliation against refusal
    
- Withholding aid for participation
    
- Falsifying consultation
    
- Treating one spokesperson as universal authority
    
- Exploiting crisis
    
- Commercial sale of community knowledge
    
- Erasing internal dissent
    
- Describing stereotype as fact
    

### Reporting Misconduct

Complaints may be submitted through:

- Community Petition Chamber
    
- Regional Ministerial Office
    
- Research Ethics Council
    
- Archive Integrity Inspectorate
    
- Office of Public Account and Civic Relations
    
- Local charter authority
    

### Remedies

Possible remedies include:

- Correction
    
- Restricted access
    
- Removal of identifying information
    
- Public apology
    
- Compensation
    
- Return of materials
    
- Suspension of research seal
    
- Disciplinary action
    
- Community-led replacement study
    
- Legal referral
    
- Retraction for misconduct
    

---

## XX. Field Contact Checklist

Before beginning cultural research, personnel should be able to answer:

### Authority

- Who authorized the research?
    
- Does the Ministry possess lawful standing?
    
- Which local authorities must be consulted?
    

### Purpose

- What are we trying to learn?
    
- Why is this knowledge needed?
    
- Could the work be completed through existing community scholarship?
    

### Consent

- Who is participating?
    
- Do they understand how the information may be used?
    
- Can they refuse safely?
    
- Can they withdraw?
    

### Representation

- Whose voices are included?
    
- Whose voices are absent?
    
- Does leadership speak for everyone concerned?
    

### Risk

- Could publication expose sacred knowledge, vulnerable locations, or marginalized people?
    
- Could the record affect land, law, trade, or political recognition?
    
- Could the Ministry’s presence worsen local conflict?
    

### Return

- How will knowledge, compensation, credit, and copies be returned to the community?
    
- Who may challenge the final record?
    

If these questions cannot be answered, the contact plan is incomplete.

---

## XXI. Summary Standard

Cultural contact beneath Ministerial seal must remain guided by five obligations:

1. **Declare the purpose of inquiry.**
    
2. **Seek meaningful consent.**
    
3. **Preserve context and internal diversity.**
    
4. **Protect knowledge whose disclosure would cause harm.**
    
5. **Remain answerable to the people represented.**
    

A researcher may leave with more information than they arrived with.

They should not leave behind less trust, dignity, safety, or ownership than they found.

---

> [!note] Public Contact Notice  
> Ministry researchers, contractors, field scribes, translators, and licensed delvers do not possess automatic authority to interview, record, photograph, enter private sites, remove materials, or publish protected cultural knowledge.
> 
> Participation in Ministerial research is ordinarily voluntary.
> 
> Receipt of aid, recognition, licensing, evacuation, or public service does not create an obligation to participate.
> 
> Individuals and communities may request identification, research purpose, intended use, access designation, compensation terms, and the process for complaint or correction.
> 
> Cultural consultation does not permit political authorities to erase criticism, but neither does Ministerial independence permit researchers to disregard the people whose lives they describe.
