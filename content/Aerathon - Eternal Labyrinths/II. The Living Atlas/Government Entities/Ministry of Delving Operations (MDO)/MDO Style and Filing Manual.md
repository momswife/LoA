> — Archivist Yelna Stonevoice, _Instructions to Junior Keepers_

# MDO Style and Filing Manual

**Issuing Authority:** Office of Ministerial Inquiry and Public Record  
**Administering Body:** Living Atlas Directorate  
**Archival Oversight:** Office of Ethics, Review, and Record Integrity  
**Document Classification:** Editorial, Archival, and Filing Standard  
**Application:** All Lorevault entries, public Ministerial records, research summaries, advisories, and institutional filings  
**Current Standard:** Seventh Editorial Standard  
**Date of Revised Filing:** 7th Cycle, 3388 A.D.

---

## I. Purpose of This Manual

The **MDO Style and Filing Manual** establishes the standard form, language, structure, and archival practices used throughout the Lorevault of Aerathon.

Its purpose is not to make every record sound identical.

A field report written during a breach should not resemble a cultural overview compiled across decades. A legal opinion should not read like a bestiary entry. A reconstruction of an ancient civilization should preserve uncertainty differently from a current regional survey.

The purpose of standardization is instead to ensure that every record makes several matters clear:

- What kind of document it is
    
- Which office issued it
    
- What evidence supports it
    
- How certain its claims are
    
- Whose perspective shaped it
    
- Whether the record remains current
    
- Which parts are disputed
    
- What restrictions apply
    
- Where readers may find related material
    
- How the filing may be challenged or corrected
    

The Ministry does not require perfect uniformity.

It requires disciplined clarity.

A well-filed document should allow a reader unfamiliar with its subject to distinguish evidence from interpretation without possessing personal knowledge of the compiler.

---

## II. Voice of the Ministry

Ministerial records should sound:

- Observant
    
- Precise
    
- Measured
    
- Informed
    
- Respectful
    
- Willing to acknowledge uncertainty
    
- Conscious of institutional power
    
- Accessible to the intended reader
    

They should not sound:

- Omniscient
    
- Triumphal
    
- Condescending
    
- Needlessly theatrical
    
- Mechanically detached
    
- Certain beyond the evidence
    
- Dismissive of local knowledge
    
- Written solely to glorify the Ministry
    

### Institutional Authority

The MDO writes with authority because it has gathered evidence, maintained records, and accepted responsibility for publication.

It should not write as though authority itself proves the conclusion.

Preferred:

> Current Ministerial surveys identify three permanently inhabited settlements along the eastern route.

Avoid:

> There are only three settlements along the eastern route.

The first statement explains the scope of knowledge.

The second implies that anything absent from Ministerial records does not exist.

### Institutional Humility

Responsible phrases include:

- “Current evidence suggests…”
    
- “The Ministry presently recognizes…”
    
- “Available records indicate…”
    
- “No consensus has been established…”
    
- “The surviving account is incomplete…”
    
- “Local scholars dispute…”
    
- “This classification remains provisional…”
    
- “Further revision is expected…”
    

Such language should be used where warranted.

It should not be added mechanically to every sentence.

A record may state established facts directly.

### Plain Language

Technical terms are appropriate where precision requires them.

Unnecessary complexity is not.

Preferred:

> The relic produces heat when exposed to concentrated spellwork.

Avoid:

> The artifact demonstrates an escalatory thaumothermal response under conditions of condensed arcane application.

The second may be appropriate in a specialist technical report.

It is poor public reference language.

---

## III. The Ministry Is Not an Omniscient Narrator

Lorevault entries represent institutional understanding.

They do not reveal an absolute authorial truth unavailable to the people of Aerathon.

A filing may contain:

- Incomplete evidence
    
- Competing traditions
    
- Scholarly disagreement
    
- Regional bias
    
- Deliberate concealment
    
- Translation uncertainty
    
- Incorrect former assumptions
    
- Information the Ministry has not yet discovered
    

Writers must avoid revealing hidden truths merely because those truths are known outside the document.

If the Ministry does not know that a ruler is secretly possessed, the public government entry should not state it as fact.

That information belongs in:

- A restricted dossier
    
- A field report
    
- A testimony record
    
- A private campaign note
    
- A later revision after discovery
    

This separation preserves the difference between:

- What is true in the setting
    
- What a witness believes
    
- What the MDO suspects
    
- What the public record states
    

---

## IV. Core Questions for Every Record

Before filing, the compiler should be able to answer:

1. **What is this document trying to explain?**
    
2. **Who is expected to read it?**
    
3. **What evidence supports it?**
    
4. **What remains uncertain or disputed?**
    
5. **Who was consulted?**
    
6. **Who may be affected by its publication?**
    
7. **What related documents should be linked?**
    
8. **What filing status should it carry?**
    

If the document cannot answer these questions, its scope is likely unclear.

---

## V. Standard Filing Elements

Formal records should use the following filing elements where applicable.

### Opening Epigraph

An epigraph may establish tone, historical memory, or institutional perspective.

It should be:

- Brief
    
- Relevant
    
- Attributed
    
- Believable as an in-world statement
    

Epigraphs should not summarize the entire document.

They should create a lens through which it may be read.

Example:

> _“Every border looks permanent from the side that drew it.”_  
> — Keeper Solenne Raithe

### Title

Titles should identify the subject directly.

Preferred:

- Ministry of Delving Operations
    
- The Four Cardinal Winds
    
- Raptorid
    
- Malarthain
    
- Relic Handling and Regulation
    

Avoid titles that are poetic but obscure the filing subject unless paired with a clear subtitle.

### Filing Header

A full header may include:

```text
Issuing Authority:
Administering Body:
Filed Division:
Document Classification:
Authenticity Grade:
Research Status:
Access Designation:
Revision State:
Primary Compiler:
Consulting Offices:
Community Consultation:
Date of Filing:
Date of Last Revision:
Supersedes:
```

Not every field is required for every record.

The following are generally expected:

- Document Classification
    
- Authenticity Grade
    
- Research Status
    
- Access Designation
    
- Revision State
    
- Date of Filing
    

### Overview

The first substantive section should explain:

- What the subject is
    
- Why it matters
    
- The scope of the entry
    
- Any major misunderstanding the reader should avoid
    

An overview should not require prior specialist knowledge.

### Main Body

The main body should progress from broad understanding toward greater detail.

### Related Records

Related links should appear near the end unless a link is essential to an earlier section.

### Public or Operational Notice

A callout may clarify rights, hazards, restrictions, or common misunderstandings.

### Authentication Footer

Formal records should conclude with:

- Filing authority
    
- Document class
    
- Authenticity grade
    
- Research status
    
- Access designation
    
- Revision state
    
- Compiler
    
- Relevant reviewers
    
- Filing date
    
- Supersession notice where applicable
    

---

## VI. Recommended Section Order

The exact structure depends upon document class.

A common public-reference structure is:

1. Epigraph
    
2. Title and filing header
    
3. Overview
    
4. Definition or classification
    
5. Historical development
    
6. Modern form
    
7. Regional or internal variation
    
8. Institutions, customs, or practical systems
    
9. Common misconceptions
    
10. Present significance
    
11. Related records
    
12. Public notice
    
13. Filing footer
    

This order should be adapted rather than followed blindly.

A bestiary record may place danger and identification before history.

A field advisory may begin with an immediate instruction.

A legal record may begin with scope and authority.

---

## VII. Headings and Hierarchy

Use a clear heading hierarchy.

```text
# Document Title

## I. Major Section

### Subsection

#### Limited Detail Heading
```

Roman numerals are preferred for major sections in formal long-form records.

They may be omitted from:

- Short public notices
    
- Monthly Ledger entries
    
- Character-focused profiles
    
- Brief field advisories
    
- Navigation pages
    

### Heading Length

Headings should be descriptive and concise.

Preferred:

> ## VII. Household Wind and Inheritance

Avoid:

> ## VII. Several Additional Matters Concerning the Ways in Which Winds Are Carried Through Families

### One Subject Per Section

A section should not combine unrelated topics merely to reduce heading count.

---

## VIII. Paragraph Style

Ministerial prose should favor:

- Clear complete sentences
    
- Moderate paragraph length
    
- Controlled imagery
    
- Specific examples
    
- Direct statements followed by nuance
    

Short isolated lines may be used for emphasis.

Example:

> The Ministry does not create a people by recognizing them.

Such lines should remain occasional.

Overuse makes the document feel theatrical rather than archival.

### Repetition

Important principles may be repeated where different sections require them.

Exact phrases should not recur so often that they become slogans unless they are formal doctrine.

### Rhetorical Contrast

The following construction is useful but should be controlled:

> Custody does not equal ownership.

> Authenticity does not equal accuracy.

> Recognition does not create legitimacy.

These statements communicate institutional distinctions efficiently.

Too many in one document will make the prose feel formulaic.

---

## IX. Lists and Tables

### Lists

Use lists when the reader benefits from scanning:

- Mandates
    
- Responsibilities
    
- Risks
    
- Evidence classes
    
- Common misconceptions
    
- Required filing fields
    

Do not turn every paragraph into a list.

### Tables

Tables are useful for:

- Regional comparison
    
- Ranks
    
- Classifications
    
- Status systems
    
- Office responsibilities
    
- Lineage concentrations
    
- Document examples
    

Tables should not contain dense prose that would read better as sections.

### Definitions Before Tables

Introduce the meaning of a table before presenting it.

A table should support understanding, not replace explanation.

---

## X. Internal Links

Obsidian links should point readers toward deeper material without duplicating it.

### Preferred Practice

Mention the subject naturally, then link it.

> The modern Wind system descends from the [[The Sky-Reeve Clans|Sky-Reeve Clans]].

### Link Display Names

Use display names where the file path or filename differs from the visible term.

```text
[[Full/Vault/Path/File Name|Visible Name]]
```

### Avoid Link Saturation

Do not link every repeated occurrence of the same term.

Usually link the first meaningful mention within a section or document.

### Missing Documents

Links to planned but unwritten records may be used intentionally.

They should represent genuine future documents rather than placeholders created without a plan.

### Section Links

Use section links where a larger document contains the relevant material.

```text
[[Birth, Bond, & Burial#Turning the Wing|Turning the Wing]]
```

---

## XI. Terminology and Names

### Use Established Canonical Terms

Once a formal term is established, use it consistently.

Examples include:

- Avianfolk
    
- Lineage
    
- Sublineage
    
- Homeland
    
- Household Wind
    
- Ministry of Delving Operations
    
- Living Atlas
    
- Labyrinth
    

Avoid silently replacing established terms with near-synonyms.

### Local and Standard Names

Where local and Ministerial names differ, record both.

Example:

> The region is filed by the Ministry as Arneria, though several inherited records preserve the older spelling _Arneia_.

### Capitalization

Capitalize:

- Formal institutions
    
- Official offices
    
- Named historical eras
    
- Recognized cultural systems
    
- Formal document classes
    
- Specific Labyrinths
    
- Named Winds
    

Do not capitalize ordinary descriptors merely to make them sound important.

### Abbreviations

Write the full name on first use.

> Ministry of Delving Operations, commonly abbreviated as MDO

Use the abbreviation afterward where clear.

---

## XII. Peoples and Cultures

Documents concerning living peoples require particular care.

### Preferred Framing

Preferred:

> North Gale households traditionally teach that preparedness is an obligation to those who may depend upon the shelter later.

Avoid:

> North Gale Avianfolk are stern and prepared.

The first describes an inherited teaching.

The second assigns personality to an entire culture.

### Distinguish Identity Layers

Where relevant, separate:

- Folk classification
    
- Lineage
    
- Sublineage
    
- Homeland
    
- Citizenship
    
- Household
    
- Cultural affiliation
    
- Faith
    
- Profession
    

### Avoid Biological Determinism

Physical traits should not be used to infer:

- Morality
    
- Intelligence
    
- Profession
    
- Political loyalty
    
- Temperament
    
- Cultural legitimacy
    

### Regional Variation

Major cultural claims should identify whether they apply to:

- A whole people
    
- A region
    
- A historical period
    
- A household tradition
    
- A religious community
    
- A profession
    
- A stereotype
    

### Consultation Language

Appropriate phrases include:

- “Community reviewers emphasized…”
    
- “Regional practice varies…”
    
- “Several households reject this term…”
    
- “This description reflects Vinyoti practice and should not be treated as universal…”
    

---

## XIII. Geography and Government

### Geography

Distinguish physical geography from political ownership.

Preferred:

> The river passes through territory claimed by both councils.

Avoid:

> The river belongs to the northern council.

unless the legal claim is settled and relevant.

### Borders

Borders may be:

- Recognized
    
- Disputed
    
- Seasonal
    
- Historical
    
- Ritual
    
- Enforced
    
- Administrative
    
- Poorly surveyed
    

State which kind is being described.

### Governments

Government entries should explain:

- Structure
    
- Source of legitimacy
    
- Jurisdiction
    
- Leadership
    
- Public institutions
    
- Internal tensions
    
- Relationship with residents
    
- Current limitations
    

Avoid reducing a region to its ruler.

### Neutrality

Ministerial neutrality does not require pretending all claims are equally supported.

The record may state that one claim possesses stronger evidence while preserving dissent.

---

## XIV. Historical Records

Historical entries should distinguish:

- Contemporary sources
    
- Later copies
    
- Oral traditions
    
- Archaeological evidence
    
- Modern reconstruction
    
- Legendary material
    

### Dates

Use precise dates where established.

Use ranges where certainty is limited.

Preferred:

> The siege occurred between 3370 and 3372 A.D., though most regional records favor 3371.

Avoid inventing a precise date merely for neatness.

### Legends

Legends may be included as cultural evidence.

Preferred:

> Later songs claim the queen crossed the burning river alone. No contemporary record confirms this, though the story remains central to local identity.

### Anachronism

Do not apply modern institutions or terminology to periods before they existed without explanation.

---

## XV. Labyrinth and Anomaly Records

Labyrinth records should be especially careful about certainty.

### Required Distinctions

Distinguish:

- Confirmed route
    
- Observed route
    
- Former route
    
- Suspected route
    
- One-way route
    
- Mimicked route
    

### Time Sensitivity

State when observations were made.

A correct map may become dangerous if presented without date.

### Hostile Interpretation

Where the Labyrinth may react to observation, language, memory, or expectation, state this prominently.

### Survivor Testimony

Do not dismiss contradictory survivor accounts automatically.

Contradiction may indicate:

- Memory alteration
    
- Temporal separation
    
- Different environmental states
    
- Mimicry
    
- Trauma
    
- Ordinary error
    

---

## XVI. Bestiary Records

Bestiary entries should separate:

- Identification
    
- Habitat
    
- Behavior
    
- Ecology
    
- Threat
    
- Cultural significance
    
- Delver guidance
    

### Threat Is Not Morality

A dangerous creature is not necessarily malicious.

A sapient hostile group should not be filed as ordinary fauna merely because conflict occurred.

### Sapience Uncertainty

Where sapience is plausible, use cautious language and refer the matter for cultural-contact review.

### Common Names

Record local names where they help identify regional experience.

---

## XVII. Legal and Institutional Records

Legal documents should prioritize clarity over atmosphere.

They should identify:

- Issuing authority
    
- Legal basis
    
- Scope
    
- Rights
    
- Limits
    
- Duration
    
- Review process
    
- Appeal process
    

### Avoid Absolute Claims

Preferred:

> The Ministry ordinarily possesses standing in matters involving active relic contamination.

Avoid:

> The Ministry controls all relics.

### Distinguish Law from Practice

A legal right may not be enforced consistently.

An illegal custom may remain widespread.

Record both where relevant.

---

## XVIII. Field Reports and Incident Records

Field reports should preserve immediacy without becoming dramatic retellings.

### Required Separation

Use clear markers for:

- Observation
    
- Testimony
    
- Inference
    
- Unknown
    
- Later amendment
    

### Emotional Response

Emotional response may be recorded when it affects:

- Perception
    
- Memory
    
- Decision-making
    
- Magical phenomena
    
- Team conduct
    

It should not replace factual description.

### Casualties

Use respectful, direct language.

Avoid euphemism that conceals what occurred.

Preferred:

> Three delvers were killed during the eastern collapse.

Avoid:

> Three assets were lost.

---

## XIX. Monthly Ledger Style

Monthly Ledger entries are provisional and time-sensitive.

They should lead with:

- What happened
    
- Where
    
- When
    
- What is confirmed
    
- What remains uncertain
    
- What readers should do
    

### Rumor Labels

Use distinct labels:

- Verified
    
- Credible but unconfirmed
    
- Official claim
    
- Circulating rumor
    
- Suspected misinformation
    

### Updates

When an entry changes, add a visible update rather than silently rewriting the original event.

---

## XX. Uncertainty Markers

Where helpful, use visible markers near the affected material.

Examples:

> **Research Status: Disputed**

> **Translation Provisional**

> **Community Objection Recorded**

> **Operational Data Expired**

> **Location Withheld for Protection**

> **Speculative Reconstruction**

These markers should be used for material differences, not every minor caveat.

---

## XXI. Quotations

### Direct Quotations

Use direct quotation where:

- Exact wording matters
    
- The speaker’s voice adds cultural meaning
    
- The phrase became historically influential
    
- The quotation demonstrates disagreement
    

### Attribution

Identify:

- Speaker
    
- Role
    
- Source or context where relevant
    

### Translation

Translated quotations should indicate if wording is approximate or disputed.

### Invented Institutional Quotations

Epigraphs and quotations must sound like something an actual person in Aerathon might say.

Avoid lines that exist only to explain lore unnaturally.

---

## XXII. Callouts and Notices

Obsidian callouts may be used for practical interpretation.

### Public Notice

```text
> [!note] Public Registry Notice
```

Used for:

- Rights
    
- Definitions
    
- Common misunderstandings
    
- Filing interpretation
    

### Warning

```text
> [!warning] Operational Warning
```

Used for:

- Immediate danger
    
- Obsolete maps
    
- Relic hazards
    
- Prohibited actions
    

### Dispute

```text
> [!question] Scholarly Dispute
```

Used where competing interpretations materially affect the record.

### Restricted Knowledge

```text
> [!caution] Restricted Cultural Material
```

Used where publication or use is limited.

Callouts should contain information that benefits from separation.

They should not become decorative summaries of every section.

---

## XXIII. Common Misconceptions Sections

A misconceptions section is useful when readers are likely to arrive with harmful or persistent assumptions.

Each misconception should use a clear claim and correction.

Example:

### “All Avianfolk can fly.”

False.

Some fly, some glide, and some possess no current aerial capability.

Misconceptions sections should not repeat the entire article.

---

A standard footer may use the following form:

```text
**Document Class:** [Class]  
**Authenticity Grade:** [Grade]  
**Research Status:** [Status]  
**Access Designation:** [Access]  
**Revision State:** [State]  
**Primary Compiler:** [Name]

**Community Consultation:** [Where applicable]

```

Not every footer requires every line.

The footer should identify real responsibility rather than merely decorate the page.

## XXV. Compiler and Reviewer Credits

### Primary Compiler

The person principally responsible for assembling the record.

### Consulting Offices

Offices that supplied specialist review.

### Community Consultation

Living communities or appointed reviewers who materially contributed.

### Anonymous Review

Where naming a reviewer creates danger, use a protected designation.

Example:

> **Community Consultation:** Three protected Bat’yan village reviewers

### Historical Compilers

A deceased or earlier compiler may remain credited through a heritage seal.

A modern reviser should also be named where the entry changed materially.

## XXVII. Document Length

A document should be long enough to fulfill its purpose and no longer.

Suggested ranges are guidance only.

|Document Type|Typical Length|
|---|--:|
|Brief public notice|150–500 words|
|Monthly Ledger entry|300–1,000 words|
|Individual lineage reference|1,500–3,000 words|
|Cultural or regional overview|2,500–4,500 words|
|Major institutional record|3,000–6,000 words|
|Comprehensive historical study|4,000–8,000 words|
|Technical manual|As required|

An overview should not contain every related custom, law, and historical event.

Create linked documents when one section begins serving as a complete article of its own.

---

## XXVIII. When to Split a Document

A section should usually become a separate record when:

- It exceeds the scope promised by the title
    
- It develops its own history, customs, and internal structure
    
- It will be linked from several other pages
    
- Changes to it would require updating many documents
    
- It interrupts the reader’s understanding of the main subject
    
- It is useful to readers who do not need the parent article
    

Example:

The Avianfolk Overview should summarize the Four Cardinal Winds.

The full territorial cultures, household traditions, and cross-Wind relationships belong in **The Four Cardinal Winds**.

---

## XXIX. Duplication and Cross-Referencing

Repeat enough information for the current page to remain understandable.

Do not reproduce entire sections from another document.

Preferred:

> Cross-Wind marriages may involve a rite known as Turning the Wing. See [[Birth, Bond, & Burial#Turning the Wing|Turning the Wing]].

Avoid copying the full ceremony into every cultural entry.

### Canonical Source

Where several pages discuss one subject, identify one as the canonical full treatment.

Other pages should summarize and link to it.

---

## XXX. Revision Practice

When changing a record:

- Preserve established canon unless deliberately revising it
    
- Check linked records for contradiction
    
- Update terminology consistently
    
- Record substantive changes
    
- Mark superseded interpretations
    
- Avoid silently removing meaningful disputes
    
- Retain former names where historically relevant
    

### Small Revisions

Spelling, broken links, and minor clarification do not require a full revision notice.

### Substantive Revisions

Changes to history, identity, political status, classification, geography, or institutional structure should be acknowledged.

---

## XXXI. Prohibited Editorial Practices

Ministerial writers must not:

- Present stereotype as fact
    
- Hide uncertainty to sound authoritative
    
- Invent evidence within an official record without establishing it as canon
    
- Use an official seal to silence dispute
    
- Reveal restricted campaign truth in a public record
    
- Copy another document so extensively that neither has a clear purpose
    
- Use outdated terminology without context
    
- Treat the Ministry as morally infallible
    
- Reduce a culture to personality traits
    
- Describe ordinary people only through rulers, wars, and delvers
    
- Use poetic language where practical clarity is required
    
- Strip all voice from a record in the name of objectivity
    

---

## XXXII. Final Editorial Review

Before filing, review the document for:

### Scope

- Does the title match the contents?
    
- Is this an overview or a complete specialist record?
    
- Should any section become its own page?
    

### Evidence

- Which claims are verified?
    
- Which are inferred?
    
- Which are disputed?
    
- Is uncertainty visible?
    

### Perspective

- Whose view dominates?
    
- Who was consulted?
    
- Is the Ministry’s own bias acknowledged where relevant?
    

### Consistency

- Are canonical names used?
    
- Do dates align?
    
- Do links point to the right files?
    
- Does the document contradict existing lore unintentionally?
    

### Accessibility

- Can a new reader understand the overview?
    
- Are technical terms explained?
    
- Are tables and lists actually useful?
    
- Is the record longer than its purpose requires?
    

### Filing

- Is the document class correct?
    
- Is the authenticity grade correct?
    
- Is the research status correct?
    
- Is the revision state correct?
    
- Are responsible offices named?
    

---

## XXXIII. Summary Doctrine

Every formal Lorevault record should strive to do five things:

1. **Name its purpose clearly.**
    
2. **Separate evidence from interpretation.**
    
3. **Preserve uncertainty and disagreement honestly.**
    
4. **Respect the people and places it describes.**
    
5. **Make responsibility for the record visible.**
    

The Ministry’s voice should be authoritative enough to guide and humble enough to be corrected.

A record should not ask the reader to trust it because it bears a seal.

It should show the reader why that trust may presently be deserved.
