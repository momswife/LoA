# The Living Atlas — Research Charter

**Issuing Authority:** Ministry of Delving Operations  
**Administering Body:** Office of Ministerial Inquiry and Public Record  
**Document Classification:** Institutional Charter  
**Application:** All Living Atlas research, surveys, classifications, and public reference records  
**Current Revision:** Seventh Charter Revision  
**Date of Filing:** 3388 A.D.

---

## I. Purpose of the Charter

The **Living Atlas Research Charter** establishes the principles by which the Ministry of Delving Operations observes, documents, interprets, preserves, and publishes knowledge concerning modern Aerathon.

It governs research conducted within the Living Atlas, including records concerning:

- Peoples and cultures
    
- Languages and oral traditions
    
- Geographic regions
    
- Settlements
    
- Governments
    
- Institutions
    
- Faiths and religious practices
    
- Economies and trade
    
- Magical disciplines
    
- Technologies
    
- Bestiary classifications
    
- Labyrinths and anomalies
    
- Relics and material culture
    
- Environmental systems
    
- Planar phenomena
    
- Delvers and expeditionary activity
    
- Contemporary events with lasting public significance
    

The Charter exists because the Ministry’s records possess consequence.

An Atlas entry may shape:

- Law
    
- Trade
    
- Migration
    
- Public reputation
    
- Delver access
    
- Cultural recognition
    
- Property claims
    
- Emergency planning
    
- Research funding
    
- Diplomatic relations
    
- Historical memory
    

A careless description can wound a people as surely as a careless blade.

A false map can kill.

A mistaken classification can become prejudice once repeated beneath an official seal.

The Living Atlas therefore does not merely collect information.

It accepts responsibility for the manner in which knowledge is gathered, interpreted, preserved, and used.

---

## II. The Nature of a Living Record

The Living Atlas records Aerathon as it is presently understood.

It is not an immutable canon.

It is not a sacred text.

It is not a declaration that the Ministry’s knowledge is complete.

Every Atlas entry represents a conclusion reached through the evidence available at the date of filing.

That conclusion may later be:

- Expanded
    
- Clarified
    
- Contested
    
- Corrected
    
- Reclassified
    
- Restricted
    
- Superseded
    
- Withdrawn
    

The Ministry recognizes that revision is not evidence of failure by itself.

A record that changes in response to better evidence has fulfilled its purpose.

A record preserved unchanged despite contrary evidence has become an instrument of institutional pride rather than public knowledge.

### The Living Principle

All Atlas records are governed by the following principle:

> **Accuracy is not measured by permanence. It is measured by the record’s willingness to remain answerable to evidence.**

No title, rank, office, reputation, or political convenience exempts a Ministerial conclusion from review.

---

## III. First Principles of Ministerial Inquiry

All Living Atlas research is expected to uphold six foundational principles.

### Observe Carefully

Researchers must distinguish what was directly observed from what was inferred.

They must account for:

- Conditions of observation
    
- Limits of access
    
- Translation difficulty
    
- Witness reliability
    
- Environmental interference
    
- Magical distortion
    
- Personal bias
    
- Institutional pressure
    
- Missing evidence
    

Observation must not be presented as comprehensive when it was partial.

### Record Honestly

A record must preserve uncertainty, disagreement, failed hypotheses, and contradictory evidence where these materially affect interpretation.

Researchers may not strengthen language merely to satisfy superiors, regional authorities, sponsors, guilds, or public expectation.

The Ministry would rather publish an incomplete truth than a complete falsehood.

### Consult Respectfully

Living communities must not be treated merely as subjects from whom information is extracted.

Where research concerns a living people, researchers must seek meaningful consultation and, where appropriate:

- Community review
    
- Local scholarship
    
- Oral testimony
    
- Translation assistance
    
- Cultural consent
    
- Shared authorship
    
- Restricted filing
    
- Compensation
    
- Return of borrowed or improperly acquired materials
    

The Ministry recognizes that a community possesses knowledge about itself that no external observer can reproduce through distance alone.

### Preserve Context

Information must be recorded with the circumstances necessary to understand it.

A ritual observed during famine must not be described as ordinary abundance practice.

A wartime decree must not be mistaken for permanent law.

A regional custom must not be presented as universal to an entire lineage or people.

A single witness must not become the voice of thousands.

Context is not decoration.

It is part of the evidence.

### Intervene Reluctantly

Research does not automatically grant the Ministry the right to alter what it studies.

Researchers must consider whether observation, excavation, collection, publication, or classification may cause greater harm than ignorance.

The pursuit of knowledge does not erase:

- Sacred boundaries
    
- Local authority
    
- Personal privacy
    
- Burial rights
    
- Ecological vulnerability
    
- Cultural ownership
    
- Public safety
    
- The right to refuse participation
    

### Correct Publicly

When a significant error has entered the public record, correction should occur with sufficient visibility to reach those affected by the original claim.

Quietly replacing a document may protect the archive.

It does not repair the harm caused by the earlier version.

---

## IV. Classes of Evidence

The Living Atlas distinguishes among several forms of evidence.

No category is automatically superior in every circumstance.

### Direct Observation

Information personally witnessed and recorded by qualified observers.

Examples include:

- Measured geography
    
- Documented anatomy
    
- Recorded speech
    
- Observed ceremony
    
- Verified magical response
    
- Confirmed Labyrinth behavior
    
- Direct material examination
    

Direct observation remains limited by the observer’s access, senses, training, and assumptions.

To witness an event is not necessarily to understand it.

### Material Evidence

Physical objects, structures, remains, inscriptions, relics, environmental traces, biological samples, and other tangible evidence.

Material evidence must be accompanied by documented provenance wherever possible.

An object removed from its original context may lose much of its interpretive value.

### Documentary Evidence

Written, engraved, illustrated, encoded, scry-recorded, or otherwise preserved records.

Researchers must consider:

- Authorship
    
- Date
    
- Intended audience
    
- Political motive
    
- Copying history
    
- Translation
    
- Alteration
    
- Suppression
    
- Forgery
    
- Preservation bias
    

An official record is evidence of what an institution recorded.

It is not automatic proof that the institution recorded truthfully.

### Oral Testimony

Spoken histories, witness accounts, songs, recitations, genealogies, ceremonial memory, and community traditions.

Oral testimony must not be dismissed merely because it lacks written form.

Researchers must record:

- The speaker’s relationship to the event
    
- The conditions of transmission
    
- Whether the account is private, sacred, public, or performative
    
- Variations among tellings
    
- Translation concerns
    
- Community rules regarding preservation
    

### Lived Practice

Knowledge established through repeated cultural, professional, ecological, or magical practice.

Examples include:

- Agricultural knowledge
    
- Navigation
    
- Healing customs
    
- Weather-reading
    
- Craft traditions
    
- Delving techniques
    
- Ritual timing
    
- Migration routes
    

Lived practice may preserve accurate knowledge even where its practitioners explain that knowledge through frameworks the Ministry does not share.

### Experimental Evidence

Information produced through controlled testing or repeatable inquiry.

All experiments must document:

- Method
    
- Materials
    
- Conditions
    
- Risks
    
- Failures
    
- Unintended consequences
    
- Ethical approval
    
- Replication attempts
    

Labyrinth phenomena may resist repetition.

Failure to reproduce a result does not always prove the first observation false, particularly where the Labyrinth itself may have changed.

### Comparative Evidence

Conclusions reached through comparison across regions, peoples, sites, specimens, or records.

Comparative work must not assume that superficial resemblance proves common origin.

Similarity may arise through:

- Shared ancestry
    
- Migration
    
- Trade
    
- Convergent development
    
- Imitation
    
- Religious exchange
    
- Environmental pressure
    
- Ministry misclassification
    

### Reconstructed Evidence

Interpretation assembled from incomplete sources.

Reconstruction is common in historical, linguistic, archaeological, and Labyrinth studies.

Reconstructed conclusions must clearly identify:

- Missing material
    
- Assumptions
    
- Competing interpretations
    
- Degree of confidence
    
- Evidence that would require revision
    

### Speculative Interpretation

A theory that may explain available evidence but lacks sufficient confirmation for formal acceptance.

Speculation may be recorded where useful.

It must be labeled as speculation.

No speculative claim may be presented as established knowledge merely because it is elegant, popular, or associated with a respected researcher.

## V. Standards of Certainty

Living Atlas records must distinguish among levels of confidence.

### Verified

Supported by repeatable evidence, multiple reliable sources, direct confirmation, or equivalent proof.

Verification does not mean eternal certainty.

It indicates that no credible competing explanation presently accounts for the evidence as well.

### Strongly Supported

Supported by substantial evidence, though gaps or unresolved objections remain.

### Provisionally Accepted

The most persuasive current interpretation, pending further research.

### Disputed

Supported by evidence but challenged by credible alternative interpretations, communities, institutions, or researchers.

### Unverified

Reported or proposed but not sufficiently confirmed.

### Speculative

Possible, but presently dependent upon assumptions or limited evidence.

### Withdrawn

Previously accepted but no longer supported.

Withdrawn claims may remain visible in archival history when understanding the error is important.

### Indeterminate

Evidence is insufficient, contradictory, inaccessible, corrupted, or impossible to evaluate confidently.

The Ministry recognizes indeterminate as a legitimate conclusion.

Ignorance must not be disguised as certainty.

---

## VI. Language of Responsible Recordkeeping

Living Atlas writers are expected to use language proportionate to the evidence.

Approved formulations include:

- “Current evidence suggests…”
    
- “The Ministry presently recognizes…”
    
- “Several traditions maintain…”
    
- “No consensus has been established…”
    
- “The surviving record is incomplete…”
    
- “This interpretation remains disputed…”
    
- “The following account reflects regional practice rather than universal custom…”
    
- “Further revision is expected…”
    
- “The Ministry has not independently verified this claim…”
    
- “Community scholars contest the standard Ministerial interpretation…”
    

Researchers must avoid language that presents:

- A regional trait as universal
    
- A stereotype as cultural fact
    
- A historical practice as current law
    
- An outsider’s interpretation as a community’s self-understanding
    
- Political claims as neutral geography
    
- Ministerial recognition as the source of a people’s legitimacy
    

The Ministry may recognize a community.

It does not create that community by recognizing it.

## VII. Cultural Research and Community Consultation

Research concerning living peoples requires standards beyond ordinary observation.

### Consultation Requirement

Where reasonably possible, a cultural entry must include consultation with individuals who possess legitimate knowledge of the community being described.

These may include:

- Local scholars
    
- Elders
    
- Cultural officers
    
- Religious custodians
    
- Craftspeople
    
- Oral historians
    
- Community-appointed representatives
    
- Regional researchers
    
- Ordinary residents
    
- Members whose experiences challenge official community narratives
    

No single person may be presumed to represent an entire people.

### Community Review

Communities may review portions of an entry concerning:

- Names
    
- Translation
    
- Sacred practices
    
- Historical claims
    
- Social structure
    
- Political status
    
- Cultural terminology
    
- Restricted information
    
- Harmful misrepresentation
    

Community review does not grant any authority the absolute power to remove criticism, erase internal disagreement, or falsify evidence.

Its purpose is correction and ethical consultation, not political censorship.

### Sacred and Restricted Knowledge

Some knowledge is not considered public by the people who preserve it.

Researchers must determine whether publication would violate:

- Religious duty
    
- Initiatory restriction
    
- Burial custom
    
- Protective secrecy
    
- Ecological stewardship
    
- Community safety
    
- Personal privacy
    
- Ongoing legal protection
    

The Ministry may preserve restricted knowledge within sealed records when necessary for continuity or public safety.

Possession of knowledge does not require its public release.

### Compensation and Attribution

Cultural knowledge provided through significant labor should be compensated where feasible.

Local contributors should receive attribution unless anonymity or safety requires otherwise.

Researchers must not present translated community knowledge as their own discovery.

### The Right to Refuse

Individuals and communities may decline participation.

Refusal may limit the completeness of the record.

It does not authorize coercion.

The absence of Ministerial access must be documented honestly rather than filled with assumption.

---

## VIII. Classification and Its Consequences

Classification allows the Ministry to organize knowledge.

It may also distort what it describes.

Living Atlas classifications must therefore be treated as practical frameworks rather than natural laws.

### Purpose of Classification

Classification may support:

- Public recognition
    
- Accessibility
    
- Legal protection
    
- Research comparison
    
- Medical care
    
- Delver accommodation
    
- Translation
    
- Historical preservation
    
- Emergency planning
    

### Risks of Classification

Classification may:

- Erase local identity
    
- Combine unrelated peoples
    
- Divide connected peoples
    
- Freeze changing traditions
    
- Reinforce prejudice
    
- Privilege biology over culture
    
- Treat administrative convenience as truth
    
- Become a basis for exclusion
    

Researchers must state the scope and limits of major classifications.

Where a community rejects a Ministry term, that rejection should be recorded.

### Self-Identification

The Ministry should recognize self-identification wherever it does not create immediate conflict with legal safety requirements.

A person may possess several identities simultaneously.

Lineage, homeland, language, religion, citizenship, household, and cultural affiliation must not be collapsed into one category merely for easier filing.

---

## IX. Researcher Conduct

Ministerial researchers act beneath the authority of the MDO and are accountable for how that authority is perceived.

They must not:

- Misrepresent their identity or purpose
    
- Coerce participation
    
- Promise legal outcomes they cannot provide
    
- Remove materials without authorization
    
- Enter sacred or private sites through deception
    
- Conceal known dangers from local participants
    
- Publish identifying information that creates foreseeable harm
    
- Trade access for political favor
    
- Accept sponsorship conditions that compromise findings
    
- Treat community disagreement as ignorance
    
- Use emergency powers for ordinary research convenience
    

### Conflicts of Interest

Researchers must disclose meaningful relationships to:

- Governments
    
- Guilds
    
- Delver companies
    
- Noble Houses
    
- Faiths
    
- Commercial sponsors
    
- Claimants to land or relics
    
- Communities under study
    
- Competing academic offices
    

A conflict does not always disqualify a researcher.

An undisclosed conflict may invalidate the work.

### Personal Bias

Researchers are expected to acknowledge that complete neutrality is impossible.

They must instead practice disciplined transparency.

A researcher’s homeland, lineage, faith, profession, political allegiance, and prior experiences may affect interpretation.

These influences should be considered during review.

---

## X. Field Research and Expedition Standards

Field research must be designed around both knowledge and survival.

### Preparation

Before deployment, researchers should establish:

- Research purpose
    
- Jurisdiction
    
- Local permissions
    
- Known hazards
    
- Required specialists
    
- Translation needs
    
- Medical support
    
- Preservation equipment
    
- Evacuation procedure
    
- Evidence-handling protocol
    
- Communication schedule
    
- Public release expectations
    

### Field Notes

Original notes must distinguish:

- Observation
    
- Testimony
    
- Interpretation
    
- Rumor
    
- Emotional response
    
- Unconfirmed inference
    

Researchers should preserve uncertainty as it occurred rather than reconstructing false confidence after returning safely.

### Specimen and Artifact Collection

Collection must be limited to what is necessary, lawful, and ethically defensible.

Researchers must consider:

- Whether removal will damage the site
    
- Whether the object belongs to a living community
    
- Whether it is funerary or sacred
    
- Whether it is dangerous
    
- Whether it can be studied in place
    
- Whether local custody is preferable
    
- Whether collection creates commercial or military risk
    

### Labyrinth Research

Labyrinth research requires additional caution because the act of observation may alter the environment.

Researchers must account for:

- Shifting architecture
    
- Memory effects
    
- False records
    
- Temporal distortion
    
- Mimicked witnesses
    
- Replicated objects
    
- Hostile interpretation
    
- Observer-dependent phenomena
    
- Contaminated samples
    
- Effects that appear only after exit
    

No conclusion should rest solely upon a single Labyrinth expedition when repetition is possible.

When repetition is impossible, the limitation must remain prominent.

---

## XI. Publication and Public Access

The Living Atlas is a public institution, but not every record is immediately public.

### Presumption of Access

Records should be publicly accessible unless a specific and defensible harm justifies restriction.

Potential grounds for restriction include:

- Immediate public danger
    
- Active containment operations
    
- Personal privacy
    
- Sacred confidentiality
    
- Location of vulnerable communities
    
- Breeding or emergence grounds
    
- Restricted relic methods
    
- Unresolved military risk
    
- Ongoing criminal investigation
    
- Information likely to trigger reckless delving
    

### Narrow Restriction

Restrictions should be no broader or longer than necessary.

A dangerous section may be sealed without sealing an entire cultural entry.

A location may be obscured while ecological findings remain public.

A witness may be anonymized while testimony is preserved.

### Commercial Secrecy

Commercial embarrassment, threatened profit, or institutional inconvenience are not sufficient grounds for restricting a record.

### Classified Knowledge

Some records may be restricted under emergency or security law.

Such restrictions must identify:

- Issuing authority
    
- Legal basis
    
- Review date
    
- Scope
    
- Conditions for release
    

Indefinite secrecy requires renewed justification.

---

## XII. Peer Review and Institutional Challenge

No significant Living Atlas entry should depend upon the judgment of one researcher alone.

### Review

Major entries should be reviewed by appropriate specialists, which may include:

- Regional scholars
    
- Cultural consultants
    
- Linguists
    
- Historians
    
- Cartographers
    
- Ecologists
    
- Medical researchers
    
- Relic specialists
    
- Planar scholars
    
- Legal officers
    
- Accessibility reviewers
    
- Community representatives
    

### Dissenting Opinion

A reviewer may submit a formal dissent.

Where the disagreement concerns material interpretation rather than minor wording, the dissent may be attached to the filing.

The Ministry does not require the appearance of unanimity where unanimity does not exist.

### External Challenge

Recognized communities, researchers, governments, guilds, and affected individuals may challenge an Atlas entry.

A valid challenge should identify:

- The disputed passage
    
- The alleged error or harm
    
- Supporting evidence
    
- The requested correction
    
- Any urgency created by continued publication
    

Lack of formal education does not invalidate a challenge supported by knowledge and evidence.

---

## XIII. Corrections, Retractions, and Revision

Corrections are part of the Living Atlas’s ordinary operation.

### Minor Correction

Used for:

- Spelling
    
- Link repair
    
- Formatting
    
- Clerical dates
    
- Non-substantive clarification
    

### Substantive Revision

Used when interpretation, classification, historical understanding, cultural description, legal status, or safety guidance changes materially.

Substantive revisions must preserve a summary of what changed.

### Public Correction

Required when an error has likely caused:

- Reputational harm
    
- Legal harm
    
- Cultural harm
    
- Safety risk
    
- Discriminatory treatment
    
- Improper territorial claims
    
- Misuse of Ministerial authority
    

### Retraction

A record may be retracted when:

- Evidence was fabricated
    
- Methods were fundamentally invalid
    
- Sources were knowingly misrepresented
    
- Publication created unacceptable harm
    
- Conclusions are no longer defensible
    
- Institutional interference corrupted the filing
    

Retraction does not erase the record from archival history.

The withdrawn document should remain traceable for review unless preserving it would create continuing danger.

### Supersession

A new document may supersede an older filing when the older record remains historically relevant but no longer represents current understanding.

## XIV. Research Harm and Institutional Responsibility

Research can cause harm even when its findings are accurate.

The Ministry must therefore consider both truth and consequence.

Potential harms include:

- Exposure of sacred places
    
- Exploitation of natural resources
    
- Tourism into vulnerable communities
    
- Commercial harvesting
    
- Political persecution
    
- Misuse of biological traits
    
- Militarization of cultural knowledge
    
- Stigmatization
    
- Theft of artifacts
    
- Destruction of Labyrinth sites
    
- Retaliation against witnesses
    

Researchers are not permitted to avoid all difficult findings merely because they may be politically inconvenient.

They are required to consider how publication may be structured to reduce foreseeable harm.

### Duty of Repair

Where Ministry research has caused demonstrable harm, possible remedies may include:

- Public correction
    
- Formal apology
    
- Return of materials
    
- Compensation
    
- Revised classification
    
- Restricted access
    
- Funding for local preservation
    
- Community-led replacement research
    
- Disciplinary action
    
- Legal restitution
    

The Ministry’s duty does not end when a document is corrected.

---

## XV. Prohibited Practices

The following practices are prohibited under the Charter:

- Fabrication of evidence
    
- Deliberate mistranslation
    
- Destruction of contradictory records
    
- Concealment of material conflicts of interest
    
- Forced cultural participation
    
- Unauthorized removal of funerary objects
    
- Publication of sacred knowledge acquired through deception
    
- Falsification of community consent
    
- Retaliation against correction petitioners
    
- Reclassification for political punishment
    
- Research designed primarily to justify a predetermined conclusion
    
- Suppression of safety findings for commercial advantage
    
- Claiming certainty known not to exist
    
- Altering evidence to preserve institutional reputation
    

Violation may result in removal from office, revocation of research seal, criminal referral, restitution, or public censure.

---

## XVI. The Limits of Ministerial Knowledge

The Ministry possesses the largest organized research body in Aerathon.

It does not therefore possess all knowledge.

Many truths remain beyond its reach because:

- Records were destroyed
    
- Peoples declined participation
    
- Languages were lost
    
- Sites remain inaccessible
    
- Labyrinths altered evidence
    
- Witnesses disagree
    
- Governments concealed archives
    
- Researchers misunderstood what they observed
    
- The phenomenon itself changed
    
- The correct questions were never asked
    

The Ministry must not confuse the absence of an Atlas entry with the absence of knowledge.

A village elder may know what the archive does not.

A delver may have witnessed what no instrument recorded.

A ritual may preserve history the Ministry once dismissed as metaphor.

An old warning may prove accurate after generations of scholarly ridicule.

The Living Atlas exists to gather knowledge.

It must never become an excuse to disregard knowledge merely because it was gathered elsewhere.

---

## XVII. Charter Summary

All Living Atlas work should answer five questions:

1. **What is being claimed?**
    
2. **What evidence supports it?**
    
3. **What remains uncertain?**
    
4. **Who was consulted or excluded?**
    
5. **What harm may result from publication or error?**
    

A record that cannot answer these questions may still be useful.

It is not ready to be treated as authoritative.

> [!note] Public Research Notice  
> Living Atlas entries represent the best-supported Ministerial understanding available at the date of filing.
> 
> They may include disputed claims, incomplete evidence, regional variations, community objections, and provisional interpretations.
> 
> Ministerial classification does not supersede lawful self-identification, community memory, sacred authority, or regional knowledge.
> 
> Any person or recognized community materially represented within an Atlas entry may petition for correction, clarification, restricted handling, attribution, or formal review.
> 
> The existence of a Ministry record does not guarantee that the Ministry was the first, only, or most rightful keeper of the knowledge it contains.
